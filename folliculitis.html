Introduction to Follicular Disorders

This chapter covers a range of conditions affecting the hair follicle, categorized broadly into:

  • Superficial Folliculitis: E.g., Staphylococcus aureus or Pseudomonas aeruginosa infections. (Aka Impetigo of Bockhart)
  • Deep Folliculitis: E.g., Acne keloidalis, Pseudofolliculitis barbae.
  • Disorders of Follicular Keratinization: Common feature is prominent keratotic follicular plugs.
    • Reviewed elsewhere: Lichen planopilaris, Pityriasis Rubra Pilaris (PRP), Keratosis Pilaris.
    • Discussed here: Lichen spinulosus, Erythromelanosis follicularis faciei.
  • Inflammatory Disorders with Follicular Predilection: E.g., GVHD, papular eczema, pityriasis rosea (in darkly pigmented skin).
  • Viral-Associated Trichodysplasia.
  • Trichostasis Spinulosa.

Superficial Folliculitis (Impetigo of Bockhart)

Clinical Presentation

  • Follicular pustules, often on an erythematous base.
  • If no pustules: follicular-based erythematous papules or a superimposed collarette of scale serve as clues.
  • Favored Areas: Areas with terminal hairs (scalp, beard), upper trunk, buttocks, lower extremities. Also occluded areas.
  • Predisposing Factor: Shaving, especially against the direction of hair growth.

Microbiology & Pathology

  • Bacterial culture of pustular contents: Most commonly normal flora, followed by Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Pathology:
    • Perifollicular infiltrate of lymphocytes, neutrophils, and macrophages.
    • Extension of neutrophils into follicular epithelium and canal, variably forming an abscess.
    • Late-stage: Follicular rupture and granulomatous response.
Clinical Clue for Eosinophilic Folliculitis

Edematous lesions of folliculitis are most suggestive of eosinophilic folliculitis.

Differential Diagnosis

  • In patients confined to bed, folliculitis on the posterior trunk needs to be distinguished from:
    • Grover disease
    • Miliaria rubra
    • Cutaneous candidiasis

Treatment Approach

  • Depends on the particular type of folliculitis, culture results, and severity.
  • For patients whose cultures fail to demonstrate a pathogenic microbe, treatments are similar to acne.

Less Common Specific Forms of Folliculitis

This table summarizes key features. Pay attention to etiology, specific clinical clues, and first-line treatments.

Type of Folliculitis Etiology / Risk Factors Clinical Features Therapy
Irritant Folliculitis
  • Application of topical medications (e.g., tar preparations), ointments.
  • Exacerbated by application opposite hair growth.
  • Follicular pustules at sites of application/exposure.
  • Favors areas with terminal hairs.
  • Discontinue inciting agent.
  • Apply meds in direction of hair growth.
  • Topical mid-potency corticosteroid lotion.
Gram-Negative Folliculitis
  • Due primarily to Klebsiella, Escherichia coli, Enterobacter, Proteus spp.
  • Usually in patients with acne vulgaris receiving long-term antibiotic therapy.
  • Adult men with oily skin.
  • Pustules in facial T-zone and perinasal distribution.
  • Topical: gentamicin, benzoyl peroxide.
  • Systemic: Quinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin), , ampicillin, or trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole.
  • Severe/recurrent: Isotretinoin (0.5-1 mg/kg/day for up to 16 weeks).
Hot Tub Folliculitis
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa from hot tub/whirlpool use 12-48 hours prior.
  • Edematous, pink to red follicular papules and pustules, primarily on trunk. (Fig. 38.4)
  • Often pruritic; resolves spontaneously in immunocompetent hosts.
  • May be serious in immunocompromised.
  • Generally self-limited; can use antibacterial soap.
  • Severe/immunocompromised: Ciprofloxacin (500 mg PO BID for 7-14 days).
  • Prevention: prevention with adequate chlorine levels (swimming pools 2–4 mg/L, hot tubs 3–5 mg/L, pH 7.2–7.4 and change water q6-8 weeks in hot tubs.
Dermatophyte Folliculitis
Tinea Barbae
  • Usually Trichophyton mentagrophytes or T. verrucosum.
  • Classically in male farm workers (zoophilic source).
  • Inflammatory papules and pustules in beard area (more often than mustache), with crusting.
  • Loosened hairs can be removed without pain.
  • Topical antifungals (may be ineffective alone).
  • Terbinafine (250 mg/day PO for 2-3 weeks).
  • Griseofulvin (micronized 500-1000 mg/day or ultramicronized 500-750 mg/day PO for 4-6 weeks).
  • Itraconazole (200 mg PO BID for 1 week/month, for 2 pulses).
  • Immunosuppressed may need longer course.
Majocchi Granuloma
  • Usually T. rubrum.
  • Characteristically in women who shave their legs.
  • Additional risk factors: occlusion, immunosuppression, potent topical corticosteroids.
  • Follicular pustules and follicular papules or nodules. (Fig. 38.5A)
  • Most often on lower legs.
  • Pustules at edges of tinea cruris or corporis.
Malassezia (Pityrosporum) Folliculitis
  • Typically young adults.
  • Aggravating factors: warm weather, occlusion, excessive sebum.
  • Association: antibiotic therapy (esp. tetracyclines), iatrogenic immunosuppression (e.g., organ transplant).
  • Pruritic follicular papules and occasionally pustules.
  • Location: Back, chest, shoulders.
  • Central white-yellow color often represents compact keratin, not pus.
  • KOH prep of follicular contents: abundant yeast forms.
  • Topical: Antifungals (ketoconazole shampoo), selenium sulfide shampoo, 50% propylene glycol in water.
  • Systemic: Fluconazole (100-200 mg/day for 3 weeks or 200-300 mg once weekly for 1-4 months); Itraconazole (200 mg/day for 1-3 weeks).
Candida Folliculitis
  • Primarily in diabetics.
  • Antibiotics, corticosteroids.
  • Pruritic satellite pustules surrounding areas of intertriginous candidiasis.
  • Facial lesions may mimic tinea barbae or acne rosacea.
  • Prevent skin-to-skin contact.
  • If possible, D/C antibiotics/corticosteroids.
  • Mild/moderate: Topical antifungals.
  • Recalcitrant/severe: Fluconazole (100 mg daily for 1 week then QOD for 1 month).
Herpes Simplex Folliculitis (Herpetic Sycosis)
  • Usually men with recurrent localized facial herpes simplex who shave with a blade razor.
  • Widespread/unusual presentations in HIV-infected or immunosuppressed.
  • Rapid development of individual and grouped follicular pustules and vesicles on erythematous base in beard area.
  • Tzanck smear or biopsy: multinucleated giant cells.
  • Positive PCR or DFA.
  • Acyclovir (200 mg PO 5x/day for 5-10 days).
  • Famciclovir (500 mg PO TID for 5-10 days).
  • Valacyclovir (500 mg PO TID for 5-10 days). (Note: Dosing may vary, often 1g BID for valacyclovir in active infection)
Demodex Folliculitis (Demodicosis)
  • May be associated with immune suppression.
  • Erythematous follicular papules and pustules on face (esp. nose) and neck. (Fig. 38.5B)
  • May have background diffuse erythema.
  • Skin scrapings: numerous Demodex mites. (Fig. 38.5D)
  • Topical: 1% ivermectin cream, 5% permethrin cream.
  • Systemic: Single dose ivermectin (200 mcg/kg PO).
Drug-Induced Folliculitis (Acneiform Eruption)
  • Most common in acne-prone patients and age groups.
  • Common drugs: Corticosteroids, androgenic hormones, EGFR inhibitors, MEK inhibitors, sirolimus, iodides, bromides, lithium, isoniazid, anticonvulsants.
  • Develops within 2 weeks of starting; risk often dose/duration proportional.
  • Acute eruption of monomorphic erythematous follicular papules and pustules.
  • Location: Trunk, shoulders, upper arms. (Figs 21.19B & 36.13)
  • EGFRi: Multiple papulopustules face/scalp, sometimes scale-crust. (Figs 21.19B & 36.15)
  • Unlike acne vulgaris, comedones are RARELY present (exception: androgens).
  • Discontinue offending drug if possible.
  • Topical: benzoyl peroxide, clindamycin, erythromycin, retinoids.
  • Systemic: Tetracycline, doxycycline, minocycline.
Necrotizing Infundibular Crystalline Folliculitis
  • Yeasts and Gram-positive bacteria in affected follicles.
  • Waxy papules favoring forehead, neck, and back.
  • Birefringent, filamentous, crystalline deposits within follicular ostia.
  • Topical or systemic antimycotics.
Actinic Folliculitis
  • Seasonal, follows initial summer sun exposure.
  • Sunscreens will not prevent. (Text: "sion ornate;" likely typo, refers to sun exposure).
  • Development of follicular pustules on upper trunk and arms 24-30 hours after first sun exposure of summer.
  • Lesions spare the face and are not pruritic.
  • Usually self-limited.
  • Consider avoiding intense sun exposure initially. If severe, standard folliculitis treatments. (Text: "if severe" implies tx like other folliculitis).

Clinical Approach to Superficial Folliculitis

Diagnostic Strategy

Categorize by etiology. Remember which require specific diagnostic tests like KOH, Tzanck, or biopsy.

Etiological Categories:

  • Bacterial:
    • Staphylococcus aureus (Common)
    • Gram-negative bacilli (including Pseudomonas spp.)
  • Fungal:
    • Dermatophytes*
    • Malassezia (Pityrosporum) spp.*
    • Candida spp.*,††
  • Viral:
    • Herpesvirus**,† (HSV > VZV)
  • Ectoparasitic:
    • Demodex spp.*,††
  • Non-infectious:
    • Irritant folliculitis
    • Drug-induced folliculitis
    • Eosinophilic pustular folliculitis (Ofuji disease)†
    • Eosinophilic pustular folliculitis in infancy†
    • Immunosuppression- (or HIV-) associated Eosinophilic pustular folliculitis†

* Requires skin scraping and KOH prep/microscopy for diagnosis.
** Requires skin scraping for Tzanck prep/microscopy, viral culture.
† Often requires biopsy for diagnosis.
†† Need to consider more strongly in immunosuppressed individuals.

Eosinophilic Folliculitis

Three major forms have been described. The common thread is eosinophilic inflammation targeting the follicle.

Eosinophilic Pustular Folliculitis (EPF) - Ofuji Disease

Not to be Confused!

Ofuji disease (EPF) vs. Papuloerythroderma of Ofuji (distinct entity).

  • Epidemiology: Rare, MC in Japan, M:F = 5:1, onset ~30 years.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Follicular papulopustules, erythematous patches/plaques with coalescent pustules.
    • Central clearing leads to figurate lesions.
    • Location: Most commonly "acne-prone" areas (face > upper extremities, trunk).
    • Digits, palms, and soles may be involved (proving not all lesions are follicular).
    • Severe pruritus is frequent.
    • Recurrent crops of lesions (involute over 7-10 days, relapse q3-4 weeks).
    • No accompanying systemic symptoms.
  • Labs: Peripheral blood eosinophilia.
  • Associations: To date, not associated with systemic disease.
  • Pathogenesis: Unknown; ?hypersensitivity to antigenic stimuli (infections, meds), immune dysfunction.
  • Histology: Eosinophils predominate around and within follicular epithelium. Hallmark: infundibular eosinophilic pustules.
  • Treatment:
    • Topical corticosteroids, tacrolimus ointment, oral antihistamines for pruritus.
    • First-line: Oral indomethacin (50 mg/day).
    • Second-line: UVB phototherapy, oral minocycline (100 mg BID), oral dapsone (100-200 mg/day for ≥2 weeks), systemic corticosteroids, colchicine (0.6 mg BID).
    • Refractory: Cyclosporine.

Immunosuppression-Associated Eosinophilic Pustular Folliculitis

  • Clinical Features:
    • Markedly pruritic follicular papules (urticarial and/or pustular).
    • Location: Face, scalp, upper trunk.
  • Setting: Immunosuppression (HIV infection, allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation [HSCT]).
  • HIV Context: CD4 count usually ≤200 cells/mm³; may be IRIS manifestation.
  • Histology: Spongiosis and exocytosis of eosinophils and lymphocytes into follicular epithelium. (Similar to Ofuji but no large coalescent pustules or figurate lesions; individual lesions more persistent).
  • Associations: HIV, lymphoma, CLL, AML, other myeloproliferative disorders, post-allogeneic HSCT.
  • Pathogenesis: Th2 immune response implicated.
    • AIDS: Elevated IL-4, IL-5, RANTES (CCL5), eotaxin (CCL11).
    • Recent study: Elevated serum CCL17, CCL26 (eotaxin-3), CCL27 in HIV-associated EPF.
  • Clinical Presentation (Detailed):
    • Chronic, pruritic, follicular papular eruption (face, scalp, upper trunk).
    • Papules often slightly edematous; pustules may or may not be present.
    • Intense pruritus.
    • Some patients may have lymphopenia.
  • Differential Diagnosis:
    • Necrotizing eosinophilic folliculitis: Distinguished by association with atopy, presence of nodules/ulceration, follicular necrosis, and eosinophilic vasculitis histologically.
  • Treatment:
    • HIV-infected: Treat underlying HIV (ART); rise in CD4 may resolve lesions.
    • All patients: Oral/topical antipruritics + topical corticosteroids.
    • If inadequate: UVB phototherapy.
    • Additional: Topical tacrolimus, topical permethrin, oral itraconazole (200-400 mg daily), oral metronidazole (250 mg TID), oral antibiotics, isotretinoin (0.5-1 mg/kg/day x 1-4 weeks, then taper), interferon (ß and γ).

Eosinophilic Pustular Folliculitis of Infancy

  • Nature: Unusual, self-limiting disorder.
  • Onset: Typically early infancy. Disease onset prior to age 14 months in 95% of patients.
  • Gender: More common in boys (ratio 4:1).
  • Clinical Features:
    • Multiple, pruritic, follicular pustules and vesiculopustules on an erythematous base.
    • Location: Primarily scalp and brow region.
    • Secondary crusting is common.
    • Cyclical course: Recurrences at 1-12 week intervals (characteristic).
  • Histology: Dense perifollicular infiltrate of eosinophils and lymphocytes (may spare sebaceous glands and outer root sheath). Typically, eosinophilic spongiosis and clusters of eosinophils in hair follicle (esp. infundibular region).
  • Key Differences from Adult EPF: Primarily involves scalp, frequent secondary crusting, lack of annular configuration.
  • Prognosis: Majority experience resolution by 3 years of age.
  • Differential Diagnosis:
    • Erythema toxicum neonatorum
    • Transient neonatal pustular melanosis
    • Acropustulosis of infancy (might be related)
    • Scabies
  • Treatment: Given self-limited nature, symptomatic relief: topical corticosteroids (CS) and antihistamines for itchiness.

Disseminated and Recurrent Infundibulofolliculitis (DRIF)

  • Epidemiology: Seen primarily in adults with darkly pigmented skin.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Hundreds of uniform, skin-colored papules likened to "goose bumps".
    • Favored sites: Trunk, neck, upper extremities.
    • May last for months to years; can be pruritic.
    • Papules are often pierced by a hair.
  • Histology: Mild inflammation of the follicular infundibulum.
  • Associations: Although lesions may resemble papular eczema, many patients do not have a history of atopy.
  • Differential Diagnosis:
    • Papular eczema
    • Lichen nitidus (not follicular based)
    • Folliculitis (has erythema)
    • Distinguished from KP, PRP, LPP, phrynoderma by lack of keratotic plugs or spines within hair follicles.
  • Treatment:
    • Topical corticosteroids (sometimes effective).
    • Lactic acid preparations (12%) or 20-40% urea.
    • If topical unsuccessful: PUVA (thrice weekly for 3 weeks, then maintenance).
    • Vitamin A (50,000 IU BID) and isotretinoin (0.5 mg/kg/day for 16 weeks) have been tried.

Disorders of Follicular Keratinization

Characterized by a prominent plug of keratin within the follicular orifice. (KP, PRP, LPP discussed elsewhere).

Erythromelanosis Follicularis Faciei (et Colli)

  • Epidemiology:
    • Begins during the second decade of life.
    • Adults: ?Men. All races and both sexes, but favors people of Asian ancestry.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Location: Lateral aspect of cheeks, sometimes lateral neck.
    • Background of red-brown colored skin (vasodilation + hyperpigmentation).
    • Numerous pinhead-sized follicular papules within involved areas, appearing relatively hypopigmented.
    • May also have keratosis pilaris of upper outer arms (with small rim of erythema around follicular keratotic plug).
  • Histology:
    • Slight follicular hyperkeratosis with increased epidermal pigmentation.
    • Decreased diameters of hair shafts and outer root sheaths; decreased thickness of inner root sheath.
    • Adnexa often surrounded by lymphocytic infiltrate.
    • Increased thickness and compactness of horny layer.
  • Differential Diagnosis:
    • Keratosis pilaris rubra: Lacks hyperpigmentation, favors children.
    • Melasma + telangiectatic erythema (photodamage): Lacks tiny follicular papules.
    • Poikiloderma of Civatte: Anterolateral neck, sparing mid-submental. Erythema from multiple interfollicular telangiectasias, sparing immediate perifollicular skin (appears hypopigmented). Can extend to mandibular region.
    • Keratosis Pilaris Atrophicans Spectrum (less often):
      • Ulerythema ophryogenes: Erythematous follicular papules, early childhood, favors eyebrows, follicular atrophy, scarring alopecia lateral eyebrows.
      • Atrophoderma vermiculatum: Affects cheeks, honeycomb scarring or "worm-eaten" appearance.
  • Treatment (largely anecdotal):
    • Topical keratolytics: Urea cream (10-20%), ammonium lactate lotion (6-12%), tretinoin cream (0.025-0.1%), adapalene (0.1-0.3%), ammonium lactate + hydroquinone (4%) cream.
    • Topical tacalcitol ointment (daily for 12 weeks): reduced roughness/scaling, limited effect on erythema.
    • Peels: Salicylic acid (20-30%), glycolic acid.
    • Severe: Limited courses of isotretinoin (0.1-1 mg/kg/day).
    • Laser for background erythema/hyperpigmentation (multiple sessions).

Keratosis Pilaris Atrophicans (KPA)

Group of disorders: follicular keratinization + atrophy + scarring alopecia. Treatment (limited efficacy): Keratolytics, topical retinoids, topical/IL corticosteroids, oral antibiotics, phototherapy. Combination therapy may benefit. Anecdotally: oral retinoids, IPL. Later stages: Laser resurfacing, dermabrasion, dermal fillers for atrophic scarring.

Lichen Spinulosus (LS)

  • Clinical Features:
    • Clusters of multiple follicular papules, each with a keratotic spine.
    • Texture likened to a "nutmeg grater".
    • Usually asymptomatic, arises suddenly, enlarges over a week.
    • Appears most often in children and adolescents. Average onset 16 years.
    • Favors: Neck, extensor arms, abdomen, buttocks. Distribution may be symmetric.
    • Face usually spared, as are hands and feet.
    • Can persist indefinitely.
    • Idiopathic LS: asymptomatic, though some pruritic with faint surrounding erythema.
    • Spinulosis of the face (tiny follicular keratotic spicules of cheeks) may be a manifestation.
  • Etiology: Unknown. Most patients do not have associated systemic abnormalities.
  • Associations (reported, some possibly coincidental):
    • LS-like lesions in HIV infection.
      • HIV-associated follicular syndrome / Type VI PRP: HIV infection, PRP, nodulocystic acne, follicular spines (including LS).
    • Seborrheic dermatitis, drug reaction (omeprazole), Hodgkin disease, Crohn disease, syphilis, id reaction to fungal infections.
  • Pathology: Similar to Keratosis Pilaris.
  • Differential Diagnosis (Key Distinctions):

    See table below for specific DDx details.

    • Phrynoderma
    • Keratosis circumscripta
    • Follicular ichthyosis
    • Juvenile pityriasis rubra pilaris
    • Facial Spines DDx: HIV-associated follicular syndrome, viral-associated trichodysplasia, demodicosis, follicular mucinosis, multiple myeloma.
    • Widespread follicular plugging (drug-induced): BRAF inhibitors, systemic lithium therapy.
  • Treatment:
    • Topical: 12% lactic acid, 20%-40% urea, 6% salicylic acid (improve texture).
    • Anecdotal: Tacalcitol cream or tretinoin gel + hydroactive adhesive (e.g., Bioré®).
    • Peels: Glycolic acid, salicylic acid.

Phrynoderma ("Toad Skin")

  • Epidemiology:
    • Primarily in Asia and Africa.
    • Rare in high-income countries, except in: intestinal malabsorption, anorexia nervosa, fad diets.
  • Etiology: Most often associated with Vitamin A deficiency. Some patients may have deficiencies in other vitamins and/or proven malnutrition.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Follicular papules of various sizes with conical keratotic plugs.
    • Favors extensor surfaces of extremities, may spread.
    • Face is last site involved; hands and feet spared.
  • Diagnosis:
    • Associated systemic findings (e.g., ocular, CNS).
    • Histologic features.
    • Measurement of serum vitamin A levels.

Deep Folliculitis

Characterized by firm, tender, erythematous papules or nodules, up to 2 cm. Critical Patient Advice: Deep lesions should NEVER be manipulated (squeezed) by the patient due to risk of bacteremia or seeding (endocarditis, brain abscess, osteomyelitis).

Major forms include: Furuncles, Sycosis (barbae, lupoid, mycotic), Pseudofolliculitis barbae, Acne keloidalis, Hidradenitis suppurativa.

Sycosis is defined as chronic inflammation of hair follicles, especially of the beard.

Pseudofolliculitis Barbae (PFB)

  • Description: Common chronic disorder, most often in beard area of men who shave.
  • Other Affected: Women who shave (especially groin). Mustache area not typically affected.
  • Epidemiology: Favors those of African descent with darkly pigmented skin and tightly curled hair.
  • Etiology: Tightly curled hairs curving back into the skin after shaving.
    • Intrafollicular penetration: Hair fails to exit follicle, pierces follicular wall.
    • Transfollicular penetration: Hair exits follicle, then re-enters skin.
    • Inflammatory response to hair keratin.
  • Genetic Risk Factor: SNP in K6hf (KRT75) hair follicle companion layer-specific keratin.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Range from inflammatory papules and pustules to firm papules and keloidal scars.
    • Chronic lesions: Hair may create grooves in skin (anterior neck, submandibular areas prone).
    • Once grooves form, shaving becomes extremely difficult.
  • Treatment Goal: Only reliable cure is to stop shaving. Shaving techniques can be optimized, laser therapy can significantly reduce severity.

Pathogenesis of Pseudofolliculitis Barbae

1. Curved Hair, Curved Follicle: Predisposition due to hair morphology.
2. Hair Re-entry:
  • Transfollicular: Hair exits and curves back into the epidermis.
  • Intrafollicular: Hair pierces the follicular wall before exiting.
3. Inflammatory Response: Foreign body reaction to hair keratin entering the dermis.

Advice on Shaving Methods for Patients with PFB

Important Points:
  • Do not pull the skin taut.
  • Do not shave against the grain/direction of hair growth.
  • Use a sharp razor each time, preferably multi-blade.
  • Take short strokes (with the grain) and do not shave over same areas more than twice.
Method:
  1. Remove pre-existing hairs with electric clippers, leaving ~1-2 mm stubble.
  2. Wash area with non-abrasive acne soap and rough washcloth; gentle massaging with soft toothbrush in areas with "ingrown hairs".
  3. Rinse, then compress face with warm tap water for several minutes.
  4. Apply shaving cream, massage lather (do not let dry).
  5. Use sharp blade, shave with the grain using short even strokes with minimal tension (no more than twice over one area). In hard-to-shave areas, may need to shave against the grain (use caution).
  6. After shaving, rinse, apply soothing aftershave. If burning/itching, topical corticosteroid cream/lotion (1-2.5% hydrocortisone) can be alternative.

Therapeutic Approach to Pseudofolliculitis Barbae

  • Shaving:
    • Mild-moderate: Continue daily shaving, follow guidelines (Table 38.5 in source).
    • Severe: Discontinue shaving until inflammation clears and ingrown hairs released. Trim beard to min 0.5 cm with scissors/clippers.
  • Compresses and Release of Ingrown Hairs:
    • Warm tap water, saline, or Burow's solution compresses (10 min TID) to soothe, remove crusts, reduce drainage, soften epidermis.
  • Topical Therapy:
    • Low-potency topical corticosteroid lotion and/or topical clindamycin after compresses and freeing embedded hairs.
  • Secondary Bacterial Infection:
    • Appropriate systemic antibiotic based on cultures.
  • Recalcitrant Disease:
    • Prednisone (45-60 mg) or equivalent QAM for 7-10 days may be necessary.
    • Topical eflornithine cream BID may be helpful*. (Slows rate of excessive hair growth. Injectable eflornithine treats African sleeping sickness).
    • Laser hair removal (appropriate laser for skin color) for permanent hair reduction (see Ch. 137).

*Based upon case series.

Acne Keloidalis (Nuchae)

  • Description: Begins as chronic folliculitis of posterior neck and occipital scalp. Over time, keloidal papules and plaques develop.
  • Comedones are NOT present (contrast to acne vulgaris).
  • +/- alopecia.
  • +/- subcutaneous abscesses with draining sinuses.
  • Epidemiology: Vast majority are young men of African descent (M:F ratio 20:1).
  • Pathogenesis Theories:
    • Shaving/cutting short, tightly curled hairs that curve back and penetrate skin.
    • Constant irritation from shirt collars, chronic low-grade folliculitis.
    • Increased mast cell density → rubbing → predisposes to folliculitis.
  • Pathology:
    • Inflammation begins in upper 1/3 of hair follicle.
    • Initial infiltrate: Neutrophils and lymphocytes OR predominantly plasma cells (varies).
    • Sebaceous glands markedly diminished or absent in all stages.
    • Advanced lesions: Disrupted hair follicles, fragments of naked hair shafts surrounded by granulomatous inflammation.
  • Treatment: (Refer to eTable 38.1 for full options)
    • Prevention (First Line):
      • Avoid head-dress/gear causing mechanical irritation to posterior hairline.
      • Do not edge posterior hairline with razor.
      • Avoid shirts/sweaters irritating posterior scalp/neck.
    • Mild/Non-inflamed: Twice-daily tretinoin gel + mid- to high-potency corticosteroid gel.
    • Inflamed/Pustules: Topical or systemic antibiotics (as for acne vulgaris).
    • Keloidal Papules:
      • Intralesional triamcinolone acetonide (5-20 mg/ml).
      • If persistent: Punch excision (hair transplant trephine punch) below follicle level, then primary closure or second intention healing.
        • Anesthesia mix: 50:50 2% lidocaine (with epinephrine) + triamcinolone (40 mg/ml) to prevent recurrence.
      • Post-suture removal (1 week): Inject excision sites with triamcinolone (40 mg/ml), repeat 3 more times at 3-week intervals.
    • Plaques 1.0-1.5 cm vertical diameter: Excision with primary closure.
    • Plaques >1.5 cm vertical diameter:
      • Do NOT close primarily (scar usually spreads).
      • Excise to fascia/deep subcutaneous tissue, include posterior hairline in ellipse, heal by second intention (8-12 weeks).
      • Do NOT inject corticosteroids (may prevent wound contracture).
    • Postoperative: Daily topical imiquimod (starting immediately, for 6-8 weeks) may prevent recurrence (some claim).
    • Other options:
      • Radiation therapy post-excision (controversial).
      • CO2 laser excision + post-op triamcinolone injections (10-40 mg/ml) q3 weeks x4.
      • Nd:YAG and diode lasers.
      • UVB therapy.
      • Cryotherapy (freeze 30-40 seconds).
      • Isotretinoin for recalcitrant inflammatory cases.

Hidradenitis Suppurativa (HS)

Introduction

  • Cutaneous disorder targeting apocrine gland-bearing skin sites (axillae, anogenital region primarily).
  • Chronic condition: Recurrent "boils," draining sinus tracts, subsequent scarring.

Epidemiology

  • Starts at or soon after puberty.
  • Gender: Women affected 3x more often than men.
  • Race: Black > Whites.

Pathogenesis

Shift in Understanding

HS is now considered an inflammatory disorder originating from the hair follicle (infundibulum), not primarily the apocrine gland.

  • Initial event: Poral occlusion of pilosebaceous units within intertriginous zones (axillary, anogenital, inframammary).
  • Secondary inflammation of apocrine glands.
  • Sequence: Occlusion → Inflammation → Follicle Rupture.
  • Rupture releases contents (keratin, bacteria) into dermis → vigorous chemotactic response, sterile abscess formation.
  • Epithelial strands (possibly from ruptured follicular epithelia) form sinus tracts.
  • Androgens: Most patients have normal androgen profiles; apocrine glands (unlike sebaceous) are not androgen-sensitive.
  • Exacerbating Factors: Smoking, lithium.
  • Genetics: Familial form with AD inheritance.
    • Mutations in genes of γ-secretase complex: NCSTN, PSEN1, PSENEN.
    • ?Role for Notch cell signaling pathway.
  • Immune Dysregulation:
    • Overproduction of IL-1β and TNF-α.
    • Increased serum IL-2 (correlates with disease activity).
    • Increased expression of IL-12, IL-17, IL-23 within lesional skin.
    • High copy numbers of beta-defensin cluster.

Clinical Features

  • Initial: Inflammatory nodules and sterile abscesses in axillae, groin, perianal, and/or inframammary areas. May be very tender and painful.
  • Over time: Sinus tracts (double-ended pseudocomedones), hypertrophic scars.
  • Chronic drainage (serous exudate, blood, pus) → frustration, embarrassment, self-consciousness, depression (especially if malodorous).

Hidradenitis Suppurativa - Grading Systems and Therapeutic Ladder

(Based on Table 38.7)

Hurley Staging System

  • Stage I: One or more abscesses with NO sinus tract or scar formation.
  • Stage II: One or more widely separated recurrent abscesses, with sinus tract and scar formation.
  • Stage III: Multiple interconnected sinus tracts and abscesses throughout an affected region; more extensive scarring.

Sartorius Grading System (Example Elements)

  • Anatomical regions involved (axilla, groin, gluteal, other): 3 points/region.
  • Number and scores of lesions: Nodules=1, Abscesses=2 (Revuz modification), Fistulae=4 (Revuz modification, original was 6).
  • Longest distance between two relevant lesions in each region (<5cm=2, 5-10cm=4, >10cm=8 - Revuz).
  • Are all lesions clearly separated by normal skin? Yes=0 / No (Hurley III like)=6 (Revuz).
  • Note: Original text Sartorius details differ slightly from common/Revuz versions. Focus on Hurley for general staging. Sartorius and HiSCR are more for clinical trials/detailed assessment.

Other Grading Systems: HiSCR (Hidradenitis Suppurativa Clinical Response), Physician's Global Assessment Scale, refined Hurley (I & II subdivided into A,B,C).

Other Follicular Disorders

Trichostasis Spinulosa

  • Description: Common, often underdiagnosed, unknown etiology.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Asymptomatic comedo-like lesions containing keratin and multiple vellus hairs.
    • Location: Primarily nose, also trunk (esp. interscapular region).
  • Dermoscopy: Multiple vellus hairs emerge from common follicular opening.
  • Histology: Follicular hyperkeratosis, multiple vellus hairs enveloped by a keratotic sheath within a dilated hair follicle.
  • Treatment (for cosmetic appearance, options limited): Keratolytics, depilatories, topical tretinoin, lasers.

Viral-Associated Trichodysplasia

  • History: First described 1995 in organ transplant recipients (original association with cyclosporine due to drug-induced immunosuppression).
  • Etiology: Trichodysplasia spinulosa-associated polyomavirus (TSPyV).
  • Clinical Features:
    • Skin-colored to pink follicular papules and follicular spines.
    • Location: Favors central face.
    • May be accompanied by loss of eyebrows and eyelashes.
    • Extrafacial lesions with associated alopecia may also develop.
  • Histology: Enlarged and distended anagen-type follicles with high number of inner root sheath cells containing excessive amounts of trichohyaline granules.
  • Diagnosis: Spicules identified by surface microscopy or light microscopy (spicule pull-test).
  • Treatment:
    • If immunosuppressive therapy cannot be reduced:
      • Topical cidofovir or tazarotene gel.
      • Oral valganciclovir (900 mg once or twice daily).
