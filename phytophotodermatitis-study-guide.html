🔹 Definition & Core Concepts

A phototoxic (non-immunologic) inflammatory reaction requiring sequential exposure to a photosensitizing plant chemical and UVA radiation.

💡 Memory Hook: The 3 R's of Phytophotodermatitis

  • Reaction: Phototoxic (not allergic), no prior sensitization needed
  • Rash: Erythema/blisters followed by marked hyperpigmentation
  • Requirements: Plant furocoumarin + UVA radiation (sequential exposure)

💭 Recall Question: What are the three essential elements needed for phytophotodermatitis to occur?

  1. A photosensitizing chemical (primarily furocoumarins from plants)
  2. UVA radiation (peak effectiveness 320-340 nm)
  3. Skin contact (topical exposure, or less commonly oral ingestion)

Unlike allergic contact dermatitis, this is a non-immunologic reaction that requires no prior sensitization.

🔹 Pathophysiology

Furocoumarins from plants cause photochemical reactions when exposed to UVA light, leading to cellular damage through multiple mechanisms.

Key Furocoumarin Types

  • Psoralens (linear structure): Bergapten (5-MOP), Xanthotoxin (8-MOP) - cause most severe reactions
  • Angelicins (angular structure): Less potent but still cause photoreactions

Oxygen-Dependent Mechanism

  1. Furocoumarins absorb UVA
  2. Interaction with molecular oxygen
  3. Formation of reactive oxygen species
  4. Cytoplasmic vacuolization
  5. Cell membrane rupture in keratinocytes

DNA Damage Mechanism

  1. Furocoumarins intercalate into DNA
  2. UVA excites the molecules
  3. Formation of covalent interstrand DNA cross-links
  4. Binding to pyrimidine bases
  5. Keratinocyte apoptosis ("sunburn cells")
  6. Desmosomal detachment → blister formation

Enhancing Factors

  • Wet skin
  • Sweating
  • Heat
  • Plant stress (e.g., fungal infection like "pink-rot" in celery)

💭 Recall Question: What is the mechanism behind the hyperpigmentation seen in phytophotodermatitis?

The hyperpigmentation results from multiple effects on melanocytes:

  • Increased melanocyte mitosis
  • Enhanced dendricity
  • Melanocyte hypertrophy
  • Increased tyrosinase activity
  • Larger melanosomes
  • Enhanced melanosome distribution in the epidermis

This can occur even without visible preceding inflammation.

🔹 Causative Agents (Plant-Related)

Several plant families contain furocoumarins that can cause phytophotodermatitis, with the Apiaceae and Rutaceae families being most common.

🥕

Apiaceae (Umbelliferae) Family

Recognizable by: Compound umbel flower structures

High-Yield Examples:

  • Celery (Apium graveolens) - especially with "pink-rot" fungus
  • Giant Hogweed (Heracleum mantegazzianum) - can cause severe reactions
  • Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa)
  • Common Hogweed/Cow Parsnip (H. maximum/lanatum/sphondylium)
  • Wild Chervil/Cow Parsley (Anthriscus sylvestris)
  • Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare)
  • Angelica (Angelica archangelica)
  • Parsley (Petroselinum crispum)

💡 Memory Hook: "HOG-CELERY-PARSNIP"

The three most important Apiaceae family plants to remember: Hogweed, Celery, Parsnip

🍋

Rutaceae (Citrus) Family

Recognizable by: Often shrubs/small trees with fleshy fruit

High-Yield Examples:

  • Lime (Citrus latifolia [Persian], C. aurantiifolia [Key]) - rind is major source, 10x more 5-MOP than pulp
  • Bergamot Orange (Citrus bergamia) - source of 5-MOP, used in colognes
  • Rue/Herb-of-Grace (Ruta graveolens) - common garden cause, contains 5-MOP, 8-MOP, angelicins
  • Bitter Orange (C. aurantium)
  • Lemon (C. limon)
  • Grapefruit (C. paradisii)
  • Sweet Orange (C. sinensis) - can cause cheilitis
  • Gas Plant/Burning Bush (Dictamnus albus) - seeds pods high in 5/8-MOP

💡 Memory Hook: "LIME-BERG-RUE"

The three most important Rutaceae family plants: Lime, Bergamot, Rue

🍇

Other Significant Plant Families

Moraceae (Mulberry) Family

  • Fig (Ficus carica) - sap of leaves and shoots (not fruit)
  • Often misused as tanning agent

Other Notable Sources

  • Fabaceae (Pea family) - contains furocoumarins
  • St John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) - hypericin can cause systemic phototoxicity

💭 Recall Question: For each of these plants, identify which part contains the highest concentration of photosensitizers

  • Lime: Rind (10x higher concentration than pulp)
  • Fig: Leaf and shoot sap (not the fruit)
  • Hogweed: Fruit > leaves > stems (highest in autumn)
  • Celery: Whole plant, especially when infected with "pink-rot" fungus
  • Bergamot: Oil from the rind (used in colognes)
  • Gas Plant: Seed pods (highest concentration)

🔹 Epidemiology

Phytophotodermatitis can affect anyone exposed sufficiently (non-immunologic reaction), with certain occupations and activities carrying higher risk.

High-Risk Occupations & Activities

  • Bartenders squeezing limes ("lime disease" or "bartender's dermatitis")
  • Grocery workers stocking celery
  • Celery harvesters/canners (especially with fungus-infected celery)
  • Gardeners/Landscapers handling rue, parsnip, hogweed
  • Users of weed-whackers/strimmers near phototoxic plants ("strimmer dermatitis")
  • Users of cosmetics/perfumes containing bergamot oil ("Berloque dermatitis")
  • Individuals using fig leaves as tanning promoters
  • Hikers brushing against Heracleum species
  • Children playing near causative plants
  • Accidental transfer via hands (can mimic non-accidental injury)

🔹 Clinical Features

Phytophotodermatitis presents with a characteristic time course and morphologic patterns that provide important diagnostic clues.

Initial Contact

Plant furocoumarin makes contact with skin

Peak Sensitivity (30-120 minutes)

Skin sensitivity to UVA peaks 30-120 minutes after contact with furocoumarins

Initial Symptoms (24 hours)

Eruption appears 24 hours after UV exposure

  • Erythema
  • Edema
  • Vesicles/Bullae
  • Pain or burning sensation (notably, often NON-pruritic)

Peak Inflammation (48-72 hours)

Inflammatory phase peaks

Hyperpigmentation (1-2 weeks)

Marked hyperpigmentation develops and may last months to years

Note: Can occur without preceding visible inflammation

Characteristic Morphology

💡 Memory Hook: "The B.I.Z.A.R.R.E. Pattern"

  • Bizarre configurations
  • Irregular borders
  • Zigzagging lines
  • Angular shapes
  • Random streaks
  • Resembling drips/splashes
  • Exactly matching plant contact areas

Special Distribution Patterns

"Berloque Dermatitis"

  • Pendant-like streaks on neck/chest
  • From bergamot oil in perfumes/colognes

"Strimmer Dermatitis"

  • Irregular macules/papules
  • Less classically linear
  • On exposed chest, arms, legs

Hand Patterns

  • Dorsal hands/fingers in bartenders
  • Specific finger patterns depending on handling

Digitate Patterns

  • Finger-shaped patterns
  • From transfer by hands
  • Can raise concerns for child abuse

⚠️ Clinical Alert: Darker Skin Types

Hyperpigmentation may be more pronounced and persistent in individuals with darker skin phototypes (relevant for Saudi population).

💭 Recall Question: How would you differentiate phytophotodermatitis from bruising in a case of suspected child abuse?

  • Phytophotodermatitis shows bizarre, sharply demarcated patterns corresponding to plant contact
  • Unlike bruises, phytophotodermatitis lesions do not change colors over time (don't progress through yellow-green-purple stages)
  • History of outdoor activity or exposure to causative plants
  • Restricted to sun-exposed areas
  • May have other family members with similar patterns
  • Hyperpigmentation is uniform in color rather than varied like bruising

🔹 Differential Diagnosis

Distinguishing phytophotodermatitis from other similar conditions is critical for accurate diagnosis.

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Allergic Contact Dermatitis
(esp. poison ivy/oak)
  • Immunologic (Type IV) - requires prior sensitization
  • Intensely pruritic (vs. painful/burning in phytophoto)
  • Less sharply defined hyperpigmentation
  • UV not required
  • Patch test positive
Chemical Irritant Dermatitis
(e.g., Ranunculus)
  • Immediate irritation
  • No subsequent hyperpigmentation
  • No UV requirement
  • Examples: Buttercup, Euphorbia, calcium oxalate plants
Toxin-Mediated Contact Urticaria
(e.g., Stinging nettle)
  • Immediate wheals and burning
  • Caused by histamine/acetylcholine/serotonin in trichomes
  • Short-lived reaction
  • No hyperpigmentation
Photoallergic Contact Dermatitis
  • Immunologic reaction (requires sensitization)
  • Much rarer from plants than phytophotodermatitis
  • Requires photopatch testing for diagnosis
Thermal or Chemical Burns
  • History and morphology usually differ
  • Immediate onset
  • More uniform pattern
Cellulitis/Infectious Lymphangitis
  • Phytophoto (especially lime-induced) can be misdiagnosed as these
  • Look for exposure history and typical morphology/pigmentation
  • Systemic symptoms may be present in infection
Child Abuse (Bruising)
  • Digitate hyperpigmentation requires careful history
  • Phytophoto lesions lack the multiple colors of evolving bruises
  • Distribution often correlates with sun exposure

💡 Memory Hook: "PACIFIC" Difference from Allergic Contact Dermatitis

  • Phototoxic (vs. immunologic)
  • Any person (no prior sensitization)
  • Chemicals + UVA required
  • Intense pigmentation afterwards
  • Feel pain/burning (not primarily itch)
  • Instant reaction to first exposure
  • Carefully demarcated patterns

🔹 Investigations

Phytophotodermatitis is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on history and characteristic features.

Diagnostic Approach

Primarily a clinical diagnosis based on:

  1. History of exposure to a known photosensitizing plant or substance
  2. Subsequent sun exposure
  3. Characteristic morphology (bizarre, linear, streaky configuration)
  4. Typical distribution (sun-exposed contact areas)
  5. Delayed onset (24-72 hours peak inflammation)
  6. Eventual marked hyperpigmentation

⚠️ Note on Testing

Patch testing / Photopatch testing: Generally not indicated or helpful for diagnosing typical phytophotodermatitis as it's non-immunologic.

Photopatch testing is only considered if true photoallergy is suspected (rare for plants per text).

🔹 Management

Management focuses on prevention, symptomatic relief during the acute phase, and addressing persistent hyperpigmentation.

Prevention

  • Avoidance: Identify and avoid contact with known phototoxic plants
  • Protection: Wear protective clothing when gardening or working outdoors
  • Washing: Prompt thorough washing with soap and water after suspected contact
  • Do not plant known phototoxic plants near play areas

Acute Phase Treatment

  • Cool compresses (e.g., Burow's solution) to soothe and dry lesions
  • Topical corticosteroids for inflammation
  • Oral analgesics for pain
  • Avoid topical sensitizers (e.g., benzocaine, neomycin)
  • Severe cases: Consider systemic corticosteroids

Hyperpigmentation Management

  • Sun protection is crucial to prevent further darkening and allow fading
  • Topical lightening agents (hydroquinone, retinoids, azelaic acid) may be considered
  • Patient education: Fading often takes months to years regardless of treatment

🔹 Prognosis & Complications

Most cases resolve without permanent sequelae, although hyperpigmentation can persist for extended periods.

Typical Course

  • Acute inflammation resolves within 1-3 weeks
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation often lasts months to years
  • More persistent in darker skin types
  • Affected areas may remain hypersensitive to UV for years

Severe Complications

  • Rarely, severe exposure (e.g., giant hogweed) can lead to:
  • Significant blistering
  • Intense pain
  • Scarring
  • Soft tissue necrosis

🔹 Must-Memorize Points for the Saudi Board

💡 Core Concepts

  • Phytophotodermatitis = Plant Chemical (Furocoumarin) + UVA Light
  • Mechanism: Phototoxic (NOT allergic). No prior sensitization needed
  • Key Chemicals: Psoralens (5-MOP, 8-MOP) & Angelicins
  • Common Culprits: Limes, Celery, Parsnip, Fig, Rue, Hogweed, Bergamot oil (Apiaceae & Rutaceae families)
  • Clinical Triad: Delayed onset (24-72h peak) → Bizarre/Linear Erythema/Bullae (often painful/burning, NOT initially itchy) → Marked Hyperpigmentation
  • Distribution: Sun-exposed areas of contact
  • "Berloque Dermatitis": Pattern from bergamot in perfumes
  • Hyperpigmentation can occur without prior obvious inflammation
  • Diagnosis: Clinical (History + Morphology). Patch testing usually NOT helpful
  • Management: Prevention (wash!), symptomatic relief (cool compress, topical steroids), sun protection for hyperpigmentation

🔹 Common Confusion Traps in the Exam

⚠️ Watch Out For These Traps!

  • Phytophoto vs. Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): The BIGGEST trap! Remember: Phytophoto = Non-immunologic, needs UV, initially non-pruritic/painful, prominent pigmentation. ACD = Immunologic (Type IV), no UV needed, intensely pruritic, less distinct/persistent pigmentation.
  • Phytophoto vs. Photoallergy: Phytophoto is common, non-immunologic. Photoallergy is rare from plants, immunologic, needs photopatch testing.
  • Timing: The delay (24-72h peak after sun exposure) is crucial. Don't confuse with immediate reactions.
  • Symptom - Itch: Initial lack of significant pruritus helps distinguish from ACD. Focus on pain or burning initially.
  • Hyperpigmentation: This is a KEY feature and often the longest-lasting sign. Don't miss the diagnosis if inflammation has resolved.
  • Causative Agent Location: For limes, it's the rind. For figs, it's the leaf/shoot sap, not the fruit. Details matter.
  • Mimics: Be aware it can look like streaky ACD, burns, infections, or even child abuse (digitate pattern). History is paramount.

💭 Final Recall Question: A 28-year-old bartender presents with painful, linear, erythematous lesions on the dorsal aspects of his hands that developed 2 days after working a beach party. The lesions are not itchy. What is the most likely diagnosis, causative agent, and appropriate management?

Diagnosis: Phytophotodermatitis

Causative Agent: Lime juice (specifically the psoralens in the rind)

Management:

  1. Cool compresses for symptomatic relief
  2. Topical corticosteroids to reduce inflammation
  3. Analgesics for pain
  4. Sun protection to prevent worsening of hyperpigmentation
  5. Education about wearing gloves when handling limes in the future
  6. Warning that hyperpigmentation may develop and persist for months
