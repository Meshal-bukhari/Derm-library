Chapter 94: Foreign Body Reactions

A comprehensive interactive study guide for dermatology residents preparing for board examinations.

Quick Overview
  • Definition: Inflammatory reaction to material introduced to the skin that is resistant to degradation
  • Key Components: Routes of entry, Pathogenesis, Clinical presentation, Histopathological findings
  • Diagnostic Approach: Clinical history, Physical examination, Histopathological examination with special stains and techniques
  • Treatment: Primarily surgical excision for most foreign body reactions

Key Concepts

Definition

Inflammatory reaction to material introduced to the skin and resistant to degradation

Routes of Entry

  • Accidental: Wood splinters, motor vehicle accidents (MVAs)
  • Surgical procedures: Sutures, powder from gloves
  • Iatrogenic: Fillers, paraffin
  • Tattooing: Both cosmetic and accidental
  • Topical application: Such as zirconium-containing deodorants
  • Self-inflicted: As seen in IV drug users
Initial neutrophilic response to foreign material
Neutrophils fail to degrade the foreign material
Macrophages are recruited to the site
Macrophages form granulomas to isolate the foreign material
Development of various reaction patterns (granulomatous, lichenoid, pseudolymphomatous)

Clinical Pearl: The granulomatous pattern is the most common reaction pattern in foreign body reactions. The pathogenesis of other patterns (lichenoid, pseudolymphomatous) is less well understood.

The clinical presentation of foreign body reactions is highly variable and typically follows a biphasic pattern:

Acute Phase

  • Predominantly neutrophilic inflammatory response
  • May present with erythema, swelling, and pain

Chronic Phase

  • Follows resolution of the acute phase
  • Most commonly presents as red to brown nodules and plaques, with or without ulceration
  • Other presentations include:
    • Lichenoid pattern
    • Pseudolymphomatous pattern
    • Pyogenic granuloma-like lesions
    • Chronic sinuses and fistulas

The histopathological findings depend on the specific foreign body reaction pattern:

Granulomatous Pattern

  • Most common pattern
  • Characterized by granulomatous infiltrate surrounding the foreign material
  • Formation of multinucleated giant cells
  • May be visible on routine H&E staining
  • Sometimes requires polarized light microscopy for proper visualization

Other Patterns

  • Lichenoid pattern
  • Pseudolymphomatous pattern
  • Suppurative pattern

Clinical Pearl: When foreign material is not easily visualized on routine H&E staining, special techniques such as polarized light microscopy, PAS staining, or energy-dispersive X-ray analysis may be necessary for proper identification.

Tattoo Foreign Body Reactions

Types:

  • Accidental tattoos: Due to deposition of pigment material like asphalt, carbon, or graphite on injured skin
  • Iatrogenic tattoos: Can occur with hemostatic agents such as ferrous subsulfate (Monsel's solution), aluminum chloride, and silver nitrate
  • Cosmetic tattoos: Intentional introduction of pigment materials
Asked in Previous Exams

The most common pigment to cause foreign body reaction in cosmetic tattoos is red.

Clinical Features

  • Reactions can occur a few weeks after tattooing, or years later
  • Tenderness and erythema vary with the degree of inflammation
  • Photoallergic reactions can occur

Treatment

  • First line: Intralesional steroids can be used on the inflamed areas
  • Second line: Surgical excision
  • Caution: Laser removal of the tattoo can lead to dissemination of the tattoo pigment leading to a generalized allergic reaction

Paraffin Foreign Body Reactions

Definition: Paraffin is a mineral oil that has been used for body augmentation

Clinical Pearl: Paraffin use has been banned in many countries but is still used in some regions.

Clinical Features

  • Most commonly injected in the breast, buttocks, and genitalia
  • Penile paraffinomas are referred to as sclerosing lipogranulomas of the penis and can lead to significant deformity

Pathology

  • Characteristic "swiss-cheese" appearance due to the presence of round cavities where the paraffin resided prior to processing
  • Stains positive for fat markers, although less intensely compared to natural fat (helpful in differentiating from silicone)

Treatment

  • Surgical excision is the only treatment option

Silicone Foreign Body Reactions

Use: Tissue augmentation

Clinical Features

  • Takes years to develop
  • Can present with lesions distant from the primary site (e.g., spillage of silicone into the arm in breast implants after rupture)

Pathology

  • Multivacuolation similar to paraffinoma
  • Negative for fat stains (important for differentiating from paraffin)

Treatment

  • Surgical excision

Silica Foreign Body Reactions

Source: Found in sand, soil, rocks, and glass

Clinical Features

  • Deposition occurs after wounds or penetrating injuries
  • Takes years to develop
  • Papules, nodules, or indurated plaques develop within the scar

Pathology

  • Sarcoidal granulomas
  • Colorless crystals that are birefringent when examined with polarized microscopy

Treatment

  • Surgical excision

Talc Foreign Body Reactions

Use: Used in antifungal and antibiotic powders

Clinical Significance

  • Common cause of foreign body granuloma due to widespread use
  • Contaminates wounds, erosions, umbilical stumps, or intravenous puncture sites
  • Dusting of raw umbilical stumps with antibiotic powders containing talc is reported to be the most frequent cause of umbilical granulomas

Clinical Features

  • Variable clinical presentation
  • Should be suspected in typical areas (e.g., the umbilicus in infants, inguinal area in patients with a history of intertrigo, injection sites in intravenous drug addicts)

Pathology

  • Sarcoidosis-like
  • needle‑shaped crystals
  • "Maltese cross"-like birefringent particles on polarized microscopy

Treatment

  • Surgical excision

Other Materials Causing Foreign Body Reactions

Zirconium, Beryllium, Aluminum and Zinc

Features: Nothing characteristic

Treatment: Surgical excision

Starch
Asked in Previous Exams

Pathology: Ovoid basophilic structures, PAS+ve, "Maltese-cross" birefringent in polarized light

Keratin

  • Sources: Ruptured epidermoid cyst, pseudofolliculitis, ingrown nails, and sacrococcygeal pilonidal sinus disease
  • Keratin is treated as a foreign body if it is dispersed within the dermis
  • Hair inoculation in the interdigital space in barbers
  • Sinus formation common
  • Treatment: Surgical excision

Sutures

  • Clinical features: The incision site may appear inflamed, red, and edematous
  • A fistula may develop, through which the suture material is extruded
  • Inflammation persists until the suture material is totally removed
  • Pathology: Foreign body, histiocytic, palisading, or a combination
  • Transepidermal elimination of the suture material occurs

Hair Implants

  • Polyamide "hair" fibers are used in androgenetic alopecia (AGA) patients when autologous hair transplantation is not feasible
  • Not approved in the USA

Mesotherapy

  • Involves placement of a pharmacologic substance into the "mesoderm" (dermis and subcutis)
  •  FDA‑approved deoxycholic acid (Kybella used to submental fat reduction) -> temporary chemical panniculitis
  • Cutaneous necrosis and atypical mycobacterial infections have been observed as well as granulomatous reactions
  • Application of moisturizers prior to microneedling for facial rejuvenation can lead to granulomatous reactions

Injectable Soft Tissue Fillers

Clinical Pearl: Any material can potentially cause a foreign body reaction, but the incidence varies significantly. Reactions are common with paraffin but rare with hyaluronic acid (HA) filler.

Types of Reactions

  • Non-immunological (vascular): Account for approximately 2/3 of reactions when injected in the glabellar region
  • Immunological (granulomatous): True foreign body reactions

Clinical Features

  • Typically appear 1-2 years after injection
  • Spontaneous resolution within 1 year in the majority of cases
  • Some cases will have waxing and waning symptoms

Specific Fillers

Hyaluronic Acid (HA) Filler
  • Most common injectable
  • Foreign body reaction occurs in approximately 0.4% of cases
  • Presents as firm, tender, edematous and erythematous papules; an abscess may form
  • Treatment: Intralesional steroids or hyaluronidase, or waiting for spontaneous resolution
Bovine Collagen
  • Rarely used now
  • More likely to develop foreign body reaction
  • Needs skin testing before injection
  • Even with negative skin testing, reactions can occur

Board Exam Preparation

Practice Questions

1. Which of the following is the most common pigment to cause foreign body reactions in cosmetic tattoos?
A. Yellow
B. Red
C. Black
D. Green

Correct Answer: B. Red

Red pigment is most commonly associated with foreign body reactions in cosmetic tattoos. This is an important MCQ point from the chapter.

2. Which of the following appears as "Maltese cross"-like birefringent particles under polarized microscopy?
A. Silica
B. Starch
C. Keratin
D. Paraffin

Correct Answer: B. Starch

Starch appears as ovoid basophilic structures with "Maltese-cross" birefringence in polarized light. This is an important MCQ point from the chapter. Talc also shows Maltese cross-like birefringence under polarized microscopy.

3. What is the most common histopathological pattern seen in foreign body reactions?
A. Lichenoid
B. Granulomatous
C. Pseudolymphomatous
D. Neutrophilic

Correct Answer: B. Granulomatous

The granulomatous pattern is the most common histopathological pattern seen in foreign body reactions. This pattern is characterized by granulomatous infiltrate surrounding the foreign material with formation of multinucleated giant cells.

4. How would you differentiate between paraffin and silicone on histopathology?
A. Paraffin stains positive for fat markers, silicone does not
B. Paraffin shows PAS positivity, silicone does not
C. Paraffin is birefringent, silicone is not
D. Paraffin shows characteristic black granules, silicone appears colorless

Correct Answer: A. Paraffin stains positive for fat markers, silicone does not

Both paraffin and silicone can have a similar "Swiss cheese" appearance on histopathology, but paraffin will stain positive for fat markers (although less intensely than natural fat), while silicone will be negative for fat stains.

5. Which of the following is the most frequent cause of umbilical granulomas in infants?
A. Suture material
B. Talc from antibiotic powders
C. Zinc from diaper cream
D. Silicone from medical devices

Correct Answer: B. Talc from antibiotic powders

Dusting of raw umbilical stumps with antibiotic powders containing talc was reported to be the most frequent cause of umbilical granulomas in infants.

Clinical Pearl for Board Exams: Always correlate the clinical presentation (site, timing, exposure history) with histopathological findings (special stains, polarization) for accurate diagnosis of foreign body reactions. Remember that red pigment is the most common cause of tattoo reactions, and starch shows the characteristic "Maltese cross" birefringence pattern.
