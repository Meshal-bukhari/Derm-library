🔬 GENERAL CONCEPTS

Definition & Classification

Definition: Skin conditions that develop as a direct result of workplace exposures

Classification of hazardous exposures:

  • Mechanical: Friction, pressure, vibration, mechanical disruption
  • Chemical: Elements and chemical compounds (organic, inorganic, proteins)
  • Physical: Heat, cold, radiation (UV and ionizing)
  • Biological: Organisms including viruses, bacteria, fungi, and parasites

💡 Recall This

What are the four major categories of occupational exposures?

Mechanical, Chemical, Physical, and Biological exposures

📊 EPIDEMIOLOGY

  • Second most common physical illness due to occupation (23%), after musculoskeletal disorders (45%)
  • Annual cost in US estimated at $1.2 billion (2010)
  • Average cost per claim: US$3550 with 24 days of disability per case
  • German estimate of hand eczema: €9000 per case with annual cost of €2128

🧴 CONTACT DERMATITIS

General Features

  • Most common occupational skin disease, predominantly affecting hands
  • Population prevalence of hand dermatitis ~0.5%, rising to 10% in industrial workforces
  • Over half of affected individuals change jobs; many miss work for a month or more

Irritant Contact Dermatitis

Pathogenesis

Cumulative damage from repeated exposure to weak irritants

Risk Factors

  • Previous atopic dermatitis
  • Filaggrin gene mutations

Most Common Causes (%) High Yield

  • Soaps (22%)
  • Wet work (20%)
  • Petroleum products (9%)
  • Solvents (8%)
  • Cutting oils/coolants (8%)

Allergic Contact Dermatitis

Most Common Causes (%) High Yield

  • Rubber (23.4%)
  • Nickel (18.2%)
  • Epoxy resins (15.6%)
  • Aromatic amines (8.6%)
  • Chromate (8.1%)
  • Fragrances/cosmetics (8%)
  • Preservatives (7.3%)

Recent increase: Allergic contact dermatitis due to methacrylates in nail technicians

Clinical Features

  • Morphologically indistinguishable from endogenous eczema
  • Primary site usually hands, with potential spread to adjacent areas
  • Airborne allergens may produce distinct exposure pattern
  • Improvement during periods away from work suggests occupational cause
  • With chronicity, work-exacerbation relationship becomes less clear

💡 Recall This

How can you distinguish irritant vs. allergic contact dermatitis clinically?

You can't! They are morphologically indistinguishable. Diagnosis depends on patch testing and history pattern.

Diagnosis

  • Thorough dermatologic and occupational history
  • Material safety data sheets (MSDS) provide guidance on handled materials
  • Patch testing essential for diagnosis (including work materials)
  • Workplace visit may be valuable for uncertain cases

Chemical Burns

Key Features

Definition: Acute irritant reaction with irreversible skin injury and cell death

Presentation: Initial burning/stinging, progressing to erythema, blisters, erosions, ulceration

Common Causes

  • Strong acids: Sulfuric, nitric, hydrochloric, chromic. Most coagulate skin proteins and as a result form a barrier that impedes further penetration. Hydrofluoric acid differs in that it causes a liquefactive necrosis
  • Hydrofluoric acid: Causes liquefactive necrosis with progressive penetration
  • Strong alkalis: Sodium/calcium/potassium hydroxides, wet cement
  • Organic/inorganic chemicals: Dichromates, arsenates, phenolic compounds
  • Solvents/gases: Acrylonitrile, ethylene oxide, carbon disulfide, mustine

⚠️ Key point for exam: Chemical burns from alkalis generally more severe than acids (except HF) due to saponification of lipids allowing deeper penetration

Prevention and Treatment

Hierarchy of Prevention

  1. Primary: Avoid disease onset through engineering measures, chemical substitution, workforce education
  2. Secondary: Early detection through health surveillance
  3. Tertiary: Treatment of active disease through medical management

Treatment Approaches

Workplace measures:

  • PPE, proper skin care (emollients), avoiding rings
  • Hand protection: Appropriate gloves for shortest time necessary, cotton liner for prolonged wear

Medical treatment:

  • Standard contact dermatitis therapy plus avoidance of cause

Chemical burns:

  • Irrigation with large volumes of water
  • Specific antidotes (e.g., calcium gluconate gel for HF)
  • Monitor for systemic toxicity

Prognosis

  • Poor in 10% of cases ("persistent post-occupational dermatitis")
  • Worse if contact allergy to chemicals present in both work and home environments
  • Changing occupation associated with better prognosis

🧶 FIBERGLASS DERMATITIS

  • Mechanism: Direct penetration of fibers >3.5 microns in diameter
  • Presentation: Pruritus, tingling, erythematous papules (often follicular)
  • Distribution: Exposed areas (airborne) or forearms (surface contamination)
  • Diagnosis: Clinical findings plus tape stripping to detect fibers
  • Course: Resolves rapidly after exposure cessation; "hardening" occurs with continued exposure

💡 Recall This

What is the key diagnostic finding in fiberglass dermatitis?

Detecting fibers by tape stripping, plus rapid resolution after exposure cessation

🌵 CONTACT URTICARIA

General Features

  • Reaction occurring within 60 minutes of exposure, resolving within 24 hours
  • Can range from localized wheal-and-flare to systemic anaphylaxis
  • Classifications:
    • Non-immunologic/irritant
    • Immunologic (IgE-mediated)
    • Uncertain mechanism

Occupational Causes

  • Plant/animal proteins (food handlers, agricultural, veterinary workers)
  • Natural rubber latex (healthcare workers)
  • Low-molecular-weight chemicals in industrial settings
  • Highest risks in bakers, nurses, restaurant workers

Latex Contact Urticaria High Yield

  • Emerged as significant problem with universal precautions in 1980s
  • Prevalence: 3-16% among healthcare workers
  • Risk factors: Female gender, atopy, frequent glove exposure
  • Problems mainly from dipped products (gloves, balloons, condoms)
  • Can cause airborne symptoms when absorbed onto starch particles
  • Cross-reactivity with foods: Banana, avocado, kiwi, chestnuts ("latex-fruit syndrome")

Diagnosis of Contact Urticaria

  • Detailed history and examination
  • Skin prick testing (with cautious graded exposure for unknowns)
  • For latex: specific IgE blood test (not sensitive), prick test with commercial extracts
  • "Usage test" wearing suspect glove on moistened hand

⚠️ Exam Alert: Latex allergy diagnostic limitations - Specific IgE blood tests are not sensitive; negative results don't rule out latex allergy

Protein Contact Dermatitis

  • Eczematous reaction to protein-containing materials
  • Mixed mechanism (both IgE and delayed-type hypersensitivity)
  • Clinical picture: Chronic eczema with episodic exacerbations after contact

Treatment

  • Avoidance of causative agent
  • Improved occupational hygiene and PPE
  • For acute episodes: Antihistamines, epinephrine (for severe reactions)
  • For latex prevention: Powder-free, low-protein gloves or non-latex alternatives

🔬 OCCUPATIONAL SKIN CANCER

General Features

  • <1% of all skin cancers
  • Most common tumor: Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)

Risk Factors and High-Risk Occupations

  • UV radiation (outdoor workers): OR 1.77 for SCC, OR 1.43 for BCC
  • Polycyclic hydrocarbons (tar, pitch, oil products workers)
  • Arsenic exposure (glass production, smelting, pesticide production)
  • Ionizing radiation (radiologists, dentists, X-ray technicians)

Clinical Features

  • Earlier age of onset than spontaneous tumors
  • Long latency period between exposure and tumor appearance
  • For polycyclic hydrocarbons: Progression from erythema → folliculitis → poikiloderma → keratotic papillomas (tar warts) → SCC

🧫 OCCUPATIONAL ACNE

Oil Acne

  • Most common form of occupational acne
  • Mechanism: Reactive hyperkeratosis → follicular occlusion
  • Causes: Cutting oils, tars, crude petroleum oils
  • Sites: Usual acne sites plus unusual locations (arms, abdomen)

Acne Mechanica

  • Inflammatory acne exacerbated by friction
  • Sites: Localized to pressure areas (back in drivers, chin with helmet straps)

Chloracne High Yield

  • Severe acne from halogenated hydrocarbons
  • Notable cause: 2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin (TCDD)
  • Presentation: Predominantly open comedones, malar crescent and retroauricular areas
  • Progressive features: Numerous cysts, thickened/hyperpigmented skin, permanent scarring
  • Associated features: Conjunctivitis, altered liver function, neurologic symptoms
  • Course: Persistent for years despite cessation of exposure

💡 Recall This

How to differentiate chloracne from acne vulgaris?

Chloracne shows predominantly open comedones (vs. closed in acne vulgaris) and has characteristic distribution in malar crescent and retroauricular areas. Also persists for years after exposure ends.

Treatment

  • Avoidance of causative agent
  • Standard acne therapy (often with limited success in chloracne)

👋 VIBRATION WHITE FINGER

General Features

  • Part of hand-arm vibration syndrome
  • Associated with vibrations between 30-300 Hz
  • Up to 50% of exposed workforce affected

Pathogenesis

  • Sympathetic nervous system hyperactivity
  • Vibration-induced endothelial damage
  • Altered α-adrenoceptors and impaired nitric oxide release
  • Risk increased by smoking

Clinical Features

  • White patch of skin in response to cold exposure
  • Well-demarcated from surrounding areas
  • Reduced sensation and dexterity
  • Progressive stages: Fingertip involvement → whole digit → trophic changes/ulceration
  • Asymmetric distribution (thumbs usually spared)

Distinguishing from Raynaud's Disease

Vibration White Finger Raynaud's Disease
Asymmetric distribution Symmetric distribution
Occupational history present No occupational correlation
Thumbs usually spared All digits affected
Improves with cessation of exposure Persists regardless of activities

Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Clinical diagnosis; can use temperature and blood pressure measurements
  • Differentiate from Raynaud disease (symmetrical) and hypothenar hammer syndrome
  • Treatment: Avoidance of vibration exposure
  • Topical nitroglycerine ointment or calcium channel blockers for symptom control

🦠 OCCUPATIONAL INFECTIONS

  • Multiple infectious conditions associated with specific occupations
  • Common in animal handlers, healthcare workers, food processors
  • Increased risk with wet work and rough physical conditions
  • Common pathogens: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, HPV (butcher's warts)
  • Environmental factors: Increased humidity may promote fungal infections

📝 MUST-MEMORIZE POINTS FOR SAUDI BOARD

  1. Most common causes of occupational allergic contact dermatitis (in order): Rubber (23.4%), nickel (18.2%), epoxy resins (15.6%), aromatic amines (8.6%)
  2. Most common causes of occupational irritant contact dermatitis (in order): Soaps (22%), wet work (20%), petroleum products (9%), solvents (8%), cutting oils/coolants (8%)
  3. Hydrofluoric acid burns: Unique penetrating injury with progressive damage, requires calcium gluconate gel treatment (2.5%)
  4. Latex cross-reactivity with foods: Banana, avocado, chestnuts, kiwi (the "latex-fruit syndrome")
  5. Chloracne diagnostic features: Predominance of open comedones, concentration over malar crescent and behind ears, persistent for years after exposure
  6. Vibration white finger: Associated with 30-300 Hz vibration, asymmetric distribution (thumbs usually spared), improves with cessation of exposure
  7. Primary prevention hierarchy: Engineering controls → chemical substitution → PPE → workforce education
  8. Chemical burns from alkalis: Generally more severe than acids (except HF) due to saponification of lipids allowing deeper penetration

1. Rubber (23.4%)

⚠️ COMMON CONFUSION TRAPS IN THE EXAM

  • Differentiating irritant vs. allergic contact dermatitis: Both look identical morphologically; diagnosis depends on patch testing and history pattern
  • Wet cement hazards: Often tested as both a chemical burn (alkaline) AND a source of allergic contact dermatitis from chromate
  • Latex allergy diagnostic limitations: Specific IgE blood tests are not sensitive; negative results don't rule out latex allergy
  • Distinguishing vibration white finger from Raynaud's disease:
    • Vibration white finger: Asymmetric, occupational history, thumbs usually spared
    • Raynaud's disease: Symmetric, no occupational correlation, all digits affected
  • Chloracne vs. acne vulgaris: Chloracne shows predominantly open comedones (vs. closed in acne vulgaris) and characteristic distribution (retroauricular)
  • PPE as both solution and problem: Examiners may test the concept that PPE can itself cause occupational dermatitis (e.g., glove allergies)
  • Exposure cessation effects: Fiberglass dermatitis resolves quickly after cessation of exposure, while chloracne can persist for years despite no further exposure
  • Persistent post-occupational dermatitis: Frequently tested concept - around 10% of cases persist despite removal from exposure
