Listeriosis

A comprehensive guide for the Saudi Board Exam with high-yield clinical pearls, diagnostic criteria, and treatment protocols.

📋 Synopsis

Listeriosis is caused by the gram-positive bacillus Listeria monocytogenes. The organism infects farm and laboratory animals worldwide and is rarely directly transferred to humans by handling infected animals.

Main sources of human infection: Ingestion of contaminated milk, cheese, poultry, and increasingly produce. Vegan cheeses have also been associated with Listeria.

Although most adult infections appear to be minimally symptomatic, clinical presentations can range from mild to severe illness. Most symptomatic adult infections occur in immunocompromised patients and in those at the extremes of age, but infection in immunocompetent hosts does occur, particularly in farmers and veterinarians.

Listeriosis is associated with increased susceptibility among pregnant individuals, who account for 1 in 4 reported cases in the United States. Infections during pregnancy can lead to stillbirth.

Severe infections present with meningitis, gastrointestinal symptoms and signs, or a glandular fever-like illness. Hematogenous spread and widespread dissemination is uncommon.

Cutaneous involvement outside of the neonatal period is rare and usually occurs from direct inoculation of the skin in farmers or veterinarians who are exposed to animal products of conception.

Pediatric patient considerations: As with adults, in children, infection and illness are more likely in the immunocompromised. Infection can occur by ingestion of contaminated or unpasteurized milk or ingestion of cheeses from unpasteurized milk.

The highest incidence of disease is in the neonatal period.

🩺 Clinical Presentation

Listeriosis can present with a wide range of clinical manifestations:

🤒 Malaise
🚽 Diarrhea
💪 Myalgia
🔴 Erythema
⚪ Smooth papules

Most adult infections appear to be minimally symptomatic, but clinical presentations can range from mild to severe illness.

Severe infections present with:

  • Meningitis
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms and signs
  • Glandular fever-like illness

Hematogenous spread and widespread dissemination is uncommon.

Cutaneous involvement outside of the neonatal period is rare and usually occurs from direct inoculation of the skin in farmers or veterinarians who are exposed to animal products of conception.

Typical findings include:

  • One or more erythematous papules, pustules, or vesicles seen on the hands and arms
  • Disseminated disease may present with single or multiple lesions, including papules and abscesses with cellulitis
  • In patients with Listeria sepsis, purpura and petechiae may be observed

Impact of skin color on clinical presentation: Erythema is more readily appreciated in lighter skin colors. Erythema may be subtle in darker skin colors, and in the case of papules, topographical change may be the sole clinical finding. A deep red, maroon, or violaceous hue may be seen.

Applying brief gentle pressure with a fingertip or microscope slide to blanch involved skin may accentuate subtle erythema as the pressure is withdrawn.

Listeriosis is associated with increased susceptibility among pregnant individuals, who account for 1 in 4 reported cases in the United States.

Listeria infection in pregnancy commonly presents with febrile gastroenteritis or bacteremia, most commonly in the third trimester.

Infections during pregnancy can lead to stillbirth.

As with adults, in children, infection and illness are more likely in the immunocompromised.

Infection can occur by ingestion of contaminated or unpasteurized milk or ingestion of cheeses from unpasteurized milk.

The highest incidence of disease is in the neonatal period.

Red papules, pustules, vesicles, purpura, and petechiae are typical in the neonatal period. These findings have been reported in children outside the neonatal period as well.

💎 Diagnostic Pearls
Key Diagnostic Considerations
  • Consider Listeria as a cause of meningitis in patients with immune compromise or in those aged older than 55 years.
  • Listeria bacteremia can present in the immunocompromised and elderly generally with fever and chills, with about one-quarter of cases including preceding diarrhea.
  • Focal infections with Listeria, such as those involving the skin or involving peritoneal dialysis catheters, are described but are less common.
  • Listeria infection in pregnancy commonly presents with febrile gastroenteritis or bacteremia, most commonly in the third trimester.

🔍 When to suspect Listeriosis?

(Hover to reveal)

Consider in:

  • Immunocompromised patients with meningitis
  • Elderly (>55 years) with meningitis
  • Pregnant patients with febrile gastroenteritis
  • Veterinarians/farmers with skin lesions after animal exposure
  • Patients with diarrhea and recent exposure to contaminated food

Thin gram-positive rods may be noted on Gram stain.

This diagnosis should be considered in veterinarians or farmers who develop lesions after recent contact with birthing animals.

🔄 Differential Diagnosis

In pregnant patients, consider:

  • Viral gastroenteritis
  • Endometritis

For CNS infections, consider:

  • Other bacterial meningitis
  • Fungal meningitis
  • Mycobacterial meningitis

For bacteremia, consider:

  • Sepsis from other types of bacteremia
  • Atypical mycobacterial infection
🔬 Best Tests
Diagnostic Tests
  • Blood cultures
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) cultures
  • CSF polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is available as well
💊 Treatment Algorithm

The preferred therapy is generally ampicillin with the addition of gentamicin in severe or CNS infections. In the setting of penicillin allergy, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) and meropenem can be used.

Treatment Protocol
1

Non-pregnant Adults

First-line: Ampicillin 2g IV q4h OR Penicillin G 4 million units IV q4h

Severe infection: Add Gentamicin

Penicillin allergy: TMP-SMX 10-20 mg/kg IV daily (divided q6-12h) OR Meropenem 2g IV q8h

2

Pregnant Patients

Treatment: Ampicillin 2g IV q4h OR Penicillin 4 million units IV q4h for 14 days

Gentamicin and TMP-SMX are not added to the regimen in pregnant patients due to potential fetal toxicity.

3

Pediatric Patients

Ampicillin: 300 mg/kg per day IV in 4 divided doses

Alternative: Penicillin G at 250,000-400,000 units/kg per day in 4-6 divided doses

Add: Gentamicin 7.5 mg/kg per day IV in 3 divided doses may be combined with either regimen

4

Duration of Therapy

Bacteremia: At least 2 weeks

CNS infection: At least 21 days

Management Pearls
  • Consultation with infectious disease and a neurologist is recommended when neurologic signs are present.
  • In the setting of infection during pregnancy, involvement of a maternal-fetal medicine specialist or obstetrician is recommended.

📝 Board Exam Quiz

1. A 35-year-old pregnant woman in her third trimester presents with fever, headache, and diarrhea. She reports consuming unpasteurized cheese at a farmers market 3 days ago. Which of the following is the most appropriate initial treatment?

✓ CORRECT! Ampicillin 2g IV q4h is the treatment of choice for Listeriosis in pregnancy. Gentamicin and TMP-SMX are avoided due to potential fetal toxicity.

✗ INCORRECT. TMP-SMX is avoided in pregnant patients due to potential fetal toxicity.

✗ INCORRECT. While ampicillin is correct, gentamicin is avoided in pregnant patients due to potential fetal toxicity.

✗ INCORRECT. Ceftriaxone is not the first-line treatment for Listeriosis.

2. Which of the following cutaneous findings would be most characteristic of Listeriosis in a veterinarian who recently assisted with animal birthing?

✓ CORRECT! Erythematous papules, pustules, or vesicles on the hands and arms are typical of direct inoculation Listeriosis in occupational exposures.

✗ INCORRECT. A generalized maculopapular rash on the trunk is not characteristic of Listeriosis.

✗ INCORRECT. Targetoid lesions are more characteristic of erythema multiforme, not Listeriosis.

✗ INCORRECT. A vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution is characteristic of herpes zoster, not Listeriosis.
