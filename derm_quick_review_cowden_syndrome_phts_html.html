PTEN Hamartoma Tumor Syndrome — Cowden Syndrome (CS) Quick Review

Board‑level, exam‑friendly summary: diagnostic anchors, mucocutaneous clues, cancer risks, and differentiators.

Essentials

Autosomal dominant — PTEN germline
DomainHigh‑Yield PointsBoard Anchors
Genetics Germline PTEN loss‑of‑function mutations cause PTEN Hamartoma Tumor Syndrome (PHTS). Autosomal dominant inheritance; variable expressivity and age‑dependent penetrance. PTEN → PI3K/AKT pathway
Core phenotype Cowden syndrome is the commonest PHTS phenotype: multiple hamartomas, pathognomonic mucocutaneous lesions, macrocephaly, and high lifetime cancer risks. Cowden = mucocutaneous clues
Diagnosis — Major clue Macrocephaly (head circumference >97th percentile). Adult cutoffs referenced: >58 cm (women), >60 cm (men). Consider as major diagnostic criterion. Measure head circumference

COWDEN Mnemonic — Mucocutaneous & Systemic Features

Use mnemonic at bedside
LetterFindingDetails / Exam Tip
C Cranial — Macrocephaly Highly constant. Defined >97th percentile; adult cutoffs: >58 cm (F), >60 cm (M). Major diagnostic criterion.
O Oral & mucocutaneous lesions
  • Oral papillomas: 1–3 mm, mucosa‑colored papules; "cobblestone" lip/tongue—pathognomonic.
  • Acral keratoses: punctate palmoplantar keratoses, often translucent with central depression.
  • Genital pigmented macules (glans penis).
  • Sclerotic fibromas: firm dermal papules — relatively specific for CS.
W Warty papules — Trichilemmomas (face) Multiple skin‑colored to yellowish keratotic papules on central face, periorificial; classic onset in 20s–30s. Highly suggestive when present with macrocephaly.
D Developmental / CNS Includes Lhermitte‑Duclos disease (adult hamartomatous cerebellar overgrowth — major criterion), autism spectrum disorder, intellectual disability (minor criteria).
E Endometrial & other cancers
  • Breast cancer: up to ~85% lifetime risk in women; mean age ~40 years.
  • Thyroid cancer (follicular type): lifetime risk 10–>30%; benign multinodular disease common (>66%).
  • Endometrial carcinoma: 20–30% lifetime risk.
  • Other: renal cell carcinoma (up to ~30% in some series; minor criterion), colon carcinoma (~10%), melanoma (~5%).
Cancer surveillance is a central management theme—early detection saves lives.
N Nodules — Lipomas, Neuromas, Fibromas Lipomas are common; testicular lipomatosis described. Neuromas (mucosal, facial, acral) may mimic MEN2B. Multiple acrochordons and sclerotic fibromas frequently seen.

Diagnostic Criteria & Red Flags

Clinical‑genetic correlation
QuestionTypical AnswerClinical Anchor
Which finding is almost always present? Macrocephaly (>97th percentile). Measure HC
Pathognomonic mucosal lesion? Oral papillomas producing cobblestone mucosa. Look inside lips & tongue
Imaging red flag? Lhermitte‑Duclos disease on MRI — think PHTS/CS. Cerebellar dysplastic gangliocytoma
Oncology priority? Breast, thyroid, endometrial surveillance; consider renal and colon surveillance based on personal/family history. High‑risk oncology pathway

Management & Surveillance (Board‑Level)

High‑yield actions
  • Genetic testing for PTEN mutation when clinical suspicion (macrocephaly + mucocutaneous lesions or family history).
  • Breast: enhanced surveillance (clinical exam, MRI ± mammography) starting earlier (often by age 30) per guidelines.
  • Thyroid: baseline ultrasound and periodic surveillance due to follicular cancer risk.
  • Endometrial: counsel on symptoms; consider screening/per protocol.
  • Renal: baseline imaging and periodic surveillance in higher‑risk individuals.
  • Multidisciplinary care: genetics, oncology, endocrinology, neurology, dermatology.
Exact surveillance intervals vary by guideline and patient factors—individualize with genetics team.
