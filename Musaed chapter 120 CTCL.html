Musaed Chapter 120: Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma

A High-Yield Review for the Saudi Board Exam

General Notes: The Foundation

  • CTCL describes a group of neoplasms of skin homing T-cells
  • ~80% of primary cutaneous lymphomas
  • *65% of them are mycosis fungoides and its variants
  • CTCLs are classified into indolent and aggressive clinical behaviors (see table below)
Diagnostic Triad: For the diagnosis of CTCL, clinical, histologic, and immunophenotyping are needed. One is not enough!

Immunophenotyping & Antigen Loss

  • Loss of one of the T-cell associated antigens like CD2, CD3, CD4, and CD5 is considered an important finding on immunophenotyping.
  • Loss of CD7 is less specific, as it can happen with any disease with chronic T cell stimulation and is not a reliable marker.
  • However, CD7 is the earliest marker to be lost (MCQ).
Board Pearl: While CD7 loss is non-specific, they love asking which T-cell marker is lost *first* in the neoplastic process. The answer is CD7.

General Guidelines for Diagnosis

Biopsy Strategy
  • Deep punch biopsies 4-6 mm or incisional/excisional are preferred.
  • May need to repeat biopsies many times before reaching a conclusion.
  • Difficult to get a definite diagnosis based on biopsy alone.
  • Therefore, clinical, histologic, and immunophenotyping are needed.
Immunophenotyping (= markers)
  • Done by immunohistochemistry on skin samples.
  • Or flow cytometry on blood samples (to check for blood involvement).
  • Each CTCL entity has a specific immunophenotype, which helps differentiate between them.
  • Loss of one of the T-cell associated antigens like CD2, CD3, CD4, and CD5 is considered an important finding.
  • Loss of CD7 is less specific, as it can happen with any disease with chronic T cell stimulation and is not a reliable marker.
  • CD7 is the earliest marker to be lost (MCQ).
T-cell receptor (TCR) rearrangement analysis
  • Used to check the clonality of T cells (MCQ).
  • Done by either PCR or Southern plot (MCQ).
  • PCR has higher sensitivity and specificity.
  • It basically measures if the cells are one clone or multiple clones.
  • Can be done on skin samples, lymph nodes, and peripheral blood.
  • Peripheral blood involvement -> poor prognosis.
  • Gamma and beta TCR are the most common (MCQ).
  • Monoclonality may not be evident in early lesions.
    • 50% of patch - 73% of plaque - 83% to 100% of tumor/erythrodermic
  • If one clone -> consistent with CTCL.
  • However, some benign conditions may show clonality as well (PLEVA, LyP, parapsoriasis), so it's not reliable on its own and needs to be combined with the other tests.

Classification and Practical Management

Once clinical, histological, immunophenotyping, and TCRR studies are consistent with a CTCL, the type of CTCL should be determined.

Three Key Questions to Answer:
  1. Is it MF or its variant?
    • 65% of CTCLs are in this group.
    • Managed by a dermatologist alone in most cases.
  2. Is it one of the CD30-positive lymphoproliferative disorders?
    • 25% of CTCLs are in this group.
    • Excellent prognosis.
    • Managed by a dermatologist alone.
  3. Is it another rare variant of CTCL?
    • 10% of CTCLs are in this group.
    • Most lymphomas in this group have an aggressive behavior.
    • Needs to be managed in collaboration with a hemato-oncologist.

Staging and Workup

  • Staging procedure will depend on the type of CTCL + the clinical stage of the disease.
  • Staging is not worthwhile in early patch/plaque MF and pagetoid reticulosis.
  • Also not needed for lymphomatoid papulosis.
  • If Sezary syndrome is suspected, staging + special emphasis on assessment of peripheral blood involvement is essential.
  • In all the other CTCLs, CBC & D, LFT/RFT, CT/PET of chest and abdomen, and BMBx are required.

Mycosis Fungoides (MF)

Most Common & Indolent: MF is the most common cutaneous lymphoma, representing ~50% of all primary cutaneous lymphomas. Median age at diagnosis is 55-60 years.

Pathogenesis

Genetically susceptible individual
Environmental trigger
Chronic antigenic stimulation of T cells
Neoplastic cells
  • The cause for chronic antigenic stimulation is not known.
  • Exposure to paper, glass and wood may play a role but it's controversial.
  • Downregulation of the CD8+ T cells leads to decreased antitumor activity.
  • The neoplastic T cells in MF are of the Th2 type.

Tissue resident T cells (Trm)

  • CCR7- and E-selectin-
  • Unable to migrate out of the skin in most cases.
  • Involved in Mycosis Fungoides.

Central memory cells (Tcm)

  • CCR7+ and E-selectin+
  • Able to migrate out of the skin and access the peripheral blood/lymphatics.
  • Involved in Sézary syndrome.

Clinical Features & Staging

Progress from patch, plaque, and tumors stages but not always. Clinical course takes years and possibly decades.

Patch and Plaque Stage

  • Non-specific eczematous or psoriasiform morphology.
  • Atrophy and poikiloderma are clues to the diagnosis.
  • Hypopigmentation may occur especially in darkly pigmented individuals.
  • Involves the buttocks and covered areas of the trunk.
  • Most patients do not progress past the plaque stage, but ~10% of patients may develop nodules or tumors.

Tumor Stage

  • Concurrently have patches, plaques, and tumors.
  • If tumors are present alone, MF is not likely.
  • Ulceration is common.
  • High risk of extracutaneous disease.
  • Extracutaneous disease first involves the regional lymph nodes followed by visceral dissemination.
  • The bone marrow is rarely involved.

Variants

  • Folliculotropic: a/w alopecia and mucin on histo (alopecia mucinosa).
  • GSS (Granulomatous Slack Skin): sagging of the axillary and inguinal skin.
  • Pagetoid reticulosis: extensive epidermotropism.
  • Poikilodermic, hypopigmented, interstitial, verrucous, pustular, bullous, all are just morphological variants.

Pathology

Patch stage:

  • Superficial band-like lichenoid interface dermatitis.
  • Consists of lymphocytes.
  • Atypical cells in the epidermis (epidermotropism).

Plaque stage:

  • Psoriasiform hyperplasia.
  • Pautrier microabscesses: nests of atypical lymphocytes in the epidermis.
  • More extensive epidermotropism.
  • Atypical cells in both the dermis and epidermis.
  • Rarely, interstitial infiltrate is present that resembles granuloma annulare (called interstitial MF).

Tumor stage:

  • Dermal infiltrate involving the whole dermis.
  • No epidermotropism (cells become so undifferentiated that they lose the ability to move to the epidermis).
  • Large cells with cerebriform nuclei, blast cells.
Large cell transformation:
  • Defined as more than 25% of the cells are large (x4 normal lymphocyte).
  • a/w poor prognosis.
  • These cells may be positive for CD30 or negative for CD30.
  • CD30 positive patients have a better prognosis.
  • CD30 should be done to screen for large cell transformation (MCQ).

Immunophenotyping

Classic MF Phenotype: CD3+, CD4+, CD7-, CD8-, CD45RO+ (MCQ)

  • CD8+ may occur rarely, especially with hypopigmented variant.

Staging and Workup

  • Based on the TNM classification + peripheral blood involvement.
  • Clinical examination of the skin and lymph nodes, skin biopsies, CBC and chemistry should be done for all patients.
  • Patients with limited patch/plaque MF (Stage 1A or 1B) -> no further workup is needed.
  • CT scan of the chest and abdomen are indicated when extracutaneous involvement is suspected.

Recommended evaluation/initial staging of the patient with mycosis fungoides/Sézary syndrome

  • Complete physical examination, including:
    • Determination of type(s) of skin lesions
    • Estimate percentage of BSA involved by patches, plaques, and tumor lesions and note any ulceration or folliculocentricity of lesions. The patient's palm (including all fingers) is approximately 1 percent of BSA*.
    • Identification of any palpable lymph node, especially those ≥1.5 cm in largest diameter or firm, irregular, clustered, or fixed
    • Identification of any organomegaly
  • Skin biopsy
    • Essential: At least two skin biopsies should be obtained of different anatomic areas and morphology for H&E evaluation. If only one biopsy can be obtained, then the lesion that has the most scaling and induration should be chosen.
    • If needed: Immunophenotyping to include at least the following markers: CD2, CD3, CD4, CD5, CD7, and CD8, beta-F1 (to confirm alpha-beta TCR), and a B-cell marker, such as CD20; CD30 may also be indicated in cases where lymphomatoid papulosis, anaplastic lymphoma, or large-cell transformation is considered. Beta-F1 -> Alpha&beta TCR
    • If needed: Evaluation for clonality of TCR gene rearrangement
  • Blood tests
    • CBC with manual differential (assess for Sezary cells), comprehensive chemistries, LDH
    • Analysis for abnormal lymphocytes by either Sezary cell count with determination of the absolute number of Sezary cells and/or flow cytometry (including CD4+/CD7- or CD4+/CD26-)
    • If needed: TCR gene rearrangement and relatedness to any clone in skin
  • Radiologic tests
    • In stage IA patients with T1a disease who are otherwise in excellent health, without adenopathy, organomegaly, or abnormal blood tests, no further studies are needed. In stage IB patients without suspicious adenopathy or abnormal blood work, a chest x-ray is sufficient, rather than a CT scan, to exclude chest or systemic involvement; and in all other patients, contrast-enhanced CT scan of chest, abdomen, and pelvis (neck included if clinically indicated) or whole body integrated PET-CT is recommended to further evaluate any potential lymphadenopathy, visceral involvement, or abnormal laboratory tests; in patients unable to safely undergo CT scans, MRI may be substituted.
  • Lymph node biopsy
    • Excisional biopsy is indicated in those patients with a node that is at least 1.5 cm (confirmed by imaging) in diameter; and/or is firm, irregular, clustered, or fixed; and/or significantly PET avid.
    • Site of biopsy: Preference is given to the largest lymph node draining an involved area of the skin or if FDG-PET scan data are available, the node with highest standardized uptake value.
    • Analysis: Pathologic assessment by light microscopy, flow cytometry, and TCR gene rearrangement

Prognosis

  • Depends on the stage of the disease.
  • Early MF patients have survival comparable to the general population.
Factors predictive of disease progression:
  • Advanced skin involvement (Bullous MF)
  • Extracutaneous disease
  • Older age, male gender, blacks
  • Folliculotropic MF
  • Large cell transformation
  • High LDH
  • Blood involvement

Treatment

Depends on the stage. (See also Table 120.5 Treatment of Mycosis Fungoides)

Stage 1A, 1B, and 2A (and sometimes 2B)

Skin directed therapy

  • Topical or intralesional steroids
  • Topical nitrogen mustard=mechlorethamine : Topical mechlorethamine gel 0.016% is FDA-approved for early-stage Mycosis Fungoides (MF), particularly stage IA–IB (patch/plaque stage) in non-ulcerated lesions.
  • Topical bexarotene if <15 BSA
  • Phototherapy
  • Radiotherapy: can be used, typically 36 Gy in 1.5–2 Gy fractions over 8–10 weeks; lower-dose regimens (10–12 Gy) permit retreatment and remain effective, especially for stages IA–IIA

If SDT is not enough -> add IFN-a or systemic retinoid

If still not enough -> add Denileukin (IL-2 inhibitor) or vorinostat/romidepsin (histone deacetylase inhibitors; HDACi) (MCQ about option for refractory MF -> vorinostat)

If still not enough -> add systemic chemotherapy

Stage 2B

Start with a combination of skin directed therapy + systemic therapy

Systemic therapies as above

NBUVB used only if patch stage

Stage 3 (erythrodermic MF)
  • Extracorporeal photopheresis:recommended as the main therapy
  • Systemic retinoids
  • IFN-a
  • Monoclonal antibodies:
    • Alemtuzumab
    • Brentuximab
    • Mogamulizumab:
      • Mechanism: Anti-CCR4 monoclonal antibody
      • Targets CCR4+ malignant T-cells (expressed on most Sézary/MF cells)
      • Also depletes regulatory T cells, enhancing immune surveillance
      • Indication:
      • FDA-approved for:
        • Relapsed/refractory MF
        • Sézary syndrome (SS) – preferred
      • Effective in erythrodermic MF, especially with B2 blood involvement
Stage 4
  • Should be managed by a hemato-oncologist
  • Six cycles of CHOP chemotherapy
  • HSCT

Additional notes on treatment: Biologics for CTCL

Alemtuzumab

Mechanism: Anti-CD52CD52 is expressed on T and B lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils Causes profound lymphodepletion

Indication: Off-label for erythrodermic MF

Side Effect: Reactivation of CMV infection (MCQ)

Denileukin diftitox

Mechanism: Anti-IL-2

Indication: FDA-approved for refractory MF

Side Effect: capillary leak syndrome, fever, and fluid retention (MCQ), thyrotoxicosis following by long term hypothyroidism

Brentuximab

Mechanism: Anti-CD30

Indication: FDA-approved for ALCL, can be used in CD30+ MF

Side Effect: peripheral neuropathy (MCQ)

Warning: Blackbox warning for progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (just like Rituximab) (MCQ)

Mogamulizumab

Mechanism: Anti-CCR4

Indication: FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory MF and Sézary syndrome (SS) - preferred

Side Effect: Infusion reactions (Common) - premedication often needed, skin rash (GVHD-like) - key exam point - can mimic MF flare; histology helps, Hepatotoxicity - monitor LFTs, Infections - HSV, CMV, PJP risk - consider prophylaxis

High-Yield MF MCQs:
  • Cure is defined as disease free without treatment for 8 years (MCQ).
  • Folliculotropic MF is worst prognosis, pagetoid reticulosis is best (MCQ).
  • Folliculotropic MF is equivalent to tumor stage MF (stage 3), even if no tumors are present (MCQ).
  • Folliculotropic is the most common variant of MF (MCQ).
  • Best phototherapy option for folliculotropic MF -> PUVA (MCQ).
  • Gold standard for screening for blood involvement is flow cytometry (MCQ).
  • Earliest test to detect MF -> immunoGENOtyping (MCQ).
  • Topical retinoids can be used only if < 15% BSA.

Sézary Syndrome (SS)

Leukemic Variant: Defined by the triad of erythroderma, generalized lymphadenopathy, and presence of Sézary cells in the peripheral blood.

Pathogenesis

Explained earlier (involves Central memory cells (Tcm) that are CCR7+ and E-selectin+, allowing access to peripheral blood/lymphatics).

Clinical Features

  • Erythroderma: Intensely pruritic.
  • Lymphadenopathy, alopecia, onychodystrophy, and palmoplantar hyperkeratosis are common findings.
  • 5-year survival of ~25%.
  • Most patients die of opportunistic infections.

Cutaneous Findings

  • Erythroderma and generalized lymphadenopathy. Associated features:
    • Extreme pruritus.
    • Palmoplantar hyperkeratosis, lichenification, edema, and exfoliation.
    • Alternating bands of pigment and erythema with uninvolved skin around flexural areas known as the "deck chair" sign or "folded luggage" sign.
    • Infiltrative plaques occurring on the face may result in exaggeration of normal skin folds, known as a lion-like face, or leonine facies.
    • Multiple nail defects, including yellowing.

Pathology & Immunophenotyping

  • Similar to MF.
  • May be non-specific in 1/3 of cases.
  • Immunophenotyping:
    • CD3+, CD4+, CD8- phenotype.
    • Characteristically lack CD7 and CD26 expression.
    • Express programmed death-1 (PD-1; CD279) in almost all cases.
Criteria for Diagnosis:
  • Erythroderma >80% (MCQ)
  • Monoclonality in the blood (and preferentially the skin) PLUS one of the following:
    • Sézary cell* count ≥1000 cells/mcL. Sezary are abnormal lymphocytes are characterized by their distinctive cerebriform (brain-shaped) nucleus, which appears grooved or convoluted under microscopic examinatio
    • CD4:CD8 >10:1 (MCQ)
    • Percentage of T cells with abnormal phenotype (>40% loss of CD7 or >30% loss of CD26)

Treatment

  • Being a leukemia, systemic treatment is needed.
  • CHOP or CHOP-like regimens.
  • ECP for erythroderma (MCQ).
  • Skin-directed therapies as adjuvant therapy.
  • Other options as above in stage 3-4 MF.

Adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (ATLL)

Caused by Human T-lymphotropic virus 1, a retrovirus (MCQ).

HTLV-1 is transmitted via body fluids (MCQ).

Subtypes

  • Acute: most common and worst prognosis.
  • Chronic: good prognosis.
  • Smoldering: best prognosis.
  • Lymphomatous: bad prognosis.

Clinical & Pathologic Features

  • Nonspecific presentation.
  • Typically erythroderma or scaly patches and plaques (resembling MF).
  • The acute type is a/w leukemia, lymphadenopathy, organomegaly, and hypercalcemia.
  • Pathology:
    • Diffuse infiltration of small, medium-sized or large pleomorphic T cells, which often display marked epidermotropism.
    • Indistinguishable from MF.
    • Differentiated by immunophenotyping: CD3+, CD4+, CD8-, CD25+, PD-1+.
  • Peripheral blood morphology:
    • "Flower cells" "Clover leaf cells": buzzwords for ATLL (MCQ).

Treatment

  • Combination of zidovudine and IFN-a.
  • Systemic chemotherapy is required in most cases.
  • Mogamulizumab is approved for ATLL in Japan.
  • For smoldering and chronic types, skin directed therapy as in MF.

CD30+ lymphoproliferative disorders

Second most common CTCL, ~25%.

Low-grade CTCL / Premalignant (LyP)
Spectrum
Malignant CTCL (ALCL)
  • Includes anaplastic large cell lymphoma (ALCL) and lymphomatoid papulosis (LyP).
  • They have the same histology.
  • Differentiation is based on clinical features (self-regressing in LyP vs non-self-regressing in ALCL).
  • When unable to differentiate between the two, "borderline case" is designated.
  • With time, borderline cases will show their true nature (like some people I know!).
  • Mutations in ALCL: MUM1/IRF4, DUSP22/IRF4 (MCQ).
  • Risk factors for ALCL: breast implant (MCQ).
Differential for CD30+ Disorders
  • Primary cutaneous ALCL or LyP
  • MF with large cell transformation and CD30+ cells
  • Secondary cutaneous ALCL from extracutaneous site
  • Pseudolymphoma with CD30+ cells
  • Other CTCLs with CD30+ cells

Clinical Features: ALCL vs. LyP

Anaplastic Large Cell Lymphoma (ALCL)

  • Solitary or grouped nodules or tumors that often ulcerate.
  • Extracutaneous involvement in ~10%, mainly lymph nodes.
  • Good prognosis (>85% at 5 years).

Lymphomatoid Papulosis (LyP)

  • Chronic, recurrent, self-healing, papulonecrotic or papulonodules.
  • They disappear spontaneously within 3 to 12 weeks.
  • Characteristically, skin lesions in different stages of evolution coexist.
  • Heals with dyspigmentation or atrophy.
  • Most commonly trunk and extremities.
  • Duration of the disease ranges from several months to more than 40 years.
  • A/W MF, C-ALCL, and Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Excellent prognosis.

Pathology: ALCL vs. LyP

ALCL

  • Diffuse non-epidermotropic infiltrate with sheets of large CD30+ve cells.
  • Cells have a characteristic morphology of anaplastic cells with irregularly shaped nuclei.
  • 20-25% will have Reed-Sternberg-like cells.
  • Immunophenotyping: CD4+, CD30+, MUM1/IRF4+.

LyP

  • Multiple types. The most common is type A (MCQ).
  • Type A: wedge-shaped infiltrate of CD30+ lymphocytes (plevA-like).
  • Type B: eBidermotropism.
  • Type C: alCI-like.
  • Type D: cD8 positive.
  • Type E: angioinvasive (engioinvasive).
  • Type F: Folliculotropic.

Pleva vs LyP: Key Differentiators

Age: Adolescents/children vs Adults

Histo: No Eosinophils vs Eosinophils

IHC: CD30-ve vs CD30+ve

Treatment

ALCL

  • Surgical excision for solitary or a few localized lesions.
  • Radiotherapy for disseminated disease.
  • Low dose methotrexate, retinoids, or interferon-a are systemic options.
  • Doxorubicin based multi-agent chemotherapy for extracutaneous involvement.
  • Patients presenting with leg lesions need aggressive therapy because it's a/w worse prognosis.
  • Brentuximab gives nearly 100% response. The FDA approved brentuximab vedotin for adults with primary cutaneous anaplastic large cell lymphoma (pcALCL) who have received prior systemic therapy. also approve for CD30+ Mycosis Fungoides

LyP

  • Treatment is often unsatisfactory.
  • Topical or systemic steroids are not useful.
  • For a few lesions -> no need for treatment.
  • If cosmetically disturbing -> low-dose oral methotrexate (5-20 mg/week) is the most effective therapy.
  • Chemotherapy and radiation therapy are effective but the disease relapses once they are stopped, so active treatment should be balanced carefully against potential side effects.
  • Long-term followup is recommended for these patients because of risk of developing lymphomas.

Other CTCL Entities

Subcutaneous panniculitis-like T-cell lymphoma (SPLTCL)
  • Defined as a CD8+ with subcutaneous infiltrates of pleomorphic T cells with an a/B+ T-cell phenotype.
  • Should be differentiated from gamma/delta T-cell lymphoma with subcutaneous involvement because the prognosis is different (good in SPLTCL, bad in GDTCL).
  • Rare, less than 1% of CTCLs.
  • 20% of patients have an associated autoimmune disease, most commonly SLE.
  • Clinical features:
    • Solitary or multiple subcutaneous nodules.
    • Primarily the extremities and the trunk.
    • Lesions heal with lipoatrophy.
    • Ulcerations is uncommon.
    • Systemic symptoms such as fever, fatigue, and weight loss may be present.
    • Cytopenias and elevated LFTs are common.
    • Hemophagocytic syndrome (HLH) is observed in only ~15% of patients = bad prognostic factor. (HLH: Phagocytosis by macrophages of erythrocytes, leukocytes, platelets, and their precursors -> very bad prognosis)
    • Rare extracutaneous involvement.
    • May be preceded by seemingly benign panniculitis for years.
    • 5 years survival 80%.
  • Pathology:
    • Simulates a lobular panniculitis.
    • Pleomorphic T cells with hyperchromatic nuclei are present with many macrophages.
    • Epidermis and dermis are spared.
    • Rimming of individual fat cells -> characteristic finding.
  • Immunophenotyping:
    • TCR a/B+ (vs GDTCL which gamma/delta)
    • CD3+, CD4-, CD8+, CD56-
    • BF1+ (vs GDTCL which ßF1-)
  • Treatment:
    • Solitary lesion - radiotherapy.
    • Systemic: Doxorubicin-based chemotherapy has traditionally been used. Recent studies suggest less aggressive immunosuppression (cyclosporine or prednisone), especially if not associated with HLH.

Extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma
  • Almost always a/w with EBV infection (MCQ).
  • Can be an NK-cell lymphoma or a CD8+ lymphoma.
  • Most commonly affects the nasal cavity or nasopharynx.
  • The skin is the second most common site.
  • The classic exam picture is an ulcerative lesion in the nasal area.
  • Clinical features:
    • Midfacial destructive tumor -> "nasal type" ENKTL.
    • Tumors on the trunk and extremities can be involved.
    • Ulceration common.
    • Has a very aggressive course, even if no extracutaneous manifestations at presentation.
  • Pathology:
    • Dense infiltrate of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue.
    • Angiocentricity and angiodestruction.
    • Extensive necrosis.
  • Immunophenotyping:
    • CD2+, CD56+ (MCQ).
    • Surface CD3 is negative.
    • Detection of EBV by in situ hybridization and expression of cytotoxic proteins is required for diagnosis.
  • Treatment:
    • Radiotherapy for localized disease.
    • Chemotherapy for extensive disease (SMILE regimen), though even with chemo it has a very bad prognosis.
CD8+ aggressive epidermotropic cytotoxic T-cell lymphoma
  • Long name.
  • Tumors characterized by epidermotropic CD8+ cells.
  • Aggressive behavior.
  • Differentiation between them vs other CTCLs with CD8+ cells is based on clinical presentation and clinical behavior.
  • Clinical features:
    • Localized or disseminated eruptive papules, nodules, and tumors.
    • Central ulceration and necrosis.
    • Can also present as superficial scaly patches or plaques.
    • Can metastasize to visceral organs, but lymph nodes are typically spared.
  • Pathology:
    • Extensive epidermotropism.
    • Band-like or diffuse infiltration of atypical lymphocytes.
  • Immunophenotyping: CD3+, CD4-, CD8+, CD7-/+, CD45RA+.
  • Treatment: systemic chemotherapy.
Gamma/Delta T-cell lymphoma
  • Gamma/delta T-cells with cytotoxic phenotype (CD8+).
  • When involving the subcutis, should be differentiated from SPLTCL (see above).
  • Involvement of the subcutaneous fat is a bad prognostic factor.
  • TCRy is not only expressed by GDTL, but also in rare cases of MF and other CTCLs.
  • This makes differentiation difficult sometimes.
  • Aggressive course and often fatal.
  • Clinical features:
    • Disseminated ulceronecrotic nodules or tumors.
    • On the extremities.
    • Mucosal involvement common.
    • Lymph nodes, spleen, and bone marrow -> often spared.
    • HLH may occur especially when it involves the subcutaneous fat.
  • Pathology:
    • Can be epidermotropic, dermal, or subcutaneous.
    • Angiocentricity and angiodestruction are commonly seen.
  • Immunophenotyping:
    • TCRy+, ßF1-
    • CD3+, CD2+, CD4-, CD5-, CD7+/-, CD8-, CD56+
  • Treatment: usually resistant to chemotherapy.
CD4+ small/medium T-cell lymphoma
  • Similar phenotype to MF.
  • Distinguished from MF by the lack of patches and plaques.
  • Good prognosis.
  • Clinical features:
    • Solitary plaque or tumor.
    • Generally on the head and neck.
  • Pathology:
    • Dense infiltrate in the dermis.
    • May involve the subcutis or show epidermotropism.
  • Immunophenotyping:
    • CD3+, CD4+, CD8-, CD30- (MF phenotype).
  • Treatment: surgical excision, intralesional steroids, or local radiotherapy.
