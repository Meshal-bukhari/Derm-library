Rosacea: Core Concepts

Epidemiology

  • Peaks at 30-40 years old
  • Gender: F > M
  • Skin Types: Usually skin types I and II

Pathogenesis

  • Chronic vascular inflammatory disorder
  • Multifactorial:
    • Vascular hyperreactivity
    • Chronic solar damage
    • Sensitivity to heat
    • Hyperirritable skin
    • Possible association with Demodex + Innate immune response (e.g., Bacillus oleronius)

Clinical Features

  • Usually limited to central face
  • Depends on subtypes (see Rosacea subtypes section)

Histopathology

  • Perivascular and perifollicular lymphohistiocytic infiltrate
  • Vascular ectasia
  • Mild edema
  • Sebaceous hyperplasia

Treatment / Clinical Course

General Measures:

  • Avoid Triggers:
    • Sunlight (use sunscreen!)
    • Heat/cold extremes
    • Stress
    • Strong emotions
    • Alcohol
    • Hot beverages
    • Spicy foods
    • Chemical irritation
  • Sunscreen: Essential daily.

Topical Treatments:

  • Metronidazole
  • Sodium sulfacetamide/sulfur
  • Azelaic acid
  • Benzoyl peroxide
  • Clindamycin
  • Green-tinted makeup (for camouflage)

Systemic Treatments:

  • Tetracyclines (TCNs) e.g., Doxycycline, Minocycline
  • Amoxicillin
  • Isotretinoin (if severe)

Other Therapies:

  • IPL (Intense Pulsed Light)
  • PDL (Pulsed Dye Laser)
  • For rhinophyma: CO2 laser and electrosurgery
Additional Boards Factoid

Haber's syndrome: A genodermatosis characterized by a rosacea-like eruption and verrucous lesions.

Rosacea Subtypes

Erythematotelangiectatic (Vascular) Rosacea

  • Key Feature: Central face with recurrent blush that eventually becomes permanent flushing (persistent erythema).
  • Symptoms: Burning, stinging sensation; easily irritated skin with roughness and scaling.
  • Can have associated edema.
  • +/- telangiectasia.
ETR Grading & Initial Treatment Focus
Grade Clinical Severity Treatment
Grade 1 Occasional flushing; faint persistent erythema; few telangiectasias
  • General skin care
  • Avoid triggers
Grade 2 Frequent troublesome flushing; moderate erythema; distinct telangiectasias
  • Laser therapy
  • Vascular lasers (PDL/KTP)
  • Brimonidine or Oxymetazoline
Grade 3 Severe flushing; pronounced erythema; numerous telangiectasias; ± edema
  • All above + consider maintenance regimens and monitor for sensitive skin reactions

Papulopustular (Inflammatory) Rosacea

  • Key Feature: Persistent central facial erythema with transient papules/pustules.
  • Similar to acne vulgaris, BUT lesions may have deeper red color and NO COMEDONES. This is a critical distinction!
  • Typically non-scarring.
  • Lesions appear in crops, may last a few years.
Papulopustular Rosacea Grading
Grade Clinical Severity Description
Grade 1 Mild
  • Few papules and/or papulopustules
  • Mild persistent centrofacial erythema
Grade 2 Moderate
  • Several papules and/or papulopustules
  • Moderate persistent centrofacial erythema
Grade 3 Severe
  • Extensive papules and/or papulopustules
  • Pronounced erythema
  • May include inflammatory plaques or facial edema

Phymatous Rosacea

  • Key Feature: Thickening of skin due to overgrowth of sebaceous glands.
  • Gender: M > F.
  • Most common on nose (rhinophyma).
  • Can also involve chin (gnathophyma), forehead (metophyma), earlobes (otophyma), eyelids (blepharophyma). (Reference: "de noro of cl mild PRP changes" - likely a typo, interpreted as locations)
Phymatous Rosacea Grading
Grade Severity Clinical Features
Grade 1 Mild
  • Puffiness
  • Mildly patulous follicles
  • No visible hypertrophy of connective tissue or sebaceous glands
  • No contour change
Grade 2 Moderate
  • Moderate swelling
  • Moderately dilated follicles
  • Mild hypertrophy of sebaceous glands/connective tissue
  • Change in nasal contour but no nodularity
Grade 3 Severe
  • Marked swelling
  • Large dilated follicles
  • Distortion of contour due to hypertrophy
  • Nodular component present (e.g., classic rhinophyma)

Ocular Rosacea

  • Affects about 50% of rosacea patients. Skin findings may not correlate with ocular severity!
  • Common Complaints:
    • Dryness
    • Foreign body sensation
    • Photosensitivity
    • Burning/stinging
  • Signs:
    • Blepharitis
    • Recurrent chalazion
    • Conjunctivitis
    • Keratitis (can be sight-threatening)
    • Iritis
    • Scleritis
  • Treatment: Oral doxycycline/minocycline often required. Eyelid hygiene, artificial tears.
Ocular Rosacea Grading
Grade Clinical Features
1 Mild itch, dryness, or grittiness; fine scaling of eyelid margins; telangiectasia of eyelid margins; mild conjunctival injection
2 Burning or stinging; crusting, irregularity, erythema and/or edema of eyelid margins; definite conjunctival injection; chalazion or hordeolum
3 Pain, photosensitivity, blurred vision; severe eyelid changes with loss of lashes, severe conjunctival inflammation; corneal changes, with potential loss of vision; episcleritis, scleritis, iritis
Management Guidelines
  • Topical medications for grade 1
  • Systemic medications for grade 2
  • Refer to ophthalmologist for persistent grade 1 or 2 disease or suspected grade 3 disease

Rosacea Variants & Special Forms

Solid Facial Edema in Rosacea (Morbihan Disease / Rosacea Lymphedema)

  • Etiology: Unknown; possibly result of chronic inflammation → obstruction of lymph vessels or fibrosis. (Similar condition in acne)
  • Clinical: Hard, non-pitting swelling of forehead, glabella, nose, and cheeks.
  • May be more pronounced during early morning hours.
  • Can impair vision if eyelids involved.
  • Spontaneous resolution DOES NOT occur.
  • ToC: Isotretinoin + Ketotifen (antihistamine).
  • Other Options: Systemic steroids, antibiotics, lymphatic drainage/compression therapy.

Pyoderma Faciale (Rosacea Fulminans)

  • Epidemiology: Females in their 20s-30s.
  • Clinical: Rapid onset of intensely inflamed coalescent fluctuant nodules and cysts on a background of dark red to cyanotic erythema.
  • Can have draining sinuses with purulent drainage.
  • Location: Centrofacial region.
  • NO involvement elsewhere and NO comedones (vs. acne fulminans).
  • Most develop scarring.
  • Systemic Symptoms: Can have low-grade fever, myalgias, ↑WBC, and ↑ESR.
  • Treatment (same as acne fulminans): Prednisone (with slow taper) AND Isotretinoin.

Granulomatous Rosacea

  • Epidemiology: Middle-aged women.
  • Clinical: Discrete yellow/brown-red firm papules or nodules on a background of diffusely reddened, thickened skin.
  • Distribution: Butterfly region (classic rosacea areas), also can be distributed around periphery of face and perioral areas. (Reference: "Retre sportened" - likely a typo, interpreted as distribution)
  • Histopathology: Non-caseating epithelioid granulomas; resembles sarcoidosis. Key differentiator from LMDF!
  • Course: Difficult to treat, no consensus.
  • Anecdotal Treatment Options: Dapsone, minocycline, isotretinoin, HCQ, 1450-nm diode laser.

Lupus Miliaris Disseminatus Faciei (LMDF)

Board Favorite: Often confused with Granulomatous Rosacea & Sarcoidosis!

Pay attention to differentiating features.

  • Epidemiology: Young adults; more common in Asians (especially Japanese).
  • Clinical: Smooth, firm, yellow-brown to red 1-3 mm monomorphous papules. (Fig. 3-77)
  • Distribution: Typical butterfly area of rosacea, but also seen laterally (below mandible) and periorificially.
  • ESPECIALLY CHARACTERISTIC: Eyelid skin involvement.
  • Heals WITH scarring. This is a major distinguishing feature.
  • Diascopy: "Apple-jelly" appearance (like sarcoid and TB).
  • Histopathology: Dermal granulomas with CENTRAL CASEATION NECROSIS. Key differentiator from Granulomatous Rosacea (non-caseating) and Sarcoidosis (typically non-caseating "naked" granulomas).
  • NO extrafacial involvement. (Typically for LMDF, but some rare reports exist. For boards, assume no extrafacial.)
  • Treatment: Difficult; can try isotretinoin and TCNs.

Other Follicular & Related Disorders

Pityriasis Folliculorum

  • Epidemiology: Women 20-40 y/o who rarely allow water on their face and instead use moisturizing and cleansing creams.
  • Clinical: Roughened whitish scaling skin surface ("frosty") and some scattered fine papules and pustules.
  • Tx: Topical sulfur or permethrin 5% cream.

Perioral/Periorificial Dermatitis

Clinical Clue!

Look for the clear zone around the vermilion border.

  • Epidemiology: Young women in their 20-30s. Children can also be affected.
  • Pathogenesis: Inflammatory condition of unknown cause, perhaps related to rosacea. Most commonly attributed to use of topical fluorinated corticosteroids or facial cosmetics.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Clusters of small, pink discrete scaly papules/pustules in perioral region.
    • Characteristic: Clear zone around the vermilion border.
    • Can also involve nasolabial folds and cheeks.
    • Symptoms: Burning sensation, minimal itching.
  • Treatment/Clinical Course:
    • Self-limited, although resolution can take months to years.
    • AVOID: Cosmetics, topical steroids, and other irritating topicals. Stopping topical steroids can lead to initial flare!
    • Systemic: TCNs (e.g., doxycycline) or erythromycin (in pediatrics) for 6-8 weeks with gradual tapering to avoid rapid rebounding.
    • Topical: TCIs (topical calcineurin inhibitors), topical antibacterials (e.g., metronidazole, erythromycin, clindamycin).
  • Variants:
    • Periorbital/periocular dermatitis
    • Periorificial dermatitis (combination of perioral and periorbital/periocular)

General Recommendations for Facial Skin Care and Education in Patients with Rosacea

(Based on Table 37.4 context)

Facial Skin Care

  • Wash with lukewarm water and use soap-free cleansers that are pH balanced.
  • Cleansers are applied gently with fingertips.
  • Use sunscreens with both UVA and UVB protection and an SPF ≥30.
  • Sunscreens containing inorganic filters titanium dioxide and/or zinc oxide are usually well tolerated.
  • Use cosmetics and sunscreens that contain protective silicones (e.g., dimethicone).
  • Water-soluble facial powder containing inert green pigment helps to neutralize the perception of erythema.
  • Moisturizers containing humectants (e.g., glycerin) and occlusives (e.g., petrolatum) help to repair the epidermal barrier.
  • AVOID:
    • Astringents, toners, and abrasive exfoliators.
    • Cosmetics that contain alcohol, menthols, camphor, witch hazel, fragrance, peppermint, and eucalyptus oil.
    • Waterproof cosmetics and heavy foundations that are difficult to remove without irritating solvents or physical scrubbing.
    • Procedures such as glycolic peels or dermabrasion (unless carefully considered by dermatologist).

Patient Education

  • Reassure the patient about the benign nature of the disorder and the rarity of rhinophyma, particularly in women.
  • Emphasize the chronicity of the disease and the likelihood of exacerbations.
  • Direct patients to information websites such as those of the National Rosacea Society (www.rosacea.org) or the American Academy of Dermatology (www.aad.org).
  • Advise to avoid recognized triggers.
  • Explain the importance of compliance with topical regimens.
  • Educate on the importance of sun avoidance.

Pathomechanisms in Rosacea

(Based on Fig. 37.1 Major pathomechanisms in rosacea)

In genetically predisposed individuals (e.g., HLA-DRB1*03:01, HLA-DQA1*05:01, HLA-DQB1*02:01, SNP rs763035), environmental triggers (e.g., heat, alcohol, UV radiation (20% +re)) and other pathogenic factors can trigger neurovascular dysregulation and an aberrant innate immune response, both leading to cutaneous inflammation and clinical manifestations.

Neurovascular Dysregulation

  • Involves TRPV cation channels (Transient Receptor Potential Vanilloid).
  • Leads to:
    • Stinging, burning (neurosensory symptoms)
    • Vasodilation
    • Plasma extravasation
  • Contributes to: Erythema, telangiectasia.

Aberrant Innate Immunity

  • Involves Toll-like receptor 2 (TLR2).
  • Increased Kallikrein 5 (KLK5), also known as stratum corneum tryptic enzyme (SCTE).
  • KLK5 cleaves antimicrobial peptide cathelicidin (hCAP18) into active pro-inflammatory form LL-37.
  • Increased LL-37 promotes:
    • Inflammation (e.g., ↑CD4+ T cells)
    • Angiogenesis (↑VEGF - Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor)
  • Contributes to: Papules, pustules, inflammation, angiogenesis.

Overall Clinical Manifestations from these pathways: Erythema, telangiectasia, papules, pustules, fibrosis, sebaceous gland and connective tissue hypertrophy.
