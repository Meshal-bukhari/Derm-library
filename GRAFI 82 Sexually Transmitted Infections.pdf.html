Syphilis

Previous Exam Question

Key MCQ Point: Syphilis can involve every organ except ovaries.

Definition & Etiology

Sexually acquired infection caused by Treponema pallidum (gram-negative spirochete)

Highest risk in homosexual males

Characteristics of Treponema pallidum
  • 6-20 microns in length, 0.1-0.18 microns in diameter
  • Regular tight spirals
  • Inability to survive outside an animal host
  • Limited capacity for DNA repair
  • Outer membrane:
    • Lipid-rich
    • Contains uniform-sized transmembrane rare outer membrane proteins (TROMP)
    • Lacks lipopolysaccharide
  • Periplasmic flagella

Primary Stage
  • Occurs ~3 weeks after infection
  • Chancre and enlarged lymph nodes
  • Th1 immune response
  • Attachment and multiplication after contact with abraded skin
Secondary Stage
  • Occurs ~3 months after chancre
  • Dissemination and multiplication in different tissues
  • Happens in ~100% of untreated patients
  • Circulating immune complexes, human anti-fibronectin antibodies, and complement contribute to lesions
  • Lasts weeks to months
  • Relapse after clearance in ~25% of patients
  • Only "wet" lesions are infectious (condyloma lata and mucous patches)
Latency
  • Period between healing of primary/secondary syphilis and appearance of late stages
  • Can last for many years
  • ~70% will remain in this stage for life
  • Positive antibodies
  • Can be early latent (≤1 year) or late latent (>1 year)
  • 90% of relapse occur in the 1st year of latency
  • Intermittent infectivity, especially mother to fetus
Tertiary Stage
  • Also known as late syphilis
  • Small number of organisms but high immune-complexes
  • Occurs in 1/3 of untreated patients
  • Gummas in the skin, cardiovascular system, or CNS
  • Neurosyphilis can happen during any stage

Syphilis and HIV: Patients with syphilis have an increased risk of having HIV

Clinical Features

Classification

Syphilis is divided into two subsets:

  • Early syphilis: includes primary, secondary, and early latent
  • Late syphilis: includes late latent and tertiary

The definition of early vs. latent syphilis differs between organizations:

  • CDC: 1 year is the cutoff
  • WHO: 2 years is the cutoff

This division is important for treatment purposes.

Indications for lumbar puncture in syphilis:

  • Tertiary syphilis
  • Neurological or ophthalmological symptoms
  • Treatment failure
  • Neonates with proven or highly probable congenital syphilis: CSF analysis should be performed prior to therapy
  • Can occur at any stage
  • Variable neurological symptoms
  • Tabes dorsalis: associated with Argyll Robertson pupils (abnormal pupillary light reflexes: loss of direct and consensual; retention of accommodation)
  • All patients have high titers of RPR/VDRL and TPHA
  • All patients should undergo lumbar puncture

Know the difference between early vs late manifestations

Congenital syphilis is divided into early and late (less than and greater than 2 years of age)

Early Congenital Syphilis

  • Manifestations within first 3 months (max within first 2 years)
  • Marasmic syphilis (cachexia)
  • Resembles the secondary stage of acquired syphilis
  • Pemphigus syphiliticus: variant with bullous lesions
  • Snuffles: bloody or purulent mucinous nasal discharge
  • Perioral/perianal fissures, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
  • Pseudoparalysis of Parrot: reduced movement of extremities due to pain
  • Anemia, thrombocytopenia

Late Congenital Syphilis

  • Syphilis "stigmata"
  • Delayed consequences of localized inflammation at sites of treponemal infection
  • Mulberry molars
  • Rhagades: perioral fissures
  • Higoumenakis sign MCQ
  • Clutton's joints
  • Saddle nose deformity
  • Hutchinson triad: interstitial keratitis, SNHL, and Hutchinson teeth
Mother-to-Child Transmission of Untreated Syphilis

Risk

  • Infection from conception to 7th month: nearly 100% transmission (often fetal demise or severe congenital syphilis)
  • Infection at least 2 years before pregnancy: reduced risk to 50%
  • Infection during 7th, 8th, or early 9th month: reduced risk
  • Infection 3-6 weeks before labor: no placental transmission; risk of perinatal transmission

Consequences

  • Spontaneous abortion (second or third trimester): 10%
  • Stillbirth: 10%
  • Infant death: 20%
  • Congenital syphilis: 20%
  • Healthy child: 40%

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made by either direct detection of treponemes/treponemal DNA or by serology

Pathology

Direct Detection Tests

  • Darkfield microscopy
    • Visual detection of motile spirochetes from skin lesions
    • Should not be done on oral lesions (spirochetes are part of normal oral flora)
  • PCR
    • Especially useful for neurosyphilis (lumbar puncture)

Non-treponemal Tests

  • VDRL and RPR
  • Used as screening tests
  • Titers correlate with disease activity
  • Can be used to monitor treatment response (4-fold decrease)
  • Can be false negative due to:
    • Prozone phenomenon: Very high antibody levels paradoxically inhibit the test reaction (flocculation); requires serum dilution to overcome
    • HIV coinfection: Temporary negative result in secondary syphilis associated with HIV infection; may become reactive on subsequent testing
    • Early primary syphilis: Lack of reactivity before sufficient antibody response has developed, even with chancre positive by darkfield microscopy or PCR
  • Can be false positive (SLAP HIM mnemonic)
  • False positive causes (SLAP HIM mnemonic):
    • SSLE
    • LLEPROSY
    • Antiphospholipid syndrome/ACUTe Febrle ilness
    • Pregnancy
    • Hepatic cirrhosis
    • Infectious diseases (e.g., malaria)
    • Miscellaneous:
      • Illicit drug abuse
      • Lymphomas
      • Vaccinations
      • Idiopathic, familial
      • Endemic treponematoses (yaws, pinta, bejel - caused by related Treponema species)
      • Borreliosis (Lyme disease - caused by Borrelia species, another type of spirochete)

Treponemal Tests

  • TPHA, ELISA, and FTA-ABS
  • Used as confirmatory tests
  • Stay positive for life - not useful for disease monitoring
  • Cross-reactivity: Cross-reactivity with other treponematoses

Primary Syphilis

  • Ulceration
  • Diffuse dermal infiltrate of plasma cells, lymphocytes, and histiocytes
  • Spirochetes usually detected with Warthin-Starry stain

Secondary Syphilis

  • Less specific
  • Psoriasiform hyperplasia
  • Dermal infiltrate of plasma cells, lymphocytes, and histiocytes
  • Think of it as two reaction patterns in the same sample (e.g., psoriasiform + lichenoid)
  • Vasculitis in lues maligna
  • Spirochetes detected in most cases

Tertiary Syphilis

  • Tuberculoid granulomas with or without caseation
  • Spirochetes rarely detected

Treatment

  • First-line: Penicillin G
    • Benzathine penicillin, 2.4 million units IM as a single dose (early) or weekly for three doses (late)
  • Second-line: Tetracyclines
  • Special case: If pregnant with penicillin allergy → desensitize then give penicillin MCQ

Gonorrhea

Definition & Epidemiology

  • Most common reportable STI in high-income countries (the most common STI overall is HPV, but sources differ)
  • Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gram-negative diplococci)

Pathogenesis

  • Pili → motility
  • Pili and Opa ligands → adhesion
  • Proteases → break down IgA
  • Lipopolysaccharide - endotoxin

Clinical Features

  • Can be asymptomatic (10% men and 50% women)
  • Acute anterior urethritis with dysuria and urethral discharge in men
  • Ascension may lead to epididymitis, prostatitis, and vesiculitis
  • "Bull head clap": swelling of the distal penile shaft
  • Increased vaginal discharge, dysuria, intermenstrual bleeding, and menorrhagia in women
  • Ascension may lead to pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)
  • Gonorrheic perihepatitis (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome): complication leading to pain in right upper quadrant mimicking acute cholecystitis
  • Extracutaneous involvement:
    • Pharynx
    • Rectal mucosa (MSM)
    • Eye (ophthalmia neonatorum, mother to neonate)

Chancroid

Definition & Etiology

  • Also known as soft chancre
  • Acute genital ulcer + inguinal adenitis
  • Caused by Haemophilus ducreyi (gram-negative anaerobic bacilli)
  • Risk factor for HIV, as with all genital ulcers

Clinical Features

  • Painful ulcer with purulent base and soft, undermined edges (vs syphilis)
  • Several ulcers may develop
  • Usually co-infection with other STDs (HSV, syphilis)
  • Painful inguinal lymphadenitis may accompany genital lesions, usually unilateral
  • Inguinal buboes may rupture → ulcers
  • Buboes are painful adenitis that form sinuses and produce pus

Diagnosis

  • A "school-of-fish" or "railroad track" appearance on smears

Treatment

  • Ceftriaxone + azithromycin single dose

Lymphogranuloma Venereum (LGV)

Definition & Etiology

  • Caused by Chlamydia trachomatis serovars L1-3

Clinical Features

Three Stages:

  1. Infection of the genital mucosa
  2. Inguinal lymphadenopathy (unilateral)
  3. Buboes formation accompanied by proctocolitis and involvement of perirectal or perianal lymphatic tissue
Detailed Clinical Manifestations of LGV
Stage Manifestations
Initial manifestations: 3-12 days
  • Papule
  • Erosion or ulcer
  • Herpetiform vesicle
  • Nonspecific urethritis or cervicitis
Inguinal syndrome: 10-30 days up to 6 months
  • Regional lymphadenopathy (mostly inguinal and femoral; also perirectal, deep iliac)
  • Overlying erythema
  • Constitutional symptoms
  • Eruption of buboes
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), back pain
Ano-genito-rectal syndrome: months to years
  • Proctocolitis
  • Hyperplasia of intestinal and perirectal lymphatic tissue
  • Perirectal abscesses
  • Ischiorectal and rectovaginal fistulas
  • Anal fistulas
  • Rectal strictures and stenoses
Other manifestations
  • Urethro-genito-perineal syndrome
  • Peno-scrotal elephantiasis
  • Erythema nodosum
  • Submaxillary or cervical lymphadenopathy associated with oropharyngeal lesions
  • Herpetiform lesion develops at site of exposure and heals rapidly without a scar
  • Most common location: coronal sulcus in men and posterior vaginal wall in women
  • Usually accompanied by local lymphangitis
  • The groove sign: lymph node enlargement on both sides of the inguinal ligament (pathognomonic for LGV)
  • Co-infection with other STDs
  • Inguinal syndrome: unilateral lymphadenopathy → buboes → rupture → sinus tracts
  • Ano-genito-rectal syndrome: local abscesses with anal fistulas → rectal strictures and stenoses

Diagnosis

  • H&E: Gamna-Favre bodies inside histiocytes
  • PCR: chlamydia DNA

Treatment

  • First-line: Doxycycline
  • Second-line: Azithromycin

Donovanosis (Granuloma Inguinale)

Definition & Etiology

  • Caused by Klebsiella granulomatis (gram-negative bacilli)

Clinical Features

  • Small papule or nodule that eventually ulcerates
  • Painless or mildly painful
  • Beefy red appearance with tendency to bleed
  • Foul-smelling exudate is characteristic
  • Bone is the most common extracutaneous site of involvement

Diagnosis

  • Pathology: Donovan bodies in histiocytes ("safety pin" appearance)
  • Smear: Giemsa, Wright, or Leishman stains

Treatment

  • First-line: Azithromycin 1 g weekly or 500 mg daily for at least 3 weeks and until all lesions have completely healed
  • Second-line: Doxycycline

MCQ Review Points

Which organ is NOT involved in syphilis?
Ovaries
What is the most sensitive and specific method for diagnosing primary syphilis?
Darkfield microscopy
What are the indications for lumbar puncture in syphilis?
  1. Tertiary syphilis
  2. Neurological or ophthalmological symptoms
  3. Treatment failure
How should you treat a pregnant patient with syphilis who has a penicillin allergy?
Desensitize then give penicillin
What is the pathognomonic finding in lymphogranuloma venereum?
The groove sign: lymph node enlargement on both sides of the inguinal ligament
What is Higoumenakis sign?
Thickening of the medial clavicle (a stigmata of congenital syphilis)
What is the classic triad of disseminated gonorrhea?
Fever, joint pain, and cutaneous eruption of hemorrhagic pustules (due to vasculitis)
What deficiency is associated with disseminated gonorrhea?
C5-C9 complement deficiency
