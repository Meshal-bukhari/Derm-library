Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD)

Comprehensive Board Review Guide

High-Yield Key Points

  • ICD is a non-immunologic inflammatory reaction, unlike allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)
  • ICD is the most common form of occupational skin disease (70-80%)
  • Most ICD is caused by cumulative exposure to weak irritants (e.g., soap and water)
  • Patients often complain of burning and stinging rather than pruritus (helpful for differentiating from ACD)
  • Common irritants: soaps, detergents, solvents, acids, alkalis, wet work
  • Avoidance of causative irritants is primary treatment

Definition & Pathogenesis

Definition: Irritant contact dermatitis (ICD) is a localized, non-immunologically initiated, cutaneous inflammatory reaction resulting from direct cytotoxic effects of chemicals or physical agents on the skin.

Mechanism of Action

  • Activation of innate immune system without prior sensitization
  • Direct cytotoxic effect on keratinocytes
  • Damaged keratinocytes release mediators (TNF, IL-1α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8)
  • Cytokine release → leukocyte recruitment → inflammation
  • Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) including Toll-like receptors may be activated

Mnemonic: "DIRECT DAMAGE"

  • Direct cytotoxicity (no prior sensitization)
  • Innate immune activation
  • Reactive oxygen species
  • Epidermal barrier disruption
  • Cytokines release (TNF, IL-1α)
  • Transcutaneous water loss increased
  • Denaturated proteins
  • Adhesion molecules upregulated (ICAM-1)
  • Membrane lipid disruption
  • Acute or chronic changes (depending on exposure)
  • Gradual onset with repeated exposure (cumulative)
  • Environmental factors influence severity

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Epidemiology

  • 70-80% of all occupational skin disorders
  • 9-10% of patch-tested patients with contact dermatitis
  • When hands are affected, ICD predominates over ACD
  • Hands are the most common site affected . Face is the second most common site
  • Female sex (higher prevalence in women)

Risk Factors

  • Host factors:
    • Atopic dermatitis (13.5× greater risk)
    • Age extremes (infants and elderly)
    • Genetic predisposition
    • Reduced filaggrin in epidermis
  • Environmental factors:
    • "Wet work" (immersion >2 hours/day or handwashing >20 times/day)
    • Low humidity and cold temperatures
    • Occlusion
    • High-risk occupations (healthcare, manufacturing)

Clinical Features & Types of ICD

Types of Irritant Contact Dermatitis

1 Acute ICD: Rapid onset (minutes to hours), "decrescendo" phenomenon, burning/stinging, sharply demarcated borders, erythema, edema, bullae, necrosis. Caused by potent irritants.
2 Acute Delayed ICD: Delayed response (8-24 hours), burning > pruritus, no previous exposure needed. Caused by anthralin, benzalkonium chloride, ethylene oxide.
3 Irritant Reaction ICD: Subclinical form with scaling, redness, vesicles in individuals with wet chemical exposure. Often begins under jewelry.
4 Cumulative ICD: Result of multiple sub-threshold insults without sufficient recovery time. Less sharply demarcated, xerosis, lichenification, hyperkeratosis. Most common form.
5 Traumatic ICD: Follows acute skin trauma. Persistent redness, infiltration, scale, fissuring. Lasts weeks to months.
6 Pustular/Acneiform ICD: "Sterile" pustules from metals, oils, tars, greases. Often in patients with atopic or seborrheic dermatitis.
7 Non-erythematous ICD: Subclinical form with barrier dysfunction without visible inflammation.
8 Subjective/Sensory ICD: Stinging/burning with no visible signs. Caused by propylene glycol(can be found in cosmetics , topical medications and emollients >30% concentration is irritant), hydroxy acids, ethanol.
9 Airborne ICD: Affects face and periorbital regions. From dusts, fibers, fragrances, solvents. Upper eyelids, philtrum and submental involvement.
10 Frictional ICD: From repeated low-grade trauma. Shows hyperkeratosis, lichenification. Often on dominant hand.
11 Asteatotic Dermatitis: Dry winter skin with ichthyosiform scale and cracked skin ("eczema craquelé").

Mnemonic: "ACID STANDS PAF"

Types of ICD:

Major Irritant Chemicals

1-Inorganic Acids

  • Hydrofluoric acid: Extremely cytotoxic cause the most severe chemical burn, penetrates intact skin deeply , bind tissue calcium and affect nerves even leat to systemic toxcity , delayed pain, neutralized with calcium gluconate
  • Chromic acid: "Chrome holes" ulcerations, nasal septal perforation
  • Sulfuric acid: Severe burns
  • Hydrochloric acid: Erythema, vesication, necrosis
  • Nitric acid: Yellow discoloration

2-Alkalis

  • Cement: Calcium hydroxide liberation, high alkalinity (pH 12-14), necrotic changes appear 8-12h after exposure
  • Sodium/potassium hydroxide: Like other strong alkalis, they disrupt barrier lipids and denature proteins. They cause fatty acid saponification, which facilitates deeper penetration into the skin. They cause severe and painful damage, often worse than most acids (except hydrofluoric acid)
  • Calcium oxide (quicklime): Releases heat with water contact-> ulceration

3-Solvents

  • Mechanism: Dissolve intercellular lipid barrier
  • Irritancy order: aromatic > aliphatic > chlorinated > turpentine > alcohols > esters > ketones
  • Examples: benzene (Petechia and purpura), toluene, chlorinated hydrocarbons, petroleum derivatives

4-Detergents/Cleansers

  • Mechanism: Lower surface tension, damage stratum corneum, bind to keratin
  • Irritancy order: anionic (most) > non-ionic > amphoteric (least)
  • Examples: sodium lauryl sulfate (anionic), cocamide DEA (non-ionic)

5-Other Common Irritants

  • Water: Osmolarity, pH, hardness, temperature, occlusion
  • Alcohol/glycols: Protein denaturation, extraction of lipids
  • Disinfectants: Aldehydes, phenolics, quaternary ammonium compounds
  • Metal salts: Arsenic, beryllium(ulcerated Granulomatous , sarcoidal like), cobalt, copper, mercury, selenium sulfide (skin irritation and conjunctivitis), thimerosal ( can be found in vaccine, common cause of ACD and ICD)
  • Bodily fluids: Urine, feces, saliva
  • Plant irritants: Bromelain (pineapple), capsaicin (Hot peppers – affect nerves, not skin. Neutralized with acetic acid (vinegar) or antacids. Best home remedy: Wash with soap 1 hr immersion in vegetable oil), isothiocyanates (mustard), calcium oxalate (found in plant families Amaryllidacea/Lilaceae such as Daffodil (Narcissus spp.), hyacinth, and tulip bulbs). Calcium oxalate in Daffodil is the most common cause of ICD in florists, referred to as "daffodil itch".

6-Special Clinical Entities

  • Diaper dermatitis: Glazed erythema on convex surfaces, spared folds
  • Granuloma gluteale infantum: Papules and nodules from chronic irritation
  • Jacquet erosive dermatitis: Punched-out erosions and ulcerations
  • Perioral ICD: From lip licking, especially in children
  • Periurethral ICD: In patients with bladder-drained pancreatic allografts or foscarnet

Diagnosis: Irritant Contact Dermatitis

Patch Test Interpretation in ICD

False Positives

  • Common with strong irritants (e.g., formaldehyde, propylene glycol)
  • Clues to identify irritant reactions:
    • "Decrescendo phenomenon": Irritant reactions peak early and fade
    • Non-specific erythema without papules/vesicles

ICD Mimics

  • Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS): Used as a positive irritant control
  • Metals (e.g., cobalt): Can cause both ICD and ACD

When to Suspect ICD Despite Positive Patch Test

Exposure History > Test Results

  • Patient reports burning pain (not itch) with a product, but patch test is positive for an ingredient
  • May indicate coexisting ICD + ACD

Repeated Open Application Test (ROAT)

  • If patch test is equivocal, apply suspect product to forearm twice daily for 7 days
  • ICD: Burning/redness within days
  • ACD: Pruritic rash after 48+ hours

Assessment Tools

  • Hand Eczema Severity Index (HECSI): Evaluates hand eczema based on clinical signs and locations
  • Modified Total Lesion Symptom Score (mTLSS): Grades erythema, scaling, lichenification, vesiculation, edema, fissures, and pruritus/pain
  • Transepidermal Water Loss (TEWL): Objective measure of barrier function

Treatment & Prevention

Primary Treatment Principles

  1. Avoidance of causative irritants (most important step)
  2. Barrier restoration: Moisturizers, emollients, barrier creams
  3. Inflammation control: Topical corticosteroids
  4. Prevention strategies: Protective equipment, education

Specific Treatments

  • Acute ICD: Remove irritant, gentle cleansing, mid-to-high potency topical corticosteroids
  • Chronic ICD: Emollients, barrier repair, mid-potency topical corticosteroids
  • Severe/Recalcitrant: Consider NB-UVB, PUVA, systemic therapy (retinoids, immunomodulators)
  • Special scenarios:
    • Hydrofluoric acid burns: Calcium gluconate gel/injection
    • Diaper dermatitis: Barrier creams, gentle cleansing, brief topical steroids
    • Granuloma gluteale: Remove irritant, antifungals if superinfected

Prevention Strategies

  • Technical avoidance: Eliminate/substitute irritants when possible
  • Engineering controls: Closed systems, automation, ventilation
  • Personal protective equipment:
    • Appropriate gloves (material selection matters)
    • Protective clothing
    • Proper removal techniques to avoid contamination
  • Barrier creams: Applied before exposure
  • Skin care regimen: Gentle cleansers, regular moisturization
  • Education: "Eczema schools," workplace training programs
  • Regular monitoring of early signs of irritation

Mnemonic: "BARRIERS"

Prevention strategies:

  • Barrier creams before exposure
  • Avoid irritants when possible
  • Restore skin barrier with emollients
  • Reduce wet work exposure time
  • Identify early signs of irritation

  • Substitute safer products when available

Prognosis & "Hardening" Phenomenon

Prognosis Factors

Poor Prognosis

  • History of atopic dermatitis
  • Female sex
  • Concurrent allergic contact dermatitis
  • Delayed diagnosis and treatment
  • Continued exposure to irritants
  • Poor compliance with preventive measures

Good Prognosis

  • Early diagnosis and treatment
  • Effective avoidance of irritants
  • Patient education and compliance
  • "Hardening" phenomenon
  • Non-atopic individuals
  • Less severe initial presentation

"Hardening" Phenomenon

Spontaneous resolution despite continued exposure occurs in many individuals through:

  • Physical barrier improvement: Thicker stratum corneum and stratum granulosum, increased ceramide 1
  • Vascular changes: Faster removal of irritants
  • Immunologic alterations: Increased IL-1Ra to IL-1α ratio (anti-inflammatory)
  • Systemic hyporeactive state: After repeated low-dose exposure

Quick Review Quiz

1. Which of the following is the most common type of irritant contact dermatitis?

Answer: Cumulative ICD from repeated exposure to weak irritants like soaps and water.

2. What is the primary pathologic mechanism in ICD?

Answer: Direct cytotoxic effect on keratinocytes with activation of the innate immune system (not requiring prior sensitization).

3. Which symptom helps distinguish ICD from allergic contact dermatitis?

Answer: Burning and stinging (rather than pruritus, which is more characteristic of ACD).

4. What is the "decrescendo phenomenon" in acute ICD?

Answer: The acute reaction peaks quickly (within minutes to hours) and then begins to heal.

5. Which factor is most strongly associated with increased risk of occupational ICD?

Answer: History of atopic dermatitis (13.5× greater risk).
