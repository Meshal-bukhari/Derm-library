Morphea & Lichen Sclerosus

Interactive Board Exam Study Guide

Quick Overview

Morphea (Localized Scleroderma)

  • Inflammatory skin disease causing cutaneous sclerosis
  • Asymmetric distribution without internal organ involvement
  • Distinct from systemic sclerosis (SSc)
  • May lead to joint contractures and muscle atrophy

Lichen Sclerosus

  • Inflammatory disease leading to ivory-white atrophic sclerosis
  • Primarily affects genital mucosa
  • Can cause scarring and functional impairment
  • ~4% risk of SCC in untreated vulvar LS

Morphea Classification

Key for Exams: Plaque-type (circumscribed) morphea is the most common variant overall, while linear morphea is most common in children (two-thirds have onset before age 18).
Morphea Subtype Clinical Features
Plaque-type (Circumscribed)
  • Most frequent variant
  • Erythematous/violaceous plaques with lilac border
  • Central hypopigmentation, later hyperpigmentation
  • Usually 2-15cm in diameter
  • Common on trunk and extremities
Linear Morphea
  • Linear bands of sclerosis
  • Often involves underlying fascia, muscle, tendons
  • Can cause joint contractures and limb asymmetry
  • Variants:
    • En coup de sabre (forehead/scalp)
    • Parry-Romberg syndrome (hemifacial atrophy)
Generalized Morphea
  • Multiple coalescing plaques affecting wide areas
  • May affect nearly entire trunk
  • Often persistent despite treatment
Deep Morphea
  • Affects deep dermis and subcutaneous fat
  • May involve underlying fascia and muscle
  • "Pseudo-cellulite" appearance
Atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini
  • Very superficial variant of plaque-type morphea
  • Hyperpigmented patches most commonly on posterior trunk
  • Depressed patches with "cliff-drop" borders
  • May follow Blaschko lines (linear atrophoderma of Moulin)
Other Variants
  • Nodular/Keloidal: Thick, keloid-like nodules
  • Bullous: Stasis of lymphatic fluid causing blisters
  • Pansclerotic: Severe variant in children
Examination Tip: Know how to differentiate Atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini from other morphea subtypes. It is distinguished by: (1) superficial involvement only, (2) hyperpigmented patches with "cliff-drop" borders, (3) minimal or no inflammation, (4) absence of sclerosis, and (5) commonly affects the trunk.

Lichen Sclerosus: Clinical Presentations

Anogenital LS
  • Severe pruritus and soreness
  • Dysuria, dyspareunia, pain with defecation
  • Figure-of-eight configuration
  • Areas of purpura and erosions
  • Progressive scarring (fusion of labia, phimosis)
  • Risk of SCC (~4% if untreated)
Extragenital LS
  • Often asymptomatic
  • Occasional dryness and mild pruritus
  • Ivory-colored, shiny papules and plaques
  • Follicular plugging
  • Possible hemorrhagic bullae
  • Common sites: trunk, shoulders, neck
Three major components in morphea pathogenesis:
  1. Vascular damage
  2. T-cell activation
  3. Altered connective tissue production by fibroblasts

Key Cytokines in Morphea Pathogenesis

Board Exam Focus: IL-4, IL-13, and TGF-β are critical cytokines in morphea pathogenesis. They promote fibroblast activation and collagen production.
  • IL-4:
    • Produced by CD4+ Th2 lymphocytes
    • Directly enhances TGF-β production
    • Most potent driver of Th2 differentiation
    • Enhances pathologic collagen production
  • IL-13:
    • Th2-associated cytokine
    • Promotes skin fibrosis
  • TGF-β:
    • Key profibrotic cytokine
    • Back-crossing TSK mice with TGF-β-deficient mice prevents skin sclerosis
  • CXCL4:
    • Promotes Th2 responses
    • Elevated in systemic sclerosis
    • Correlates with progression of skin fibrosis

Mnemonic: "TIFT" for Key Morphea Cytokines

  • Transforming growth factor (TGF-β)
  • Interleukin-4 (IL-4)
  • Fibroblast activation via these cytokines
  • Thirteen (IL-13)

Autoantibodies in Morphea

Key Associations:
  • Anti-ssDNA antibodies in linear morphea are associated with increased risk of functional impairment
  • Anti-histone antibodies (AHA) in linear morphea
  • High titers of ANA in juvenile patients with linear morphea and generalized morphea

Bullous Morphea Pathogenesis

Mechanism: In patients with sclerosis and rapidly progressive edema, lymphoceles may result from stasis of lymphatic fluid. When these develop into bullae, it is referred to as bullous morphea. Mechanical blisters may also occur centrally within plaques due to impaired stability of the dermal-epidermal junction.

Lichen Sclerosus Pathogenesis

  • Genetic factors: Association with MHC class II antigen HLA-DQ7
  • Autoimmunity: 80% of patients have IgG autoantibodies against extracellular matrix protein 1 (ECM-1)
  • Inflammatory mediators: Increased production of IL-6, TNF, and collagen (types I, III, and V)
  • Oxidative stress: Lipid peroxidation, oxidative DNA damage

Morphea: Histopathologic Features

Important: Histopathologic findings in morphea depend on:
  1. Stage of the lesion (inflammatory vs. sclerotic)
  2. Area sampled (inflammatory margin vs. central sclerosis)
  3. Depth of involvement
Early/Inflammatory Margin
  • Vascular changes with endothelial swelling and edema
  • Perivascular infiltrate of CD4+ T cells
  • Sometimes eosinophils, plasma cells, and mast cells
  • Edema in the dermis and upper subcutis
Late/Central Sclerosis
  • Inflammatory infiltrate wanes
  • Normal or flattened epidermis with loss of rete ridges
  • Densely packed collagen bundles aligned parallel to the dermal-epidermal junction
  • Significant reduction in capillaries and small vessels
  • Atrophic eccrine glands "trapped" in thickened dermis
  • Homogenized and hyalinized subcutis
Deep Morphea: Involves extensive sclerosis and hyalinization extending into the underlying fascia, sometimes with vacuolated muscle.

Lichen Sclerosus: Histopathologic Features

Characteristic Findings:
  1. Thinned epidermis with orthohyperkeratosis
  2. Vacuolar degeneration of the basal layer
  3. Homogenization and hyalinization of the upper dermis (pale staining)
  4. Loss of elastic fibers (distinguishes from morphea)
  5. Subepidermal clefting and hemorrhage
  6. Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate in early lesions
Board Exam Tip: When comparing morphea and lichen sclerosus histologically, remember that loss of elastic fibers is typical for LS but not observed in morphea.

Laboratory Findings in Morphea

Common Laboratory Abnormalities:
  • Eosinophilia may occur, especially during early active phases of the disease
  • ESR and serum protein levels are usually normal

Autoantibodies in Morphea

Autoantibody Association with Morphea Subtype
Anti-nuclear antibodies (ANA) High titers in juvenile patients with linear morphea and individuals with generalized morphea (40-80%)
Anti-single strand DNA (ssDNA) Associated with linear morphea and increased risk of functional impairment
Anti-histone antibodies (AHA) Associated with linear morphea and increased risk of functional impairment
Anti-topoisomerase IIα Increased prevalence in morphea
Anti-phospholipid antibodies Increased prevalence in morphea
Anti-fibrillin-1 antibodies Increased prevalence in morphea
For the Exam: Remember that autoantibodies are usually not prevalent in plaque-type morphea in adults but are common in linear and generalized morphea.

Laboratory Findings in Lichen Sclerosus

Key Finding: IgG autoantibodies against extracellular matrix protein 1 (ECM-1) are found in 80% of patients with LS
  • Higher prevalence of autoimmune diseases in female patients with LS
    • Especially autoimmune thyroid disease
    • ANA positivity
  • Association with HLA-DQ7 (important susceptibility gene)

Treatment of Morphea

Treatment Selection Factors:
  • Level of disease activity
  • Subtype of morphea
  • Depth of involvement
  • Location and extent of disease
  • Degree of current or potential functional impairment
First-line Treatment for Severe or Joint-Crossing Linear Morphea in Children: Weekly methotrexate (0.3-0.5 mg/kg/week) plus systemic corticosteroids (typically pulsed IV methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day for 3 consecutive days per month)

Phototherapy for Morphea

Most Effective Phototherapy Modalities (in order of preference):
  1. UVA1 (first choice when available)
  2. Broadband UVA (BB-UVA)
  3. PUVA
  4. NB-UVB (only for superficial morphea)
Exam Tip: For UVA1 therapy, 36 treatments with either 30J/cm² or 50-60J/cm² markedly improves morphea in most patients. The optimal dose for UVA1 is medium-dose (40-70J/cm²).
Morphea Subtype Recommended Treatment
Plaque-type (limited)
  • Topical class I corticosteroids
  • Intradermal corticosteroids into active rim
  • Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment
  • Topical vitamin D analogues
  • Lesion-limited phototherapy
Linear (superficial)
  • Phototherapy
Linear (deep, facial, or crossing joints)
  • Weekly methotrexate + systemic corticosteroids
  • Second-line: Mycophenolate mofetil, JAK inhibitors, tocilizumab
Generalized
  • Phototherapy (if superficial)
  • Weekly methotrexate + systemic corticosteroids
  • Second-line: Mycophenolate mofetil, JAK inhibitors, tocilizumab

Treatment of Lichen Sclerosus

First-line Treatment for Genital LS: Ultrapotent topical corticosteroids (e.g., clobetasol propionate 0.05%)
Genital LS Treatment
  • Ultrapotent topical corticosteroids
  • Intralesional corticosteroids for hyperkeratosis
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
  • Frequent emollients
  • Long-term maintenance therapy to prevent malignancy
  • Surgery for SCC or scarring
  • Circumcision for phimosis
Extragenital LS Treatment
  • Potent topical corticosteroids
  • Topical vitamin D analogues
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
  • Phototherapy (UVA1 preferred)
  • For widespread cases: weekly methotrexate ± corticosteroids

Clinical Course of Morphea

  • Most patients: progresses over 3-5 years, then arrests and resolves
  • Residual atrophy and dyspigmentation are common
  • Linear morphea often has prolonged periods of quiescence followed by reactivation

Complications of Morphea

Serious Complications:
  • Joint contractures and decreased range of motion
  • Growth retardation and limb asymmetry (linear morphea)
  • Ocular involvement (15% of juvenile and 25% of adult patients with linear morphea of the head)
  • Neurologic symptoms (e.g., seizures) in en coup de sabre morphea
  • Calcinosis cutis as sequelae
Functional Impact: In ~10% of patients with morphea, sclerosis leads to significant contractures, growth retardation, and even micromelia, resulting in long-term functional disability.

Complications of Lichen Sclerosus

  • Genital LS:
    • Scarring (burying of clitoris, fusion of labia, phimosis)
    • Sexual dysfunction
    • Urinary obstruction
    • Development of squamous cell carcinoma (~4% in untreated vulvar LS)
  • Extragenital LS:
    • Usually only cosmetic concerns
    • Hemorrhagic bullae
Important for Exams: A recent prospective cohort study of 507 women with vulvar LS noted the development of SCC or VIN in 5% of patients partially compliant with topical corticosteroid treatment and in none of the compliant patients, supporting the importance of maintenance therapy.

High-Yield Board Examination Points

Morphea Subtypes and Classification
  • Plaque-type (circumscribed) morphea is the most common variant overall
  • Linear morphea is most common in children (two-thirds have onset before age 18)
  • Atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini is considered by some to be a very superficial variant of plaque-type morphea
  • Parry-Romberg syndrome is a severe variant of linear morphea with progressive loss of subcutaneous fat and little or no sclerosis
Pathogenesis and Laboratory Findings
  • IL-4, IL-13, and TGF-β are critical cytokines in morphea pathogenesis
  • Eosinophilia may occur during early active phases of morphea
  • Anti-ssDNA antibodies and anti-histone antibodies in linear morphea are associated with increased risk of functional impairment
  • IgG autoantibodies against ECM-1 are found in 80% of patients with lichen sclerosus
  • Association of lichen sclerosus with HLA-DQ7
Histopathology
  • Morphea: Densely packed collagen bundles aligned parallel to the dermal-epidermal junction; perivascular infiltrate of CD4+ T cells; atrophic eccrine glands "trapped" in thickened dermis
  • Lichen Sclerosus: Thinned epidermis with orthohyperkeratosis; vacuolar degeneration of basal layer; homogenization of upper dermis; loss of elastic fibers (distinguishes from morphea)
Treatment
  • First-line treatment for severe or joint-crossing linear morphea in children: Weekly methotrexate plus systemic corticosteroids
  • Most effective phototherapy for morphea: UVA1, followed by BB-UVA and PUVA
  • Genital LS: Ultrapotent topical corticosteroids, long-term maintenance therapy
  • Extragenital LS: Potent topical corticosteroids, phototherapy (UVA1)
Complications
  • Morphea: Joint contractures, growth retardation, limb asymmetry, ocular/neurologic involvement (linear morphea of the head)
  • Lichen Sclerosus: ~4% risk of SCC in untreated vulvar LS; scarring (burying of clitoris, fusion of labia, phimosis)
