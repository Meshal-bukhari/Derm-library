Subcorneal Pustular Dermatosis
(Sneddon-Wilkinson Disease)

A High-Yield Board Review

At a Glance: The Core Concept

Subcorneal pustular dermatosis (SPD) is a rare, chronic, relapsing dermatosis characterized by crops of sterile subcorneal vesicles or pustules occurring in an annular configuration on normal to slightly erythematous flexural skin.

Board Exam Crux: SPD is distinguished from pustular psoriasis by (1) histology showing subcorneal pustules without spongiform pustules and (2) a notable response to dapsone.

Demographics & Nature

  • Prevalence: Rare, no racial or ethnic predilection.
  • Population: More common in women, typically over the age of 40.
  • Nature: A benign condition, though its presentation may look severe.
  • Symptoms: Lesions are often asymptomatic, but patients may report itching or a burning skin sensation.

Clinical Picture: What to Look For

Key Lesion Morphology

  • Small, discrete, flaccid pustules or pustulovesicles.
  • Arise in crops on clinically normal skin.
  • Tendency to coalesce into Annular, polycyclic, or serpiginous patterns.
  • Classic Buzzword: "Fluid level" may be seen (sterile pus on lower half, clear fluid on top).

Distribution & Symmetry

  • Primarily affects truncal and flexural areas.
  • Common sites: Axillae, groin, abdomen, inframammary skin, flexural extremities.
  • Distribution is usually widespread and bilaterally symmetrical.

Evolution of Lesions

Pustules dry up after a few days, forming scaly annular plaques and crusts (may resemble impetigo). New crops develop at the periphery of older lesions, reinforcing the annular or polycyclic shapes. The cycle repeats every few days or weeks if untreated. Lesions heal without scarring but may leave postinflammatory hyperpigmentation.

Beyond the Skin: Associated Conditions

The etiology of SPD is unknown, but it has critical systemic associations. Always screen for these.

⚠️ High-Yield Association: Don't Miss!

SPD is strongly associated with IgA monoclonal gammopathy (paraproteinemia) and can be a presenting sign of IgA myeloma. This can develop years after the initial skin diagnosis.

Other Reported Associations:

  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Ulcerative colitis, Crohn disease
  • Rheumatologic: Rheumatoid arthritis, Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)
  • Autoinflammatory: SAPHO syndrome (synovitis, acne, pustulosis, hyperostosis, osteitis), Pyoderma gangrenosum
  • Hematologic/Endocrine: Polycythemia vera, thyroid dysfunction, endocrine tumors

Confirming the Diagnosis: Workup & Histology

A punch biopsy for routine histopathology and direct immunofluorescence (DIF) is the best diagnostic test. Always perform a KOH, bacterial Gram stain, and culture from a pustule to rule out infectious mimics.

Click to Reveal Key Histopathology Findings
  • Location: A strictly subcorneal pustule.
  • Contents: Filled primarily with neutrophils (rarely eosinophils).
  • Key Negative: No spongiform pustules (this points away from psoriasis).
  • Epidermis: Surrounding epidermis is normal. Secondary acantholysis may occur.
  • Dermis: Perivascular infiltrate of neutrophils, with occasional mononuclear cells or eosinophils.
Click to Reveal Direct Immunofluorescence (DIF) Results

In classic SPD, DIF is typically NEGATIVE.

Clinical Pitfall: If DIF shows intraepidermal IgA deposits (especially against desmocollins), the diagnosis shifts to SPD-type IgA pemphigus, not classic SPD.

Differential Diagnosis: Don't Get Fooled!

Pustular Psoriasis
Histology: Shows intraepidermal spongiform pustules.
Systemics: Can present with fever/malaise, which are typically absent in SPD.
SPD-type IgA Pemphigus
DIF is key: Shows intraepidermal IgA deposits on immunofluorescence.
Bacterial Impetigo
Asymmetrical distribution.
Gram stain/culture: Positive for bacteria (e.g., Staph).
Response: Responds to antistaphylococcal antibiotics.
Dermatitis Herpetiformis
Location: Favors extensor surfaces.
Histology: Subepidermal neutrophils (not subcorneal).
Pemphigus Foliaceus
Histology: Shows acantholysis in the granular layer.
Acute Generalized Exanthematous Pustulosis (AGEP)
Widespread eruption on a background of erythroderma, often with associated systemic symptoms and a clear drug history.
Tinea Corporis
KOH prep: Positive for hyphae. Hyphae also seen on biopsy.
Necrolytic Migratory Erythema (Glucagonoma Syndrome)
Lacks true pustules. Patients have oral mucous membrane erosions.
Histology: Necrobiosis of the upper epidermis.
Eosinophilic Folliculitis of Ofuji
This is a common distractor but not explicitly detailed in the source text. Key features would be perifollicular pustules, often in HIV patients or infants, with prominent eosinophils on biopsy.

Management Strategy & Pearls

Screening & Monitoring

Given the strong associations, a detailed review of systems is required. Focus on rheumatologic, hematologic, and GI symptoms.

ACTIONABLE PEARL: Long-Term Surveillance

Screen for urine and serum paraproteins (SPEP, UPEP) at diagnosis and repeat periodically during active disease. Myeloma can develop years later. Positive cases usually show IgA paraprotein (kappa or lambda light chains). IgG/IgM are rare.

Therapeutic Ladder (for Adult Patients)

  1. First-Line Therapy:

    Dapsone (50-200 mg PO once daily). This is the treatment of choice. Note: The response is slower than in dermatitis herpetiformis. Most patients achieve remission and are maintained on a minimal effective dose.

  2. Other Reported Therapies:
    • Topical and systemic corticosteroids
    • Sulfapyridine (compounding) or Sulfasalazine
    • Colchicine
    • Systemic Retinoids (Acitretin, Isotretinoin)
    • Phototherapy (Broad/Narrowband UVB, PUVA)
  3. Recalcitrant / Severe Cases:
    • Immunosuppressants: Cyclosporine, Methotrexate, Mycophenolate Mofetil (weigh risks vs. benefits in this benign condition).
    • Biologic Agents: Infliximab, Etanercept, Adalimumab have been tried with success.
  4. Topical Adjuncts:

    Topical vitamin D3 analogs and topical dapsone have been postulated to be beneficial.
