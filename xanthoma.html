Xanthoma Types: Clinical Summary

Comprehensive guide to xanthoma classification, clinical presentation, and disease associations

Xanthoma Type Clinical Presentation Key Lipid Abnormality Disease Associations & Special Notes
1. Eruptive Xanthomas
Morphology: Abrupt onset of small (1-5mm) yellow papules with erythematous "inflammatory" halo

Distribution: Extensor surfaces of extremities, buttocks, hands

Symptoms: Tender and pruritic

Special Sign: Koebner phenomenon
SEVERE Hypertriglyceridemia

Triglycerides often > 3000-4000 mg/dl
Primary Associations:
• Frederickson Types I, IV, V
• LPL deficiency

Secondary Causes:
• Uncontrolled Diabetes
• Obesity, alcohol abuse
• Medications (retinoids, protease inhibitors)
RED FLAG: Risk of acute pancreatitis

Resolution: Prompt (within weeks) when triglycerides lowered
2. Tuberous & Tuberoeruptive
Morphology: Pink-yellow papules (tuberoeruptive) coalescing into large, firm, non-tender nodules (tuberous)

Distribution: Pressure areas, especially elbows and knees
Elevated both cholesterol AND triglycerides

Chylomicron and VLDL remnant accumulation
Most Characteristic:
• Dysbetalipoproteinemia (Type III, Broad Beta Disease)

Also seen in:
• Familial Hypercholesterolemia (Type II)
Resolution: Slow to regress even with therapy
3. Tendinous Xanthomas
Morphology: Firm, smooth, deep subcutaneous nodules attached to tendons. Normal overlying skin

Distribution: Achilles tendons and extensor tendons of hands, knees, elbows
Primarily Hypercholesterolemia

(Elevated LDL)
Most Common:
• Familial Hypercholesterolemia (Type II)
• Dysbetalipoproteinemia (Type III)

Rare Normolipemic ("Zebra"):
• Cerebrotendinous Xanthomatosis (cholestanol)
• Sitosterolemia (plant sterols)
Resolution: Slow to regress
4. Plane Xanthomas
Location is key to diagnosis
General: Yellow to orange, non-inflammatory macules, patches, or thin plaques

Xanthelasma Palpebrarum
Most common type, on eyelids
Xanthoma Striatum Palmare
In palmar creases
Intertriginous
Finger web spaces, body folds
Diffuse
Large plaques on neck, trunk, flexures
Variable by subtype:

• Xanthelasma: Only 50% have hyperlipidemia

• Palmar creases: Mixed hyperlipidemia

• Intertriginous: Severe hypercholesterolemia

• Diffuse: NORMOLIPEMIC
Xanthelasma: Still warrants lipid workup

Palmar Creases: Nearly diagnostic for Dysbetalipoproteinemia (Type III)

Intertriginous: PATHOGNOMONIC for HOMOZYGOUS Familial Hypercholesterolemia

Diffuse: MUST workup for monoclonal gammopathy (plasma cell dyscrasia, lymphoma)
5. Verruciform Xanthomas
Morphology: Asymptomatic, solitary, verrucous (wart-like) or planar plaque

Distribution: Primarily in the mouth, also anogenital and acral sites

Histology: Foamy macrophages limited to dermal papillae
NORMOLIPEMIC

Not associated with systemic lipid disorder
Usually: Isolated finding

Can be associated with:
• Lymphedema
• Lichen planus
• GVHD
• Other inflammatory conditions

Syndromic: CHILD Syndrome (NSDHL gene mutation)
