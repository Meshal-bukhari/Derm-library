LIPOBLASTOMA

Dermatology Board Review

Subject Profile & Initial Findings

Benign neoplasms of immature adipose tissue, primarily affecting neonates and infants.

Gender Predilection: Males affected twice as often as females.

The term "Lipoblastoma" typically refers to encapsulated lesions, while "Lipoblastomatosis" is used for unencapsulated, more infiltrating forms.

Lipoblastoma ENCAPSULATED

  • Soft, subcutaneous neoplasm
  • Well-defined, encapsulated
  • Rare recurrence after complete excision

Lipoblastomatosis INFILTRATING

  • Unencapsulated, more diffuse
  • More infiltrating nature
  • Higher incidence of recurrence
Typical Size: Ranges from 1-12 cm.

Field Observations: Clinical Presentation

Parents usually notice a:

  • Slow-growing mass
  • Soft consistency
  • Mobile (especially Lipoblastoma)
  • Painless nodule or deep soft tissue mass
Common Locations: Extremities (most common), trunk, and face.

Typically observed in neonates and infants.

Investigator's Hunch: Key Diagnostic Pearl

BOARD EXAM ALERT!
In an infant or neonate, true lipomas are RARE. If you encounter a lesion resembling a lipoma in this age group, your primary suspicion should be LIPOBLASTOMA!

Potential Misidentifications: Differential Diagnosis

Liposarcoma

Key Distinctions from Lipoblastoma:

  • Age: Generally found in children and young adults older than 10 years (Lipoblastoma is in neonates/infants).
  • Location: Typically in deep soft tissue.
  • Histology: Shows cellular atypia and more frequent mitoses (see Best Tests).

Laboratory Analysis: Best Tests

Excisional Biopsy is key. Histopathological findings include:

Microscopic Evidence:
  • Lobules of mature AND immature fat cells.
  • Presence of lipoblasts (immature fat cells) with varying degrees of differentiation.
  • LACK of significant cellular atypia.
  • RARE mitoses.

These latter two features (lack of atypia, rare mitoses) are crucial for distinguishing it histologically from liposarcoma.

Recommended Action Plan

Referral: To a dermatologist or pediatric plastic surgeon.
Treatment of Choice: Complete surgical excision of the lesion.

Prognosis & Recurrence:

  • Lipoblastomas (encapsulated): Easily treated by complete excision and rarely recur.
  • Lipoblastomatosis (diffuse): Has a higher incidence of recurrence due to its infiltrative nature. Careful and complete excision is paramount.
