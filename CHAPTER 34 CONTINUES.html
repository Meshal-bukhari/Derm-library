Neonatal Dermatology: High-Yield Board Review

A targeted review of key neonatal dermatologic conditions for the Saudi Board Exam

Newborn Injuries HIGH YIELD

Memory Aid: "CCS" - The Scalp Trio

Caput Succedaneum: Crosses sutures, above periosteum

Cephalohematoma: Confined by sutures, subperiosteal

Subgaleal Hematoma: Severe, potentially life-threatening, subgaleal space

Caput Succedaneum

  • Location: Above periosteum
  • Borders: Crosses suture lines
  • Presentation: Diffuse swelling, ± ecchymoses
  • Timing: Present at birth
  • Resolution: Several days

Cephalohematoma

  • Location: Subperiosteal
  • Borders: Does NOT cross suture lines
  • Presentation: Unilateral, most commonly over parietal bones
  • Labs: CBC, bilirubin if severe
  • Resolution: Weeks

Subgaleal Hematoma

  • Location: Subgaleal space
  • Borders: Crosses suture lines
  • Risk Factor: Traumatic/instrumented delivery
  • Complications: Anemia, DIC, shock
  • Management: Close monitoring, fluids, blood products
What differentiates Subcutaneous Fat Necrosis (SCFN) from Sclerema Neonatorum histologically?

Both show lipid crystals and needle-shaped clefts, but:

  • SCFN: Has granulomatous inflammatory infiltrate
  • Sclerema Neonatorum: Lacks granulomatous inflammation

Clinically, SCFN occurs in healthy neonates while Sclerema affects debilitated preterm/term infants.

Miscellaneous Neonatal Findings

Congenital Milia

  • Prevalence: 16%-50% of newborns
  • Origin: Inclusion cysts from infundibula of vellus hairs
  • Appearance: Minute, white, smooth papules
  • Location: Face
  • Treatment: None
  • Resolution: Several months

Epstein Pearls/Bohn's Nodules

  • Origin: Microkeratocysts along embryonic fusion lines
  • Appearance: 1-2 mm gray-white, smooth papules
  • Epstein location: Palatal raphe
  • Bohn location: Alveolar ridge
  • Treatment: None
  • Resolution: Most by 5 months

Which birthmark is associated with Tuberous Sclerosis and appears as a "shagreen patch"?

Answer: Connective Tissue Nevus (CTN)

The collagenoma variant of CTN manifests as a "shagreen patch" in Tuberous Sclerosis. It appears as a firm, skin-colored to yellowish plaque with a leather grain texture, typically on the trunk or limbs.

Congenital Infections HIGH YIELD

📝 Multiple Choice Alert: CMV is the most common infectious cause of congenital deafness and mental retardation!

📝 Multiple Choice Alert: Neonatal HSV typically presents at 5-14 days after birth

Memory Aid: "TORCH" Infections & Findings

Toxoplasmosis - Intracranial calcifications

Other (Syphilis) - Hutchinson triad(interstitial keratitis, hutchinson teeth, 8th nerve deafness), snuffles

Rubella - "Blueberry muffin baby" (EMH)

CMV - #1 cause of congenital deafness

Herpes - Vesicles, scarring, high mortality

"Blueberry Muffin Baby" Causes

  • Rubella - EMH: soft spongy erythematous/violaceous papules
  • CMV - Most common congenital infection
  • Toxoplasmosis - Often with intracranial calcifications
  • All show extramedullary hematopoiesis (EMH) on biopsy

Congenital vs. Neonatal HSV

  • Congenital (Intrauterine) HSV:
    • Present at birth/first few days
    • Vesicles, widespread erosions, congenital scars
    • Often affects scalp (aplasia cutis)
  • Neonatal HSV:
    • Presents at 5-14 days
    • Types: SEM (Skin, Eyes, Mouth), Disseminated, CNS
    • 40% with CNS infection have neurologic sequelae despite treatment

What is Hutchinson's Triad in congenital syphilis?

Answer: Hutchinson's Triad consists of:

  1. Interstitial keratitis
  2. Hutchinson teeth (notched incisors)
  3. 8th nerve deafness

These are late manifestations (appearing after 2 years of age) in congenital syphilis.
