Neonatal HSV – Quick Review for MCQ Exam

A. Core Knowledge

Definition & Classification of Risk

  • High Risk: Primary maternal genital HSV infection in 3rd trimester → Transmission risk 30%-50%.
  • Low Risk: Recurrent maternal HSV infection (maternal IgG protects neonate) → Transmission risk <1%-3%.

Management Algorithm

  1. Maternal Assessment at Labor:
    • Thorough exam: vulva, perineum, vagina, cervix.
    • Check for active lesions or prodromal symptoms (tingling, burning, pain).
  2. Mode of Delivery:
    • No lesions/symptoms: Vaginal delivery acceptable (risk persists due to asymptomatic shedding).
    • Lesions/symptoms present: Cesarean delivery strongly recommended.
  3. Asymptomatic High-Risk Neonate Post-Delivery:
    • Close monitoring by pediatrics.
    • Parental education: Watch for lethargy, irritability, poor feeding, temp instability, seizures, bulging fontanelle, vesicles (may be absent in disseminated/CNS disease).
    • No prophylactic antivirals mentioned → observation is standard.
  4. If Neonate Becomes Symptomatic:
    • Medical emergency → full diagnostic evaluation + empiric treatment.
    • Diagnostics: HSV cultures (mouth, NP, conjunctiva, anus), blood HSV PCR, CSF PCR + cell analysis.
    • Treatment: Start IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h immediately. Duration: 14–21 days depending on disease extent.

B. High-Yield Exam Facts

  • First-line treatment: IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h → inhibits viral DNA polymerase.
  • Side effects: Renal toxicity (crystalluria), neurotoxicity.
  • Cesarean section: Indicated if maternal lesions present at delivery.
  • Memorize Mortality Data:
    • Transmission risk – primary 3rd trimester: 30%-50%
    • Transmission risk – recurrent infection: <1%-3%
    • Untreated CNS/disseminated neonatal HSV: >50% mortality
    • Treated: ~15% mortality (many survivors with neuro deficits)

C. Exam Tricks & Pitfalls

The Distractor Disease (Primary vs. Recurrent):
If history of recurrent HSV with last outbreak weeks ago → low risk (<3%) → routine neonatal care. Spot the word "primary."
No Lesions = No Risk Fallacy:
Absence of lesions ≠ zero risk. Risk still high (30%-50%) with recent primary infection due to asymptomatic shedding.
Timeline Trap:
Primary infection early in pregnancy (e.g., 12 weeks) → time to develop maternal IgG → neonate becomes low risk by delivery.
“Next Best Step” Dilemma (Asymptomatic Neonate):
Distractor: Start prophylactic IV acyclovir.
Correct (per text): Close observation + parental counseling.
