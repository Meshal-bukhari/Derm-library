🩺 Alopecia Areata Comprehensive Guide

Saudi Board Dermatology Exam Preparation

🎯 KEY EXAM POINTS - MEMORIZE THESE!

🔥 High-Yield Abstract Points

Alopecia Areata (AA) is a complex autoimmune disease manifesting as a chronic inflammatory disease characterized by non-scarring patches of hair loss over face, scalp, and body.

Board Pearl: Several treatments have been proposed for AA, but NONE are curative nor achieve a state of remission. Remember this for true/false questions!

📊 Success Criteria - Must Know for Exam!

The success of AA treatment is achieved by meeting ANY or ALL of the following at week 24:

  • SALT 75 reduction
  • SALT score 20 or less
  • Achieving DLQI of 5 or less
⚠️ Special Exception: For Janus kinase inhibitors (JAKi), success of treatment should be reached maximum by week 36.

🧬 Background and Definition

Pathophysiology - High Yield!

AA is a complex auto-immune disorder mediated by a local surge of interferon gamma that:

  1. Collapses the hair follicle immune privilege
  2. Elicits an autoimmune response targeting exposed hair follicle autoantigens
  3. Activates autoreactive cytotoxic CD8+NKG2D+T cells
2.3-5.2%
Prevalence in Saudi Arabia
20-50
Peak incidence age (years)
33-75%
Relapse rates with corticosteroids
Saudi-Specific Pearl: The prevalence of AA in Saudi Arabia (2.3%-5.2%) is higher than Western countries, and patients present at younger ages compared to Western populations.
Memory Tip: Remember "High, Young, Relapse" - Saudi Arabia has HIGH prevalence, YOUNG presentation, and HIGH relapse rates regardless of therapy.

🔬 Clinical Features, Diagnosis, and Investigation

🎭 AA Patterns - Must Know All!

Patchy Alopecia

Most typical presentation - single or multiple patches of localized hair loss

Alopecia Totalis (AT)

Complete loss of scalp hair

Alopecia Universalis (AU)

Total loss of body hair and scalp terminal hair

Ophiasis Pattern

Band-like hair loss at the periphery of the occipital and temporal areas

Ophiasis Inversus-Sisaipho

Band-like hair loss in the frontoparietal area

AA Incognita

Diffuse hair loss and thinning simulating androgenetic alopecia and telogen effluvium

Peri Nevoid AA

Very rare variant - hair loss surrounding a pigmented nevus

Board Pearl: AA can present with signs localized to specific hairy regions outside the scalp, such as the beard, eyelashes, and eyebrows.

🩺 Clinical Symptoms and Signs

💭 Associated Symptoms (Click to expand)

Patients with AA do not usually report other associated symptoms. However, they may experience:

  • Cutaneous dysesthesia - may precede or be associated with hair loss and is a sign of active disease
  • Scalp itching and tingling at the site of hair loss

🔍 Physical Examination Findings

Skin Appearance:

  • Underlying affected patches usually appear normal and smooth
  • May rarely exhibit pink discoloration
  • White hairs may initially be spared in AA

⚠️ Important Clinical Signs:

  • Canieties Subita: In extensive AA, sparing of white hairs leads to "sudden greying of the hair"
  • Regrowth Sign: Fine white vellus hairs may be a sign of hair regrowth

🔬 Diagnosis

AA diagnosis is mainly clinical and is established through physical examination and trichoscopic findings.
🧬 Supporting Diagnostic Elements (Click to expand)

Family History Support:

Clinical diagnosis is supported by obtaining a detailed family history for autoimmune disorders including but not limited to:

  • Thyroid disorders
  • Atopy
  • Celiac disease

Activity Assessment:

The activity of hair loss can be determined by noting:

  • "Exclamation point hairs" at the periphery of bald areas
  • The qualitative positive AA "hair pull test"

🚫 When NOT to Biopsy

Scalp biopsy is NOT recommended unless the diagnosis is not clear.

✅ Characteristic Clinical Features

  1. Intact follicular ostia visualized either directly or through dermoscopy
  2. Positive pull test
  3. Complete loss of terminal hairs in at least one patch of hair loss
  4. Absence of signs of active follicular inflammation (such as perifollicular erythema, scaling, pustules, etc.)

🔬 Supportive Dermoscopic Features

  • Yellow and/or black dots at the ostia on dermoscopy
  • Trachyonychia
  • Fine pitting of the nails
  • 20-nail dystrophy
Memory Acronym for AA Diagnosis: "IPCT" - Intact ostia, Positive pull test, Complete terminal hair loss, Total absence of inflammation.

📏 Assessment and Evaluation

🎯 Assessment Tools - High Yield!

📊 Multiple Severity Assessment Tools Available:

  • Severity of Alopecia Tool (SALT)
  • SALT II
  • Alopecia Density and Extent (ALODEX)
  • Alopecia Areata Progressive Index
  • Quality-of-life measures including Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI)
  • Alopecia Areata Symptom Impact Scale (AASIS)
  • AA Scale (2022) - designed specifically for clinical practice
🎯 Board Consensus:
  • Use SALT score as measure of disease severity in children and adults
  • Use ClinRO for eyebrows and eyelashes involvement assessment

📐 SALT Scoring - Must Master!

SALT I Scoring History:

  • First published in 1999
  • Updated in 2004
  • Provides visual guide to estimate area of terminal hair loss
  • Divides scalp into four quadrants
  • Generates percentage of scalp hair loss and SALT score

🔢 How SALT Works:

  1. Estimate area of terminal hair loss in each of the four quadrants of the scalp
  2. Generate the percentage of scalp hair loss
  3. Calculate SALT score by adding up estimates from the four quadrants
  4. Allows clinicians to estimate total area of scalp with loss of terminal hair
Memory Tip: "SALT = Scalp Areas Loss Total" - Remember it measures total scalp hair loss across 4 quadrants.

🧪 Laboratory Evaluation

⚠️ Important Principle:

Laboratory evaluation is warranted ONLY upon clinical suspicion of an underlying disorder, and there is NO set of tests that is to be routinely ordered for patients presenting with AA.

🔬 When to Order Laboratory Tests (Click to expand)

Specific Clinical Scenarios:

🦠 Tinea Capitis Suspicion:

When there are clinical signs of tinea capitis infection → fungal microscopy should be performed

🩸 Blood Tests as Needed:

When patients present with clinical signs and symptoms pointing towards another underlying etiology:

  • Complete blood cell count
  • Renal and liver function
  • Screen for other diseases:
    • Autoimmune diseases
    • Connective tissue disease
    • Celiac disease
    • Pernicious anemia
    • Diabetes
🦠 Viral Serology:

May be useful in patients who present with clinical symptoms suggestive of a triggered alopecia areata episode

❌ What NOT to Order:

Measuring early morning cortisol levels is NOT indicated in patients who believe that stress may have triggered their current episode.

💊 Pre-Systemic Treatment Labs:

In preparation for initiation of systemic treatment for AA patients, laboratory investigations must be done and are identical to those performed in other dermatological diseases.

🧠 Psychosomatic Evaluation

🔑 Fundamental Component:

Identifying patients and families who need psychological support is a fundamental component of AA management.

🧒 Children & Adolescents

At increased risk for psychiatric comorbidity, particularly mood and anxiety disorders

👨‍⚕️ Adults

Also at increased risk for psychiatric comorbidity, especially with severe or recalcitrant disease

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Parents

Parents of children affected by AA may also experience anxiety and depression

Board Pearl: Psychological impact is especially significant in patients with severe or recalcitrant disease, and extends to family members, particularly parents.

🎲 Prognosis and Prognostic Indicators

⚠️ Unpredictable Course:

AA is a chronic disease with an unpredictable course.

70%
Spontaneous remission of single lesion in first year
7%
Progress to AT or AU

📉 Poor Prognostic Indicators - High Yield!

🚨 Must Know These 8 Factors:

  • 1. Childhood onset of AA
    Particularly those presenting at less than 6 years of age
  • 2. Severity of disease at onset in adults
    This is the most important negative prognostic indicator
  • 3. Duration of active disease
    • Prognosis worse when AA persists beyond 5 years (2% potential for hair regrowth)
    • Disease can be irreversible if persists for 10 years
    Important Note: This is NOT a contraindication for therapy trial. Successful hair regrowth cases have been seen in patients with disease duration exceeding 10 years.
  • 4. AA phenotype
    Especially ophiasis pattern
  • 5. Involvement of eyebrows, eyelashes, and non-scalp hair loss
  • 6. Nail involvement
    Nail pitting and trachonychia suggest increased risk of developing AT/AU and worsen prognosis overall
  • 7. Prior and/or current atopic conditions
    Including: atopic dermatitis, asthma, hay fever/allergic rhinitis, and/or allergic conjunctivitis
  • 8. Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome)
    • Associated with increased prevalence (9%) and severity of AA
    • Estimated that 40% of patients with trisomy 21 will develop AT/AU
Memory Acronym for Poor Prognosis: "CHILD-NAT" - Childhood onset, High severity, Illness duration >5yr, Long ophiasis pattern, Down syndrome, Nails involved, Atopy present, Total non-scalp involvement.

🔬 Associated Factors (Not Confirmed Poor Prognostic)

🧬 Systemic and Immunological Factors (Click to expand)

May Influence AA Emergence:

  • Personal history of diabetes mellitus
  • Peptic ulcer
  • Bilateral oophorectomy
  • Hypertension
  • Atherosclerosis
  • Grave's disease
  • Myxedema
Important: These are NOT confirmed to be poor prognostic factors.

🧬 Genetic Associations:

Type 1 diabetes and other autoimmune disorders such as celiac disease and rheumatoid arthritis have been shown to have a genetic association with AA, suggesting common molecular pathways in their pathogenesis.

❌ Not a Poor Prognostic Indicator:

Family history of AA has NOT been proven to be a poor prognostic indicator.

💊 Management of Alopecia Areata

🎯 Treatment Goals - Memorize!

🎯 Primary Objectives:

  1. Restore hair density
  2. Improve psychological wellbeing of impacted patients

✅ Success Criteria at Week 24:

Success is reached by meeting ANY or ALL of the following:

  • A) SALT 75 reduction
  • B) SALT score 20 or less
  • C) Achieving DLQI of 5 or less
⚠️ JAK Inhibitors Exception: Success should be reached maximum by week 36.

When Conservative Approach is Appropriate:

Reassurance can be enough for patients with limited stable disease.

🔬 Non-Pharmacologic Treatment

☀️ Photochemotherapy

❌ PUVA NOT Recommended:

Psoralen plus ultraviolet A (PUVA) is NOT recommended due to:

  • Risk of large cumulative doses of UVA long term
  • AA is a chronic disease
  • Increases risk of cutaneous malignancy

✅ Alternative: UVA-1

UVA-1, which has greater penetration into the skin and is more effective, may be an alternative option in AA treatment.

🔬 Excimer Laser/Light

Mechanism and Use:

  • AA is a T-cell mediated autoimmune disorder
  • 308-nm excimer laser and light have immunosuppressive properties by inducing T-cell apoptosis
  • Can be used as alternative treatment and adjuvant therapy for acute/subacute resistant patches

Side Effects:

  • Mild erythema
  • Contact eczema
  • Blistering
  • Pruritus
  • Hyperpigmentation

💉 Intralesional Treatment

💊 Intralesional Corticosteroid Therapy

Intralesional corticosteroids are the standard of therapy for patients with clinical evidence of active inflammation.

🔍 Indicators of Active Inflammation:

  • Positive pull test
  • Black dots
  • Exclamation point hairs
💉 Triamcinolone Acetonide (TAC) Protocol (Click to expand)

📊 Dosing and Administration:

  • Most commonly used corticosteroid: Triamcinolone acetonide (TAC)
  • Recommended dose for scalp patchy AA: 2.5 mg/ml at higher volume of 8 ml per session
  • Diluted with saline
  • 0.05 – 0.1 mL per injection into dermis or upper subcutaneous tissue
  • Injections spaced 0.5-1 cm apart
  • Time interval: 4-6 weeks between sessions

⚠️ Special Precautions:

  • Caution required near frontal hairline - higher risk of atrophy
  • Lidocaine can be mixed with TAC, but some components can increase flocculation risk → more atrophy
  • Apply local anesthetics, cooling, or vibration to minimize discomfort
  • Needle-free devices are another option

🛑 When to Stop:

Treatment should be discontinued if no improvement within 6 months.

🚨 Side Effects:

  • Pain, bleeding, headaches
  • Systemic absorption
  • Dyspigmentation
  • Reversible skin atrophy (lasting a few months)
  • Rarely: anaphylaxis

🧴 Topical Treatment

💊 Topical Corticosteroid Therapy

Topical corticosteroid therapy is the first-line topical treatment for scalp AA in children and adults who refuse intralesional therapy.

👨‍⚕️ Adult vs Pediatric Recommendations (Click to expand)
👨 Adults

Ultrapotent topical corticosteroid recommended:

  • Clobetasol propionate foam
  • Cream or ointment

More effective under occlusion (but also more side effects)

👶 Children < 10 years

Less potent corticosteroid preferred:

  • Mometasone

📅 Treatment Duration and Monitoring:

  • Apply daily for 6 weeks to 6 months
  • Complete regrowth = indication for discontinuation
  • After regrowth: tapering based on individual situation and clinical judgment

🚨 Common Side Effects:

  • Folliculitis
  • Acneiform eruption
  • Atrophy
  • Dyschromia
  • Telangiectasia
  • Adrenal suppression (less common)

💊 Minoxidil

🔬 Mechanism (Still Unknown):

Proposed theories include:

  • Vasodilation
  • Potassium channel opening
  • Angiogenesis
  • Induction of follicular dermal papilla proliferation

📊 Clinical Use:

  • Increases duration of anagen phase
  • Increases length and thickness of new hair
  • In adults: 5% Minoxidil once or twice daily
  • 5% Minoxidil alone is insufficient in AA
  • Used as adjuvant therapeutic agent with other treatments
  • May help maintain hair growth induced by other treatments

✅ Advantages:

  • Easy to use
  • Minimal side effects (mainly mild dermatitis and itching)
  • Less than 5% may grow unwanted hair

💊 Topical Immunotherapy (TIT)

TIT is used in extensive chronic AA.

🧪 TIT Agents and Protocols (Click to expand)

🔬 Available Agents:

  • Diphenylcyclopropenone (DPCP)
  • Squaric acid dibutyl-ester (SADBE)

Mechanism: Not fully understood. Proposed theory is that TIT allows follicle recovery through induction of inflammatory process in a place other than the hair bulb.

💊 DPCP - Most Used TIT:

  • Success rate: 30-48%
  • Overall response rate: 72.2% across all grades
  • Limited evidence for extensive resistant AA
  • Only available at compounding pharmacies
  • Formulated with acetone in amber bottle (light protection)

📋 DPCP Protocol:

  1. Sensitization Phase:
    • Apply 2% DPCP on 4 cm diameter area (behind ear)
    • Cover scalp, avoid light and water for 48 hours
  2. Treatment Phase:
    • Start with 0.001% DPCP one week after sensitization
    • Apply unilaterally by physician/nurse
    • Increase concentration weekly until effective concentration reached
    • Ideal concentration: mild tolerable dermatitis lasting 36 hours
    • Once response on one side → treat both sides
  3. Weaning Phase:
    • After hair regrowth: reduce frequency to biweekly → monthly → discontinue
    • Do NOT discontinue abruptly (increases relapse risk)

🚫 Contraindications and Side Effects:

  • Contraindicated in pregnant women
  • For DPCP failures: may try SADBE
  • Side Effects:
    • Pigmentary changes
    • Vitiligo (particularly in dark skin)
    • Severe eczema
    • Blistering
    • Lymph node enlargement
    • Flu-like symptoms

💊 Anthralin

🔬 Background and Mechanism:

  • Historically used for psoriasis
  • Reduces inflammation of hair follicle by diverting it from the bulb
  • Causes irritant dermatitis
  • No systemic side effects → can be used in children
📋 Anthralin Protocols (Click to expand)

📊 Available Concentrations:

Anthralin can be found in concentrations from 0.5% to 2%

🕐 Protocol 1 - Daily Application:

  1. Apply daily for 30 minutes
  2. Apply on affected area and up to 1 cm into healthy area
  3. Every 3 days: increase time by 15 minutes
  4. Maximum time: 2 hours
  5. Wash area thoroughly to remove product
  6. Initial response expected in 3 months
  7. Complete response in 15 months

🕐 Protocol 2 - Alternative Method:

  1. Apply 0.5% or 1% cream for 20-30 minutes daily
  2. Increase contact time by 10 minutes biweekly
  3. Maximum: 1 hour or until mild dermatitis
  4. Alternative: start at 0.1% for 10-20 minutes
  5. Gradually increase until overnight application tolerable

✅ Combination Therapy:

Combination of Anthralin + DPCP has been reported to be more effective than DPCP alone.

🚨 Side Effects and Limitations:

  • Follicular ostia pigmentation - appears as black dots
  • Can be differentiated by dermoscopy: brown pigmentation at edge of ostium (vs black dots where pigmentation is in center)
  • Lack of pigmentation = treatment failure
  • Failure causes: inappropriate application, inadequate time, low-quality preparation
  • Can stain hair, skin, and clothes (limits use in fair-haired patients)

💊 Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors

📍 Usage:

  • Can be used to treat scalp, eyebrow, or beard AA
  • NOT effective as monotherapy
  • Usually used with other agents
💊 Specific Agents (Click to expand)
Tacrolimus
  • Used for AA especially in face region
  • Preferred in pediatric population
  • Applied twice daily for 6 months
Pimecrolimus
  • Cream (1%)
  • Efficacy in AA was NOT proved

⚠️ Overall Recommendation:

The data available on these medications is lacking and their routine use in AA is NOT recommended.

💊 Systemic Treatment

✅ Eligibility Criteria - High Yield!

⚠️ Important Note:

There are NO set universal indications for initiating systemic treatment for AA.

🎯 Possible Indications (Ages 18-50 years):

  1. Extensive disease (SALT score ≥ 50)
  2. SALT score < 50 AND DLQI score ≥ 10
  3. Rapid progression of hair loss to ≥ 50% within 4 months of disease onset
  4. All lesions that have failed a 6-month trial of topical or serial intralesional corticosteroid
  5. Large or multiple lesions that cannot be easily managed with intralesional therapy due to:
    • Discomfort from number of injections required
    • Dermal atrophy following intralesional corticosteroid
    • Side effects from topical steroid use
  6. Significant facial involvement (eyebrows or eyelashes) + SALT 50 or above (any age within SFDA approval)
Memory Acronym for Systemic Indications: "EXRAFF" - EXtensive (SALT≥50), RApid progression, Failed topical/intralesional, Face involvement.

💊 Systemic Treatment Options

💊 Systemic Corticosteroids (Click to expand)

🥇 Prednisolone (Preferred Choice):

  • Preferred choice when systemic corticosteroid therapy instituted
  • Optimally administered daily
  • Suitable for severe AA in adolescents aged 13-18 years

📊 Dosing Protocol:

  • Initial dose: 0.4 to 0.6 mg/kg/day
  • Gradually tapered over minimum 12-week duration for durable remission
  • Adults with extensive AA: may need longer than 12 weeks tapering for durable remission

💊 Dexamethasone Oral Pulse:

  • For adults with extensive AA
  • 5mg/day two days a week for at least 12 weeks
  • Study results: 63% of 30 patients had excellent response
  • Mild side effects noted in 25% of patients
💊 Other Systemic Agents (Click to expand)
Cyclosporine
  • Effective in adults with severe AA
  • Dose: 3 to 5 mg/kg/day
  • Duration: does not exceed 6-12 months
Methotrexate
  • Can be used as monotherapy in severe AA
  • Adults: 15 to 20 mg weekly
  • Adolescents (13-18 years): 0.4 mg/kg/week
Azathioprine
  • Start: 0.5-1 mg/kg daily
  • Gradually titrate every 4-6 weeks
  • Maximum: 2-3 mg/kg
  • Based on response and tolerance
Sulfasalazine
  • 0.5 g twice daily for 1 month
  • → 1 g twice daily for 1 month
  • → 1.5 g twice daily for 4 months
  • Relapse rate: 45.5%
  • 32% suffered adverse effects

⚠️ Group Consensus:

Due to lower efficacy of sulfasalazine and azathioprine, there is group consensus that these medications should NOT be used as first line and are to be used only as alternative therapy.

🔥 JAK Inhibitors - High Yield! (Click to expand)

JAK inhibitors are small molecule drugs with a few currently in the pipeline for treatment of moderate to severe AA.

💊 Available JAK Inhibitors:

Baricitinib
  • JAK 1/2 inhibitor
  • Recently approved by SFDA for severe AA in adults
  • Dose: 2 to 4 mg orally once daily
Ritlecitinib
  • JAK3 selective inhibitor
  • Recently approved by FDA
  • For individuals aged 12 years and older
  • Dose: 30mg or 50mg once daily
Tofacitinib
  • JAK 1/3 inhibitor
  • Dose: 5 mg orally twice daily to 10 mg twice daily in severe AA
Ruxolitinib
  • JAK 1/2 inhibitor
  • Dose: 20 mg orally twice daily
  • Open-label pilot study: 75% achieved ≥50% hair regrowth after 3-6 months

🚨 JAK Inhibitor Side Effects:

  • Infections, viral reactivation
  • Dyslipidemia, transaminase changes
  • Leukopenia, headache
  • Gastrointestinal complaints
  • Thromboembolic events in high-risk patients
  • Fatigue, weight gain, acne
  • Long-term safety data still limited

📊 Response Characteristics:

Response to JAK inhibitors treatment does NOT correlate with disease severity nor duration, and there are no predictors of response up to this date.

🔄 Combination Systemic Agents (Click to expand)

💊 Proven Combinations:

1. Methotrexate + Steroids:
  • Methotrexate 10-25 mg per week + systemic steroids
  • Clinical response rate: 63%
2. Cyclosporine + Low-dose Corticosteroids:
  • Cyclosporine 2.5-5 mg/kg/day
  • + Low-dose corticosteroids (methyl prednisolone 20 mg/day → taper to 2 mg/day)
  • Adequate efficacy and minimal toxicity
3. Tofacitinib + Steroids:
  • Tofacitinib 5 mg twice daily + systemic steroids
  • For resistant cases of AA
4. Tofacitinib + Low-dose Oral Minoxidil:
  • Tofacitinib 5 mg twice daily
  • + Low-dose oral minoxidil: 2.5 mg once daily for women, 2.5 mg twice daily for men
  • Positive outcomes with low adverse effects
5. Baricitinib Combinations:

The treating physician may consider similar combinations with baricitinib, however further evidence is necessary to determine the best specific combination.

6. Simvastatin + Ezetimibe:
  • Simvastatin 40 mg + Ezetimibe 10 mg orally daily for 3 months
  • Alternative treatment for recalcitrant AA
  • Data still limited and controversial
Memory Tip for JAK Inhibitors: "BRTR" - Baricitinib (SFDA approved), Ritlecitinib (FDA approved 12+), Tofacitinib (5mg BID), Ruxolitinib (20mg BID, 75% response).

👶 Treatment in Special Situations

🧒 AA in Children

🥇 First-line Management:

In children with AA, topical corticosteroids are used as the first therapeutic option:

  • Preferably medium to high potency
  • Mometasone 1% to Clobetasol 0.05%
📋 Pediatric Treatment Algorithm (Click to expand)
🥇 First-line

Topical corticosteroids (medium to high potency)

🥈 Second-line

When contraindication or lack of response to topical corticosteroids:

  • Anthralin
  • DPCP
  • Minoxidil

Combination of two or all three is considered second-line management

🥉 Third-line

Systemic therapy is considered third-line in children below age 12 years

⚠️ Important Considerations for Children <12 years:

  • In the absence of data confirming that systemic therapy reduces risk of subsequent AT or AU development
  • There is NO consensus on systemic therapy use in children below 12 years
  • Decision can be taken by an experienced dermatologist on case-by-case basis

🧔 AA in the Beard Area

🎯 Treatments of Choice:

  • Topical corticosteroid therapy
  • Intralesional corticosteroid therapy

⚠️ Special Considerations:

  • Higher risk of atrophy associated with corticosteroid use in beard area
  • Triamcinolone 2.5mg/ml is the most suitable concentration for this area

💊 Additional Options:

  • Topical minoxidil
  • Anthralin
Note: There is NO consensus on usage of calcineurin inhibitors for AA in the beard.

👁️ AA in Eyelashes and Eyebrows

👁️ Eyelash Hypotrichosis Treatment (Click to expand)

🥇 Treatment of Choice:

Analogues of prostaglandin F2a:

  • Latanoprost in solution used once daily
  • Bimatoprost in solution used once daily

⏰ Treatment Duration:

Should be used for a minimum of 12 months to assess response.

🚨 Important Side Effects:

  • Eyelid hyperpigmentation
  • Darkening of the iris
🤨 Eyebrow AA Treatment (Click to expand)

💊 Treatment Options:

  • Topical corticosteroid of medium potency in cream
  • Intralesional triamcinolone (2.5 mg/mL)
  • Alternatively: 0.03% bimatoprost
  • Alternatively: Topical minoxidil

💊 Systemic Treatment Consideration:

Systemic treatment may be considered after failure of topical and intralesional modalities in case a patient reaches a ClinRo score of 2 or more.

📅 Follow Up After Treatment Initiation

🕐 Follow-up Schedule by Treatment:

DPCP Patients

Assessed weekly or biweekly

Intralesional Corticosteroids

Follow up every 4-6 weeks

Topical Treatment

Usually assessed every 2-3 months

💊 Systemic Treatment Follow-up (Click to expand)

💊 Systemic Corticosteroids:

Patients should be weaned over duration of at least 3 months.

💊 Steroid-sparing Agents:

  • Continue treatment for 3-6 months after complete remission
  • Tapering can be attempted during that period
  • Weaning helps decrease risk of relapse
  • Relapse can occur during weaning

🚨 Major Relapses Management:

Patients taking adequate dosage of systemic agents who develop major relapses (multiple new patches):

  • Treatment should be discontinued
  • Other treatment options considered
  • Relapses can be treated by:
    • Dose escalation
    • Intralesional triamcinolone acetonide
    • Short course of systemic corticosteroids
📊 Response Assessment Methods (Click to expand)

📋 Clinical Assessment Tools:

  • History taking
  • Assessing hair-shedding severity
  • Serial photography of affected areas
  • Measuring SALT score
  • Quality of life indexes
  • Dermoscopic photos of affected areas (recommended)

🎯 Successful Treatment Goal:

A SALT reduction of 75 (75% improvement in SALT) is an appropriate goal for successful treatment regimen.

🚨 Indicators of Active Disease:

  • Increased hair shedding
  • Positive pull test (especially at edge of patches)
  • Exclamation mark hairs
  • Black dots on dermoscopy

🎯 Final Board Review

AA is a condition that impacts patients from all ages and can present in many ways. It is commonly encountered by general dermatologists and other healthcare providers. AA is a psychologically distressing disease and successful management is key to improve quality of life. An adequate clinical assessment and evaluation, and understanding of treatment options available, are essential for healthcare providers. An individualized plan should always be considered for patients with AA to achieve the best outcomes possible.

  • Childhood onset (<6 years)
