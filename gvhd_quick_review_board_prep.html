GVHD QUICK REVIEW

Graft‑versus‑Host Disease — High‑Yield Board Summary

One-page, exam-focused review for rapid memorization (Dermatology boards).

Timeframe: 4–6 wks (classic aGVHD)
Skin ➜ Most common organ
Core Concept Donor T cells → host tissues

Immune-mediated attack after allogeneic HSCT. Skin, liver, gut are classic targets.

Classification
  • Acute GVHD (aGVHD): morbilliform exanthem, usually 4–6 wks post‑HSCT; skin, liver, gut.
  • Chronic GVHD (cGVHD): polymorphous, autoimmune‑like — lichenoid, sclerotic, morpheaform, etc.
  • Overlap/Delayed: NIH criteria focus on manifestations more than strict day 100 rule.
Key Epidemiology
  • ~35,000 allogeneic HSCTs/yr worldwide.
  • GVHD in ~40% (HLA-identical) and 60–70% (HLA-mismatched).
  • Post‑transplant cyclophosphamide reduces incidence.
Risk factors
  • HLA mismatch, unrelated donor
  • Female donor → male recipient (multiparous)
  • Older recipient
  • Stem cell source: PB > BM > cord blood
  • T cell‑replete grafts, myeloablative conditioning
Pathophysiology (aGVHD) Conditioning damage → host APC activation → donor T cell activation → cytotoxic effect (TNF, IFN‑γ, IL‑1). Targets: skin, liver, gut.
Pathophysiology (cGVHD) Autoimmune‑like: T/B cell dysregulation, BAFF, autoantibodies, profibrotic pathways (PDGFR, fibroblast activation) → sclerosis/fibrosis.
Clinical — aGVHD (skin)
  • Morbilliform exanthem; acral predilection (dorsal hands/feet, palms/soles, ears).
  • Stages I–IV by BSA: IV = generalized erythroderma + bullae (TEN‑like) → very high mortality.
  • May be folliculocentric or hemorrhagic if thrombocytopenic.
Clinical — cGVHD (skin & mucosa)
  • Lichenoid: violaceous papules/plaques (LP‑like).
  • Lichen sclerosus‑like: shiny gray‑white atrophic plaques.
  • Morphea/sclerotic: indurated, hyperpigmented plaques; deep fibrosis → contractures.
  • Eosinophilic fasciitis‑like: deep fascial involvement, limited ROM, subtle rippling.
  • Mucosa: oral LP‑like changes, genital scarring/stenosis, ocular sicca.
Investigations
  • Skin biopsy: aGVHD → basal vacuolar change, keratinocyte apoptosis; cGVHD → lichenoid or sclerotic changes.
  • MRI for suspected fascial involvement; labs nonspecific (autoantibodies, eosinophilia in some cGVHD).
Management — aGVHD
  • Grade I–II limited: high‑potency topical steroids.
  • Extensive/III–IV or visceral: systemic corticosteroids (prednisone 0.5–1 mg/kg BID or IV methylpred).
  • Steroid‑refractory: Ruxolitinib (JAK inhibitor) — preferred 2nd‑line (FDA‑approved).
Management — cGVHD
  • Local disease: topical steroids, calcineurin inhibitors, phototherapy.
  • Systemic disease: systemic corticosteroids (prednisone up to 1 mg/kg/day).
  • Steroid‑refractory (FDA‑approved options): Ibrutinib, Ruxolitinib, Belumosudil (ROCK2 inhibitor).
  • Other: ECP, sirolimus, imatinib, rituximab; PT/OT for sclerotic disease.
Prognosis & complications
  • aGVHD Stage IV: high mortality; overall severity predicts outcomes.
  • cGVHD → chronic morbidity, infections (from immunosuppression), skin cancer, functional loss from sclerosis.
High‑yield differential
  • Engraftment syndrome: occurs earlier (~10–12 days), fever, pulmonary edema, rapid steroid response; vs aGVHD 4–6 wks.
  • Drug/viral exanthem can mimic aGVHD—biopsy and timing help.
Quick mnemonics
Acral start → think aGVHD
Lichen/sclerosis = cGVHD
10–12 days = Engraftment syndrome
Ruxolitinib = aGVHD second‑line
Exam tricks & confusion tacticsBoard tips
  • Always check timing: 10–12 days → engraftment syndrome; 4–6 wks → classic aGVHD.
  • If a question describes TEN‑like widespread sloughing after HSCT, select severe aGVHD (Stage IV) over primary TEN unless medication history strongly suggests TEN.
  • For lichenoid or sclerotic chronic changes months after transplant, choose cGVHD (not simple drug eruption).
  • Remember FDA approvals: ruxolitinib for steroid‑refractory aGVHD; ibrutinib, ruxolitinib, belumosudil for cGVHD—these can be answer choices on treatment questions.
  • Bone marrow donor characteristics (female multiparous donor → higher risk) are a common distractor.

  • Inspect dorsal hands/feet, palms, soles, and ears for early aGVHD.

  • Collaborate with transplant team — systemic disease needs multi‑disciplinary management.
