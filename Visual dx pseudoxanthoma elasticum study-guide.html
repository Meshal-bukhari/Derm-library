Definition & Pathophysiology

Pseudoxanthoma elasticum (PXE), also known as Grönblad-Strandberg syndrome, is a rare autosomal recessive disorder resulting in abnormal calcification and fragmentation of elastic connective tissue in the skin, eyes, and blood vessels.

PXE Mnemonic: "EYE SEE PXE"

E - Elastin fiber abnormality

Y - Yellow papules and plaques

E - Eye findings (angioid streaks)

S - Systemic vascular calcification

E - ECTRIM (Extracellular matrix) affected

E - Elastic tissue in dermis, eyes, vessels

P - PPi deficiency (pyrophosphate)

X - Xanthoma-like appearance

E - ENPP1 enzyme implications

Genetic & Molecular Basis
  • Gene defect: Loss of function mutation in both alleles of the gene that encodes for the cellular transport protein ATP-binding cassette C6 (ABCC6)
  • Function: ABCC6 is an efflux pump that transports ATP from hepatocytes
  • Pathophysiology: ATP is converted extracellularly to AMP and inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi)
  • Key problem: Insufficient PPi leads to mineralization of elastic fibers (PPi normally inhibits this)
Epidemiology
  • Prevalence: 1 in 25,000 to 1 in 100,000
  • Gender: Slight female preponderance
  • Age of onset: As early as first or second decade of life

Clinical Manifestations

👨‍⚕️
Cutaneous

Yellow papules/plaques on flexural areas, "plucked chicken skin" appearance

👁️
Ocular

Angioid streaks, peau d'orange retinal changes, neovascularization

❤️
Cardiovascular

Vessel calcification, premature atherosclerosis, GI bleeding

  • Primary lesions: Yellowish (xanthoma-like) papules on flexural areas
  • First site: Lateral neck (most common initial area of involvement)
  • Other sites: Antecubital fossa, popliteal fossa, axillae, wrists, groin
  • Mucosal involvement: Lower lip (look for yellow papules)
  • Progression: Papules coalesce into plaques resembling "plucked chicken skin"
  • Advanced findings:
    • Perforating PXE (dermal calcium extruding through epidermis)
    • Prominent creases on chin (by age 30 in majority of patients)
    • Redundant and sagging skin folds (secondary cutis laxa) in axillae and groin
  • Angioid streaks (AS):
    • Most characteristic ocular finding in PXE
    • Breaks in Bruch's membrane due to calcification of elastic lamina
    • Appear as reddish-brown, jagged, irregular lines with serrated edges
    • Radiate from optic disc
    • Almost all patients exhibit AS by age 30
  • Peau d'orange:
    • Mottled retinal changes
    • Represents subconfluent calcification of Bruch's membrane
    • More commonly seen than angioid streaks
  • Complications:
    • Choroidal neovascularization at fracture sites
    • Hemorrhage and subsequent scarring
    • Gradual vision loss
  • Pathophysiology: Calcification of elastic media and intima of vessels
  • Primary target: Medium-sized vessels of extremities
  • Symptoms:
    • Intermittent claudication
    • Angina pectoris
    • Decreased or lost peripheral pulses
    • Renovascular hypertension
  • Cardiac findings:
    • Increased rates of mitral valve prolapse
    • Higher risk of myocardial infarction
    • Higher risk of stroke
  • Gastrointestinal complications:
    • Involvement of vessels in gastric and intestinal mucosa
    • Increased incidence of GI bleeding

🔑 Clinical Pearls

  • Horizontal and oblique mental creases on the chin are highly suggestive of PXE, especially when accompanied by secondary cutis laxa changes
  • Calcification in visceral areas and blood vessels on routine radiographs should prompt suspicion
  • A PXE-like syndrome may be precipitated by long-term D-penicillamine use (treatment for cystinuria or Wilson disease)
  • Unlike true PXE, penicillamine-induced PXE does not exhibit calcification histologically

Diagnostic Criteria

2014 Diagnostic Criteria for PXE

Major Criteria

  1. Cutaneous:
    • Characteristic papules and plaques on flexural areas
    • Histopathology showing elastic fiber fragmentation and calcification in the reticular dermis
  2. Ophthalmic:
    • Angioid streaks larger than one disc diameter in length
    • Peau d'orange changes
  3. Genetic:
    • Molecular genetic testing for the presence of biallelic ABCC6 pathogenic variants

Minor Criteria

  1. PXE in a first-degree relative
  2. Genetic test with pathologic variant in one allele of ABCC6
  3. PXE changes in the histopathology of nonlesional skin

Diagnosis Classification

  • Definitive diagnosis: Both major cutaneous criteria plus either one ophthalmic criterion OR positive genetic testing
  • Probable diagnosis: An affected first-degree relative plus either both major cutaneous criteria OR one major ophthalmic criterion

🔍 High-Yield for Exam

  • First manifestation is usually in the skin, but ocular findings are most specific
  • Recall the classic triad: Skin involvement + Eye findings + Cardiovascular complications

What to Look For

Key Examination Findings
  • Skin:
    • Yellow papules/plaques on lateral neck (earliest sign)
    • Flexural areas: axillae, subclavicular areas, antecubital fossae, popliteal fossae
    • Inguinal and periumbilical areas often involved
    • Yellow papules on mucosal surface of lower lip
    • Possible rectal and vaginal involvement
    • Advanced: "plucked chicken skin" appearance of plaques
    • Horizontal/oblique mental creases (chin)
    • Redundant skin folds (axillae, groin)
  • Eyes (Fundoscopic examination):
    • Mottled "peau d'orange" appearance
    • Angioid streaks - reddish-brown, jagged lines radiating from optic disc
    • Possible neovascularization, hemorrhages, and scarring

Most Common Initial Presentation

Yellow papules and plaques on the lateral neck in adolescence or early adulthood.

These may be subtle and easily missed if not specifically looked for.

  • Paget disease of bone
  • Sickle cell anemia
  • Thalassemia
  • Lead poisoning
  • Age-related degeneration

🔑 Differential Diagnosis Pearl

Remember the mnemonic "PEPSI" for causes of angioid streaks:

  • P - PXE
  • E - Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
  • P - Paget's disease of bone
  • S - Sickle cell anemia
  • I - Idiopathic/age-related

Best Tests

Diagnostic Approach
  • Gold standard: Skin biopsy with special stains
    • Verhoeff-van Gieson stain (for elastin)
    • von Kossa stain (for calcium)
  • Key finding: Clumped and frayed calcified elastic fibers in the reticular dermis ("pink or blue squiggles")
  • Alternative for high suspicion without skin findings: Biopsy of pre-existing scar may be diagnostic
  • Note: Penicillamine-induced PXE does not exhibit calcification

🔍 High-Yield Workup & Monitoring

  • Blood pressure assessment and monitoring
  • Complete hematology panel
  • Fasting lipid profile
  • Fecal occult blood test
  • Comprehensive liver and kidney profiles
  • Funduscopic examination with retinal photos
  • Regular use of Amsler grid for self-monitoring of visual changes
  • Doppler ankle-brachial index (ABI) measurements in patients with claudication
  • ECG for patients with heart murmur

Management Pearls

Multidisciplinary Approach
  • Ophthalmologic: Routine ophthalmologist follow-up, Amsler grid for self-monitoring
  • Activity restrictions: Avoid contact sports (risk of head trauma) and heavy lifting/straining
  • Lifestyle modifications:
    • Smoking cessation (tobacco may exacerbate disease)
    • Limit alcohol intake
    • Balanced diet including antioxidants
    • Regular exercise
    • Weight control
  • Genetic counseling: For patients and family members
  • Subspecialty referrals:
    • Gastroenterologist for GI bleeding
    • Cardiologist for cardiovascular manifestations
  • Medication precautions: Avoid long-term anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents when possible
  • Prophylactic antibiotics: Required prior to dental/surgical procedures for patients with mitral valve insufficiency

🔑 Management Pearls

PXE International is a patient support and advocacy group for patients and their families

Gene testing may be warranted in individual cases and for the first affected child in a family

Always remember the triad of specialists: Dermatologist + Ophthalmologist + Cardiologist

Therapy

Current Treatment Options
Manifestation Treatment
Skin findings No specific treatment; surgical intervention for lax skin if needed (usually not necessary for children)
Ocular disease
  • Intravitreal VEGF antagonist injections
  • Laser coagulation
  • Photodynamic therapy with verteporfin
  • Macular translocation (for choroidal neovascularization)
  • Vitamin/mineral supplementation (A, C, E, zinc, selenium, copper) - anecdotal evidence
Cardiovascular disease
  • Dietary optimization
  • Correction of hyperlipidemia and hypertension
  • Medications for claudication:
    • Pentoxifylline (400 mg PO before each meal)
    • Cilostazol
    • Low-dose aspirin
    • Clopidogrel
Emerging Therapies

Target: Systemically address low levels of the mineralization inhibitor PPi

Direct approaches:

  • Direct PPi supplementation
  • PPi analog etidronate

Indirect approaches:

  • Increase ENPP1 activity (enzyme that converts extracellular ATP to AMP and PPi)
  • Decrease TNAP activity (enzyme that degrades PPi)
  • Lansoprazole (proton pump inhibitor) has shown effectiveness

Clinical trials:

  • PROPHECI trial - phase II clinical trial for PXE with oral pyrophosphate
  • Etidronate trials for prevention of ectopic mineralization

Self-Test Questions

1. What is the genetic defect in PXE?
Loss of function mutation in both alleles of the gene that encodes for the cellular transport protein ATP-binding cassette C6 (ABCC6).
2. What is the characteristic ocular finding in PXE?
Angioid streaks (breaks in Bruch's membrane due to calcification of the elastic lamina).
3. By what age will almost all PXE patients exhibit angioid streaks?
By age 30 years.
4. What is the most common initial area of cutaneous involvement in PXE?
Lateral neck.
5. What stains are used to assist in the diagnosis of PXE in a skin biopsy?
Verhoeff-van Gieson stain (for elastin) and von Kossa stain (for calcium).
6. What is a unique feature of penicillamine-induced PXE compared to true PXE?
Penicillamine-induced PXE does not exhibit calcification.
7. What criteria are required for a definitive diagnosis of PXE?
Both major cutaneous criteria plus either one ophthalmic criterion OR positive genetic testing.
8. What molecule is deficient in PXE that normally inhibits mineralization?
Inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi).
9. What facial finding is highly suggestive of PXE, especially when accompanied by secondary cutis laxa changes?
Horizontal and oblique mental creases on the chin.
10. Name three other conditions besides PXE that can cause angioid streaks.
Paget disease of bone, sickle cell anemia, thalassemia, lead poisoning, and age-related degeneration.

Summary

Key Points to Remember
  • PXE is an autosomal recessive disorder with ABCC6 mutations causing abnormal calcification of elastic tissue
  • Classic triad: skin (yellow papules on flexural areas), eyes (angioid streaks), and blood vessels (calcification)
  • Lateral neck is the most common initial site of involvement
  • Angioid streaks are the most characteristic ocular finding
  • Complications include vision loss, GI bleeding, and accelerated cardiovascular disease
  • Diagnosis requires both cutaneous criteria plus either ocular findings or genetic confirmation
  • Skin biopsy with elastic tissue stains is the gold standard for diagnosis
  • Management requires a multidisciplinary approach
  • Emerging therapies target the PPi pathway to prevent mineralization
