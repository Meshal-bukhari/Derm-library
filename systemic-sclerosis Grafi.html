Systemic Sclerosis and Related Disorders

A comprehensive board review guide for dermatology residents

High-Yield Board Facts

  • Mortality: Most common cause is pulmonary disease
  • Organ System: Most commonly involved is GI
  • Bad Prognosis: Male, African, older age, internal organ involvement, trunk fibrosis, elevated ESR, joint disease, digital ulcers, Anti-Scl70
  • Antibodies: Anti-topoisomerase I (diffuse SSc, ILD), Anti-centromere (limited SSc, PAH), Anti-RNA polymerase III (renal disease, R-R-R)
  • Treatment: Digital ulcers (IV iloprost for acute, Bosentan for prevention)

1. Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) Overview

Systemic sclerosis is an uncommon autoimmune connective tissue disease of unknown etiology characterized by vascular dysfunction, immune activation, and fibrosis affecting multiple organ systems.

Epidemiology

  • Female predominance (F:M = 3-4:1)
  • Peak onset: 35-50 years of age
  • African race associated with earlier disease onset and worse prognosis
  • Overall 10-year survival: ~70%
  • Increased risk of malignancy (breast, hematologic, liver, bladder, lung)

Clinical Classification Memory Aid

Limited SSc: "CREST stays LIMITED to extremities"

  • Calcinosis
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Esophageal dysmotility
  • Sclerodactyly
  • Telangiectasia

Diffuse SSc: "PORTRAIT includes the TRUNK"

  • Pulmonary fibrosis
  • Organ involvement (early)
  • Raynaud's (rapid onset)
  • Trunk involvement
  • Renal crisis risk
  • Anti-Scl70 antibodies
  • ILD common
  • Tendon friction rubs

The modified Rodnan skin score, durometer, or imaging can be used to grade skin fibrosis in systemic sclerosis. The Rodnan scoring assesses 17 anatomical sites with scores from 0-3 (max score of 51).

Pathogenesis

  • Affects vessels of all sizes (small capillaries to large pulmonary arteries)
  • Involves impaired angiogenesis, vascular leakage, vasospasm, capillary dysfunction, and devascularization
  • Elevated VEGF and its receptors (VEGFR)
  • Manifestations:
    • Raynaud phenomenon and digital ulcers (reversible vasospasm)
    • Scleroderma renal crisis and PAH (large vessel dysregulation)

Key antibody associations:

  • Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl70): Associated with diffuse SSc, ILD, and cardiac involvement
  • Anti-centromere: Associated with limited SSc, PAH, digital ulcers
  • Anti-RNA polymerase III: Associated with renal disease, rapidly progressive pulmonary fibrosis (R-R-R), and malignancy-associated SSc
  • Th2-predominant profile with increased production of profibrotic cytokines (IL4 and IL13)
  • Elevated Th17, IL-17, and IFNs
  • CXCL4 correlates with pulmonary disease (marker for lung involvement)
  • Increased TGF-β leads to collagen production
  • Increased production of collagen III and collagen I

Clinical Features

The progression of skin changes follows phases:

  1. Early phase: Pitting edema of digits and/or Raynaud's phenomenon
  2. Indurated phase: Fibrosis develops
  3. Late atrophic phase: Gradual skin thinning

Common skin findings include:

  • Digital pits (characteristic)
  • Fingers with flexure contractures and ulcerations
  • Facial involvement: beaked nose, microstomia, more youthful appearance
  • Sclerodactyly: thickening of skin of fingers/toes distal to MCP but proximal to PIP
  • Dyspigmentation and leukoderma in areas of fibrosis
  • "Salt and pepper" sign: pigment retained in skin overlying superficial veins or perifollicular skin
  • Telangiectasia: facial, palmar, and labial ("matted or squared-off")
  • Nailfold changes (present in >90%): capillary loss ("drop out") alternating with dilated loops
  • Calcinosis cutis in distal joints

Ventral Pterygium (Pterygium inversum unguis) is a characteristic nail finding in systemic sclerosis.

Capillaroscopy is the most specific sign for distinguishing between primary and secondary Raynaud phenomenon. SSc is the leading cause of secondary RP.

Raynaud's crisis can lead to rapid tissue ischemia and cutaneous ulceration, requiring admission and IV iloprost treatment.

The most commonly involved organ system is the GI tract, associated with morbidity but not mortality.

Pulmonary disease is the most common cause of mortality in systemic sclerosis.

Gastrointestinal System

  • Dysphagia, aspiration, and esophagitis
  • Gastroparesis and incontinence
  • Gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE, "watermelon stomach")

Pulmonary System

  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) - more common in diffuse SSc
  • Pulmonary artery hypertension (PAH) - more common in limited SSc

Cardiac System

  • Increased risk of atherosclerosis, MI, and stroke
  • Myocardial fibrosis leading to restrictive cardiomyopathy, CHF, and arrhythmias

Renal System

Scleroderma renal crisis: rapid increase in creatinine, never seen in limited SSc, occurs in 20% of diffuse SSc. ACE inhibitors decrease the risk.

  • Hypertension
  • Generalized edema

Musculoskeletal System

  • Arthralgias/arthritis
  • Palpable tendon friction rubs

Treatment

The most important goal of treatment is to control internal organ involvement.

  • Renal disease: ACE inhibitors
  • ILD: Cyclophosphamide, MMF, N-acetylcysteine, HSCT, lung transplantation, nintedanib(FDA approved for ILD), tocilizumab(FDA approved for ILD)
  • PAH: Endothelin receptor antagonists(Bosentan), phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors, prostanoids
  • GI disease: PPIs, ondansetron
  • Cardiac disease: Antihypertensives, MMF, cyclophosphamide, and ACE inhibitors

Skin disease treatment:

  • Raynaud phenomenon: Vasodilators (CCB first line) and trigger avoidance
  • Digital ulcers: IV iloprost for acute treatment, Bosentan for prevention
  • Fibrosis: UVA1(340-400)/PUVA, ECP, immunosuppressives (MTX, MMF, Rituximab), HSCT
  • Calcinosis cutis: Bisphosphonates, Calcium channel blockers (e.g., diltiazem), Sodium thiosulfate, surgical removal

Prognosis

  • 10-year survival for diffuse SSc: 70%
  • 10-year survival for limited SSc: 80%

Bad prognostic factors in systemic sclerosis:

  • Male gender
  • African race
  • Older age at diagnosis
  • Internal organ involvement
  • Fibrosis involving the trunk
  • Elevated ESR
  • Joint disease
  • Cutaneous digital ulcers
  • Anti-Scl70 (most important prognostic factor)

2. Primary vs Secondary Raynaud Phenomenon

Raynaud's phenomenon is present in 90% of diffuse SSc and 99% of limited SSc patients.

Feature Primary Raynaud Secondary Raynaud
Sex F:M = 20:1 F:M = 4:1
Age at onset Puberty >25 years
Frequency of attacks Usually <5 per day 5-10+ per day
Precipitants Cold, emotional stress Cold
Ischemic injury Absent Present
Abnormal capillaroscopy Absent >95%
Antinuclear antibodies Absent/low titer 90-95%
Anti-centromere antibody Absent 30-40%
Anti-topoisomerase I antibody Absent 20-30%
In vivo platelet activation Absent >75%

Treatment of Raynaud Phenomenon

Emergency Treatment:

  • IV prostaglandin if severe acute attack

First-line:

  • Cold avoidance
  • Hand and feet warming packets
  • Discontinue all tobacco products

Second-line:

  • Calcium channel blockers (e.g. nifedipine SR 30 mg daily–BID, amlodipine 2.5–10 mg daily)

Third-line (especially when accompanied by ulceration):

  • Phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors (e.g. sildenafil, tadalafil)
  • α-Adrenergic blockers (e.g. prazosin)
  • Angiotensin II receptor blockers (e.g. losartan)
  • Endothelin receptor antagonists (e.g. bosentan)

Fourth-line (if patients can't tolerate previous medications due to hypotension):

  • Serotonin reuptake inhibitors (e.g. fluoxetine)

Capillaroscopy is the most specific sign for distinguishing between primary and secondary Raynaud phenomenon. The presence of giant loops or vascular dropout strongly suggests secondary Raynaud's.

3. Eosinophilic Fasciitis (Shulman Syndrome)

Eosinophilic fasciitis is a fibrosing disorder characterized by the rapid onset of woody induration of the extremities associated with eosinophilia. It predominantly affects females and Caucasians.

Clinical Features

  • History of strenuous physical activity preceding cutaneous findings in 30% of patients
  • Initially presents with edema and pain of the involved extremity
  • Rapidly progresses to fibrosis with a pseudo-cellulitis appearance
  • Typically has symmetric distribution
  • Spares the hands, feet, and face
  • "Groove sign" or "Dry river bed" sign: linear depressions where veins appear to be sunken within the indurated skin
  • Lacks Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Laboratory findings: elevated ESR, hypergammaglobulinemia, and peripheral eosinophilia
  • Normal ANA and complement levels
  • May be associated with pancytopenia, anemia, thrombocytopenia, myeloproliferative disorders, and monoclonal gammopathy

Eosinophilic fasciitis + unexplained anemia should prompt bone marrow biopsy to rule out hematological malignancy.

Pathology

  • Thickening of the deep fascia (10-15 times the normal thickness)
  • Lymphocytic infiltrate ± eosinophils and mast cells
  • Diagnosis made through MRI or fascial biopsy

Rapid treatment is crucial for eosinophilic fasciitis:

  • First-line: Oral corticosteroids 1-2 mg/kg
  • Second-line: Hydroxychloroquine, cyclosporine, dapsone, methotrexate, PUVA, or infliximab

4. Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis

Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis is a fibrotic disorder strongly associated with exposure to gadolinium-based contrast agents in patients with kidney disease.

Clinical Features

  • Onset typically 2-4 weeks after gadolinium exposure, but can be several years
  • Thick, indurated plaques distributed symmetrically on the extremities and trunk
  • Erythematous to hyperpigmented appearance
  • Irregular advancing edge with an "ameboid" appearance
  • Can lead to contractures
  • Scleral plaques: white-yellow plaques with dilated capillaries
  • Extracutaneous involvement: fibrosis affecting the heart, lungs, and skeletal muscle

The most common location for nephrogenic systemic fibrosis is the extremities.

Lung involvement is the main cause of death in nephrogenic systemic fibrosis.

Pathology

  • Deep biopsy is required
  • Haphazard arrangement of thickened collagen bundles
  • Increased dermal fibroblast-like cells (CD34 and procollagen I positive)

Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis is generally refractory to treatment:

  • Renal transplant can improve the fibrosis
  • Anecdotal treatments include:
    • Imatinib
    • Rapamycin
    • UVA1
    • ECP
    • PDT
    • Plasmapheresis
    • IVIg
    • Discontinuation of erythropoietin

5. Stiff Skin Syndrome

Stiff skin syndrome is a genetic disorder that presents congenitally or develops during childhood, characterized by rock-hard induration of the skin and subcutaneous tissue.

Stiff skin syndrome has autosomal dominant inheritance and is caused by Fibrillin-1 (FBN1) mutation, which is also involved in Marfan syndrome.

Clinical Features

  • Rock-hard induration of the skin and subcutaneous tissue
  • Most pronounced on the buttocks and thighs
  • Sparing of the inguinal folds, hands, and feet
  • Mild hypertrichosis over affected areas may be seen
  • Characteristic posture of hip and knee flexion and prominent lumbar lordosis while standing due to contractures
  • Slowly progressive course
  • No extracutaneous involvement

Pathology

  • Thickened, hyalinized fascia without inflammation

Treatment for stiff skin syndrome is limited:

  • Physical therapy for contractures
  • No definitive cure available

6. Sclerodermoid Syndromes Induced by Exogenous Substances

  • Results from ingestion of aniline-degraded and reprocessed rapeseed oil
  • Affects the skin, CNS, lungs, and salivary glands
  • Starts as a morbilliform eruption with fever/headache, progresses into LP-like or morphea-like eruption
  • Eosinophilia in 50% of cases

Eosinophilia-Myalgia Syndrome results from exposure to L-tryptophan.

  • Characterizerd by severe myalgias, fever, dyspnea, edema, and a macular exanthem
  • Peripheral eosinophilia is a hallmark feature
  • Diffuse, deep sclerodermoid induration of the extremities (with acral sparing)

Bleomycin is a commonly tested drug that can induce sclerodermoid changes, including pulmonary fibrosis and Raynaud's phenomenon.

  • Several drugs, especially bleomycin and taxanes (docetaxel, paclitaxel)
  • Mimic the clinical features of SSc
  • Generally lack autoantibodies
  • Caused by vinyl chloride, organic solvents, pesticides, or epoxy resins
  • Clinically indistinguishable from idiopathic SSc
  • Removal of the trigger may lead to resolution
  • Incidence of SSc is 20-fold higher in individuals with occupational exposure to silica
  • Indistinguishable from the idiopathic form of SSc

7. Differential Diagnosis of Sclerodermoid Conditions

Scleredema

  • Type 1 (55%): Following URI (especially Strep pharyngitis), affects middle-aged women, involving face, neck, trunk, proximal upper extremities; usually resolves after 6 months-2 years
  • Type 2 (25%): Same clinical presentation as type 1, but with associated IgGκ (kappa) monoclonal gammopathy
  • Type 3 (20%, "scleredema diabeticorum"): Associated with diabetes, typically in obese middle-aged men, involving posterior neck and upper back
  • Histologically shows wide spaces between collagen bundles filled with mucin

Scleromyxedema

Scleromyxedema is associated with IgGλ (lambda) paraproteinemia and can be treated with IVIG.

  • Progressive condition with widespread, symmetric, linearly distributed waxy papules
  • Typically involves face/neck, dorsal hands, extensor forearms, elbows, and upper trunk
  • Diffuse infiltration can mimic scleroderma and result in leonine facies
  • Extracutaneous involvement is common (GI, MSK, neurological, cardiac)

Pretibial Myxedema

Waxy indurated nodules or plaques with a peau d'orange appearance on the shins, most commonly in Grave's disease.

Chronic GVHD

  • Morpheaform plaques on trunk, which may become generalized
  • Usually shows some degree of interface dermatitis histologically
  • Can develop lesions resembling eosinophilic fasciitis

POEMS Syndrome

POEMS syndrome includes polyneuropathy, organomegaly, endocrinopathy, M-protein, and skin changes. Glomeruloid hemangiomas are strongly associated.

Carcinoma en cuirasse

  • Sclerodermoid induration of chest wall due to infiltration by cancerous cells
  • Most commonly associated with breast cancer

Diabetic Cheiroarthropathy

  • Symmetric, painless loss of joint mobility and stiffness of small joints of the hand
  • Scleroderma-like skin thickening of the dorsal aspects of hands and feet
  • Positive "prayer sign"
  • Affects 30-50% of chronic DM2 patients
  • Correlates with microvascular disease

Porphyria Cutanea Tarda

Porphyria cutanea tarda can present with morpheaform plaques in sun-exposed areas, along with hyperpigmentation and hypertrichosis.

Self-Assessment Questions

1. Which of the following is the most common cause of mortality in systemic sclerosis?

Correct Answer: C. Pulmonary disease

Pulmonary disease is the most common cause of mortality in systemic sclerosis. This includes both interstitial lung disease (more common in diffuse SSc) and pulmonary arterial hypertension (more common in limited SSc).

2. Which antibody is most commonly associated with diffuse systemic sclerosis and interstitial lung disease?

Correct Answer: B. Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl70) antibody

Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl70) antibody is associated with diffuse SSc, ILD, and cardiac involvement. Anti-centromere antibody is associated with limited SSc and PAH.

3. Which of the following is the most specific sign for distinguishing between primary and secondary Raynaud phenomenon?

Correct Answer: C. Abnormal capillaroscopy

Abnormal capillaroscopy (giant loops or capillary dropout) is the most specific sign for distinguishing between primary and secondary Raynaud phenomenon. It is present in >95% of secondary Raynaud's cases but absent in primary Raynaud's.

4. Which exogenous substance is associated with Eosinophilia-Myalgia Syndrome?

Correct Answer: B. L-tryptophan

Eosinophilia-Myalgia Syndrome is associated with exposure to L-tryptophan. It presents with severe myalgias, fever, peripheral eosinophilia, and sclerodermoid skin changes.

5. What is the characteristic finding in Stiff Skin Syndrome?

Correct Answer: A. Fibrillin-1 (FBN1) mutation

Stiff skin syndrome is an autosomal dominant condition caused by Fibrillin-1 (FBN1) mutation, which is also involved in Marfan syndrome. The condition presents with rock-hard induration of the skin, most pronounced on the buttocks and thighs.

Name the key differences between limited and diffuse systemic sclerosis.

Limited SSc:

  • Skin involvement limited to fingers, hands, face
  • Long preceding history of Raynaud's
  • Later onset of internal organ involvement (10-15 years)
  • PAH more common than ILD
  • Anti-centromere antibody (40%)
  • Better prognosis (10-year survival 80%)

Diffuse SSc:

  • Generalized skin involvement including trunk
  • Sudden onset of Raynaud's
  • Early onset of internal organ involvement (within 5 years)
  • ILD more common than PAH
  • Anti-topoisomerase I (20%) and Anti-RNA polymerase III (10%)
  • Worse prognosis (10-year survival 70%)
What are the primary treatments for digital ulcers in systemic sclerosis?

Acute treatment: IV iloprost

Prevention: Bosentan

Other approaches:

  • Avoid excessive debridement
  • Moist, non-adherent dressings
  • Treatments for Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Low-dose aspirin or clopidogrel
  • In refractory cases, nerve blocks and sympathectomies
