HISTIOCYTOSES CLASSIFICATION - Board Review Summary
GROUP 1: "CLASSIC" NON-LCHs - CUTANEOUS ("C" GROUP)
A. PRIMARILY IN CHILDREN & OFTEN SELF-RESOLVING
JUVENILE
XANTHOGRANULOMA
(JXG)
Clinical: Most common histiocytosis of childhood
Pink-to-yellow papulonodules on head and neck
Key Associations: NF1, JMML, rare ocular involvement (critical)
Prognosis: Self-resolving
BENIGN CEPHALIC
HISTIOCYTOSIS
(BCH)
Clinical: Red-brown macules/papules strictly on face, neck, ears
Infants only
Prognosis: Self-limiting, rare
GENERALIZED
ERUPTIVE
HISTIOCYTOMA
(GEH)
Clinical: Recurrent crops of hundreds of small red-brown papules
Axial distribution (trunk, proximal extremities)
Age: Children or adults
B. OFTEN IN ADULTS & FREQUENTLY PERSISTENT/PROGRESSIVE
XANTHOMA
DISSEMINATUM
(XD)
CLASSIC TRIAD: 1) Flexural xanthomas 2) Mucous membrane lesions 3) Diabetes insipidus
Key: Normolipemic patient
NECROBIOTIC
XANTHOGRANULOMA
(NXG)
Clinical: Yellow, ulcerating periorbital plaques
Association: ~80% have IgG monoclonal gammopathy
(Multiple Myeloma)
MULTICENTRIC
RETICULOHISTIO-
CYTOSIS (MRH)
Clinical: "Coral bead" periungual papules
Severe destructive arthritis → arthritis mutilans
Association: Internal malignancy ~30%
PROGRESSIVE
NODULAR
HISTIOCYTOSIS
(PNH)
Clinical: Elderly patients, relentlessly progressive
Two lesion types: small superficial papules + deep large nodules/tumors
Spares flexures
PROGRESSIVE
MUCINOUS
HISTIOCYTOSIS
Clinical: Skin-colored to red-brown papules/nodules
Primarily women
Histology: Abundant dermal mucin
Genetics: Often autosomal dominant
GROUP 2: ROSAI-DORFMAN DISEASE ("R" GROUP)
ROSAI-DORFMAN
DISEASE
(RDD)
Clinical: Massive, painless, bilateral cervical lymphadenopathy
Nonspecific skin lesions
Pathognomonic: Emperipolesis
(Intact cells within histiocyte cytoplasm)
GROUP 3: HEMOPHAGOCYTIC SYNDROMES ("H" GROUP)
HEMOPHAGOCYTIC
LYMPHOHISTIO-
CYTOSIS
(HLH)
Clinical: Life-threatening cytokine storm
High fevers, splenomegaly, cytopenias
Nonspecific skin: morbilliform rash, purpura, erythroderma
Types: Primary (genetic, infants) or Secondary (acquired, any age)
Secondary causes: infections, malignancy, autoimmune
GROUP 4: GENETICALLY LANGERHANS-RELATED (MAPK PATHWAY MUTATIONS)
INDETERMINATE
CELL
HISTIOCYTOSIS
(ICH)
Clinical: Red-brown papules and nodules
"In-Between" Histiocytosis:
CD1a positive (like LCH)
CD207/Langerin negative (like non-LCH)
ERDHEIM-CHESTER
DISEASE
(ECD)
Clinical: Severe multisystem disease of adults
Pathognomonic: Symmetric osteosclerosis of long bones
Retroperitoneal fibrosis ("hairy kidney")
Periaortic fibrosis ("coated aorta")
GROUP 5: MALIGNANT HISTIOCYTOSES ("M" GROUP)
HISTIOCYTIC
SARCOMAS
Clinical: Very rare, aggressive, anaplastic tumors
Diagnosis: Exclusion diagnosis
Requires ≥2 histiocyte markers (CD68, CD163, CD4, lysozyme)
GROUP 6: MISCELLANEOUS ENTITIES
ALK+
HISTIOCYTOSIS
Clinical: Young children, often systemic
Very large histiocytes
Marker: ALK-positive (anaplastic lymphoma kinase)
PAPULAR
XANTHOMA
Clinical: Generalized discrete yellow papules
Spares flexural sites
SEA-BLUE
HISTIOCYTE
SYNDROME
Histology: Histiocytes with azure blue granules
Clinical: Rare systemic disease, skin involvement rare
Inherited or acquired
🔑 HIGH-YIELD BOARD FACTS
Most Common Childhood: JXG
Periorbital Yellow Plaques: NXG + IgG gammopathy
Coral Bead Papules: MRH + arthritis mutilans
XD Triad: Flexural xanthomas + mucous membranes + DI
Emperipolesis: Rosai-Dorfman Disease
Life-Threatening: HLH (cytokine storm)
