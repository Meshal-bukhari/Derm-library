🔬 Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)

📌 Quick Overview

SSSS is caused by S. aureus exfoliative toxins (ETA/ETB) that cleave desmoglein 1 (Dsg1) in the superficial epidermis, leading to widespread blistering. Most common in infants/children, but can affect adults with renal failure.

Etiology & Pathophysiology +
  • Organism: Staphylococcus aureus, specifically phage group II (types 55 and 71)
  • Toxins: Exfoliatoxins A (ETA - chromosomally encoded) and B (ETB - plasmid encoded)
  • Mechanism: Toxins act as proteases that specifically cleave desmoglein 1 (Dsg1)
  • Pathogenesis: Primary infection (e.g., nasopharynx, conjunctiva) produces toxins that disseminate hematogenously. This leads to widespread cleavage of Dsg1 in the superficial epidermis and acantholysis at the subcorneal or intragranular level.
  • Distinction: In SSSS, toxins act systemically (hematogenous spread), unlike bullous impetigo where toxins act locally
Epidemiology +
  • Primary Age Group: Most commonly affects infants and young children (< 5 years old)
    • Attributed to lack of neutralizing antibodies and immature renal clearance
    • Low mortality in children (<5%)
  • Adults: Less common, primarily affects those with:
    • Chronic renal failure (CRF)
    • Immunosuppression
    • Much higher mortality in adults (>50%)
  • Gender: May be slightly more common in males (M:F ratio 2-4:1 reported in adults)
  • Primary Infection Sites:
    • Children: Nasopharynx or conjunctivae most common
    • Adults: Pneumonia and bacteremia are more common primary sources
Clinical Features +
🔥 Febrile prodrome
🤕 Skin tenderness
😮 Periorificial involvement
💧 Flaccid bullae
👆 Positive Nikolsky sign
❌ NO mucosal involvement

Mucous Membranes are NOT involved! This is a critical diagnostic feature differentiating SSSS from TEN and other conditions.

Eruption Onset & Progression:

  • Begins on the face with characteristic periorificial radial fissuring/crusting around mouth and eyes
  • Also affects intertriginous zones (axillae, groin, neck)
  • Generalizes within 48 hours
  • Skin appears wrinkled
  • Develops flaccid bullae that easily rupture
  • Nikolsky sign is positive, often elicitable even on seemingly uninvolved skin

Desquamation:

  • Flexural areas often exfoliate first, leaving moist, erythematous skin with thin, varnish-like crusting
  • Desquamation continues for up to 1 week
  • Healing occurs without scarring
Diagnosis +

Clinical Diagnosis: Primarily based on characteristic clinical presentation:

  • Age (infants/children or adults with renal failure)
  • Prodrome, skin tenderness
  • Characteristic rash morphology/distribution
  • Positive Nikolsky sign
  • Lack of mucosal involvement

💡 Diagnostic Pearl

Cultures from intact bullae are typically negative in SSSS because it is toxin-mediated with bacteria at a distant site. This is in contrast to bullous impetigo, where bullae cultures are positive.

Cultures:

  • Bullae cultures: Typically negative (toxin-mediated, bacteria are at a distant site)
  • Blood cultures: Almost always negative in children but often positive in adults
  • Cultures from primary site of infection if identifiable (e.g., nasopharynx, conjunctivae)

Histology:

  • Shows acantholysis (split) high in the epidermis, within the stratum granulosum or subcorneal layer
  • Resembles Pemphigus foliaceus histologically
  • Crucially, lacks significant inflammatory cells or bacteria within the blister cavity (unlike bullous impetigo)
  • Dermis typically lacks significant inflammatory infiltrate

Biopsy: Can confirm the level of cleavage if diagnosis is uncertain

Differential Diagnosis +

Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN): Key differential

  • TEN is usually drug-induced, affects adults
  • TEN involves mucous membranes (SSSS does not)
  • TEN shows full-thickness epidermal necrosis with dermal-epidermal separation
  • Nikolsky sign may be harder to elicit/patchy in TEN
  • TEN has higher mortality generally

Bullous Impetigo:

  • Caused by the same toxins but acting locally
  • Presents with localized flaccid bullae, often with collarettes of scale after rupture
  • Minimal surrounding erythema
  • Culture of bullae fluid is positive for S. aureus
  • Histology shows neutrophils in the blister

Other differentials:

  • Severe sunburn
  • Other toxin-mediated erythemas (e.g., scarlet fever, TSS)
  • Kawasaki disease
  • Viral exanthems
  • Severe drug reactions other than TEN
Treatment +

Supportive Care:

  • Essential, similar to burn care
  • Fluid and electrolyte management
  • Temperature regulation
  • Gentle skin handling

Antibiotics: Target S. aureus

  • Mild disease: Oral β-lactamase resistant penicillin (e.g., dicloxacillin) or first-generation cephalosporin (e.g., cephalexin)
  • Severe disease: Hospitalization with IV antibiotics (e.g., nafcillin, oxacillin). Consider Vancomycin if MRSA is suspected or prevalent
  • Clindamycin: Often added as it may help reduce bacterial toxin production (protein synthesis inhibition)

⚠️ Important

Corticosteroids are generally contraindicated in SSSS.

Prognosis & Complications +

Prognosis:

  • Generally excellent in children (<5% mortality) with prompt treatment
  • Significantly worse in adults (>50% mortality), especially with renal failure

Complications:

  • Secondary infection
  • Sepsis
  • Fluid and electrolyte imbalance
  • Hypothermia

Healing: Typically occurs without scarring

🔬 Streptococcal Toxic Shock Syndrome (Strep-TSS)

📌 Quick Overview

Strep-TSS is caused by Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes) producing superantigen toxins (SPEs, SMEZ) that trigger a massive cytokine release. It primarily affects young, previously healthy adults, with a high mortality rate (30-60%).

Etiology & Pathophysiology +
  • Organism: Group A β-hemolytic Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes), particularly M types 1 and 3
  • Toxins: Various toxins acting as superantigens:
    • Streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins (SPEs) A, B, and C
    • Streptococcal mitogenic exotoxin Z (SMEZ)
    • Streptolysin O
  • Mechanism: Superantigens bypass normal antigen processing. They bind directly to the Vβ region of the T-cell receptor (TCR) and MHC class II molecules on antigen-presenting cells (APCs)
  • Pathogenesis: This leads to nonspecific activation of a large number of T-cells, resulting in a massive release of pro-inflammatory cytokines ("cytokine storm"), including TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, involving TLR2 and TLR4 signaling. This systemic inflammation causes capillary leak, tissue damage, hypotension, and multi-organ failure
Epidemiology +
  • Typical Patient: Often affects young (20-50 years), previously healthy adults
  • Primary Source: Frequently associated with a florid skin or soft-tissue infection:
    • Cellulitis
    • Necrotizing fasciitis
    • Infected surgical site
    • Puerperal sepsis
    • Varicella superinfection
    • Minor trauma like lacerations, bruises, bug bites
  • Contrast to Staph-TSS: In Strep-TSS, the primary infection site is usually obvious, whereas in Staph-TSS it may be occult
Clinical Features +
⚡ Abrupt onset
😣 Severe pain
🔥 High fever
📉 Hypotension (100%)
🫁 Multi-organ failure

Severe localized pain, often out of proportion to visible findings, is a hallmark early sign of Strep-TSS/Necrotizing Fasciitis.

Onset: Often abrupt

Pain: Severe localized pain, often in an extremity, is a hallmark and may be out of proportion to visible skin findings initially. This pain may precede systemic symptoms.

Local Skin/Soft Tissue: May show signs of underlying infection such as redness, swelling, or frank necrotizing fasciitis.

Systemic Symptoms: Rapid progression (within 24-48 hours) to systemic toxicity:

  • High fever
  • Hypotension (present in 100% of cases by definition)
  • Multi-organ involvement (renal impairment, coagulopathy, liver impairment, ARDS are common criteria)

Rash: A generalized erythematous macular rash (scarlatiniform) is less common than in Staph-TSS. Desquamation may follow 1-2 weeks later if present. Vesicles and bullae are uncommon (5%).

Diagnosis +

Case Definition: Requires a combination of:

  1. Isolation of Group A Strep (GAS):
    • From a normally sterile site (e.g., blood, CSF, tissue biopsy) = Definite case
    • From a non-sterile site (e.g., throat, skin lesion) = Probable case (if other criteria met and no other etiology found)
  2. Hypotension: Systolic BP <90 mmHg (adults) or <5th percentile (children)
  3. Multi-organ Involvement: ≥2 of the following:
    • Renal impairment
    • Coagulopathy (platelets <100k or DIC)
    • Liver impairment
    • ARDS
    • Generalized erythematous rash +/- desquamation
    • Soft tissue necrosis (e.g., necrotizing fasciitis, myositis, gangrene)

💡 Diagnostic Pearl

Blood cultures are positive in >50% of Strep-TSS cases - a key difference from Staph-TSS, where blood cultures are usually negative.

Cultures: Blood cultures and culture of the primary infection site are crucial.

Imaging: May be needed to assess the extent of soft tissue infection (e.g., MRI for necrotizing fasciitis).

Labs: Show evidence of multi-organ dysfunction (elevated creatinine, liver enzymes, low platelets, DIC parameters), marked leukocytosis often.

Differential Diagnosis +

Staphylococcal Toxic Shock Syndrome (Staph-TSS): Key differential

  • Staph-TSS often has an occult infection site
  • Rash is very common in Staph-TSS
  • Blood cultures usually negative in Staph-TSS
  • Lower mortality in Staph-TSS (<3-20%)

Other differentials:

  • Sepsis from other organisms
  • Necrotizing fasciitis (without TSS criteria initially)
  • Severe cellulitis/erysipelas
  • Kawasaki disease (in children)
  • Viral exanthems with shock (e.g., dengue)
  • Rickettsial diseases
Treatment +

Aggressive Supportive Care:

  • ICU admission
  • Fluid resuscitation for hypotension
  • Vasopressors
  • Management of organ failure

⚠️ Critical

Surgical Debridement is crucial and often urgent if necrotizing fasciitis or other deep soft tissue infection is present. May require fasciotomy or amputation.

Antibiotics:

  • Penicillin G (IV): Drug of choice for GAS
  • Clindamycin (IV): Essential adjunct. It suppresses bacterial toxin production (inhibits protein synthesis) and has activity even against stationary-phase bacteria
  • Broad-spectrum coverage may be needed initially if diagnosis is uncertain or polymicrobial infection is suspected

Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG): May be considered as adjunctive therapy to neutralize circulating toxins, although definitive evidence varies.

Prognosis & Complications +

Prognosis: Severe condition with high mortality (30-60%), even with aggressive treatment. This is significantly higher than Staph-TSS.

Complications:

  • Multi-organ failure
  • DIC
  • Limb loss (due to necrosis or amputation)
  • Long-term sequelae from organ damage

Other Key Exam Traps +
  • Pain in Strep-TSS: Recognizing severe pain disproportionate to early skin findings as a potential harbinger of Strep-TSS/Necrotizing Fasciitis.
  • Nikolsky Sign in SSSS: Can be positive even in clinically uninvolved skin due to circulating toxin.
  • Role of Clindamycin: Used in both SSSS and Strep-TSS for its toxin-suppressing effects (protein synthesis inhibition), in addition to its antibacterial activity.
  • Case Definitions: Knowing the specific criteria (esp. for Strep-TSS involving organism isolation + hypotension + multi-organ signs) is crucial for scenario-based questions.
