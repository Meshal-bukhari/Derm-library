Introduction to Systemic Sclerosis

Terminology & Synonyms
  • Systemic sclerosis (SSc) - Preferred name
  • Scleroderma - Traditional name
  • Progressive systemic sclerosis - Less preferred term

Definition

Systemic sclerosis is an uncommon autoimmune connective tissue disease of unknown etiology that affects the skin, blood vessels, and internal organs. It is characterized by symmetric hardening of the skin of the fingers, hands, and face that may generalize, in association with Raynaud phenomenon and internal organ involvement.

Epidemiology

  • Incidence: ~20 cases per million annually
  • Prevalence: ~275 cases per million
  • Gender ratio: Women affected 3-4× more than men
  • Age of onset: Typically 35-50 years
  • Race: Black individuals have earlier onset and higher likelihood of diffuse disease
  • Familial risk: 1.5% have affected first-degree relatives (10-15× higher risk than general population)

Prognosis

  • Overall 10-year survival: ~70%
  • Poor prognostic factors:
    • Male sex
    • Older age at diagnosis
    • African-American race
    • Internal organ involvement at diagnosis (especially pulmonary)
    • Cutaneous sclerosis affecting the trunk
    • Elevated ESR
🔔 Key Clinical Features of SSc
  • Symmetric hardening of the skin (fingers, hands, face → may generalize)
  • Raynaud phenomenon (common)
  • Digital ulcers may develop
  • Internal organ involvement (lungs, GI tract, heart, kidneys)
  • Pulmonary disease is the leading cause of death

The name "systemic sclerosis" is intended to convey the systemic nature of the disease. While the skin is the most visible target, internal organ involvement determines prognosis. Pulmonary disease has replaced renal crisis as the leading cause of death in SSc patients.

Classification of Systemic Sclerosis

Major Clinical Subtypes

Limited Cutaneous SSc
Diffuse Cutaneous SSc
CREST Syndrome
SSc sine scleroderma

Limited Cutaneous SSc (lcSSc)

  • Cutaneous sclerosis limited to fingers, hands and face
  • Long preceding history of Raynaud phenomenon
  • Slower development of skin changes
  • Later onset of internal organ involvement (after 10-15 years)
  • Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) more common than interstitial lung disease
  • More favorable long-term prognosis
  • Often associated with anti-centromere antibodies

Diffuse Cutaneous SSc (dcSSc)

  • Cutaneous sclerosis extends proximally to forearms, arms, face, trunk and lower extremities
  • Sudden onset of Raynaud phenomenon
  • Rapidly progressive, more widespread cutaneous sclerosis (usually peaks within 12-18 months)
  • Early internal organ involvement (within 5 years of disease onset)
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) more common than PAH
  • Higher risk of renal disease
  • Often associated with anti-topoisomerase I (Scl-70) or anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies
  • Worse prognosis

CREST Syndrome

CREST is an acronym describing a subset of patients with lcSSc:

  • Calcinosis cutis
  • Raynaud phenomenon
  • Esophageal involvement
  • Sclerodactyly
  • Telangiectasias

Not every patient has all five features, so the term lcSSc is generally favored over CREST syndrome.

Systemic Sclerosis sine scleroderma (ssSSc)

  • Characteristic internal organ involvement
  • Raynaud phenomenon present
  • Abnormal nail-fold capillaries
  • Positive serologies
  • No cutaneous sclerosis (key distinguishing feature)
  • Behaves clinically similar to lcSSc

💡 Remembering Limited vs Diffuse SSc

Limited: "CRUTE" or "CUTE" distribution - Cutaneous involvement limited to Clavicle, Radius, Ulna, Tibia, Extremities

Diffuse: "PAT" prognosis - Poor prognosis, Accelerated onset, Trunk involvement

SSc Disease Course Timeline

Early Phase (0-1 years)

Raynaud phenomenon, puffy fingers, arthralgia. In dcSSc, rapid skin thickening begins.

Established Disease (1-3 years)

In dcSSc: Peak skin thickness reached (12-18 months), early organ involvement, pain and pruritus prominent. In lcSSc: Slower progression.

Late Phase (3-5 years)

In dcSSc: Skin thickness may begin to improve, organ involvement progresses. In lcSSc: Stable skin, minimal organ involvement.

Very Late Phase (5-15+ years)

In dcSSc: Skin atrophy, fixed organ damage. In lcSSc: Development of PAH and other organ involvement (10-15 years).

Pathogenesis of Systemic Sclerosis

The pathogenesis of SSc is unknown but involves three major pathophysiologic processes:

Vascular Dysregulation

  • Early endothelial cell injury
  • Perivascular edema
  • ↑ Adhesion molecules
  • ↑ VEGF and receptors
  • Altered responsiveness to vasoconstrictive/dilatory factors
  • Structural abnormalities → luminal occlusion → hypoxia

Immune Dysregulation

  • SSc-specific autoantibodies
  • T-cell oligoclonal expansion in lesional skin
  • Th2 predominant profile
  • ↑ Profibrotic cytokines (IL-4, IL-13)
  • B-cell dysfunction
  • ↑ CXCL4 levels (correlate with progression)
  • Innate immune activation (plasmacytoid DCs)

Extracellular Matrix Dysregulation

  • Excessive deposition of collagens
  • ↑ Proteoglycans, fibronectin, fibrillins
  • ↑ Adhesion molecules (β1-integrins)
  • ↑ TGF-β and CTGF
  • Fibroblast/myofibroblast activation
  • Altered microenvironment
  • Primarily ↑ synthesis (not ↓ degradation)
Pathogenesis Highlights for Exam
  • Vascular events occur first - endothelial damage precedes fibrosis
  • Key cytokines - TGF-β, CTGF, and IL-4/IL-13 play crucial roles in fibrosis
  • CXCL4 - elevated in circulation, correlates with disease progression
  • Fibroblast dysfunction - may be inherent defect, autocrine loop, or hypersensitivity to growth factors
  • Clinical correlation - understand how the pathogenic triad explains Raynaud phenomenon, digital ulcers, and fibrosis

All three pathogenic processes (vascular injury, immune activation, and fibrosis) are interconnected. For example, endothelial injury leads to hypoxia, which induces profibrotic cytokines that activate fibroblasts, culminating in tissue fibrosis.

Clinical Features of Systemic Sclerosis

Cutaneous Features

Phases of Skin Involvement
  1. Edematous phase
    • Early disease marked by pitting edema of digits
    • Puffy fingers/hands
    • Often the initial presentation
  2. Indurated phase
    • Hardening of the skin
    • Development of taut, shiny appearance
    • Loss of skin creases/wrinkles
    • Limitation of movement
  3. Late atrophic phase
    • Gradual thinning of the skin
    • Fixed contractures
    • Fingertip pitted scars may be prominent
Salt and Pepper Sign

Leukoderma with sparing of the perifollicular skin, creating a characteristic "salt and pepper" appearance. This typically involves the upper trunk and central face and may overlie uninvolved or sclerotic skin.

Mat Telangiectasias

Dilated blood vessels with a squared-off or matted appearance. More common in lcSSc (90%) than dcSSc (60%). Most often involve the face, lips, and palms. Distinctive pattern from other causes of telangiectasias.

Digital Pitting & Ulcers

Pitted scars on the digital pulp are characteristic. Ulcers can develop over fingertips (ischemic) or interphalangeal joints (traumatic). May lead to osteomyelitis or even amputation.

Calcinosis Cutis

Calcium deposits in the skin, most commonly on extremities near joints. Can lead to pain, ulceration, and secondary infection. More common in lcSSc (40%) than dcSSc (20%).

Facial Changes

Microstomia (small mouth), beaked nose, and a more youthful appearance due to tight skin with loss of wrinkles. Perioral radial furrowing ("purse string" appearance).

Nail-fold Capillary Changes

Present in >90% of SSc patients. Characterized by capillary "drop out" alternating with dilated loops. Changes have prognostic value for fingertip ulceration and lung disease progression.

Raynaud Phenomenon

Definition

Raynaud phenomenon is characterized by episodic vasospasm of the digital arteries resulting in white (pallor), blue (cyanosis), and red (hyperemia) discoloration of the fingers secondary to cold exposure or emotional stress.

Primary Raynaud

  • No underlying disease
  • F:M ratio 20:1
  • Onset during puberty
  • Usually <5 attacks per day
  • Triggered by cold AND emotional stress
  • No ischemic injury
  • Normal capillaroscopy
  • Negative autoantibodies

Secondary Raynaud (SSc)

  • Associated with underlying disease
  • F:M ratio 4:1
  • Age of onset >25 years
  • 5-10+ attacks per day
  • Primarily triggered by cold
  • Ischemic injury present
  • Abnormal capillaroscopy (>95%)
  • Positive autoantibodies (90-95%)
Clinical Pearl: Evaluating Raynaud Phenomenon

When a patient presents with Raynaud phenomenon, key features suggesting secondary Raynaud (associated with SSc):

  • Onset after age 25
  • Asymmetric or severe attacks
  • Digital pits or ulcers
  • Abnormal nail-fold capillaries
  • Presence of sclerodactyly
  • Positive SSc-specific antibodies

Pulmonary Involvement

Pulmonary disease has replaced renal crisis as the leading cause of death in SSc. Two major forms:

Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)

  • More common in dcSSc (70%)
  • Associated with anti-topoisomerase I antibodies
  • Fibrotic thickening of visceral pleura
  • Progressive dyspnea, cough
  • Decreased DLCO on PFTs
  • Ground-glass opacities and reticular pattern on HRCT

Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH)

  • More common in lcSSc (30%)
  • Associated with anti-centromere antibodies
  • Usually develops later (10-15 years after onset)
  • Progressive dyspnea, fatigue
  • Elevated right ventricular pressure
  • Requires right heart catheterization for diagnosis
Important for Exam

Pleuroparenchymal fibroelastosis (PPFE) is a rare subtype of ILD in SSc that portends a poor prognosis. It affects the upper lobes and requires close monitoring with serial DLCO and HRCT.

Gastrointestinal Involvement

  • Esophageal dysfunction (80-90%)
    • Most common GI manifestation
    • Decreased lower esophageal sphincter pressure
    • Diminished/absent peristalsis
    • Symptoms: dysphagia, heartburn, regurgitation
    • Complications: Barrett's esophagus, strictures
  • Small bowel involvement (40-60%)
    • Hypomotility, bacterial overgrowth
    • Symptoms: bloating, diarrhea, malabsorption
    • May lead to malnutrition and weight loss
  • Other GI issues
    • Gastric antral vascular ectasia ("watermelon stomach")
    • Colonic hypomotility
    • Anorectal dysfunction

Renal Involvement

Scleroderma renal crisis (SRC) is the most serious renal manifestation:

  • More common in dcSSc (20%) than lcSSc (1%)
  • Associated with anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies
  • Usually occurs within first 4-5 years of disease
  • Characterized by:
    • Accelerated hypertension
    • Rapidly progressive renal failure
    • Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
    • Proteinuria, hematuria
  • Risk factors:
    • Rapidly progressive skin disease
    • Use of high-dose corticosteroids
    • New cardiac events
Treatment Pearl

ACE inhibitors have dramatically improved outcomes in scleroderma renal crisis but should be instituted early for treatment. However, they are not helpful for prevention.

Cardiac Involvement

  • Primary myocardial involvement
    • Patchy fibrosis of myocardium
    • Microvascular disease
    • Diastolic dysfunction
  • Secondary cardiac involvement
    • Right heart failure secondary to PAH
    • Cor pulmonale
  • Conduction disturbances
  • Pericardial effusion

Musculoskeletal Involvement

  • Arthralgia/arthritis (80-98%)
    • Usually symmetric, polyarticular
    • Small joints of hands most commonly affected
  • Tendon friction rubs (more common in dcSSc)
  • Joint contractures due to skin tightening
  • Myopathy/myositis
    • May be inflammatory or fibrotic
    • Proximal muscle weakness (60-80%)
  • Acro-osteolysis (bone resorption of distal phalanges)
Monitoring Priorities

The majority of SSc patients have internal organ involvement which is the major cause of morbidity and mortality. Initial screening tests for all patients should include:

  • Pulmonary function tests (DLCO, spirometry)
  • High-resolution CT (if abnormal PFTs)
  • Transthoracic echocardiography
  • Serum N-Tpro-BNP level
  • Close monitoring of blood pressure
  • BUN/Creatinine and urinalysis

Autoantibodies in SSc

Anti-centromere Antibodies
  • Present in 30-70% of lcSSc, 5-10% of dcSSc
  • Associated with:
    • Limited cutaneous disease
    • Digital gangrene
    • Pulmonary arterial hypertension
    • Better long-term survival
  • Discrete speckled (centromere) ANA pattern
Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl-70)
  • Present in 30-70% of dcSSc, 5-15% of lcSSc
  • Associated with:
    • Diffuse cutaneous disease
    • Interstitial lung disease
    • Pulmonary fibrosis
    • Poorer prognosis
  • Speckled ANA pattern
Anti-RNA polymerase III
  • Present in 5-25% of dcSSc, 1-5% of lcSSc
  • Associated with:
    • Rapidly progressive diffuse skin disease
    • Renal crisis
    • Increased risk of malignancy (esp. breast cancer)
  • Nucleolar or speckled ANA pattern
💡 Antibody Mnemonic: "CAT"

Centromere: Limited disease, PAH

Anti-topo (Scl-70): Diffuse disease, ILD

Transcriptase (RNA polymerase III): Renal crisis, Rapid progression

Pathology

Histologically, areas of cutaneous induration in SSc are characterized by:

  • Compact or hyalinized collagen
  • Excessive collagen deposition
  • Atrophic eccrine and pilosebaceous glands
  • "Miniaturization" of adnexal structures
  • Adnexal structures appear "trapped" by collagen
  • Loss of subcutaneous fat
  • Sparse lymphocytic infiltrate
  • Direct immunofluorescence usually negative
  • Endothelial damage visible only at ultrastructural level

In its end stage, areas of sclerosis may be indistinguishable histologically from other diseases characterized by collagen deposition, such as morphea, which tends to have a more robust inflammatory infiltrate than SSc in its earlier stages.

Treatment of Systemic Sclerosis

SSc is a challenging disease to treat. Therapeutic interventions focus primarily on internal organ involvement, but unfortunately most of these treatments have minimal effect on cutaneous sclerosis.

Raynaud Phenomenon & Digital Ulcers

First-line
Second-line
Third-line

First-line for Raynaud

  • Non-pharmacologic
    • Cold avoidance
    • Hand and feet warming packets
    • Discontinue all tobacco products
    • Biofeedback
  • For digital ulcers
    • Avoid excessive debridement
    • Moist, non-adherent dressings
    • Autolytic debridement via occlusive dressings

Second-line for Raynaud

  • Calcium channel blockers
    • Nifedipine SR: 30mg daily, increasing to BID after 2 weeks if tolerated
    • Amlodipine: 2.5mg daily, with increments of 2.5mg every 2 weeks as tolerated, up to 10mg daily
    • Caution: may exacerbate GERD and cause peripheral edema
  • For digital ulcers
    • Low-dose aspirin or clopidogrel
    • Pentoxifylline

Third-line for Raynaud (especially with ulceration)

  • Phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors
    • Sildenafil: initial dose as low as 5mg PO TID, but most patients tolerate and require 50mg PO BID
    • Tadalafil: initially 20mg PO every other day, increased to 40mg PO daily
  • Other options
    • α-Adrenergic blockers (e.g., prazosin)
    • Angiotensin II receptor blockers (e.g., losartan)
    • Endothelin receptor antagonists (e.g., bosentan) - approved in Europe for prevention of new digital ulcers

Fourth-line for Raynaud

  • Serotonin reuptake inhibitors (e.g., fluoxetine)
    • Option for patients who cannot tolerate vasodilators due to hypotension
  • Botulinum toxin type A
    • Injections into base of digits
    • Mixed results in clinical trials
  • For severe digital ulcers
    • IV prostanoids (e.g., epoprostenol, iloprost)
    • Surgical options: sympathectomy, debridement

Cardiac
  • ACE inhibitors
  • Standard heart failure therapy
  • Treatment of underlying PAH if present
Corticosteroid Warning

Concern exists that concomitant administration of systemic corticosteroids may increase the risk of developing renal crisis. If needed, low-dose corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone <10 mg daily) may be safer.

Newer/Emerging Therapies

  • Autologous HSCT: Three randomized controlled trials have demonstrated superiority of HSCT over cyclophosphamide, with greater improvement in lung and skin disease as well as quality-of-life. Consider for selected patients with rapidly progressive SSc.
  • Autotaxin inhibitors: Ziritaxestat showed improvement in cutaneous sclerosis based on mRSS in a phase II trial.
  • IL-4/IL-13 inhibitors: Romilkimab, a humanized antibody that neutralizes IL-4/IL-13, under investigation.
  • Melanocortin 1 receptor agonists: Dersimelagon being studied in preclinical models.

Distinguishing SSc from Other Sclerodermoid Conditions

Which of the following is NOT a typical feature that helps distinguish systemic sclerosis from generalized morphea?
  • Raynaud phenomenon
  • Abnormal nail-fold capillaries
  • Perifollicular sparing of pigment (salt and pepper sign)
  • SSc-specific autoantibodies
  • Internal organ involvement

Explanation: The "salt and pepper sign" (perifollicular sparing of pigment) can be seen in both systemic sclerosis and generalized morphea, so it's not a distinguishing feature. Raynaud phenomenon, abnormal nail-fold capillaries, SSc-specific autoantibodies (anti-centromere, anti-topoisomerase I, anti-RNA polymerase III), and internal organ involvement are all features that help distinguish systemic sclerosis from generalized morphea.

Key Distinguishing Features of SSc

  • Raynaud phenomenon (90-99%)
  • Abnormal nail-fold capillaries (>90%)
  • Specific autoantibody patterns
  • Significant internal organ involvement
  • Digital ulcers
  • Characteristic mat telangiectasias

Features Suggestive of Alternative Diagnoses

  • Asymmetric cutaneous involvement
  • Rapid onset following identifiable trigger (chemical, drug)
  • Monoclonal gammopathy (scleromyxedema)
  • Eosinophilia with "pseudo-cellulite" appearance (eosinophilic fasciitis)
  • Exposure to gadolinium with renal disease (NSF)
  • Induration limited to buttocks/thighs with sparing of inguinal folds (stiff skin syndrome)

Eosinophilic Fasciitis

Synonyms
  • Shulman syndrome
  • Diffuse fasciitis with eosinophilia

Clinical Features

  • History of strenuous physical activity in ~30% of cases
  • Initial manifestations: edema and pain of involved extremities
  • Progresses to sclerosis with dimpled or "pseudo-cellulite" appearance
  • Distribution is symmetric and usually spares hands, feet and face
  • "Groove sign" - linear depressions where veins appear sunken within indurated skin
  • Laboratory findings:
    • Elevated ESR
    • Hypergammaglobulinemia
    • Peripheral eosinophilia (may be absent if receiving corticosteroids)
    • ANA typically not elevated
    • Normal complement levels
  • May be associated with hematologic abnormalities:
    • Pancytopenia
    • Anemia
    • Thrombocytopenia
    • Myeloproliferative disorders
    • Monoclonal gammopathy

Pathology

  • Thickening of deep fascia (10-50 times normal width)
  • Patchy infiltrate of lymphocytes, plasma cells, and eosinophils
  • Dermal sclerosis in some cases

Treatment

  • Prompt treatment essential to preserve mobility and prevent contractures
  • Oral corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone 1-2 mg/kg daily)
  • Response typically noted within first few weeks
  • Prednisone tapered over 6-24 months
  • Concurrent immunosuppressive drugs often added:
    • Methotrexate
    • Mycophenolate mofetil
  • For inadequate response:
    • Pulse-dose corticosteroids
    • IVIg
    • Hydroxychloroquine
    • JAK inhibitors
    • UVA-based phototherapy
    • Cyclosporine

Stiff Skin Syndrome

Synonym

Congenital fascial dystrophy

Pathogenesis

  • Due to heterozygosity for a mutation in the gene encoding fibrillin-1 (FBN1)
  • Presence of giant "amianthoid-like" collagen fibrils in affected fascia
  • Disturbance in organization of collagen and glycosaminoglycans in the extracellular matrix
  • Segmental form may be associated with IL-17C mutation

Clinical Features

  • May be congenital or develop during early childhood
  • Familial occurrences described
  • "Rock hard" induration and thickening of skin and subcutaneous tissues
  • Most pronounced on buttocks and thighs
  • Characteristic sparing of the inguinal folds
  • Hands and feet not involved
  • Mild hypertrichosis often observed in affected areas
  • Restricted joint mobility leading to:
    • Hip and knee flexion
    • Prominent lumbar lordosis while standing
  • Patients may have short stature
  • Condition is stable or slowly progressive
  • Internal organs typically not involved

Pathology

  • Markedly thickened, hyalinized fascia
  • No associated inflammatory infiltrate
  • Thickened collagen bundles and mucin deposition may be present in overlying dermis

Treatment

  • Chronic course with no generally effective treatments
  • Physical therapy helps prevent progressive joint contractures
  • Segmental form with IL-17 mutation has been successfully treated with secukinumab

Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis

Synonym

Nephrogenic fibrosing dermopathy

Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

  • Incidence declining since identification of causative agent
  • Nearly all patients had history of exposure to gadolinium-based contrast agents
  • Vast majority had advanced chronic kidney disease or acute kidney injury at time of exposure
  • By 2010, FDA contraindicated three gadolinium-based contrast agents in such patients:
    • Gadopentetate dimeglumine (Magnevist®)
    • Gadodiamide (Omniscan™)
    • Gadoversetamide (OptiMARK™)

Clinical Features

  • Thick, indurated plaques distributed symmetrically on trunk and extremities
  • Often results in joint contractures
  • Plaques are erythematous to hyperpigmented
  • Irregular advancing edge with "ameboid" appearance
  • Yellow scleral plaques may be present
  • Fibrosis can develop within heart, lungs, and skeletal muscle

Pathology

  • Disease process extends along fibrous septa into subcutis (deep biopsy required)
  • Haphazard arrangement of thickened collagen bundles
  • Increased dermal fibroblast-like cells that stain positively for CD34 and procollagen I
  • Mucin deposition and osseous metaplasia may be seen
  • Energy dispersive spectroscopy can detect gadolinium particles within involved tissue

Treatment

  • Generally refractory to corticosteroids and other immunosuppressive drugs
  • Improvement in renal function (following renal transplantation) may improve NSF
  • Potential therapeutic options similar to those for other sclerodermoid conditions

Sclerodermoid Syndromes Induced by Exogenous Substances

Toxic Oil Syndrome

  • 1981 outbreak in Spain affecting >25,000 people (600 deaths)
  • Caused by ingestion of aniline-degraded and reprocessed rapeseed oil
  • Initial manifestations:
    • Morbilliform eruption
    • Flu-like symptoms
    • Peripheral eosinophilia (in 50%)
  • Late manifestations:
    • Affected CNS, lungs, and salivary glands
    • Skin involvement either resolved, evolved into lichen planus-like eruption, or progressed to morpheaform or sclerodermoid picture

Eosinophilia-Myalgia Syndrome

  • 1989 outbreak in US affecting >1,500 people (≥30 fatalities)
  • Caused by contaminated preparation of L-tryptophan
  • Acute phase:
    • Severe myalgias
    • Fever, dyspnea
    • Edema
    • Macular exanthem
    • Peripheral eosinophilia
  • Chronic phase (~50% of patients):
    • Diffuse, deep sclerodermoid induration of extremities (with acral sparing)
    • Progressive peripheral neuropathy
    • Myopathy

Chemical-Induced Sclerodermoid Syndromes

  • Organic chemicals associated with SSc-like picture:
    • Vinyl chloride
    • Organic solvents (trichloroethylene, trichloroethane, perchloroethylene)
    • Pesticides
    • Epoxy resins
  • Slowly progressive process (unlike toxic oil and eosinophilia-myalgia syndromes)
  • Begins with insidious acrosclerosis and Raynaud phenomenon
  • May involve acral osteolysis, hepatic toxicity, pulmonary involvement
  • Also associated with fatigue, weight loss, arthralgias, myalgias
  • Autoantibodies typically absent
  • Disease process ceases or reverses with removal of exposure

Silicosis

  • Incidence of SSc ~20-fold higher in individuals with occupational silica exposure
  • Common in miners and foundry workers
  • Mean duration of exposure: 14 years
  • Mean delay in onset: 24 years from first exposure
  • Once absorbed, silica cannot be removed from body
  • HLA and TNF allele associations suggest need for specific pre-existing immunophenotype
  • SSc associated with pulmonary silicosis is clinically indistinguishable from idiopathic form

Drug-Induced Sclerodermoid Syndromes

  • Bleomycin - acrosclerosis, Raynaud phenomenon, pulmonary fibrosis
  • Taxanes - edema followed by sclerosis of lower extremities, acrosclerosis
  • Other chemotherapeutic agents:
    • Cisplatin
    • Pemetrexed
    • Carboplatin
    • Topotecan
    • Gemcitabine (especially of the lower extremities)
  • Additional agents:
    • Ergot alkaloids
    • L-5-hydroxytryptophan +/- carbidopa
    • Appetite suppressants
    • Cocaine

High-Yield Board Exam Summary

🎯 Top 10 Exam Targets
  1. Classification: Distinguish lcSSc from dcSSc based on extent of skin involvement, timing of organ involvement, and autoantibody associations
  2. ACR/EULAR criteria: Know the 2013 classification criteria and scoring system (score ≥9 classified as definite SSc)
  3. Pathogenesis triad: Vascular injury → immune activation → fibrosis (know key mediators like TGF-β, CTGF)
  4. Antibodies: Anti-centromere (lcSSc, PAH), anti-topoisomerase I (dcSSc, ILD), anti-RNA polymerase III (dcSSc, renal crisis, malignancy)
  5. Raynaud phenomenon: Distinguish primary vs. secondary Raynaud and know treatment approach
  6. Organ involvement: Know that pulmonary disease (ILD/PAH) is the leading cause of death and how to screen for it
  7. Treatment: Nintedanib and MMF for ILD; ACE inhibitors for renal crisis; vasodilators for Raynaud
  8. Differential diagnosis: Know how to distinguish SSc from eosinophilic fasciitis, morphea, and other sclerodermoid conditions
  9. Related conditions: Recognize eosinophilic fasciitis, stiff skin syndrome, NSF and their key distinguishing features
  10. Prognosis factors: Male sex, older age, African-American race, internal organ involvement at diagnosis, truncal skin involvement

A 45-year-old woman presents with 6 months of progressive skin tightening affecting her fingers, hands, forearms, and face. She reports Raynaud phenomenon for the past year. Nail-fold capillaroscopy shows dilated loops with areas of capillary dropout. She has dyspnea on exertion, and pulmonary function tests show decreased DLCO. Which autoantibody is most likely to be positive in this patient?
  • Anti-centromere antibody
  • Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl-70) antibody
  • Anti-RNA polymerase III antibody
  • Anti-U1-RNP antibody
  • Anti-Jo-1 antibody

Explanation: This patient has diffuse cutaneous systemic sclerosis (dcSSc) with interstitial lung disease (ILD), evidenced by decreased DLCO. Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl-70) antibody is strongly associated with dcSSc and increased risk of ILD. Anti-centromere antibody is associated with limited cutaneous systemic sclerosis and PAH. Anti-RNA polymerase III is associated with dcSSc and renal crisis. Anti-U1-RNP is associated with mixed connective tissue disease. Anti-Jo-1 is associated with polymyositis/dermatomyositis with ILD.
