Overview of Facial Circulation

The face has an extensive vascular network that originates from two major arterial systems:

Internal Carotid Arterial Supply

Ophthalmic Artery

The internal carotid artery contributes to the vascular supply of the:

  • Eyelids
  • Upper nose and nasal dorsum
  • Forehead
  • Scalp

These areas are supplied via sub-branches of the ophthalmic branch.

🧠 Memory Aid: "OOSS"

The ophthalmic artery arises behind the eye and branches into the Orbital and Ocular groups. The ocular group supplies the eye and its muscles. The orbital group includes the Supravorbital, Supratrochlear, dorsal nasal, and anterior ethmoidal arteries.

Supraorbital Artery

The supraorbital artery:

  • Exits the orbit through the supraorbital foramen
  • Travels alongside the supraorbital nerve
  • Perforates the frontalis muscle
  • Ultimately courses in the subcutaneous tissue of the forehead and scalp

Supratrochlear Artery

The supratrochlear artery:

  • Exits the medial orbit
  • Courses medial to the supraorbital artery
  • Supplies the nasal root and low midline forehead

💎 Clinical Pearl: Forehead Flap

This artery serves as the axial blood supply for the midline forehead flap often used to repair nasal defects. The success of this flap depends upon isolation and preservation of the supratrochlear artery.

Anterior Ethmoidal Artery

The anterior ethmoidal artery:

  • Exits the nasal passage at the interface of the nasal bone and nasal cartilage
  • Provides arterial supply to the nasal dorsum

Dorsal Nasal Artery

The dorsal nasal artery:

  • Is a terminal branch of the ophthalmic artery
  • Supplies the dorsal nose
  • Crosses the midline over the nasal root
  • Anastomoses with the contralateral angular artery

⚠️ Risk Zone: Blindness with Fillers

Knowledge of the ophthalmic artery and its branches is of particular importance when planning cosmetic filler injection. Because of their proximal connections, the orbital group of vessels represents a potential site for embolus introduction into the central retinal artery that can lead to complications including blindness.

External Carotid Arterial Supply

While the internal carotid artery contributes to some areas of the scalp and upper face, the external carotid artery supplies most of the face.

Facial Artery

The facial artery:

  • Branches off from the external carotid artery
  • Courses deep to the mandible
  • Passes through or behind the submandibular salivary gland
  • Passes over the mandible onto the face
  • Can be palpated anterior to the masseter muscle
  • Follows a tortuous diagonal and superior course passing alongside the nose
  • Terminates at the medial canthus where it anastomoses with the dorsal nasal artery

🧠 Memory Aid: "PRAZ"

The facial artery is protected along its course by surrounding muscles: Platysma and Risorius near the mandible, followed by Zygomaticus muscles in the midface, and orbicularis oculi as it approaches the medial canthus.

Labial Arteries

The facial artery gives rise to:

  • Inferior labial artery: Courses horizontally below the lips
  • Superior labial artery: Courses horizontally above the lips

💎 Clinical Pearl: Aging Changes

These arteries are found deep to the orbicularis oris in the submucosal plane, but can course more superficially into the submucosa of the lip in elderly patients.

Angular Artery

After forming the superior labial branch, the facial artery becomes known as the angular artery.

  • Terminates near the medial canthal tendon
  • Anastomoses with the dorsal nasal branch of the ophthalmic artery

🎯 High-Yield Connection

This anastomosis joins the internal and external carotid arterial systems - a critical connection to understand for the exam.

Superficial Temporal Artery

The superficial temporal artery and its branches supply the lateral face, scalp, and forehead.

  • Arises in the superficial lobe of the parotid gland as the terminal branch of the external carotid artery
  • Courses superficially to the main facial nerve trunks
  • Gives off the transverse facial artery before exiting the parotid gland
  • Exits the parotid and enters the subcutaneous fat in the preauricular crease
  • Assumes an ascending vertical course over the zygomatic arch
  • Can be easily palpated just medial to the upper tragus of the ear
  • Forms the frontal (anterior) and parietal (posterior) arterial branches that originate just above the uppermost attached portion of the ear

💎 Clinical Pearl: Surgical Landmark

The superficial temporal artery runs alongside and superficial to the auriculotemporal nerve within, then above, the SMAS layer of the temple and lateral forehead. It is often visible in aged patients within the subdermal fat above the galea aponeurotica.

Transverse Facial Artery

The transverse facial artery:

  • Branches from the superficial temporal artery
  • Runs parallel to and 2cm below the zygomatic arch

Venous Drainage

Most of the arteries of the face run anterior to and parallel with their corresponding veins. The veins lack valves and therefore permit two-way flow of venous blood.

Facial Vein

  • Connects to the deep facial vein as it drains the cheek
  • Crosses over the submandibular gland (while its corresponding artery passes beneath)
  • Drains into the internal jugular vein

Deep Facial Vein

  • Parallels the internal maxillary artery
  • Anastomoses with the pterygoid venous plexus medial to the upper mandibular ramus

⚠️ Risk Zone: Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis

The facial vein can communicate with the cavernous sinus of the brain via the ophthalmic vein or the pterygoid plexus. The paranasal area and upper lip are the regions drained by this network.

This interface may permit skin or wound infections to gain access to the cavernous sinus of the brain from the draining facial or ophthalmic veins, with potentially devastating consequences.

Clinical Applications

Wound Healing and Flaps

Arterial blood supply to the face is delivered by a rich subdermal plexus that is fed by larger perforating arteries. Wound healing and flap success depend on maximal blood supply to the area.

💎 Flap Types - Know the Difference

  • Axial flaps (e.g., paramedian forehead flap): Incorporate a named subcutaneous artery (like the supratrochlear artery) into their design
  • Random pattern flaps: Maintained by the subdermal arterial plexus and do not rely on a single feeder artery

The anastomotic vascular network permits facial arteries to be clamped or tied off during surgery without compromising tissue viability. Nearby ipsilateral or contralateral anastomotic arterial branches can often compensate for any loss in local blood supply.

Key Takeaways for the Saudi Board Exam

Essential Points to Remember

  1. The face has dual blood supply from both internal and external carotid systems
  2. Key danger zones for filler injections related to the ophthalmic artery branches
  3. The supratrochlear artery is crucial for forehead flap viability
  4. Critical anastomosis between internal and external carotid systems occurs at the medial canthus
  5. Venous connections between the face and cavernous sinus create risk for intracranial infection spread
  6. Rich anastomotic networks allow for reliable flap designs and good wound healing
