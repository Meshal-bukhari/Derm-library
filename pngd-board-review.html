Palisaded Neutrophilic Granulomatous Dermatitis (PNGD)

A rare inflammatory cutaneous reaction with important systemic associations

⚡ Quick High-Yield Facts
Board Exam Focus:
  • PNGD is extremely rare and almost never seen independently from an underlying systemic illness
  • Most common associations: SLE and Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • Female predominance (3:1 ratio)
  • Classic presentation: Symmetrically distributed, skin-colored to erythematous papules on elbows/extensor surfaces
  • May precede malignancy diagnosis by up to 5 years

🧠 Memory Aid: "PNGD" Mnemonic

  • Papules on extensor surfaces (symmetrical)
  • Neutrophilic infiltrates (early histology)
  • Granulomas (later histology)
  • Disease associations (SLE, RA, malignancy)
What are the common underlying diseases associated with PNGD?
  • Most common: SLE and Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • Less common: Inflammatory bowel disease, systemic vasculitides
  • Rare: Myelodysplastic syndrome and lymphoproliferative conditions
ℹ️ Definition & Etiology

Palisaded neutrophilic granulomatous dermatitis (PNGD) is an extremely rare inflammatory cutaneous reaction seen in the setting of connective tissue disease or rheumatoid arthritis. It is almost never seen independently from an underlying systemic illness.

Pathophysiologic Progression
1
Early Phase: Neutrophilic inflammation and leukocytoclastic vasculitis
2
Intermediate Phase: Collagen degeneration
3
Late Phase: Granuloma formation and associated fibrosis
Remember: PNGD is likely a reactive process to underlying systemic disease. The exact etiology remains unknown.
👥 Demographics & Disease Associations HIGH YIELD
  • Affects patients of all ages
  • Reports in children are rare
  • Female predominance (3:1 ratio)
    • Likely reflects demographics of underlying autoimmune diseases

Most Common

  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)

Less Common

  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Systemic vasculitides

Rare Associations

  • Myelodysplastic syndrome
  • Lymphoproliferative conditions
Board Alert: PNGD may precede malignancy diagnosis by up to 5 years, or may be present at the time of diagnosis. Consider occult malignancy when PNGD is identified without other clear associations.
🔍 Clinical Presentation HIGH YIELD
Diagnostic Pearl: Consider PNGD in a patient with connective tissue disease or rheumatoid arthritis who presents with a skin-colored to erythematous papular eruption distributed symmetrically on the elbows or extensor surfaces.

Clinical Features

  • Morphology: Skin-colored to erythematous papules that are umbilicated or crusted
  • Distribution: Symmetrical, primarily on extremities
    • Upper extremities (50% of cases)
    • Lower extremities (33% of cases)
    • Trunk, head, and neck (<20% of cases)
  • Symptoms: Usually asymptomatic; some lesions may be tender

Morphological Variants

  • Urticarial plaques
  • Annular plaques
  • Erythematous or violaceous papules, plaques, and nodules
What is the distribution pattern of PNGD lesions?

Classic Distribution:

  • Symmetrical involvement
  • Upper extremities (50%) - especially elbows
  • Lower extremities (33%)
  • Trunk, head, and neck (<20%)
🔄 Differential Diagnosis HIGH YIELD
Condition Key Differentiating Features
Interstitial granulomatous dermatitis
  • Classically presents as linear subcutaneous cords or bands ("rope sign")
  • Typically on proximal trunk rather than extensor surfaces
  • No vasculitis on histology (key differentiator from PNGD)
Interstitial granulomatous drug reaction
  • Erythematous to violaceous annular or nonannular plaques
  • Distribution: trunk, inner upper arms, proximal thighs, intertriginous skin
  • Typically preceded by a new drug exposure
Granuloma annulare
  • Nonscaly erythematous papules or plaques
  • Histology: lymphocytic infiltrate with mucin deposition (not neutrophilic)
Bowel-associated dermatosis arthritis syndrome
  • History of inflammatory bowel disease or bowel bypass surgery
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, arthralgia) often present
Erythema elevatum diutinum
  • Persistent, symmetric, red-purple to yellow-brown papules/nodules
  • Predilection for extensor surfaces over joints
  • Association with paraproteinemias

🧠 Memory Aid: "PIDGE" - Differentiating Granulomatous Dermatoses

  • PNGD: Neutrophilic infiltrates, vasculitis present, SLE/RA associated
  • Interstitial granulomatous dermatitis: No vasculitis, "rope sign"
  • Drug-induced granulomatous reaction: New medication exposure
  • Granuloma annulare: Lymphocytes & mucin, not neutrophils
  • Erythema elevatum diutinum: Persistent, often yellow-brown lesions
🔬 Diagnostic Workup BOARD FAVORITE

Skin Biopsy Findings

Early Lesions

  • Dermal neutrophilic infiltrates
  • Karyorrhectic debris
  • Leukocytoclastic vasculitis

Later Lesions

  • Palisaded granulomas around degenerated collagen
  • Areas of fibrosis

Laboratory Workup

  • CBC with differential
  • Antinuclear antibody (ANA)
  • Antineutrophilic cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCA)
  • Rheumatoid factor (RF)
  • Cyclic citrullinated antibodies (CCP)
Additional Workup: If no underlying connective tissue disease or vasculitis is present and CBC/differential are normal:
  • Perform a full review of systems
  • Complete lymph node examination
  • Consider further directed imaging or bone marrow studies depending on clinical picture
💊 Management & Treatment
Management Pearls:
  • All patients with PNGD should be evaluated for underlying systemic diseases
  • A minority of cases resolve with treatment of the underlying disease
  • Consult a rheumatologist or hematologist/oncologist to further evaluate and manage the underlying systemic disease

Treatment Options

PNGD lesions may resolve spontaneously or with treatment of the underlying systemic illness. Because the disease is so rare, there have not been any prospective trials determining the efficacy of potential treatments.

Treatment Dosing Notes
Intralesional triamcinolone Injection as needed May cause hypopigmentation in darker skin colors
Systemic corticosteroids Variable based on severity Consider side effect profile
Hydroxychloroquine 200 mg orally twice daily Useful particularly with underlying SLE
Dapsone 50-200 mg orally daily Check G6PD before initiating
Important Note: Topical corticosteroids are generally NOT effective for PNGD.
📝 Quick Board Review Quiz
1. Which of the following is the most common disease association with PNGD?
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus and rheumatoid arthritis
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome
  • Lymphoproliferative disorders
Correct! SLE and RA are the most common associations with PNGD. This is a high-yield fact for board exams.
2. What histopathologic finding distinguishes PNGD from interstitial granulomatous dermatitis?
  • Palisaded granulomas
  • Neutrophilic infiltrates
  • Leukocytoclastic vasculitis
  • Collagen degeneration
Correct! The presence of leukocytoclastic vasculitis in PNGD distinguishes it from interstitial granulomatous dermatitis, which lacks vasculitis.
3. Which treatment is generally NOT effective for PNGD?
  • Dapsone
  • Intralesional triamcinolone
  • Hydroxychloroquine
  • Topical corticosteroids
Correct! Topical corticosteroids are generally not effective for PNGD, unlike the other treatments listed.
