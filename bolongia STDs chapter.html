Introduction to STIs

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between major STIs based on clinical presentation
  • Understand key diagnostic approaches for each STI
  • Identify appropriate treatment regimens based on condition and patient factors
  • Recognize high-yield board exam topics related to STIs

The term "venereal diseases" was historically used to refer to infections that are sexually transmitted, i.e. syphilis, gonorrhea, chancroid, lymphogranuloma venereum, and granuloma inguinale. These disorders are now referred to as "sexually transmitted diseases" (STDs) or "sexually transmitted infections" (STIs), terms that directly reflect recognition that they are caused predominantly by sexual contact with an infected person.

Syphilis

Key Features

  • Sexually acquired, chronic infection caused by Treponema pallidum with multiple organ system involvement
  • Can also be transmitted before birth (congenital)
  • Intermittently active disease with primary, secondary, and tertiary stages as well as a latent period
  • Mucocutaneous manifestations vary from genital ulcers to widespread papulosquamous eruptions to granulomatous nodules
  • Syphilis facilitates the transmission of HIV

Biology of T. pallidum

T. pallidum is a member of the genus Treponema of the order Spirochaetales, identified in 1905 by Schaudinn and Hoffmann.

Characteristics of T. pallidum

  • 6-20 microns in length, 0.1-0.18 microns in diameter
  • Regular tight spirals
  • Cannot survive outside an animal host
  • Cannot be cultured in vitro for an extended time period
  • Limited capacity for DNA repair
  • Lipid-rich outer membrane containing uniform-sized transmembrane rare outer membrane proteins (TROMP)
  • Lacks lipopolysaccharide
  • Contains periplasmic flagella

Natural History and Pathogenesis

Primary Stage

Develops 10-90 days after infection (median of 3 weeks) as an indolent papule, followed by surface necrosis and the typical well-circumscribed ulceration (chancre). Accompanied by enlarged regional lymph nodes.

Secondary Stage

Characterized by dissemination and multiplication in different tissues, appearing up to 6 months after the primary lesion has healed. Broad spectrum of cutaneous manifestations and systemic signs (malaise, fever, lymphadenopathy).

Latency

Period between healing of clinical lesions and appearance of late manifestations (can last for many years). Early latent (≤1 year) and late latent (>1 year) phases. About 70% remain in this stage for life.

Tertiary Stage

Characterized by few organisms and high cellular immune reactivity. Organisms may invade CNS, cardiovascular system, skin, bones, producing granulomatous inflammation (gummas).

Natural History of Untreated Syphilis

  • ~33% disease arrested by host (serum RPR becomes negative)
  • ~33% disease does not progress during patient's lifetime (serum RPR remains positive)
  • ~33% develop late (tertiary) syphilis:
    • ~17% develop "benign" tertiary syphilis (gummas)
    • ~8% develop cardiovascular syphilis
    • ~8% develop neurosyphilis

Clinical Features

Presents as an indolent, round or oval, indurated ulcer (chancre) that is associated with regional adenopathy. The time of onset ranges from 10 to 90 days (average 3 weeks) after T. pallidum exposure. Untreated, chancres heal within a few weeks.

Primary Syphilis Diagnosis

The most specific method to diagnose primary syphilis is darkfield microscopic examination of fluid from the surface of the chancre or PCR from a swab of a chancre.

Laboratory Diagnosis of Primary Syphilis

  • Darkfield microscopic examination of fluid from the chancre
  • Detection of T. pallidum DNA via PCR from a swab of a chancre
  • Immunohistochemical staining of biopsy specimens
  • Antibodies to cardiolipin present in ~80% of individuals at onset of symptoms (RPR/VDRL)
  • Specific antibodies (TPHA, MHA-TP, FTA-ABS) present in 90% of patients with clinical symptoms

Results from hematogenous and lymphatic dissemination of treponemes after a few weeks or months (3-10 weeks). Characterized by recurrent disease activity, with mucocutaneous and systemic manifestations.

Clinical Features of Secondary Syphilis

  • Prodromal symptoms: Low-grade fever, malaise, sore throat, generalized lymphadenopathy
  • Cutaneous findings (80%): Generalized, non-pruritic papulosquamous eruption
  • Characteristic locations: Trunk, palms, soles
  • Mucosal lesions: Superficial ulcers, mucous patches
  • Condylomata lata: In moist regions of anogenital area
  • Other: "Moth-eaten" alopecia, split papules at oral commissures

Laboratory Diagnosis of Secondary Syphilis

  • Presence of treponemes by darkfield examination of serous exudates
  • Serologic tests (non-treponemal and treponemal) always positive
  • Immunohistochemical staining of biopsy specimens

Defined by no clinical evidence of treponemal infection but positive serologic tests. Divided into early latent (≤1 year, CDC definition) and late latent (>1 year) stages.

Key Points for Latent Syphilis

  • Early latent: Possibility of recurrence of disease (90% of secondary relapses occur within first year)
  • Late latent: Relapses very infrequent
  • Natural history: ~1/3 have non-reactive RPR, no reactivation; ~1/3 remain asymptomatic but with positive RPR; ~1/3 develop tertiary syphilis

Appears months to years after initial infection. Manifestations include skin, bones, CNS, heart, and great vessels involvement.

"Benign" Late Syphilis

Gummas in skin, bones, and organs. Nodular or noduloulcerative lesions, often with arciform pattern. Remain for weeks to months, then involute with scarring.

Cardiovascular Syphilis

Late onset (15-30 years latent period). T. pallidum affects vasa vasorum of proximal aorta, producing transmural inflammatory lesions.

Neurosyphilis

Chronic meningitis with vascular and parenchymatous sequelae. Types include asymptomatic, meningeal, meningovascular, parenchymatous, and gummatous.

Laboratory Diagnosis of Syphilis

Test Type Examples Characteristics
Direct Detection Darkfield microscopy, PCR Highly specific, can confirm early infection before serology positive
Non-treponemal Tests VDRL, RPR, USR, RST, TRUST Detect antibodies to cardiolipin; useful for screening and monitoring treatment response; can have false positives
Treponemal Tests TPHA, MHA-TP, TPPA, FTA-ABS Detect antibodies to T. pallidum; confirm reactive non-treponemal tests; usually remain positive indefinitely

Limitations of Serologic Tests

Non-treponemal Tests
  • Lack of reactivity in early primary syphilis
  • Prozone phenomenon (false-negative in high-titer specimens)
  • Biological false positives (pregnancy, autoimmune disease, etc.)
Treponemal Tests
  • Remain positive indefinitely (not useful for monitoring treatment)
  • Cannot differentiate between active and treated infection
  • Cross-reactivity with other treponematoses

Treatment of Syphilis

Treatment Recommendations

Early Syphilis (Primary, Secondary, Early Latent)

Recommended: Benzathine penicillin G, 2.4 million units IM as a single dose

Alternative (penicillin-allergic): Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID for 14 days

Late Latent or Unknown Duration

Recommended: Benzathine penicillin G, 2.4 million units IM weekly for three doses

Alternative (penicillin-allergic): Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID for 28 days

Neurosyphilis

Recommended: Aqueous penicillin G, 3-4 million units IV q4h for 10-14 days

Syphilis Treatment in Pregnancy

If a pregnant patient has a penicillin allergy, the correct management is to desensitize her and then administer penicillin. Alternative regimens are not recommended as they may not adequately treat the fetus.

Gonorrhea

Key Features

  • Most common reportable STI in high-income countries
  • Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae, which infects mucosal surfaces
  • Cutaneous pustules and systemic symptoms can result from hematogenous dissemination
  • Increasing antimicrobial resistance is a major concern

Biology and Pathogenesis

Previous Exam Topic

Gonorrhea Pathogenesis: Key Virulence Factors

  • Pili: Increase adhesion to host cells and provide motility
  • Opa ligands: Aid in epithelial adherence and cellular invasion
  • Transferrin receptor: Allows acquisition of iron needed for growth
  • Lipo-oligosaccharide (LOS): Increases infectivity
  • Enzymes (phospholipase, peptidases): Cause tissue destruction

Clinical Features

Gonococcal Infection in Men

Acute anterior urethritis with dysuria and purulent urethral discharge. Without treatment, symptoms disappear in ~6 months. Complications include epididymitis, prostatitis, and vesiculitis.

Gonococcal Infection in Women

~50% asymptomatic. Primary site is endocervical canal. Symptoms include increased vaginal discharge, dysuria, intermenstrual bleeding. Major complication is PID (10-20% of infected women).

Extragenital Gonorrhea

Pharyngeal, rectal gonorrhea often asymptomatic. Rectal gonorrhea may cause proctitis. Gonococcal ophthalmia can lead to blindness if untreated.

Previous Exam Topic

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

Arthritis-dermatosis syndrome occurs in ~0.5-1% of patients with mucosal gonorrhea. Classic symptoms include:

  • Fever
  • Tenosynovitis of larger joints (knees, elbows, wrists, ankles)
  • Characteristic skin lesions: scattered hemorrhagic pustules on distal extremities

Risk factors include menstruation, complement deficiencies (C5-C9), and inhibitors of C5 cleavage.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods for Gonorrhea

  • Gram stain: Gram-negative diplococci within neutrophils (95-98% sensitive in symptomatic men, ~50% in women)
  • Culture: Gold standard, allows for antimicrobial resistance testing (80-95% sensitive for genital samples)
  • Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAATs): Highest sensitivity and specificity, can be performed on non-invasive specimens (urine, self-collected vaginal swabs)

Treatment

Treatment Recommendations for Gonococcal Infections

Uncomplicated Gonococcal Infections

Recommended: Ceftriaxone, 500 mg IM as single dose

If chlamydial infection not excluded, also treat with doxycycline 100 mg PO BID for 7 days

Alternative Regimens (except for pharynx)

Gentamicin 240 mg IM + azithromycin 2 g PO as single dose OR Cefixime 800 mg PO as single dose

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

Recommended: Ceftriaxone, 1 g IM or IV q24h until 24-48 hours after improvement begins

Note: Rising antimicrobial resistance is a major concern. The CDC no longer recommends fluoroquinolones, and decreased susceptibility to azithromycin and some cephalosporins has been reported.

Chancroid

Key Features

  • Characterized by acute genital ulcers and inguinal adenitis
  • Caused by Haemophilus ducreyi
  • Rare in high-income countries, occurrence linked with commercial sex workers
  • Important risk factor for transmission of HIV

Clinical Features

The incubation period is 3-10 days, with onset of clinical symptoms usually between days 4 and 7.

Feature Description
Primary Lesion Starts as papule → pustule → painful ulcer with purulent base and soft, undermined edges
Number 50% of men develop multiple ulcers that may coalesce
Location (men) Prepuce, coronal sulcus, frenulum (especially in uncircumcised men)
Location (women) Introital area, cervix, vaginal wall, perianal area
Lymphadenopathy Unilateral in ~40% of men; buboes may rupture causing inguinal ulceration

Laboratory Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods for Chancroid

  • Gram stain: Shows "school-of-fish" or "railroad track" pattern of small Gram-negative bacilli (only ~50% sensitive)
  • Culture: Isolation of H. ducreyi on special media (60-80% sensitive)
  • PCR: More sensitive than culture but not widely available
  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on typical presentation and exclusion of other causes (syphilis, herpes)

Treatment

Treatment Regimens for Chancroid

  • Azithromycin, 1 g PO, single dose
  • Ceftriaxone, 250 mg IM, single dose
  • Ciprofloxacin, 500 mg PO twice daily for 3 days (contraindicated in pregnancy)
  • Erythromycin base, 500 mg PO three times daily for 7 days

Improvement should be seen within 3 days, with healing within 14 days.

Lymphogranuloma Venereum (LGV)

Key Features

  • STI caused by Chlamydia trachomatis serovars L1-3
  • Can be associated with abscesses, fistulas, and lymphatic tissue involvement
  • Endemic in parts of Africa, Asia, and South America
  • Recent outbreaks of LGV proctocolitis in MSM in high-income countries
  • Progresses through three stages: initial infection, inguinal lymphadenopathy, and complications

Clinical Features

Stage Time Frame Clinical Manifestations
Initial Manifestations 3-12 days Papule, erosion or ulcer, herpetiform vesicle, nonspecific urethritis or cervicitis
Inguinal Syndrome 10-30 days up to 6 months Regional lymphadenopathy (inguinal, femoral, perirectal), constitutional symptoms, overlying erythema, eruption of buboes
Ano-genito-rectal Syndrome Months to years Proctocolitis, hyperplasia of lymphatic tissue, perirectal abscesses, fistulas, rectal strictures and stenoses

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods for LGV

  • PCR/NAAT: Detection of C. trachomatis DNA in lesional tissue, followed by genotyping for L1-3 serovars
  • Serology: High-titer antibodies to C. trachomatis
  • Histopathology: Mixed infiltrate with stellate abscesses in lymph nodes

Treatment

Treatment Regimens for LGV

Recommended: Doxycycline, 100 mg PO twice daily for 3 weeks

Alternative (pregnancy): Erythromycin base, 500 mg PO four times daily for 3 weeks

Surgical intervention may be required for large buboes.

Donovanosis (Granuloma Inguinale)

Key Features

  • Rare, chronic, progressive, ulcerative bacterial infection
  • Caused by Klebsiella granulomatis (previously Calymmatobacterium granulomatis)
  • Ulcers primarily in the genital region
  • Responsible microorganisms found within macrophages (Donovan bodies)

Clinical Features

The incubation period ranges from 1 day to 1 year (average ~17 days). A small papule or nodule initially appears and eventually ulcerates.

Clinical Characteristics

  • Lesions are usually painless or mildly painful
  • Highly vascular, beefy red appearance
  • Tendency to bleed
  • Foul-smelling exudate with necrosis
  • Inguinal lesions in up to 20% of patients
  • Common sites (men): prepuce, glans penis, frenulum, coronal sulcus
  • Common sites (women): vulvar area
  • Extragenital lesions can occur

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods for Donovanosis

  • Smears: Demonstration of Donovan bodies (bipolar staining giving "safety pin" appearance) in histiocytes
  • Stains: Giemsa, Wright, or Leishman stains of tissue scrapings or touch preparations
  • Histopathology: Ulceration with exuberant granulation tissue, pseudoepitheliomatous hyperplasia, and diffuse infiltrate
  • Culture: Difficult and not routinely available

Treatment

Treatment Regimens for Donovanosis

Recommended: Azithromycin, 1 g PO once weekly OR 500 mg PO daily

Alternative:

  • Doxycycline, 100 mg PO BID
  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, 1 double-strength tablet PO BID
  • Erythromycin base, 500 mg PO four times daily

Duration for all regimens: until all lesions completely healed (at least 3 weeks)

MCQ Review: High-Yield Board Exam Topics

Primary Syphilis Diagnosis

What is the most specific diagnostic method for primary syphilis?

A. VDRL test

Incorrect. VDRL is a non-treponemal test that detects antibodies to cardiolipin. It's useful for screening and monitoring treatment but is not the most specific test for primary syphilis.

B. FTA-ABS test

Incorrect. FTA-ABS is a treponemal test that's more specific than VDRL, but it's not the most specific method for diagnosing primary syphilis.

C. RPR test

Incorrect. RPR is a non-treponemal test similar to VDRL. It's not the most specific test for primary syphilis.

D. Darkfield microscopy

Correct! Darkfield microscopy permits the definite diagnosis of syphilis by visual detection of motile spirochetes from lesions of the skin. It is the most specific method for diagnosing primary syphilis, especially early in the course when serologic tests may still be negative.

E. MHA-TP test

Incorrect. MHA-TP is a treponemal test that detects antibodies to T. pallidum. While specific, it's not the most specific method for diagnosing primary syphilis.

Congenital Syphilis

Which of the following is NOT a manifestation of early congenital syphilis?

A. "Snuffles" (bloody nasal discharge)

Incorrect. "Snuffles" (bloody or purulent mucinous nasal discharge) is a common manifestation of early congenital syphilis.

B. Pseudoparalysis of Parrot

Incorrect. Pseudoparalysis of Parrot (reduced movement of extremities due to pain from osteochondritis) is a manifestation of early congenital syphilis.

C. Bullous skin lesions

Incorrect. Bullous skin lesions (pemphigus syphiliticus) can be seen in early congenital syphilis.

D. Hutchinson teeth

Correct! Hutchinson teeth (peg-shaped, notched permanent incisors) are a manifestation of late congenital syphilis, not early congenital syphilis. They are part of the Hutchinson triad along with interstitial keratitis and neural deafness.

E. Hepatosplenomegaly

Incorrect. Hepatosplenomegaly is a common manifestation of early congenital syphilis.

Late Congenital Syphilis

Which of the following findings is known as Higoumenakis sign in late congenital syphilis?

A. Saddle nose

Incorrect. Saddle nose is a depression of the nasal root due to destruction of cartilage/bone in late congenital syphilis, but it's not called Higoumenakis sign.

B. Frontal bossing

Incorrect. Frontal bossing ("Olympian brow") is a finding in late congenital syphilis, but it's not Higoumenakis sign.

C. Thickening of the medial clavicle

Correct! Higoumenakis sign is thickening of the medial clavicle, which is one of the skeletal stigmata of late congenital syphilis.

D. Mulberry molars

Incorrect. Mulberry molars (multiple rounded rudimentary cusps on permanent first molars) are a dental stigmata of late congenital syphilis, but they're not called Higoumenakis sign.

E. Rhagades

Incorrect. Rhagades (radial periorificial scars at sites of previous fissures) are cutaneous stigmata of late congenital syphilis, not Higoumenakis sign.

Syphilis Treatment in Pregnancy

What is the appropriate management for a pregnant woman with syphilis who reports penicillin allergy?

A. Doxycycline therapy

Incorrect. Doxycycline is contraindicated in pregnancy. Also, alternative antibiotics do not reliably cross the placenta to treat the fetus.

B. Azithromycin therapy

Incorrect. Azithromycin does not adequately cross the placenta and is not recommended for treatment of syphilis in pregnancy.

C. Ceftriaxone therapy

Incorrect. While ceftriaxone may be used in non-pregnant penicillin-allergic patients, its efficacy in treating maternal and fetal syphilis has not been established.

D. Penicillin desensitization followed by penicillin therapy

Correct! Penicillin is the only documented effective treatment for syphilis during pregnancy. Pregnant women with penicillin allergy should undergo penicillin desensitization followed by penicillin therapy to ensure proper treatment of both mother and fetus.

E. Tetracycline therapy

Incorrect. Tetracycline, like doxycycline, is contraindicated in pregnancy due to potential adverse effects on fetal development.

Gonorrhea Pathogenesis

Which virulence factor of Neisseria gonorrhoeae is primarily responsible for its motility and adherence to host cells?

A. Lipopolysaccharide

Incorrect. N. gonorrhoeae has lipo-oligosaccharide (LOS) rather than lipopolysaccharide, and while it contributes to infectivity, it's not primarily responsible for motility and adherence.

B. Pili

Correct! Pili (fimbriae) are hair-like projections that extend from the bacterial surface and are important for both motility and attachment to host cells. Non-piliated gonococci have a reduced ability to cause infections in humans.

C. IgA protease

Incorrect. IgA protease helps N. gonorrhoeae evade host immunity by cleaving IgA antibodies, but it doesn't play a primary role in motility and adherence.

D. Por proteins

Incorrect. Por (porin) proteins form channels in the outer membrane and play a role in invasion and serum resistance, but they're not primarily responsible for motility and adherence.

E. Transferrin receptor

Incorrect. Transferrin receptor helps N. gonorrhoeae acquire iron needed for growth, but it's not primarily responsible for motility and adherence.

Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

Which of the following is NOT a typical feature of disseminated gonococcal infection?

A. Fever

Incorrect. Fever is a common feature of disseminated gonococcal infection (arthritis-dermatosis syndrome).

B. Tenosynovitis

Incorrect. Tenosynovitis, especially of larger joints, is a typical feature of disseminated gonococcal infection.

C. Diffuse maculopapular rash

Correct! A diffuse maculopapular rash is NOT typical of disseminated gonococcal infection. The characteristic skin lesions are scattered pustules (often hemorrhagic) on distal extremities, not a generalized rash.

D. Hemorrhagic pustules on extremities

Incorrect. Hemorrhagic pustules on the extremities are characteristic skin lesions of disseminated gonococcal infection.

E. Asymmetric arthritis

Incorrect. Asymmetric arthritis is a typical feature of disseminated gonococcal infection.

Syphilis Organ Involvement

Which organ is classically spared in tertiary syphilis?

A. Brain

Incorrect. The brain is commonly affected in tertiary syphilis, particularly in neurosyphilis.

B. Heart

Incorrect. The heart and great vessels are commonly affected in tertiary syphilis (cardiovascular syphilis).

C. Liver

Incorrect. The liver can be affected in tertiary syphilis with hepatic gummas.

D. Ovaries

Correct! The ovaries are classically spared in tertiary syphilis. While syphilis can involve almost every organ system, the ovaries are often cited as an exception.

E. Bones

Incorrect. Bones are commonly affected in tertiary syphilis with gummas, periostitis, and osteitis.
