Overview of Disorders

This chapter focuses on two groups of disorders:

Autoinflammatory Disorders
  • Due to abnormalities in the innate immune system
  • Includes Still disease and inherited inflammatory syndromes
  • Characterized by recurrent fevers and inflammation flares
  • Often involves the skin, joints, eyes, and serosae
  • Features sterile neutrophilic inflammation
  • Often due to overexpression of IL-1
Autoimmune Disorders
  • Due to abnormalities in the adaptive immunity
  • Involves antibody production
  • Examples include Sjögren syndrome and mixed connective tissue disease
  • Often involves specific antibodies against self-antigens
  • May have overlapping features with other connective tissue diseases

Systemic-Onset Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (Still Disease)

Overview
  • One of 7 types of juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)
  • Most common rheumatological disorder in children
  • Equal sex ratio (F:M is 1:1) unlike other types that have female predominance
  • Possible LACC1 mutation
Pathogenesis
  • Activation of the innate immune system
  • Upregulation of IL-1 plays a critical role (due to inflammasome activation)
  • IL-1 → expression of leukocyte adhesion molecules by endothelial cells → perivascular infiltrate of neutrophils and monocytes
  • Activated neutrophils and macrophages produce TNF-α, IL-1, and IL-6
  • IL-1 expression → fever
  • IL-6 → reduced osteoblast and increased osteoblast activity, and increase CRP
Lab Findings
  • Leukocytosis, granulocytosis, thrombocytosis
  • Elevated LFTs
  • Polyclonal gammopathies
  • Elevated ESR/CRP and ferritin
Clinical Features
  • Fever: Daily fevers occurring in late afternoon or early evening (characteristic feature)
  • Arthritis: Polyarticular arthritis, most commonly involving knees, ankles, hips, followed by small joints of hands
  • Skin:
    • Exanthem present in up to 90%, accompanies fever spikes ("Resident's rash")
    • Begins prior to arthritis onset in 25% of patients
    • Transient, salmon-colored, non-pruritic
    • Predilection for axillae and waist
    • Linear lesions secondary to Koebner phenomenon are common
    • Other features: periorbital edema/erythema, persistent linear plaques
    • If exposed to MTX → may develop rheumatoid nodule-like lesions
  • Systemic: Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, serositis
Complications
  • Up to 10% develop macrophage activation syndrome (MAS)
    • Characterized by fever, cytopenias, liver dysfunction, coagulopathy, hypofibrinogenemia, hypertriglyceridemia, and very high serum ferritin
    • Increased serum IL-18 levels and depressed NK cell cytolytic function
    • Treatment: high-dose systemic steroids
  • Other complications: hepatitis, pericarditis, and rarely secondary amyloidosis
Pathology

"Neutrophilic urticarial dermatosis"

  • A reaction pattern seen in all autoinflammatory disorders
  • Perivascular + interstitial dermal mixed infiltrate, primarily neutrophilic
  • Dyskeratosis in the upper layers of the epidermis may be seen

Diagnostic Criteria for Still Disease

  1. High episodic fevers (>38.9°C) daily for ≥ 2 weeks and documented to be quotidian for ≥ 3 days
    • Classically arise in late afternoon to early evening
  2. Plus one of the following features:
    • Transient evanescent, salmon pink, blanching eruption (90%): typically arises in late afternoon/evening (corresponds with fever spikes); with generalized distribution (favors axilla and waist); Koebnerization with linear lesions
    • Generalized lymphadenopathy
    • Hepatomegaly/splenomegaly
    • Serositis (pericarditis, pleuritis, and peritonitis)
  3. Symmetric polyarthritis > oligoarthritis; erosive in 20% - Knees, ankles, hips > small joints
Treatment
  • Spontaneous resolution in ~40-50%
  • The rest will have a chronic course and require treatment
  • Symptoms lasting >6 months → poor prognosis
  • Mild symptoms: NSAIDs
  • Moderate-severe symptoms: steroids + MTX
  • MAS → high dose systemic steroids
  • Others: thalidomide, tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor), anakinra (IL-1 inhibitor)
  • HSCT if recalcitrant

Adult-Onset Still Disease

Overview
  • Can be a severe debilitating systemic illness that is often difficult to diagnose
  • The vast majority of patients have an exanthem - helpful in diagnosis
  • Affects young adults
Pathogenesis
  • Unclear but overlaps with Still disease
  • Linked to several HLAs
  • May be triggered by viral or bacterial infections
  • Serum levels of IL-2, IL-6, IL-18, TNF-α, and IFN-γ are elevated
Clinical Features
  • Preceded by sore throat and constitutional symptoms
  • Spiking fevers in the later afternoon or early evening
  • Skin:
    • Eruption occurs in accordance with the fever
    • Macular eruption with characteristic salmon-pink color
    • Favors sites of pressure + can exhibit the Koebner phenomenon
    • Trunk is most commonly involved
    • Violaceous to reddish-brown, scaly, persistent papules and plaques in Taiwanese patients
    • Eyelid edema and flagellate erythema are rare findings
  • Joints:
    • Arthritis (65-100%)
    • Symmetrically involving the knees, wrists and ankles, associated with fever spikes
    • A distinctive feature is carpal ankylosis → minimal pain but a limited range of motion in the carpal joints
  • Other: Hepatomegaly, rarely splenomegaly
  • Internal Organs: Involvement is rare (lung, heart, kidneys)
Laboratory Findings
  • Similar to Still disease
  • Ferritin correlates with disease activity
  • High ferritin is also associated with chronic pattern of disease, recurrent flares, and poor prognosis
Complications

Macrophage activation syndrome (15%)

Treatment
  • High dose aspirin and NSAIDs
  • Systemic corticosteroids in acute setting
  • MTX is the most commonly used steroid-sparing agent
  • Biologics (anakinra, tocilizumab) + Rituximab are third line

Relapsing Polychondritis

Overview
  • Autoimmune disease primarily affecting cartilage
  • Men and women are equally affected
  • Most common in Caucasians
  • Age: 20-60 years
  • Relapsing polychondritis + Behçet disease = MAGIC syndrome (mouth and genital ulcers, inflamed cartilages)
  • Associated with other autoimmune disorders (30% of cases) + myelodysplasia
  • Presence of cutaneous small vessel vasculitis (CSSV) and aphthous ulcers → higher risk of having myelodysplasia
Pathogenesis
  • HLA-DR4 association
  • HLA-DR6 is protective against internal organ involvement
  • Immunologic reaction against type 2 collagen
  • Antibodies are identified in 30%-50% of cases
  • Antibodies against type IX and XI collagen as well
  • Type II collagen is found in cartilage → any cartilaginous structure may be affected
Clinical Features
  • Ears:
    • Most prominent: erythema, swelling, and pain of the cartilaginous portion of the auricle
    • Spares the earlobe
    • Chronic inflammation → cartilage destruction → deformity
    • 90% of patients have auricular involvement
    • When it involves the external auditory meatus → conductive hearing loss
  • Nose:
    • Nasal cartilage involvement → saddle nose deformity, pain, stuffiness, crusting, rhinorrhea, epistaxis, and compromise of olfaction
  • Respiratory Tract:
    • Most serious complication
    • Symptoms: cough, hoarseness, choking, dyspnea, wheezing, airway obstruction or collapse, flail chest, secondary pulmonary infections
    • Pneumonia is #1 cause of mortality
  • Joints:
    • Common
    • Episodic, migratory, asymmetric, non-erosive oligo- or polyarticular arthritis can affect any joint
  • Eyes:
    • Conjunctivitis, scleritis, corneal ulcerations, uveitis, or optic neuritis
  • Skin:
    • Reactive cutaneous features: vasculitis, aphthous ulcers, erythema elevatum diutinum, Sweet's syndrome, erythema nodosum
Pathology
  • Early: Cartilaginous neutrophilic infiltrate
  • Late: Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrates with replacement of cartilage by granulation tissue and fibrosis

Diagnostic Criteria for Relapsing Polychondritis

Diagnosis is based on:

  • Histologically confirmed chondritis in two of the following:
    • Auricular cartilage
    • Nasal cartilage
    • Laryngotracheal cartilage
  • OR one of them plus two or more of the following:
    • Ocular inflammation
    • Vestibular dysfunction
    • Hearing loss
    • Seronegative inflammatory arthritis
Investigations
  • ↑ESR/CRP, mild leukocytosis
  • Normochromic/normocytic anemia (associated with worse prognosis)
  • ↑Cr/BUN and microscopic hematuria
  • PET scan needs to be done prior to initiation of therapy
Treatment
  • Prednisone (0.5-1 mg/kg/day, or higher if systemic involvement)
  • Dapsone, colchicine, and NSAIDs can be used as adjunctive therapy
  • Other immunosuppressives (MTX most effective) and biologics can be used

Sjögren Syndrome

Overview
  • Also known as sicca syndrome
  • Autoimmune disorder that affects secretory glands (lacrimal and salivary glands in particular)
  • Second most common autoimmune connective tissue disease after rheumatoid arthritis
  • Mucocutaneous manifestations may be the first sign of the disease
  • Can be primary or secondary to other autoimmune CTDs (SLE, RA, SSc)
  • Demographics: Females 30-40s, F:M is 9:1
  • Males have a better prognosis (usually the opposite in autoimmune CTDs)
  • 20x increased risk of Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (B-cell)
  • Most common lymphoma is extranodal marginal zone B-cell lymphoma in the salivary gland
  • Increased risk of neonatal lupus in pregnant women (especially if positive serologies)
Pathogenesis
Genetics
  • Germline abnormality of TNFAIP3 → ↑risk of antigen-driven B-cell lymphomas
  • Increased expression of Bcl-2 → ↑risk of marginal zone B-cell lymphoma
Triggers
  • EBV, coxsackievirus, HTLV-1, and HCV are possible triggers
Immune Response
  • Anti-Ro/SSA, Anti-La/SSB are associated with:
    • ↑risk of subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE)
    • ↑risk of vasculitis
    • ↑risk of extraglandular disease
    • ↓age of onset
  • Anti-Fodrin → most sensitive and specific antibody
  • ↑TLR function, ↑Th17, ↑costimulatory molecules (CD40-CD40L)
Clinical Features
  • Most common cutaneous feature is xerosis
  • Most important cutaneous feature is vasculitis → associated with systemic involvement, positive serologies, high ESR, high mortality, and risk of lymphoma
  • Most prominent feature is mucosal xerosis (occurs after >50% of the gland is destroyed)
    • Xerostomia: Difficulty swallowing, oral thrush, dental caries, transient gland enlargement
    • If gland enlargement is persistent → need to rule out lymphoma
    • Tests: salivary gland scintigraphy, sialometry, parotid sialography, or salivary gland biopsy
    • Xerophthalmia: Keratoconjunctivitis sicca, photophobia, foreign body sensation
    • Tests: Schirmer test (tear production), Rose Bengal dye (corneal integrity)
  • Other cutaneous features: purpura, urticarial vasculitis, SCLE, annular erythemas, erythema nodosum, amyloidosis, Raynaud's, Waldenstrom's hypergammaglobulinemic purpura
  • Internal organs: lungs, kidneys, bone marrow, neurological involvement, and arthritis (common)
Diagnosis
  • Skin biopsies are non-specific and depend on the particular manifestation
  • Minor salivary gland biopsy is one of the criteria for diagnosis: presence of one or more foci (50 or more lymphocytes)

ACR/EULAR 2016 Classification Criteria for Primary Sjögren's Syndrome

Score ≥4 required for classification when the weight from the five criteria items below are summed:

  • Labial salivary gland with focal lymphocytic sialadenitis and focus score of ≥1 foci/4 mm²: 3 points
  • Anti-SSA/Ro-positive: 3 points
  • Ocular staining score ≥5 (or van Bijsterveld score ≥4) in at least one eye: 1 point
  • Schirmer's test ≤5 mm/5 min in at least one eye: 1 point
  • Unstimulated whole saliva flow rate ≤0.1 mL/min: 1 point

Note: Applies to patients with symptoms of ocular or oral dryness and without exclusion criteria (head/neck radiation, HCV, AIDS, sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, GVHD, IgG4-related disease)

Prognosis

Bad prognostic factors:

  • Hypocomplementemia
  • Cryoglobulinemia
  • Vasculitis
  • Parotid enlargement
  • Lymphoproliferative diseases
Patient Instructions for Xerostomia Management

General Measures

  • Avoid smoking cigarettes, cigars
  • Reduce intake of salty, spicy and acidic foods
  • Limit carbohydrate intake, including sugars
  • Avoid acidic (low pH) drinks
  • Reduce alcohol and caffeine consumption
  • Avoid excessive sipping
  • Avoid OTC antihistamines and decongestants
  • Use sodium bicarbonate mouth rinse twice daily
  • Use a humidifier in bedroom overnight

Saliva Substitutes (Oral Moisturizers)

  • Spray: Use prior to speaking and during the day to assist in talking (e.g. Biotene® Moisturizing Mouth Spray)
  • Solution: Use prior to eating to assist in swallowing (e.g. Salivart® Oral Moisturizer)
  • Gel: Use at bedtime (e.g. Biotene® Oral Balance Gel)
  • Lozenges: Use overnight (e.g. Xylimelts®)

Saliva Stimulants - Sialagogues

  • Local: Sugar-free, acid-free gum or candy that contains xylitol; dried fruit slices
  • Systemic: Pilocarpine (Salagen®) 5 mg three times daily or Cevimeline (Evoxac®) 30 mg three times daily

Dental Care

  • Avoid toothpastes with detergents, whiteners, or flavorings
  • Avoid commercial mouthwashes with alcohol or peroxide
  • Professional dental cleaning 3-4 times per year
  • Use topical fluorides and remineralizing agents
Treatment
  • Symptomatic treatment: emollients, eye drops, saliva substitutes
  • If systemic involvement/vasculitis → systemic therapy:
    • Systemic corticosteroids
    • Hydroxychloroquine
    • Steroid-sparing immunosuppressants (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil)
    • Rituximab in resistant cases

Mixed Connective Tissue Disease

Overview
  • Strong female predominance (9:1)
  • Associated with HLA-DR4
  • Anti-U1RNP (high titers) antibodies thought to play pathogenic role
  • The function of U1RNP is to splice pre-mRNA into mRNA
  • Anti-hnRNP-A2 have been described
  • May be associated with EBV and cytomegalovirus infection → molecular mimicry
Clinical Features
  • Overlapping features of two or more of the following:
    • SLE
    • DM/PM
    • SSc
    • RA
  • Raynaud's phenomenon is the earliest and most common sign
  • Pulmonary artery hypertension is the most common cause of mortality
  • Lacks anti-dsDNA, Smith antibodies, and hypocomplementemia - helps to differentiate from SLE
  • High titer of U1RNP antibody → hallmark
Diagnosis & Treatment

Pathology and treatment depend on the clinical entity and manifestations present.

Extra-Articular Manifestations of Rheumatoid Arthritis

Overview
  • RA affects ~2% of the population
  • F:M is 2-3:1
Pathogenesis
  • A trigger in predisposed individuals leads to an immune response
  • Trigger: EBV and E. coli infections → ?molecular mimicry
  • Genetics: HLA-DR1 and -DR4, PTPN22 gene (also seen in SLE, Graves, and T1DM)
  • Immune response: Interplay between the innate and adaptive immunity, antibodies that target citrullinated proteins, increased TNF-α and IL-6
Cutaneous Manifestations
  • Rheumatoid Nodules:
    • 20% of RA patients
    • Associated with high titer of RF
    • Firm semi-mobile papulonodules on pressure areas/extensors
    • May also occur in visceral organs
    • Accelerated rheumatoid nodulosis: sudden appearance of multiple RNs following treatment with MTX, rapid tapering of steroids, or TNF-α inhibitors
  • Rheumatoid Vasculitis:
    • Late complication of RA
    • Affects any vessel
    • Cutaneous features depend on the size of involved vessels
    • Associated with neuropathy, scleritis, alveolitis, carditis, intestinal ulcers, and proteinuria
    • Bad prognosis (mortality ~40%)
  • Bywaters Lesions:
    • Nailfold thromboses and purpuric papules on the distal digits
    • LCV on histopathology but not associated with systemic vasculitis
  • Felty Syndrome:
    • Rare but severe (occurs in seropositive patients)
    • Triad of neutropenia, splenomegaly, and RA
    • Therapy-resistant leg ulcers (pretibial) → "PG-like"
    • Increased risk of systemic infections
    • Increased risk of lymphoma and leukemia
    • Treatment with G-CSF or splenectomy
  • Neutrophilic Dermatoses:
    • PG, Sweet syndrome, and rheumatoid neutrophilic dermatitis are the primary disorders, but any neutrophilic dermatosis can occur
    • Rheumatoid neutrophilic dermatitis resembles Sweet syndrome clinically and histologically, but no tenderness + no fever + seropositive for RF

When evaluating a patient with suspected rheumatoid vasculitis and negative/inconclusive cutaneous histologic findings, consider:

  • Nerve conduction studies/electromyography (to detect mononeuritis multiplex)
  • Sural nerve biopsy
  • Muscle biopsy
  • Angiogram (if strong suspicion remains)
Laboratory Findings
  • RF (sensitivity 80%, specificity 85%)
  • Anti-CCP (sensitivity 70%, specificity 95%)
Treatment
  • Articular disease: NSAIDs, low-dose systemic corticosteroids, disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis of medium/large vessels: aggressive therapy with systemic steroids + cyclophosphamide ± plasmapheresis
  • Others: AZA, MMF, TNF-α inhibitors, Rituximab, cyclosporine

Board Exam Question

Which of the following cutaneous manifestations of rheumatoid arthritis warrants peripheral nerve study work-up to rule out neuropathy?

  1. Non-healing lower extremity ulcers of Felty Syndrome
  2. Palpable purpura associated with rheumatoid vasculitis
  3. Nailfold thromboses of Bywater's lesions
  4. Tender truncal plaques of Sweet's Syndrome
  5. Lower extremity ulceration associated with pyoderma gangrenosum

An evaluation of a patient with palisaded necrotizing granuloma and suspected rheumatologic disorder should consider:

  • Basophilic vs. Eosinophilic collagen degeneration
  • Neutrophil vs. Eosinophil dominant infiltrate
  • ANCA status and Rheumatoid factor
  • If ANCA+, determine C-ANCA vs. P-ANCA pattern

Key differential diagnoses include: Granulomatosis with polyangiitis, Rheumatoid nodule, Palisaded neutrophilic and granulomatous dermatitis, and Rheumatic fever nodule.

Autoinflammatory Disorders

Overview
  • Group of diseases due to aberrant activation of the innate immune system
  • Some degree of autoimmunity or immune deficiency can be seen
  • Characterized by recurrent fevers and flares of multi-organ inflammation
  • Frequently involves the skin, joints, eyes, and serosae
  • Features sterile neutrophilic inflammation
  • Most are likely due to overexpression of IL-1, whether due to increased activation or decreased inhibition
  • Improvement on anakinra, rilonacept, and canakinumab (Anti-IL-1) supports this theory

Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF)

  • Ethnicity: Sephardic Jewish, Arab, Turkish, Italian, Armenian
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Gene/Protein: MEFV / Pyrin (16p13.3)
  • Attack Length: 1-3 days
  • Skin: Erysipeloid erythema and edema (favors lower leg, foot)
  • Abdomen: Peritonitis > pleuritis > pericarditis
  • Joints: Monoarthritis > exercise-induced myalgia
  • Treatment: Colchicine prophylaxis, NSAIDs

Hyperimmunoglobulinemia D Syndrome (HIDS)

  • Ethnicity: Predominantly Dutch, northern European
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Gene/Protein: MVK / Mevalonate kinase (12q24)
  • Attack Length: 3-7 days
  • Skin: Erythematous macules and edematous papules, may become purpuric; occasional oral and vaginal ulcers (widespread)
  • Abdomen: Abdominal pain, but rarely serositis
  • Joints: Arthralgia > oligoarticular arthritis > myalgia
  • Lab: High serum IgD and IgA; mevalonate in urine during attacks
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids for acute attacks, anakinra, TNF inhibitors, simvastatin

TNF Receptor-Associated Periodic Syndrome (TRAPS)

  • Ethnicity: Any ethnic group
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Gene/Protein: TNFRSF1A / TNF receptor-1A (12p13)
  • Attack Length: Often >7 days
  • Skin: Erythematous patches and edematous plaques (often annular or serpiginous); later ecchymotic
  • Distribution: Migrate distally on extremities with underlying myalgia
  • Abdomen: Peritonitis > pleuritis, pericarditis
  • Joints: Migratory myalgia > arthralgia > monoarthritis
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids, TNF inhibitors (e.g., etanercept)

Cryopyrin-Associated Periodic Syndromes (CAPS)

Includes: Muckle-Wells Syndrome (MWS), Familial Cold Autoinflammatory Syndrome (FCAS), and NOMID/CINCA

  • Ethnicity: Any ethnic group
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Gene/Protein: NLRP3 (CIAS1) / Cryopyrin (1q44)
  • Attack Length:
    • MWS: 1-2 days
    • FCAS: Minutes-3 days
    • NOMID: Continuous + flares
  • Skin: Urticarial papules and plaques (widespread)
  • FCAS-specific: Cold-induced urticarial papules and plaques
  • Negative ice-cube test (only with acquired cold urticaria)
  • Treatment: IL-1/IL-1R antagonists (rilonacept, canakinumab, anakinra)
Other Autoinflammatory Disorders
DIRA (Deficiency of IL-1 Receptor Antagonist)
  • Gene/Protein: IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL1RN)
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Age of Onset: Neonatal
  • Features: Pustules within areas of erythema (similar to pustular psoriasis), sterile osteolytic bone lesions
  • Treatment: IL-1 antagonists (often dramatic response)
PAPA Syndrome
  • Gene/Protein: PSTPIP1
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of Onset: Childhood
  • Features: Pyoderma gangrenosum, pathergy, severe acne, recurrent painful sterile arthritis
  • Treatment: TNF inhibitors, IL-1 antagonists, corticosteroids
Blau Syndrome
  • Gene/Protein: NOD2/CARD15
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of Onset: Childhood
  • Features: Numerous tiny papules, granulomatous infiltrate in dermis, uveitis, early-onset symmetric granulomatous polyarticular arthritis
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids, TNF inhibitors, MTX, CSA, IL-1 antagonists
SAVI
  • Gene/Protein: TMEM173
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of Onset: Early infancy
  • Features: Acral infarcts/ulcerations/gangrene, bone resorption with loss of digits, violaceous plaques, nasal septum perforation, interstitial lung disease
  • Treatment: JAK inhibitors (minimal response to corticosteroids, DMARDs)
