Normal Hair Cycle - Foundation Knowledge

Anagen

~90%

Growth phase

Catagen

~<1%

Transition phase

Telogen

~10%

Rest phase (3 months)

Clinical Pearl: Compared to other areas of the body, percentage of anagen is higher and telogen is lower in the scalp, and this is why scalp hair grows longer (MCQ)
Normal Hair Facts:
  • ~100,000 hair follicles are present in normal scalp
  • Normally, 50-200 hairs are shed daily
  • Telogen phase typically lasts 3 months
Androgenic Alopecia (AGA)
Key Fact: Most common cause of hair loss
Epidemiology:
  • 80% of Caucasian men show evidence by age 70
  • ~50% of women show evidence by age 70

Genetic Predisposition:

  • Strong genetic predisposition
  • Multiple loci identified:
    • Males: androgen receptor
    • Females: estrogen receptor and aromatase

In Men → DHT Expression:

  • Testosterone is converted to DHT by enzyme 5-α-reductase
  • Three types of 5-α-reductase exist:
    • Type I: sebaceous glands and liver
    • Type II: scalp, beard, and chest hair follicles, liver and prostate
    • Type III: epidermis and dermis, unknown function
  • Increased 5-α-reductase activity in balding areas compared non-balding areas

In Women:

  • Similar pathogenesis probably
  • Aromatase may play a more significant role compared to male AGA

Male Pattern Hair Loss (MPHL):

  • Symmetric progressive hair loss over the bitemporal, frontal, and vertex
  • Graded by the Hamilton-Norwood scale (MCQ)
  • Women with MPHL should be graded by the HN classification as well

Female Pattern Hair Loss (FPHL):

  • Diffuse central thinning of the crown with preservation of the frontal hairline
  • "Christmas tree" pattern
  • Graded by the Ludwing or Sinclair scales (MCQ)

Trichoscopy:

  • Diversity of hair thickness (>20% diversity is characteristic) (MCQ)
  • Peripilar sign: brown halo around hair follicles
  • One or two terminal hairs per hair follicle (normally 3-5)
  • Looks similar to sebaceous hyperplasia, sebaceous glands without hair

FDA-Approved Treatments:

Topical minoxidil. 2% and 5% are FDA approved for both genders
Low-level laser
Finasteride: 1mg/d approved for men only not approved for women
Important: Continued treatment indefinitely is paramount

Finasteride Side Effects:

  • Decreased ejaculate fluid
  • Loss of libido
  • Erectile dysfunction
  • PSA abnormalities
  • Decreased risk of low grade prostate cancer, but increased rate of high grade cancers

Other Options:

  • Dutasteride: blocks both DHT I and II, more potent compared to finasteride
  • Hair transplant
  • For women: anti-androgens but contraception is very important
For further information about anti-androgens, see chapter 70
Telogen Effluvium
Key Fact: TE is the most common cause of hair loss caused by medication
  • A trigger leads to abrupt shift of anagen phase to telogen phase
  • These may be medication, malnutrition, or medical illness
  • The duration of TE will depend on the underlying cause
  • Hair loss occurs ~3 months after the offending agent
  • Most drugs associated with hair loss cause TE, except chemotherapy (AE)
  • When no underlying cause is identified for >6 months, patient is labeled as chronic TE
  • Chronic TE mainly occurs in women 30-60 years of age, and it's a diagnosis of exclusion
  • Thinning of hair involves the whole scalp
  • May also be evident on other hairy areas like the pubic area and axilla
  • Hair pull test will be positive
  • Hair pluck test and trichogram will show a percentage of TE to be >20% (diagnostic)
  • Eventually hair is expected to fully grow
  • In women with chronic TE, the prognosis is also excellent
  • Complete balding is not expected

In patients with no identifiable cause:

  • CBC, chemistry, ESR, ferritin
  • Ferritin level should be above 40 ng/dL (MCQ)
Correct the underlying cause
Alopecia Areata
  • Non-scarring autoimmune disease
  • Prevalence 1-2%
  • 1/5 patients report family history of alopecia areata
  • Most common cause of hair loss in children

Genetically susceptible individuals → exposed to a trigger (stress, vaccine, viral infection) → loss of immune tolerance → lymphocytic destruction of the hair follicle

Immune mediated hypothesis is supported by the association of alopecia areata with other autoimmune disease, and response to immunosuppressives
  • Round or oval patches of non-scarring alopecia
  • Exclamation point hair: distal portion is thicker than the proximal portion, characteristic
  • Non-pigmented hairs may be spared, and regrowth may initially be non-pigmented
  • Other variants include totalis, universalis, ophiasis, sisapho, and perinevoid
  • Nail pitting most common nail finding (regular)
  • Trachyonychia ,Brittle nails ,Onycholysis , Koilonychia and Onychomadesis
  • 5% of patients develop totalis
  • 1% of patients develop universalis

Bad Prognostic Factors:

  • Atopy
  • Universalis, totalis, or ophiasis pattern
  • Younger age at presentation
  • Long-standing alopecia areata leads to follicular dropout, which can become irreversible. Prognosis worsens after 5 years, and hair loss may become irreversible if it continues beyond 10 years.
  • Nail changes
  • Family history
  • History of other autoimmune disease
  • HLA-DQ7 and DR4

Trichoscopy:

  • Yellow dot most common (MCQ)
  • Micro exclamation hair
  • Black dots
  • Peribulbar lymphocytic infiltrate (swarm of bees)
  • Nanogen hair on horizontal section: small atrophic hair that shows features of anagen, catagen and telogen
  • Eosinophils may be seen (MCQ)
Trichotillomania
  • Self-induced plucking or breakage of hair
  • Associated with psychological stress and personality disorders
  • More common in female children
  • One of the OCDs, however, many of the patients do not meet the DSM-5 criteria
  • Patchy or full alopecia of the scalp
  • Other hair bearing areas can be involved
  • Bizarre shape, irregular border, erosions, hair of different length
  • Window test: hair is shaved, and re-evaluated if the hairs are the same length or not
  • Many patients swallow their hair → trichobezoar - Rapunzel syndrome
  • Friar tuck sign

Trichoscopy:

  • Hook hairs
  • Coiled hairs
  • The v-sign (two hairs broken at the same level in one follicle)
  • Hair powder
  • Flame hairs (semi-transparent, wavy, cone-shaped hair residues)
  • Trichoptilosis (split ends)
  • Disrupted follicular anatomy
  • Pigment cast and trichomalacia
  • Minimal inflammation
  • Psychotherapy
  • Clomipramine (TCA) is first line
  • N-acetylcysteine supplement can be used (MCQ)
  • SSRIs
Other Non-cicatricial Alopecias
  • Seen mostly in patients with history of long admission or surgery
  • Due to decreased blood flow to the hair follicle
  • Roughly oval patch at the site of greatest pressure
  • Most commonly upper occiput
  • Halo scalp ring is a variant of pressure induced alopecia in which the cervix pressures the scalp during labor (discussed in earlier chapters)
  • At birth or during the first decade of life
  • Can happen outside the temporal area
  • Numerous vellus hairs seen on trichoscopy
  • Persists for life
  • Boggy scalp associated with hair loss
  • Mainly seen in women with dark skin type
  • Doubling of the scalp thickness, due to increased subcutaneous fat
  • When not associated with alopecia, called lipedematous scalp
  • Surgery is the only treatment option
  • Alopecia in psoriasis is more common than we think
  • Typically the alopecia is localized
  • A specific finding is hair loss in tufts as thick psoriatic plaques are shed from the scalp
  • Typically non-scarring, but rarely can be scarring
  • Infliximab and adalimumab can lead to psoriasiform alopecia
  • Inability to grow hair after neonatal physiological shedding
  • Follicular cysts and milia-like lesions appear on the skin later in life (with papules)
  • Mutation in the hairless gene (MCQ)
  • Associated with Vitamin D-dependent rickets (MCQ)
Cicatricial Alopecia Overview
  • Can be primary or secondary
  • Primary cicatricial alopecia: hair follicle is the primary target of immune insult
  • Secondary cicatricial alopecia: hair follicle is an innocent bystander, such as burns, trauma, neoplastic conditions, or scarring diseases (morphea, cicatricial pemphigoid)
Biopsy Technique: When taking a biopsy for a cicatricial alopecia, both horizontal and vertical sections should be examined. This can be done by two 4mm punch biopsies extending to the fat, or by the HoVert technique.

Primary Alopecias Classification:

  • Lymphocytic: DLE, LPP, and CCCA
  • Neutrophilic: dissecting cellulitis, folliculitis decalvans
  • Mixed: acne keloidalis, erosive pustular dermatosis
  • Folliculitis decalvans is controversial because it is poorly defined
  • Bolognia classifies folliculitis decalvans as a variant of CCCA with severe pustules
  • The same applies for pseudopelade and pseudopelade of Brocq, which represent any end-stage scarring alopecia
Central Centrifugal Cicatricial Alopecia (CCCA)
  • AKA hot comb alopecia
  • Chronic and progressive disease, with eventual burn out
  • Most common scarring alopecia in black patients
  • Majority are women
  • Premature desquamation of the inner root sheath
  • In addition to chronic use of hair cosmetic products (hot comb alopecia)
  • These two lead to inflammation, primarily lymphocytic, which leads to destruction of the hair follicle and subsequent scarring
  • Begins on the vertex or the crown area
  • Expands symmetrically and centrifugally
  • Symptoms may be minimal or absent
  • A few isolated hairs showing polytrichia may be present ("doll hair appearance")
  • Pustules and crusting can occur rarely
  • Pustules are most likely due to secondary bacterial infection, and a short course of antibiotics can eliminate these pustules
  • Folliculitis decalvans may be a variant of CCCA with prominent pustules and staph infection
  • Premature desquamation of the inner root sheath
  • Onion skin-like fibrosis of affected follicles
  • In pustular lesions ("folliculitis decalvans"): neutrophils

Standard Treatment:

  • Oral tetracyclines (doxy or mino) + Potent topical steroids is usually enough to halt progression of the disease
  • Prolonged treatment for years is usually needed

For "Folliculitis Decalvans":

  • 10-week regimen of oral rifampin and clindamycin (300 BID for both), followed by oral tetra + TCS

Other Options:

  • Nd:YAG laser
  • Photodynamic therapy
  • TNF-α inhibitors
Lichen Planopilaris and Frontal Fibrosing Alopecia
  • Most common cicatricial alopecia (MCQ)
  • F > M
  • Caucasians > others
  • Less than 30% of patients have LP elsewhere
  • Etiology unknown, but probably the same as lichen planus

Classic LPP:

  • Several scattered patches of alopecia with associated perifollicular erythema, follicular spines (hair casts), and scarring
  • Pruritus and tenderness are often present

Variants:

Frontal Fibrosing Alopecia (FFA):
  • Most patients are elderly women
  • Associated with leave-on cosmetics and sunscreens use
  • Progressive hair loss of the anterior hairlines and eyebrows
  • Lonely hair sign
Graham-Little Syndrome:
  • Scarring alopecia of the scalp
  • Non-scarring alopecia of the axilla and pubic area
  • Keratosis pilaris-like papules
  • Band-like infiltrate at the interface between follicular epithelium and the dermis
  • Vacuolar alterations, Colloid or Civatte bodies
  • Inflammation affects the upper portion of the follicle (infundibulum and isthmus) (MCQ)
  • Intralesional steroids
  • Hydroxychloroquine
  • Pioglitazone hydrochloride (a PPAR-γ agonist)
  • Cyclosporine, isotretinoin, MTX, MMF
  • Finasteride/dutasteride and Griseofulvin for FFA (MCQ)
Discoid Lupus Erythematosus
  • Can be the sole cutaneous manifestation lupus
  • 5-10% develop SLE in localized DLE, 15-28% in generalized DLE
  • F > M
  • Resembles DLE lesions elsewhere
  • Erythema, epidermal atrophy, follicular plugging, and alopecia
  • Central hypopigmentation and peripheral hyperpigmentation are commonly seen in dark-skinned individuals
  • Vacuolar interface alteration of the epidermis and follicular epithelium
  • The interface change in follicles is usually vacuolar rather than lichenoid
  • Periadnexal lymphocytic infiltrate
  • Increased dermal mucin
  • Granular deposits of IgG and C3 (less commonly IgM or IgA) at the dermal-epidermal junction
  • Hydroxychloroquine and steroids (topical, intralesional, and systemic)
  • MMF, retinoids, thalidomide, AZA, MTX, CSA, TNF-α all can be used, but anecdotal evidence
Other Scarring Alopecias

Demographics:

  • Young black males
  • 10 times more common in blacks than in whites

Pathogenesis:

  • "Mechanical folliculitis" from haircuts and clothing may play a role
  • Frequently associated with CCCA, suggesting a common or related pathogenesis
  • Bacterial role is minimal, as demonstrated by pathology

Clinical Features:

  • Begins as small, smooth, firm papules with a few pustules
  • Posterior neck and occipital scalp
  • Papules resolve with small areas of alopecia
  • Papules coalesce and form firm, hairless, keloid-like protuberant plaques

Pathology:

  • Non-specific folliculitis with scarring

Treatment:

  • Early treatment is important
  • Doxycycline + potent steroids is first line
  • Laser hair removal
  • Nd:YAG (MCQ)
  • Surgery

Background:

  • Part of the follicular occlusion tetrad
  • Young adult black men
  • Mainly a disease of follicular hyperkeratosis, but secondary infections can occur

Clinical Features:

  • Multiple firm nodules
  • Mid/posterior vertex and upper occiput
  • Nodules rapidly develop into interconnecting, boggy, fluctuant sinuses that produce pus
  • Foul smelling, occasionally painful

Trichoscopy:

  • Yellow structureless areas
  • Yellow dots
  • Dystrophic hair

Pathology:

  • Lymphocytes predominate initially but is then replaced by neutrophils
  • Vascular proliferation and granulation tissue
  • Sinus tracts

Treatment:

  • Isotretinoin, but relapses are common
  • TNF-α inhibitors
  • Intralesional corticosteroids, oral antibiotics
  • Oral zinc sulphate
  • Laser hair removal, photodynamic therapy, radiation therapy
  • Surgical approaches ranging from incision and drainage to excision with grafting

Brocq's Alopecia (Pseudopelade of Broca):

  • This term should be abandoned
  • It's a pattern of end-stage cicatricial alopecia
  • Can be due to DLE, LPP, CCCA, or other cicatricial alopecia

"Folliculitis Decalvans":

  • Highly inflammatory forms of cicatricial alopecia
  • Inflammatory, follicular papules and pustules dominate the clinical picture
  • Staphylococcus aureus can be grown from pustular or crusted lesions often, but not always

"Tufted Folliculitis":

  • Describes tufting of hair
  • It's a pattern of hair loss or a sign rather than a diagnosis
  • Common in CCCA, but can also be seen acne keloidalis, dissecting cellulitis, pemphigus, thermal burns, and inflammatory tinea capitis

Tractional Alopecia:

  • Biphasic alopecia, meaning that it starts as nonscarring, then turns to scarring with chronicity
  • African-American women
  • "Fringe sign"
Hair Shaft Abnormalities
  • Seen in women
  • Caused by traumatic hair care techniques involving heat
  • Localized areas of fragile uneven hair
  • The involved hair is straighter and stiffer than normal
  • By light microscopy, the hair shafts contain large, irregularly spaced "bubbles"
  • Trimming the damaged hair resolves the issue

Genetics:

  • Autosomal dominant
  • KRT86 (most often) and KRT81/KRT83
  • Autosomal recessive form → DSG4 (MCQ)

Clinical Features:

  • Normal appearing hair at birth
  • Short, fragile, brittle hair within a few months
  • "Beaded hair"
  • Perifollicular erythema and follicular hyperkeratosis are common
  • Nail involvement and other hairy areas
  • Cataract, abnormal teeth, and keratosis pilaris

Description:

  • Flattening of the shaft and twisting of the hair shaft on its own axis
  • Associated with ectodermal abnormalities (keratosis pilaris, nail dystrophies, dental abnormalities)
  • Hair sparse or abnormal since birth, or develop later in life
  • Light microscopic examination reveals grouped twists of flattened hair

Associations:

  • Associated with Björnstad syndrome (MCQ)
    • Autosomal recessive, BCS1L mutation
    • Pili torti + SNHL
  • Also associated with Menkes disease, Bazex-Dupre-Christol, and many others
  • Acquired twisting of the hair may be associated with retinoids and anorexia (MCQ)
  • AKA bamboo hair
  • Associated with Netherton syndrome
  • Intussusceptions of the distal shaft ("ball") into the proximal shaft ("socket")
  • The eyebrow hairs best location for testing (MCQ)
  • The most common hair shaft abnormality (MCQ)
  • Splayed paint brush appearance
  • Associated with argininosuccinic aciduria (MCQ)
    • ASL gene - defective ammonia clearance
    • Treated by arginine supplement + protein avoidance (MCQ)
  • Discussed earlier in details in Chapter 57
  • AR - NER pathway mutation
  • Alternating light and dark bands under polarizing light
  • Low sulfur content of hair
  • "PIBIDS"

Acquired Progressive Kinking of the Hair:

  • Retinoids and HIV (MCQ)

Loose Anagen Hair Syndrome:

  • KRT75 mutation plays a role (MCQ)
  • Anagen hair can be easily and painlessly pulled from the scalp
  • Microscopic examination reveals a ruffled proximal cuticle, absent root sheath, and bent matrix
  • Improves with aging

Pili Annulate:

  • Bright and dark bands under reflected light
  • Autosomal dominant (MCQ) do not confuse it with trichothiodystrophy (AR)
  • Light microscopy: air-filled cavities are seen as dark regions

Pili Bifurcate:

  • Bifurcation of the hair fiber at multiple irregular intervals along the shaft

Pili Multigemini:

  • Multiple hair shafts arising from one papilla

Uncombable Hair:

  • AKA Spun-glass hair
  • AKA pili trianguli et canaliculi
  • Hair is stiff and difficult to comb
  • Abnormal keratinization of the internal root sheath is postulated to be the cause
  • A triangular shape on cross-section and a longitudinal groove that is best seen by scanning electron microscopy
  • PADI3 mutation, autosomal recessive (MCQ)

Woolly Hair:

  • Associated with Naxos and Carvajal syndromes (discussed in PPK chapter)
  • Also associated with Noonan syndrome and cardio-facio-cutaneous syndrome
Clinical Assessment of Alopecia
The clinical history should be directed toward establishing the diagnosis, detecting precipitating factors where relevant, identifying associated comorbidities, and obtaining information pertinent to the proposed treatment.
  • Determine if patient has increased hair shedding (suggestive of telogen effluvium) or a reduction in hair density with visible balding or both; the latter is suggestive of patterned hair loss
  • If increased hair shedding, is the hair "coming out by the roots"?
    • (suggestive of telogen effluvium) vs breaking near the scalp? (suggestive of acute alopecia areata, anagen effluvium) vs breaking near the ends?
  • If increased hair shedding and hair "coming out by the roots":
    • Duration <3 months suggests acute telogen effluvium
    • >6 months is diagnostic of chronic telogen effluvium
    • Recurrent episodes of acute telogen effluvium without a clear trigger is a common prodrome to female pattern hair loss
  • In patients with acute telogen effluvium: search for triggers such as high fever, surgery, or serious illness that occurred 2-3 months prior to onset
  • In patients with chronic telogen effluvium: consider hypo- or hyperthyroidism, drugs, chronic illnesses, exposures (e.g. thallium), and rarely diffuse alopecia areata
  • If visible balding, determine if congenital or acquired and then if:
    • Patchy: (suggestive of alopecia areata, tinea capitis, lichen planus)
    • Diffuse: (suggestive of acute or postpartum telogen effluvium, anagen effluvium, diffuse alopecia areata)
    • Patterned: (suggestive of androgenetic alopecia, frontal fibrosing alopecia, central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia)
  • Determine if any associated symptoms such as pain, burning, pruritus, or dandruff as:
    • Trichodynia may accompany telogen effluvium or female pattern hair loss with excessive shedding
    • Localized pain can indicate pustules
    • Diffuse pain and pruritus may accompany a cicatricial alopecia
    • There may be altered or crawling sensations in patients with trichotillomania
    • Dandruff can be a feature of seborrheic dermatitis, psoriasis, or tinea capitis
  • A positive family history may point to common disorders such as alopecia areata or androgenetic alopecia or rare hair shaft abnormalities
  • Organ-specific autoimmune diseases in patients with alopecia areata
  • Hyperandrogenism (e.g. hirsutism, acne, irregular menses) and metabolic syndrome in women with androgenetic alopecia
  • Personal or family history of ischemic heart disease and prostate cancer in men with androgenetic alopecia
  • Review previous treatments including duration and if used correctly
  • Determine expectations (e.g. halting progression, preventing relapse, hair regrowth)
  • Determine commitment to repeated or long-term therapies

Bedside Diagnostic Tests

Gentle Hair Pull Test

"Tug" test

Hair not washed for 24 hours

Unit Area Trichogram

Plucking of hairs within defined area (e.g. 60 mm²)

Determine ratio of anagen:telogen hairs

Hair Card Test

Assessment for hair shaft fragility

Assessment of hair ends (e.g. broken vs tapered), diameters, and growth

Trichoscopy

Visualization of hair shafts, follicular openings, and interfollicular skin (10x)

Computerized Trichometric Analysis

In addition to trichoscopy (50x), measures and records density/cm² and average hair shaft diameters

Additional investigations include fungal culture, Wood's lamp examination, scalp biopsy, hair shaft analysis, and blood tests.

  • Shedding of the newborn (physiologic)

Idiopathic
  • Chronic telogen effluvium? (no attributable cause or illness)

  • Postfebrile (extremely high fevers, e.g. malaria)
  • Severe infection
  • Severe chronic illness (e.g. HIV disease, systemic lupus erythematosus)

  • Hypothyroidism and other endocrinopathies (e.g. hyperparathyroidism, hyperthyroidism)

  • Crash or liquid protein diets; starvation/malnutrition

  • Discontinuation of oral contraceptives
  • Retinoids (acitretin, isotretinoin) and vitamin A excess
  • Anticoagulants (especially heparin)
  • Antithyroid (propylthiouracil, methimazole)
  • Anticonvulsants (e.g. phenytoin, valproic acid, carbamazepine)
  • Heavy metals
  • β-blockers (e.g. propranolol)

Topical Immunotherapy for Alopecia Areata

Treatment Protocol for Diphencyprone (DPCP) or Squaric Acid Dibutyl Ester (SADBE)*

Week 1: Sensitize with a 2% solution in acetone applied to a 4x4 cm area on one side of the scalp
Week 2 (or 3 if severe reaction): Apply 0.001% solution to all affected areas on the same side of the scalp
Weekly thereafter: DPCP or SADBE is applied to the same side of the scalp
  • The concentration is titrated according to the severity of the reaction the previous week
  • Goal: maintaining a low-grade, tolerable degree of erythema, scaling, and pruritus for 24-36 hours after application
  • Concentrations are increased incrementally as follows: 0.001%, 0.01%, 0.025%, 0.05%, 0.1%, 0.25%, 0.5%, 1%, 2%
Once hair growth is established: The other side is also treated
Response timeline: Initial responses are usually seen after 12 weeks, and therapy is discontinued if there is no response by 24 weeks

Additional Information:

  • Acetone solutions should be applied using a generous amount of cotton at the end of a wooden stick†
  • Following application:
    • The sensitizer is left on the scalp for 48 hours, then washed off
    • Patients should avoid touching the scalp for 6 hours
    • The scalp should be protected from light to avoid degradation
  • SADBE must be refrigerated
  • Frequency of treatment can be decreased in the presence of full regrowth

Side Effects:

  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Severe or widespread eczematous reactions
  • Postinflammatory pigment alterations (particularly in patients with darkly pigmented skin)

Contraindications:

  • Pregnancy (although teratogenicity has not been established)
  • Malignancies and blood dyscrasias
*Both not FDA-approved. †Application by medical personnel is recommended.

Histologic Features of Various Forms of Alopecia
  • Normal total number of follicles and no significant inflammation
  • Increased number and percentage of vellus hairs
  • Numerous fibrous "streamers"
  • Slightly increased telogen count
  • Uninvolved scalp (e.g. occiput) appears normal
  • Normal total number of hairs
  • Normal number of terminal (large) hairs
  • Increase in the telogen count to >20% (>15% is suggestive); telogen count seldom exceeds 50%; >80% is inconsistent with telogen effluvium
  • Absence of inflammation and scarring
  • Follicles are normal size
  • Total number of hairs (both terminal and vellus) is normal
  • Incomplete, disrupted follicular anatomy
  • Increased number of terminal catagen and/or telogen hairs
  • Trichomalacia and pigment casts
  • No significant inflammation

Early, Progressive Disease ("Acute" and "Subacute"):

  • Normal total number of hairs
  • Peribulbar mononuclear cell infiltrate (with occasional eosinophils), predominantly affecting terminal anagen and catagen hair bulbs
  • Occasional exocytosis of inflammatory cells into bulbar epithelium
  • Degenerative changes of hair matrix
  • Increased number of terminal catagen and telogen hairs
  • Increased number of miniaturized hairs
  • Trichomalacia and marked narrowing of hair shafts

Longstanding and Stable Disease ("Chronic"):

  • Majority of hairs in catagen or telogen phases
  • Numerous miniaturized, "arrested", rapidly cycling hairs, referred to as nanogen hairs
  • Mild peribulbar mononuclear cell infiltrate around those nanogen hairs possessing anagen-like or catagen-like bulbs
  • Premature desquamation of the inner root sheath
  • Eccentric epithelial atrophy (thinning) with hair shafts in close proximity to the dermis
  • Concentric lamellar fibroplasia (onion skin-like fibrosis) of affected follicles
  • Variably dense lymphocytic perifollicular inflammation, primarily at the level of the upper isthmus and lower infundibulum
  • Occasional fusion of infundibula (polytrichia)
  • In advanced lesions, total destruction of the follicular epithelium with retained hair shaft fragments and granulomatous inflammation
  • In pustular lesions ("folliculitis decalvans"), intrafollicular and perifollicular infiltrates rich in neutrophils and lymphocytes
  • Band-like mononuclear cell infiltrate obscuring the interface between follicular epithelium and dermis; vacuolar alteration at the interface and hypergranulosis within affected infundibula is typical
  • Colloid or Civatte bodies are occasionally found as part of the interface alteration
  • Inflammation affects the upper portion of the follicle (infundibulum and isthmus) most severely
  • Occasionally, epidermal changes of lichen planus are found
  • Over time, perifollicular fibrosis becomes prominent and the lymphocytic infiltrate seems to "back away" from the follicle
  • An artifactual cleft between epithelium and stroma is often found, with epithelium "floating" within the cleft (Max-Joseph spaces)
  • Grouped globular immunofluorescence (usually IgM) adjacent to the follicular epithelium is the characteristic pattern
  • Vacuolar interface alteration of the epidermis and follicular epithelium; epidermis may be spared
  • The interface change in follicles is usually vacuolar rather than lichenoid (less inflammation)
  • Chronic inflammation (often including plasma cells) of the eccrine sweat glands and arrector pili is sometimes present
  • Increased dermal mucin is often present
  • Granular deposits of IgG and C3 (less commonly IgM or IgA) at the dermal-epidermal junction and/or the junction of the follicular epithelium and dermis are typical
  • Perifollicular, chronic inflammation (lymphocytic and plasmacytic), most intense at the level of the isthmus and lower infundibulum
  • Lamellar fibroplasia, most marked at the level of the isthmus
  • Loss of sebaceous glands
  • Total follicular destruction in advanced disease
  • Moderately dense, perifollicular lymphocytic inflammation affecting the lower half of the dermis and extending down into the subcutaneous fat
  • Numerous intact and seemingly undamaged follicles with intact sebaceous glands surrounded by an infiltrate of acute and chronic inflammation (early disease)
  • Increased percentage of catagen/telogen hairs
  • Initially, lymphocytes dominate the infiltrate, but eventually neutrophils become more numerous, especially in longstanding, fluctuant lesions
  • Vascular proliferation accompanies the infiltrate as the disease evolves, and the deep dermis and subcutaneous fat begin to exhibit the histologic findings of granulation tissue
  • Eventually, chronic abscesses become lined with squamous epithelium derived from the overlying epidermis, and true sinus tracts are formed
  • When inflammation subsides, there is dense fibrosis of the dermis and the superficial fat with loss of follicles

Postoperative (Pressure-induced) Alopecia:

  • Early stage includes vascular thrombosis and inflammation within the dermis and superficial fat
  • Necrosis of the epidermis and eccrine coils when severe
  • In conjunction with alopecia, synchronized conversion of most or all of the terminal hair follicles to the catagen/telogen phase (without hair miniaturization)
  • Pigment casts, incontinont pigment within collapsed root sheaths, and trichomalacia (but not distorted follicular anatomy)

Psoriasiform Alopecia:

  • Psoriasiform epidermal changes may or may not be present
  • Marked increase in percentage of catagen/telogen hairs, associated with hair miniaturization
  • Occasional miniaturized follicle showing peribulbar chronic inflammation; plasma cells rare or absent
  • Marked diminution in number of sebaceous glands

TNF-α Inhibitor-induced Psoriasiform Alopecia:

  • Psoriasiform dermatitis often present
  • Increase in catagen/telogen hairs and miniaturization of hairs, similar to alopecia areata
  • Some peribulbar and superficial perivascular inflammation present, often with several eosinophils and plasma cells in the infiltrate

"End-stage" Cicatricial Alopecia:

  • A decreased total number of hairs, especially terminal hairs
  • Loss of the sebaceous glands in zones of follicular loss
  • Residual, "naked" hair shafts surrounded by mild, granulomatous inflammation
  • Follicular streamers (stelae) without overlying follicles
  • Cylindrical columns of connective tissue representing the sites of former follicles

Traction Alopecia (End-stage):

  • The total number of terminal hairs is markedly reduced
  • Distinct columns of connective tissue replace some follicles, leaving obvious "blank spaces"
  • The sebaceous glands associated with the remaining hair follicles are still intact and sebaceous glands often persist in follicular units that seem to have lost all of their hair-producing epithelium
  • No significant perifollicular inflammation
  • Rarely, retained "naked" hair shafts are seen in otherwise typical cases of end-stage traction alopecia; the significance of the finding is unclear
Trichomalacia is characterized by pleated or twisted hair shafts within follicular infundibula.

Trichoscopy Findings
  • Anisotrichosis:A diversity of hair shaft diameters affects >20% of the hairs
  • Brown halos around hair shafts (peripilar sign) is a subtle clue
  • The hair shafts are of equal diameter (unlike AGA)
    1. Exclamation Point Hairs
      • Appearance: Broken short hairs, thinner at the base (proximal) and thicker at the tip (distal)
      • Significance: Pathognomonic. Indicates acute arrest of mitosis in hair matrix → weakened shaft → fracture
      • Histology: Marked narrowing of hair shaft
    2. Black Dots
      • Appearance: Dark, broken hair remnants within follicular openings
      • Significance: Represent exclamation point hairs broken at scalp level → sign of high disease activity
      • Differential: Trichotillomania, tinea capitis, anagen effluvium
    3. Tapered Hairs
      • Appearance: Long exclamation point hairs, not yet broken
      • Significance: Indicates moderate disease activity
    4. Broken Hairs
      • Appearance: Fractured shafts at varying lengths
      • Significance: Non-specific, but suggests fragility and active disease when seen with AA
      • AAPS: Negative prognostic marker
      5. II. Signs of Chronic / Long-Standing Disease
    5. Yellow Dots
      • Appearance: Small, round, yellow-orange dots across alopecic patches (subtle in children)
      • Significance: Empty follicles filled with keratin/sebum → chronic/inactive AA
      • Histology: Dilated follicular infundibula
      • Differential: Also seen in androgenetic alopecia
      6. III. Signs of Hair Regrowth (Positive Prognostic)
    6. Upright Regrowing Hairs
      • Appearance: Short, non-tapered, straight-growing hairs
      • Significance: Most reliable indicator of healthy regrowth
    7. Pigtail (Circle) Hairs
      • Appearance: Short, fine, curled regrowing hairs on scalp surface
      • Significance: Definite sign of regrowth → recovery phase
  • Broken Hairs of Varying Length
  • Flame Hairs: Semi-transparent, wavy, and cone-shaped hair residues from fractured shafts
  • V-Sign: A classic finding where two hairs, emerging from the same follicular unit, are broken off at the same level
  • Coiled Hairs: Broken hairs that recoil and bunch up into a small spiral on the scalp surface
  • Tulip Hairs: Short hairs with dark, frayed distal ends that resemble a closed tulip flower
  • Hook Hairs: Hairs that are bent or hooked near the scalp surface
  • Trichoptilosis: The medical term for split ends, visible on trichoscopy
  • Hair Powder: A subtle finding of small, whitish, keratinous debris from fractured hairs
  • Comma hairs are a clue to the diagnosis
  • Zigzag hairs can also be seen
  • Vellus-like, depigmented hair shafts
  • No yellow dots or dystrophic hairs as are seen in AA
  • Follicular red dots are a characteristic sign

I. Features of Lupus-Related Alopecia (Active Phase)

  • Thick Arborizing Vessels
    • Appearance: Prominent, tortuous, tree-like branching vessels (interfollicular)
    • Significance: Diagnostic clue for lupus alopecia (DLE & SLE); reflects vascular inflammation and dilatation
  • Large Yellow Dots
    • Appearance: Enlarged yellow follicular dots
    • Significance: Dilated follicles plugged with keratin/scale (follicular plugs)
  • Fine Scaling
    • Appearance: Delicate surface scale
    • Significance: Non-specific indicator of inflammation/abnormal keratinization
  • Scattered Dark-Brown to Bluish Discoloration
    • Appearance: Patchy pigment changes (brown/blue)
    • Significance: Seen in darker phototypes; due to pigment incontinence from interface dermatitis

II. Features of Late-Stage / Chronic Cicatricial Alopecia

  • Milky-Red or White Areas with No Follicular Openings
    • Appearance: Smooth, structureless white (fibrosis) or reddish areas, lacking follicular ostia
    • Significance: Indicates scarring and irreversible follicular destruction

    I. Features of Active LPP

    • Perifollicular Scaling
      • Appearance: Tubular scales forming a sleeve around the hair shaft, extending 2–3 mm above scalp.
      • Significance: Most specific sign of active LPP; reflects infundibulo-isthmic inflammation and hyperkeratosis.
    • Perifollicular Erythema
      • Appearance: Redness centered around follicular openings.
      • Significance: Direct indicator of lymphocytic inflammation targeting hair follicles.
    • Hair Casts
      • Appearance: Movable, whitish, cylindrical sheaths around hair shafts.
      • Significance: Sign of abnormal keratinization; overlaps with perifollicular scaling.
    • Elongated Linear Blood Vessels
      • Appearance: Thin, stretched-out vessels.
      • Significance: Characteristic vascular pattern of LPP; helps differentiate from other alopecias.

    II. Features of Late-Stage (Scarring) LPP

    • Milky-Red Areas with No Follicular Openings
      • Appearance: Smooth, shiny white-to-pink patches lacking follicular dots.
      • Significance: End-stage fibrosis → irreversible hair loss and scarring.
    • "Lonely Hairs"
      • Appearance: Isolated surviving hairs in areas of scarring.
      • Significance: Classic sign of burnt-out LPP and FFA; indicates patchy progression and permanent follicular damage.

Structural Hair Shaft Abnormalities:

  • Bubble Hair
    • Cause: Heat on wet hair
    • Defect: Gas bubbles within shaft → fracture
    • Type: Acquired
  • Monilethrix ("Beaded Hair")
    • Appearance: Regular elliptical beads with constrictions
    • Defect: Fractures at internodes
    • Type: Inherited
  • Pili Torti
    • Appearance: Flattened, twisted hairs (180°)
    • Effect: Spangled sheen
    • Type: Can be inherited or acquired
  • Trichorrhexis Invaginata ("Bamboo Hair")
    • Appearance: Ball-and-socket telescoping shaft
    • Association: Pathognomonic for Netherton syndrome
  • Trichorrhexis Nodosa
    • Appearance: Frayed, brush-like broken ends
    • Cause: Trauma
    • Note: Most common hair shaft defect
  • Trichoschisis
    • Appearance: Clean transverse shaft fracture
    • Association: Trichothiodystrophy (sulfur-deficient hair)
  • Trichothiodystrophy
    • Appearance: Brittle hair with "tiger tail" under polarized light
    • Defect: Sulfur deficiency
    • Type: Inherited disorder
  • Pili Annulati ("Ringed Hair")
    • Appearance: Alternating light/dark bands
    • Cause: Air-filled cortex cavities
  • Pili Bifurcati
    • Appearance: Single hair bifurcates and re-fuses along shaft
    • Rarity: Very rare
  • Pili Multigemini
    • Appearance: Multiple hair shafts from one papilla
    • Structure: Shared outer root sheath
Microscopic Features of Anagen and Telogen Hairs

Telogen Hair:

  • Telogen shaft showing a club-shaped bulb

Anagen Hair:

  • Anagen shaft with attached root sheaths
  • Demonstrating a pigmented and distorted bulb
  • Components: M (matrix), I (inner root sheath), O (outer root sheath)

Loose Anagen Hair:

  • Ruffled cuticle of loose anagen hair
Quick Reference Summary

High-Yield MCQ Facts:

  • Grading: Hamilton-Norwood scale (male), Ludwing/Sinclair scales (female)
  • Trichoscopy AGA: >20% diversity of hair thickness
  • Ferritin level: should be above 40 ng/dL
  • Alopecia areata: Yellow dot most common trichoscopy finding
  • Eosinophils: may be seen in alopecia areata
  • LPP inflammation: affects upper portion of follicle (infundibulum and isthmus)
  • Most common cicatricial alopecia: LPP
  • Trichotillomania: N-acetylcysteine supplement can be used
  • Congenital atrichia: mutation in hairless gene + Vitamin D-dependent rickets
  • Hair shaft abnormalities genetics: Multiple specific genes mentioned
  • Scalp hair growth: Higher anagen %, lower telogen % vs other body areas

Treatment Pearls:

  • Finasteride/dutasteride and Griseofulvin for FFA
  • Nd:YAG laser for acne keloidalis
  • Most drugs causing hair loss → TE (except chemotherapy → AE)
  • Clomipramine first line for trichotillomania
  • CCCA: Oral tetracyclines + potent topical steroids

Exam Strategy Tips:

  • Focus on distinguishing features between different alopecias
  • Remember genetic patterns and specific mutations
  • Know trichoscopy findings for each condition
  • Understand pathophysiology of androgenic alopecia
  • Master the classification of cicatricial alopecias
  • Remember treatment protocols and first-line therapies
