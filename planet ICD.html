Understanding Plant Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD)

Plant-Induced Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD) is a non-immunologic inflammatory skin reaction. It results from direct physical or chemical damage, unlike allergic contact dermatitis (ACD) which requires prior immune sensitization. Anyone can be affected with sufficient exposure.

This page structures the information based on cognitive principles to aid memory, dividing ICD into its two main forms:

  • Mechanical ICD: Physical injury from plant structures.
  • Chemical ICD: Damage from plant-derived chemicals.

I. Mechanical Irritant Dermatitis

Mechanism: Direct physical trauma to the skin from specialized plant structures. Severity often increases with smaller structures (e.g., microscopic glochids).

Causative Structures & Examples:

Large Spines/Thorns
Modified leaves/branches (e.g., Rosa spp. - roses, Kali tragus - tumbleweed). Risk of penetration, secondary infections.
Glochids
Microscopic, barbed hairs in cacti areoles (esp. Opuntia spp. - prickly pears). Embed easily, cause significant itch/irritation (Sabra Dermatitis). Common houseplant source: Opuntia microdasys ("bunny ears").
Sharp/Stiff Hairs (Trichomes)
Found on leaves/stems (e.g., Borago officinalis - borage, Hedera helix - ivy [stellate], Verbascum thapsus - mullein [woolly], Bougainvillea [crystal-containing]).
Abrasive Bristles
On leaves/fruit (e.g., Ficus spp. - figs, Morus spp. - mulberries).
Coarse Fibers
Bulb tunics (e.g., Tulipa spp. - tulips, contributing to "tulip fingers").
Other Structures
Sharp leaf points (Grevillea spp.), hooked prickles (Galium aparine - goosegrass), cutting leaf edges (Poaceae - grasses).

Clinical Features:

  • Glochid Dermatitis ("Sabra Dermatitis"): Pruritic papules (itchy bumps), resembling fiberglass dermatitis or scabies. Can be airborne during harvesting.
  • Penetrating Injuries: Risk of secondary infections: Bacteria (Clostridium tetani, Staphylococcus aureus), Fungi (Sporothrix schenckii - roses, grass, moss), Atypical Mycobacteria (M. kansasii - blackberries, M. marinum - cactus, M. ulcerans - tropical vegetation).

Treatment (Glochids):

  1. Remove visible clumps with tweezers.
  2. Apply glue (e.g., wood glue) over the area, cover with gauze.
  3. Allow glue to dry completely.
  4. Peel off gauze (removes ~95% of embedded glochids).

II. Chemical Irritant Dermatitis

Mechanism: Skin inflammation from plant chemicals directly damaging skin cells or triggering non-immune inflammatory pathways. Severity depends on chemical, concentration, duration, and skin condition.

Key Chemicals, Mechanisms & Examples:

Calcium Oxalate (Raphides)
Very Common. Insoluble crystals released with moisture. Found in Araceae (Dieffenbachia - dumb cane, Philodendron), Amaryllidaceae (Narcissus - daffodils), Liliaceae (Hyacinthus - hyacinth), Bromeliaceae (Ananas - pineapple), Rheum (rhubarb leaves), Agavaceae (Agave). Causes direct irritation and micro-abrasions, enhancing other irritants (like bromelain). Responsible for "Daffodil Itch". Chewing Dieffenbachia causes severe oral/mucosal edema, pain, potential aphonia.
Proteolytic Enzymes (e.g., Bromelain)
Found in Ananas comosus (pineapple). Causes skin damage (cracks, fissures, fingerprint loss, microhemorrhages), especially aided by calcium oxalate micro-abrasions.
Thiocyanates
Found in Alliaceae (Allium sativum - garlic) and Brassicaceae (Brassica nigra - black mustard, Raphanus - radish). Garlic can cause severe irritation, burns, subungual hyperkeratosis/hemorrhage (mimicking psoriasis).
Phorbol Esters (Diterpenes)
Found in milky latex of Euphorbiaceae (spurge family), including Euphorbia spp., Codiaeum variegatum (croton), Hippomane mancinella (manchineel tree). Cause painful, blistering dermatitis; eye contact -> temporary blindness. Manchineel is extremely potent (severe burns even from rain transfer under tree). Euphorbia pulcherrima (poinsettia) is only mildly irritating.
Protoanemonin
From ranunculin upon plant injury in Ranunculaceae family (Ranunculus - buttercups, Aquilegia - columbine). Causes severe, often linear vesiculation resembling early phytophotodermatitis but with NO subsequent hyperpigmentation. Only fresh, damaged plants are reactive (polymerizes quickly).
Capsaicin
Active principle in Capsicum annuum (hot peppers, Solanaceae). Acts on nerve endings (TRPV1 receptors) -> vasodilation, sensory nerve activation. Results in erythema, edema, intense burning without vesicle/bulla formation (no true epidermal damage). Symptoms can be delayed. Fat-soluble.
Saponins
Found in Agave americana (century plant) along with calcium oxalate, contributing to pruritus/stinging. Can cause purpura.
Urushiol (in Black-Spot Reaction)
In Toxicodendron spp. (poison ivy/oak), oxidized resin acts as both an irritant (ICD) and allergen (ACD), causing black staining and combined dermatitis.
Cashew Nut Shell Oil
From Anacardium occidentale. Concentrated phenols cause immediate vesication.

Clinical Features Examples:

  • "Daffodil Itch": Dryness, scaling, fissures, erythema on fingers/hands/forearms (Narcissus - Calcium oxalate). Common in florists.
  • Bulb Dermatitis: Fingertip/periungual dermatitis (Tulipa - fibers/oxalate, Hyacinthus - oxalate). Note: Tulip allergy (ACD) also common.
  • Pineapple Worker Hands: Cracks, fissures, fingerprint loss, microhemorrhages (Bromelain + Calcium oxalate).
  • Garlic Dermatitis: Fingertip (often non-dominant hand), hyperkeratosis, fissuring, potential burns, subungual changes.
  • "Chili Burns": Erythema, edema, intense burning (Capsicum - Capsaicin). No blisters.
  • Linear Vesiculation (No Pigment): Characteristic of Protoanemonin (fresh Ranunculus).
  • Severe Blistering/Bullae: Phorbol esters (Euphorbiaceae - esp. Manchineel).
  • Oral Toxicity: Severe edema/pain from chewing Dieffenbachia.

Treatment Notes:

  • General: Avoidance, prompt washing, cool compresses, emollients, topical anti-itch (pramoxine), analgesics.
  • Dieffenbachia (Oral): Parenteral corticosteroids, antacid mouthwashes, analgesics. (Antihistamines NOT helpful).
  • Capsaicin: Wash soap/water, then immerse in vegetable oil (~1 hour) to dissolve.
  • Pineapple Workers: Nitrile gloves are protective.
  • Garlic/Onion Handlers: Disposable gloves (vinyl, latex, nitrile) are NOT protective; reusable domestic rubber gloves ARE protective.

Key Takeaways for Board Exam Memorization

Focus on these core concepts and associations, structured for recall:

  • Primary Distinction: Mechanical (physical injury) vs. Chemical (toxin damage). Both are non-immunologic ICD.
  • Key Mechanical Agents:
    • Glochids: Opuntia (Prickly Pear) -> Sabra Dermatitis (papular, itchy).
    • Thorns: Rosa -> Penetrating injury + infection risk.
    • Coarse Fibers: Tulipa bulbs -> Part of "Tulip Fingers".
    • Hairs (Trichomes): Borago, Hedera.
  • Key Chemical Agents & Associated Plants:
    • Calcium Oxalate: Dieffenbachia (Dumb Cane - oral toxicity), Narcissus (Daffodil Itch), Philodendron, Ananas (Pineapple), Hyacinthus, Agave.
    • Phorbol Esters: Euphorbiaceae (Euphorbia, Hippomane [Manchineel - severe blisters], Codiaeum [Croton]).
    • Protoanemonin: Ranunculaceae (Ranunculus [Buttercup] - linear vesicles, NO pigment, fresh plant only).
    • Capsaicin: Capsicum (Hot Peppers) -> Burning/Erythema ONLY (nerve action), NO blisters.
    • Thiocyanates: Allium sativum (Garlic - burns, fingertip dermatitis), Brassica nigra (Mustard).
    • Bromelain: Ananas (Pineapple - enzyme + oxalate damage).
    • Saponins: Agave (Itch/Sting, Purpura).
  • Classic Presentations to Recognize:
    • Sabra Dermatitis: Itchy papules (Opuntia glochids).
    • Daffodil Itch: Dry, fissured fingers (Narcissus oxalate).
    • Dumb Cane Reaction: Oral edema/pain if chewed (Dieffenbachia oxalate).
    • Buttercup Reaction: Linear vesicles, NO hyperpigmentation (Ranunculus protoanemonin).
    • Chili Burns: Intense burning/redness, NO blisters (Capsicum capsaicin).
    • Manchineel Reaction: Severe blistering (Hippomane phorbol esters).
    • Garlic Fingers: Irritation/burns on fingertips (Allium thiocyanates).
  • Treatment Pearls:
    • Glochids: Glue + Gauze technique.
    • Capsaicin: Vegetable Oil immersion.
    • Pineapple: Nitrile gloves.
    • Garlic/Onion: Domestic rubber gloves (most disposables ineffective).
    • Dieffenbachia Oral: Steroids, Analgesics (Antihistamines ineffective).
  • Secondary Infection Risk: Primarily with Mechanical penetrating injuries (Bacteria, Sporothrix, Atypical Mycobacteria).

Potential Areas of Confusion / Exam Pitfalls

Actively differentiate these concepts to avoid common errors:

  • ICD vs. ACD (Allergic Contact Dermatitis):
    • ICD: Non-immune, affects anyone, rapid onset possible, often burns > itches.
    • ACD: Immune (Type IV), requires prior sensitization, delayed onset (usually), typically very itchy, often eczematous/vesicular.
    • Overlap Alert: Some plants cause BOTH (e.g., Garlic - thiocyanates are irritant, diallyl disulfide is allergen; Poison Ivy Black Spot - urushiol is irritant + allergen; Tulips - fibers/oxalate are irritant, tulipalin A is allergen). Be ready for questions asking for *all* possible mechanisms.
  • ICD vs. Phytophotodermatitis (PPD):
    • PPD: Requires UV light exposure, typically leaves significant hyperpigmentation.
    • ICD (Chemical): Does NOT require UV light. Protoanemonin (Ranunculus) causes linear blisters but NO hyperpigmentation. Capsaicin causes erythema/burning but NO blisters.
  • ICD vs. Toxin-Mediated Contact Urticaria:
    • Contact Urticaria (e.g., Stinging Nettle - Urtica dioica): Injection of pre-formed chemicals (histamine, acetylcholine) via specialized hairs -> Immediate wheals (hives) and intense itch/burn.
    • ICD: Broader mechanism (direct chemical/physical damage), variable presentation/timing (not typically immediate wheals). Nettles = Urticaria.
  • Specific Chemical Actions (Trick Potential):
    • Capsaicin: Acts on nerves -> neurogenic inflammation (burning/redness). Does NOT cause direct epidermal damage/blistering.
    • Protoanemonin: Only active when freshly released from damaged plants (e.g., crushed buttercups). Dried plants are non-irritant.
  • Primary vs. Secondary Issues:
    • A festering rose thorn wound is primarily mechanical trauma + secondary infection, NOT primary chemical ICD.
  • Occupational Context: Expect scenarios involving Florists (Daffodil Itch), Cooks (Garlic Dermatitis), Agricultural Workers (Pineapple Hands, Glochids).

Focus Strategy: For each case, identify: Plant -> Chemical/Structure -> Mechanism (Mech/Chem, Nerve/Epidermis) -> Clinical Pattern (Papules, Fissures, Blisters, Erythema only?) -> Differentiating Features (Light? Pigment? Wheals?).
