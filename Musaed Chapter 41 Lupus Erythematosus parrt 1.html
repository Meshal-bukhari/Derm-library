Lupus Erythematosus

A multisystem disorder with frequent cutaneous involvement.

Introduction & High-Yield Epidemiology

Strongest Risk Factor
Gender MCQ
SLE Gender Ratio (F:M)
At least 9:1
Cutaneous LE Ratio (F:M)
Closer to 3:1
Ethnicity Risk (Black:Caucasian)
4:1

80% of patients with Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) will have cutaneous involvement.

Bad Prognostic Factor: Black race is associated with earlier age of onset and a higher mortality rate.

Mortality Causes:

  • First 5 years: Infection of cutaneous lesions
  • Thereafter: Thrombosis

Pathogenesis: Genes, Triggers, & Immunity

Central Pathway: Genetically predisposed individuals + Environmental Trigger → Defective clearance of apoptotic bodies → Loss of immune tolerance → Immune-mediated response.

Genetic Factors

HLA Subtypes (High-Yield Associations)

  • SCLE: HLA-B8 and -DR3 MCQ
  • ACLE: HLA-DR2
  • Drug-induced SLE (Hydralazine): HLA-DR4 MCQ

Complement Deficiencies

  • Associated with C1, C2, C4 deficiencies.
  • Highest Risk: C1q deficiency. ~90% of patients develop SLE. MCQ
  • Most Common Deficiency Overall: C2 deficiency. However, risk is lower (10-20% develop SLE) compared to C1/C4. MCQ
  • Screening Test: CH50 MCQ

Other Genes

  • TREX1 (DNA exonuclease): Associated with familial chilblain lupus.
  • TYK2, IRF5, CTLA4 also confe an increased risk for developing DLE and SCLE
Environmental Triggers
  • UV Radiation: UVB > UVA
  • Smoking: Also decreases antimalarial efficacy.
  • Viral Infections
  • Drugs:

    Classic Drug-Induced SLE (anti-histone): SHIPP

    • Sulfasalazine
    • Hydralazine
    • Isoniazid
    • Procainamide / Phenytoin
    • Penicillamine
    Dermatologic Triggers:
    • Minocycline: Causes P-ANCA positive DILE.
    • TNF-α inhibitors: Cause anti-dsDNA positive DILE (Infliximab 48%, Etanercept/Adalimumab 20%).

    Others: OCPs, chlorpromazine, methyldopa, and hydrochlorothiazide (MC overall).

Immune Dysfunction
  • Increased type I interferons (IFNs)
  • Defective immune complex clearance
  • Activation of B- and T-lymphocytes

Classification of Lupus Erythematosus

This classification is based on the presence of lupus features on histopathology. "Lupus specific" findings have an interface dermatitis.

Lupus Specific Findings

  • Acute Cutaneous LE (ACLE)
    • Localized ACLE (malar rash)
    • Generalized ACLE (morbilliform rash)
    • TEN-like ACLE
  • Subacute Cutaneous LE (SCLE)
    • Annular SCLE
    • Psoriasiform/papulosquamous SCLE
    • Drug-induced SCLE
    • Neonatal LE
  • Chronic Cutaneous LE (CCLE)
    • Discoid LE (DLE) - Localized, Generalized, Hypertrophic/verrucous, Mucosal
    • Chilblain LE
    • Lupus panniculitis/profundus
    • Tumid lupus
  • Other Specific Forms
    • Rowell's syndrome
    • LE/LP overlap

Lupus Non-Specific Findings

These findings (e.g., vasculitis, periungual telangiectasias) do not have lupus-specific histology. See table at end of chapter.

Chronic Cutaneous LE (CCLE)

Discoid Lupus Erythematosus (DLE)

Accounts for the majority of CCLE cases.

Key Associations & Risks:

  • Risk of progressing to SLE:
    • Localized DLE: 5-10%
    • Generalized DLE: 15-28% MCQ (20%)
    • Overall: 10-20% will develop SLE within 5 years MCQ
  • 40-70% of SLE patients will have discoid lesions.
  • Childhood DLE is associated with increased risk of developing SLE. MCQ
  • More common in African descent.
  • Increased risk of: Photosensitivity and leukopenia. MCQ
  • Decreased risk of: Serositis and arthritis. MCQ
  • Drug-Induced DLE: "3 Hyphens" -> 5-FU, IFN-α, TNF-α inhibitors.

Clinical Features:

  • Well-defined erythematous scaly papules and plaques with follicular plugging.
  • "Carpet tack" sign: Removing adherent scale reveals keratotic spikes on the undersurface.
  • Heals with peripheral hyperpigmentation, central scarring, atrophy, and hypopigmentation.
  • Common sites: Face, scalp, and external ear (characteristic).
  • Can cause irreversible scarring alopecia (1/3 of patients).
  • Can show Koebner phenomenon.
  • Chronic inflammation and scarring → increased risk of SCC.
  • Mucosal involvement (25%): Buccal mucosa most common. Lip involvement → SCC risk. Nasal involvement → higher SLE risk.

Pathology (H&E):

  • Compact orthohyperkeratosis, epidermal atrophy, follicular plugging.
  • Vacuolar interface dermatitis with necrotic keratinocytes.
  • BMZ thickening.
  • Superficial and deep perivascular and periadnexal (isthmus level) lymphohistiocytic infiltrate. MCQ
  • Mucin deposition.
  • Hypertrophic DLE: Shows pseudoepitheliomatous hyperplasia.

Pathology (DIF):

Positive in 75%. Ideal to biopsy a lesion present for a few months. MCQ

Chilblain LE

Key Points:

  • Familial cases associated with TREX1 and SAMHD1 mutations.
  • Least photosensitive form of cutaneous LE. MCQ
  • Aicardi-Goutières syndrome: sterile fever, SLE, chilblains, progressive neurologic disease.

Clinical Features:

  • Tender, red/dusky papules and plaques on cold-exposed acral areas.
  • Often persist year-round (vs. idiopathic pernio which is seasonal).

Pathology:

  • Papillary edema. MCQ
  • Superficial and deep lymphocytic vascular reaction.

Treatment:

  • Does NOT respond well to antimalarials.
  • Nifedipine + cold avoidance is first-line. MCQ
Tumid Lupus

Key Points:

  • Most photosensitive form of cutaneous LE. MCQ
  • 0% risk of developing SLE.
  • On a spectrum with Jessner's lymphocytic infiltrate and reticular erythematous mucinosis.

Clinical Features:

  • Edematous, indurated, smooth erythematous plaques with no epidermal involvement.
  • Often annular or arcuate. Located on face and trunk.
  • Heals without scarring.

Pathology:

  • Marked mucin deposition in the dermis.
  • Perivascular and periadnexal lymphocytic infiltrate.
  • Epidermis and DEJ are spared.
Lupus Erythematosus Panniculitis / Profundus

Panniculitis: Involves subcutaneous fat only.
Profundus: Involves overlying dermis/epidermis (often DLE lesions).

Key Points:

  • 5-10% risk of SLE.
  • Must differentiate from Subcutaneous Panniculitis-like T-cell Lymphoma (SPTCL).

Clinical Features:

  • Indurated, non-tender, subcutaneous nodules/plaques that heal with lipoatrophy.
  • Involves fatty areas of the upper part of the body (proximal extremities, trunk, head, neck).

Pathology:

  • Lobular panniculitis.
  • Hyaline necrosis of fat lobules. MCQ
  • Nodular lymphocytic aggregates or lymphoid follicles.
  • Mucin deposition.
  • Dystrophic calcifications may be seen. MCQ
LE Panniculitis vs. SPTCL: LE Panniculitis has plasma cells, lymphoid follicles, and hyaline fat necrosis. SPTCL shows atypical lymphocytes rimming adipocytes and may have "bean bag" cells (cytophagia).
Discoid Lupus-Lichen Planus Overlap

Coexisting DLE and LP with overlap lesions. Palmoplantar involvement is characteristic.

Subacute Cutaneous LE (SCLE)

SCLE: Key Facts & Features

Key Associations:

  • More common in Caucasians.
  • Strongly a/w HLA-B8 and HLA-DR3. MCQ
  • Strongly a/w early complement pathway deficiencies.
  • Antibodies: Anti-Ro/SSA (75-90%), Anti-La/SSB (30-50%).
  • Drug-induced: Anti-histone.
  • Increased risk of: Photosensitivity and arthritis. MCQ
  • Decreased risk of: Nephritis. MCQ
The Rule of 50s for SCLE:
~50% have positive ANA MCQ
~50% will develop SLE MCQ
~50% have positive DIF
~50% have photosensitivity

Drug-Induced SCLE

Common Causes: THANG

  • Terbinafine
  • Hydrochlorothiazide (most common)
  • ACEI
  • NSAIDS
  • Griseofulvin MCQ
  • Also: TNF-α inhibitors (esp. etanercept), PPIs

Clinical Features:

  • Annular or Psoriasiform/papulosquamous plaques.
  • Sun-exposed areas (V of neck, shoulders, trunk, lateral face).
  • Spares the central face (vs. ACLE).
  • Non-scarring, but may cause dyspigmentation.
  • Psoriasiform variant has a higher risk of developing SLE. MCQ
  • Most common systemic finding is arthritis (in both idiopathic and drug-induced forms). MCQ
  • Risk factors for the development of SLE:
    • Psoriasiform variant
    • Leukopenia
    • High ANA titer
    • Presence of localized ACLE
    • Anti-dsDNA

Pathology:

  • Hyperkeratosis + parakeratosis, epidermal atrophy. MCQ
  • Vacuolar interface dermatitis.
  • Dermal edema & mucin.
  • DIF: Granular deposition in basal keratinocytes (dust-like), indicates binding of anti-Ro/La. MCQ

Neonatal LE (NLE)

NLE: Maternal Antibodies & Infant Risk

Results from transplacental passage of maternal anti-Ro/SSA antibodies.

Key Points & Risks:

  • Infants have an increased risk of developing SLE later in life. MCQ
  • Risk of child having NLE:
    • Mother w/ Abx but no CTD: 1-2%
    • Mother w/ Abx and CTD: 15%
    • Mother w/ 1 previous child w/ NLE: 25% MCQ
    • Mother w/ 2 previous children w/ NLE: 75%
  • 50% of mothers are asymptomatic at time of diagnosis.
  • U1RNP antibodies: Associated with CNS manifestations but protective against cardiac manifestations (good prognosis). the function of U1RNP is to splice pre-mRNA into mRNA (MCQ) MCQ

Clinical Manifestations

Cutaneous Features
  • Annular erythematous scaly plaques.
  • Typically face and periorbital area: "Racoon eyes". MCQ
  • Resolve without scarring by 6 months (as maternal antibodies disappear).
Cardiac Features (CRITICAL)
  • 30% develop congenital third-degree heart block. MCQ
  • Cardiac block develops in utero, typically between 18-20 weeks gestation. MCQ
  • Cardiac manifestations are non-reversible. MCQ
  • Once COMPLETE heart block occurs, it is irreversible. MCQ
  • 30% mortality for infants with cardiac involvement. MCQ
  • 2/3 will require a pacemaker. MCQ
  • Mothers with Sjogren or undifferentiated autoimmune syndrome: higher risk of developing complete heart block compared to mothers with SLE.
Other Systemic Features
  • Hematological: Thrombocytopenia (MCQ), hemolytic anemia, neutropenia. Resolve by 2-6 months.
  • Liver: Hepatomegaly with elevated enzymes. Usually resolves.
  • CNS: Hydrocephalus, macrocephaly (a/w U1RNP).

Workup

  • Labs:
    • CBC and LFTs
    • Mother and baby serologies (including U1RNP)
  • Imaging:
    • U/S CTG of fetal heart rate from 16-24 weeks of pregnancy
    • ECG and cardiac monitor after birth
    • Echo
    • Cerebral U/S
  • Pathology: Same as SCLE

Treatment

  • Skin disease: TCS and sun-protection
  • Hematological and liver: no need
  • Cardiac disease:
    • Prenatal systemic steroids -> ↓risk of cardiac but no effect on skin
    • Hydroxychloroquine throughout pregnancy -> ↓risk of cardiac, indicated for women with a previous child with NLE
    • Once COMPLETE cardiac block occurs -> irreversible MCQ
    • Low-grade AV blocks are reversible
    • 2/3 of patients with cardiac disease will require pacemaker MCQ
