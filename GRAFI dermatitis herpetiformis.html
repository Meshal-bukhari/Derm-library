🔬 Dermatitis Herpetiformis (DH)

Core Facts:
  • 90% are HLA-DQ2 positive (others DQ8)
  • 90% have gluten-sensitive enteropathy on biopsy
  • 20% develop symptomatic malabsorption
  • 50% have thyroid disease (especially Hashimoto)

🧬 Pathogenesis Cascade

Step 1: Gliadin (alcohol-soluble fraction of gluten) enters small bowel
Step 2: Deamidation occurs in small bowel
Step 3: Th1 + NK cell activation
Step 4: Crypt hyperplasia + villous atrophy + B-cells produce IgA anti-TG2
Step 5: IgA anti-TG3 forms via epitope spreading
Step 6: IgA/TG3 immune complexes deposit in papillary dermis
Key Antibodies:
  • IgA anti-TG2: Correlates with enteropathy degree (pathogenic marker for celiac)
  • IgA anti-TG3: Specific for DH, useful for monitoring gluten-free diet response
  • ⚠️ IgA anti-TG2 antibodies are NOT responsible for skin IgA deposition!
Triggers: Iodide (both systemic and local) can precipitate DH flares!

Associated Autoimmune Conditions

Common: Hashimoto thyroiditis, Type 1 DM
Uncommon: Pernicious anemia
Rare: Addison disease, Systemic sclerosis, Sjögren syndrome, SLE, Vitiligo, Myasthenia gravis, Sarcoidosis, Alopecia areata, Autoimmune hepatitis
Clinical Presentation:
  • 📍 Distribution: Symmetric on elbows, knees, extensor forearms, back, buttocks
  • 🔍 Primary lesions: Pleomorphic - urticarial plaques, papules, vesicles
  • 🎯 Pathognomonic: Grouped/"herpetiform" papulovesicles on erythematous base
  • 🔥 Symptom: INTENSE pruritus → secondary excoriation and crusting
Less Common Presentations: • Isolated scalp involvement • Isolated facial involvement • Exclusively macular lesions • Hemorrhagic acral macules (usually palmoplantar)

🔬 Pathology & Diagnosis

Biopsy Technique:

  • ✅ Best site: Intact vesicle > erythema
  • ✅ DIF site: Normal-appearing skin immediately adjacent to lesion

Histopathology:

  • Dermal papillae filled with neutrophils (Papillary microabscesses)
  • Sparing of lowermost tips of intervening rete ridges (characteristic!)
  • Subepidermal blister with predominantly neutrophils

DIF Pattern (Required for diagnosis):

  • 85% - Granular IgA1 localized at papillae
  • 10% - Continuous granular at BMZ
  • Rare - Fibrillar pattern
Laboratory Findings:
  • Anti-endomysial antibodies: ~80% in DH, >95% in active celiac disease
  • IgA anti-reticulin antibodies: High sensitivity/specificity in DH with villous atrophy
  • 💡 The endomysium = connective tissue covering smooth muscle of esophagus, stomach, small intestine

🔬 Linear IgA Bullous Dermatosis (LABD)

Age Distribution:
  • Adults: >60 years, F > M
  • Children: Peak at 4 years old
Clinical Presentations:

Adults:

  • BP-like with herpetiform arrangement
  • DH-like presentation
  • MMP-like variant
  • Ocular MMP-like
  • TEN-like (with vancomycin)

Children:

  • 🎯 "Crown of jewels" - annular erythema with peripheral blisters
  • Predominantly flexural areas
  • Lower trunk, thigh, groin preference

Two Distinct Types:

Lamina lucida type (majority): Target antigen = LAD-1 and LABD97
Sublamina densa type: Target antigen = Collagen VII

Associated Conditions

GI: UC, Crohn's, gastric hypochlorhydria
Autoimmune: SLE, DM, RA, thyrotoxicosis, autoimmune hemolytic anemia, glomerulonephritis
Malignancy: B-cell lymphoma, CLL, bladder, thyroid, colon, esophagus, plasmacytoma, hydatidiform mole, RCC, ocular melanoma
Infections: VZV, antibiotic-treated tetanus, URTI

🏆 Drug-Induced LABD

Most Common Culprit: VANCOMYCIN

Common Causes

  • Vancomycin (can cause TEN-like variant)

Less Common

  • Penicillins
  • Cephalosporins
  • Captopril > other ACE inhibitors
  • NSAIDs (diclofenac, naproxen, oxaprozin, piroxicam)

Uncommon

  • Phenytoin
  • Sulfonamide antibiotics

Rare

  • Allopurinol, Amiodarone
  • ARBs, Atorvastatin
  • Carbamazepine, Cyclosporine
  • Furosemide, Gemcitabine
  • And many others...
Histopathology:
  • Usually indistinguishable from DH by light microscopy
  • 💡 Features favoring LABD over DH:
    • Linear distribution of neutrophils along BMZ
    • Neutrophils at very tips of dermal papillae
Laboratory:
  • Circulating anti-BMZ IgA antibodies: 60-70% positive
  • 💡 DH patients do NOT have circulating antibodies that bind to skin!

💊 Treatment Protocols

DH Treatment: Dual Approach

"GLUTEN-FREE + DAPSONE = DH CONTROL"

1. Gluten-Free Diet

AVOID: Wheat, rye, barley, and hybrids (kamut, spelt, triticale)
SAFE: Corn, rice, oats
  • Takes several months to improve DH
  • ⚠️ ONLY way to prevent MALT lymphoma

2. Dapsone Protocol

Starting dose: 25-50mg/day (0.5mg/kg in pediatrics)
Maintenance: 100mg/day typical
Response: Pruritus resolves in 48-72 hours
Relapse: Within 24 hours if discontinued
Pre-treatment Screening:
  • G6PD screen (MANDATORY)
  • CBC baseline
  • LFT baseline

Monitoring Schedule:

  • CBC: Weekly for 1st month, twice monthly for 2nd month, monthly for next 4 months, then semiannually.
  • LFT: At 6 months → Yearly
    • Wee
  • Acceptable: 1-2 lesions/week on optimal treatment
Refractory Facial/Scalp Lesions: Break vesicles + apply potent corticosteroid gel

Dapsone Side Effects:

Expected

  • Hemolysis in ALL patients (1-2g Hgb drop expected),Dapsone is secreted in breast milk and can cause hemolytic anemia in breastfed infants
  • Methemoglobinemia (most on 100mg/day)
  • Arterial desaturation on pulse ox (okay if asymptomatic)

Serious

  • Fatal agranulocytosis: Rare, 2-12 weeks, D/C if fever/sore throat
  • Dapsone hypersensitivity: 2-7 weeks, fever + rash + hepatitis
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Motor > sensory, with high doses, reversible

Alternative: Sulfapyridine

  • Less hemolysis and agranulocytosis than dapsone
  • Dose: 500mg TID up to 2g TID
  • Side effect: Nephrolithiasis → increase fluids + alkalinization

Sulfasalazine

  • Use: Second alternative if sulfapyridine is unavailable/ineffective
  • Metabolism: Metabolized to sulfapyridine and 5-aminosalicylate by intestinal bacteria, making dosing less predictable
  • Dosage (Adults): Standard starting dose 500 mg QID
  • Monitoring: Similar to dapsone/sulfapyridine

LABD Treatment

  1. First: Discontinue culprit medications
  2. Primary: Dapsone or sulfapyridine ± systemic corticosteroids
  3. Alternatives: Dicloxacillin, erythromycin, tetracycline (age >9), Bactrim
  4. Refractory cases: MMF, azathioprine, IVIG
Prognosis:
  • Adults: Several years, then remission in 30-60%
  • Children: Remits within 2-4 years
  • Drug-induced: Usually remits within 2-6 weeks (may persist for months)

🧠 Quick Board Review Quiz

Q1: A 45-year-old presents with intensely pruritic grouped vesicles on elbows and knees. DIF shows granular IgA in dermal papillae. What percentage likely has gluten-sensitive enteropathy?

Answer: >90% have gluten-sensitive enteropathy (though only 20% are symptomatic)

Q2: What is the MOST COMMON drug cause of LABD?

Answer: Vancomycin (can cause TEN-like variant)

Q3: A child with "crown of jewels" pattern bullae in flexural areas. What's the most likely diagnosis and prognosis?

Answer: Childhood LABD - typically remits within 2-4 years

Q4: DH patient on dapsone develops fever and sore throat at week 3. What's your concern?

Answer: Fatal agranulocytosis (rare hypersensitivity reaction, occurs 2-12 weeks) - STOP dapsone immediately!

Q5: What's the ONLY way to prevent enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma in DH?

Answer: Gluten-free diet (dapsone only controls skin manifestations, not GI complications)

🔄 Diagnostic Approach to Suspected DH

Step 1: DIF of normal-appearing skin adjacent to lesion
Step 2: Check total serum IgA level
If IgA Normal:
  • IgA anti-TG2 Ab
  • IgA anti-endomysial Ab
  • Consider IgA anti-TG3 Ab (limited availability)
If IgA Deficient:
  • IgG anti-TG2 Ab
  • IgG anti-endomysial Ab
  • Consider small bowel biopsy
Do NOT recommend anti-gliadin antibody testing - high false-positive rates!

🎯 Differential Diagnosis Pearls

Bullous LE:
  • Can mimic LABD and DH clinically and histologically
  • Key: Patients have clinical + serologic findings of SLE
  • DIF is crucial for differentiation
Crusted papules on elbows/knees also seen in:
  • Wegener granulomatosis
  • Churg-Strauss syndrome
