1. Seborrheic Dermatitis

Common Condition

Pathogenesis and Malassezia's Role

  • Malassezia (lipophilic yeast) acts as opportunistic pathogen
  • No direct relationship between yeast quantity and disease severity
  • Altered sebum composition more significant than quantity (↑triglycerides, ↓free fatty acids)
  • Imbalance of skin microbiome: reduced Cutibacterium acnes population
  • Inflammation possibly caused by fungal metabolites, lipase, and reactive oxygen species
Memory Aid: Think "Malassezia SALTS" - Sebum composition altered, Active sebaceous glands, Lipophilic yeast, Triglycerides increased, Sebum-rich areas affected.

Clinical Features: Infantile vs Adult

Infantile Adult
Begins ~1 week after birth, persists several months Chronic with peak in 4th-6th decades
"Cradle cap": thick adherent yellow scale on vertex/fontanelle Strikingly symmetric distribution
Can involve axillae, inguinal creases, neck, retroauricular folds Affects scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial folds, ears, presternal area
Possible disseminated psoriasiform eruption on trunk/extremities Ranges from dandruff to inflammatory plaques with yellow-red, bran-like scales
Generally resolves spontaneously Men affected more often than women

Association with HIV/Neurological Conditions

  • Extensive/therapy-resistant seborrheic dermatitis can indicate HIV infection
  • 2% of patients with seborrheic dermatitis tested HIV-positive
  • Parkinson's disease: facial immobility leads to sebum accumulation
  • Other associations: cerebrovascular accidents, immunosuppression

2. Asteatotic Eczema

Age-related

Clinical Features

  • "Dried riverbed" or "crazy-paving" pattern of superficial skin cracks
  • Predilection: shins, lower flanks, posterior axillary line
  • Initially dry, dull with fine scaling; progresses to pink/red with fissures
  • Can develop pseudoichthyosis appearance
  • Painful cracks, occasionally with hemorrhage

Pathogenesis/Predisposing Factors

  • Aging-related stratum corneum dysfunction (not sebum deficiency)
  • Decreased intercellular lipids and altered ceramide ratio
  • Reduced water-binding capacity (decreased natural moisturizing factor)
  • Exacerbating factors: low humidity, frequent bathing, soaps, xerosis
  • Associated with: malnutrition, renal disease, hypothyroidism, HIV

Treatment

  • Topical corticosteroid ointments for acute inflammation
  • Regular emollients (petrolatum, water-in-oil formulations)
  • Moisturizers containing urea, ceramides, or lactic acid
  • Bath oils and reduction of irritating factors
  • Treatment of underlying conditions

Common Traps in Board Questions

  • ❌ Patient with cracked skin and edema: If edema present, think stasis dermatitis, not just asteatotic eczema
  • ❌ Asteatotic eczema that doesn't respond to treatment: Think malignancy (e.g. lymphoma) or contact dermatitis

3. Disseminated Eczema (Autosensitization)

Secondary reaction

Pathophysiology

  • Secondary eczematous eruption distant from primary site
  • Inflammatory processes lower irritancy threshold of distant skin
  • Circulating activated memory T cells likely involved
  • Possible hematogenous dissemination of allergens

Clinical Features

  • Symmetric distribution of eczematous patches
  • Appears days to weeks after primary lesion
  • Predilection for analogous sites (extensor extremities, palms, soles)
  • Common with allergic contact dermatitis associated with stasis dermatitis

Risk Factors / Triggers

  • Allergic contact dermatitis (esp. with stasis dermatitis)
  • Stasis dermatitis alone
  • Tinea pedis
  • Venous ulcers with superinfection
  • Patch testing reactions ("angry back syndrome")
🧠 Important: The inciting dermatosis may even be subtle or missed.

Treatment

  • Topical corticosteroids and systemic antihistamines
  • Short courses of systemic corticosteroids for severe cases
  • Identification and treatment of inciting dermatosis

4. Nummular Dermatitis

Coin-shaped

Clinical Features

  • Coin-shaped (discoid), well-demarcated eczematous patches
  • Primarily on extremities (lower legs in men, forearms/hands in women)
  • Size: 1-3cm diameter
  • Acute: vesicular, weeping; Chronic: lichenified, hyperkeratotic
  • Intense pruritus with excoriations
  • Chronic relapsing course
  • Resistant form called "oid-oid" (aka Sulzberger-Garbe syndrome, exudative discoid, lichenoid chronic dermatitis)
Memory Aid: "COINS" - Circular lesions, On extremities, Intense pruritus, Nickel/chromate sensitization common, Size 1-3cm.

Histopathology

  • Spongiosis, irregular acanthosis
  • Parakeratotic scale and crust
  • Lymphohistiocytic infiltrate
  • Resembles other forms of eczematous dermatitis

Treatment

  • Medium to high-potency topical corticosteroid ointments
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
  • Tar preparations
  • Phototherapy for resistant cases
  • Methotrexate for persistent cases in children
  • Dupilumab for recalcitrant disease

5. Dyshidrotic Eczema

Vesicular

Clinical Features

  • Symmetric, firm, deep-seated vesicles on palms, lateral/medial fingers, soles
  • When clustered resembles "tapioca pudding"
  • Intensely pruritic
  • Resolves with thick desquamation
  • Chronic relapsing course
Memory Aid: "PALMS" - Palms/soles affected, Acutepruritus, Lateral/medial fingers, Misnomer (not related to sweat), Small firm vesicles.

Pathogenesis

  • Not related to sweat gland dysfunction (despite the name)
  • Often manifestation of atopic dermatitis or contact dermatitis
  • Aggravated by hyperhidrosis
  • Potential triggers: emotional stress, heat, ingested allergens
  • Can follow IVIg administration

Differential Diagnosis

  • Tinea manuum/pedis
  • Scabies
  • Palmoplantar pustular psoriasis
  • Dyshidrosiform pemphigoid
  • Vesicular T-cell lymphoma

Treatment

  • Topical corticosteroids and emollients
  • Calcineurin inhibitors
  • Botulinum toxin for associated hyperhidrosis
  • Phototherapy (PUVA)
  • Systemic therapy for severe cases: retinoids, corticosteroids, cyclosporine

6. Juvenile Plantar Dermatosis

Pediatric

Clinical Features

  • Symmetrical glazed, erythematous, fissured plantar surface of forefoot
  • Toe pads and balls of feet affected; dorsal of the feet and interdigital spaces spared
  • Prepubertal children (age 3+)
  • Worse in winter, slightly more common in boys

Pathogenesis

  • Atopic predisposition plus exogenous factors
  • Occlusive plastic/rubber footwear creates humid environment
  • Hydration of stratum corneum followed by drying and fissuring
  • Mechanical friction removes protective layers
Memory Aid: "SHOES" - Sweaty environment, Hydration-drying cycle, Occlusive footwear, Erythematous glazed skin, Spares toe webs.

Treatment

  • Avoid impermeable footwear and socks
  • Emollients, keratolytics, paraffin-based ointments
  • Change damp socks immediately
  • Self-limiting condition

7. Stasis Dermatitis

Venous

Epidemiology & Pathophysiology

  • Affects ~15% of adults with chronic venous insufficiency
  • ~1% of adults develop venous ulcers; prevalence increases with age
  • Caused by venous hypertension from valvular incompetence
  • Capillary distension leads to extravasation of fluid, proteins, and RBCs
  • Fibrin cuffs form around vessels causing microangiopathy
  • Reduced oxygen diffusion and tissue hypoxia
  • Elevated plasma homocysteine levels often present
  • Contact sensitization to topical agents is a common complication
Memory Aid: "VEINS" - Venous hypertension primary cause, Edema precedes dermatitis, Incompetent valves, Near medial malleoli, Secondary autoeczematization common.

Clinical Features & Progression

  • First sign: cushion-like pitting edema of medial aspect of shin and calf
  • Early stage: confined to MEDIAL ankle and calf around communicating veins
  • Hemosiderin deposition (stasis purpura) from RBC extravasation
  • Pruritic erythema and scaling around inner malleoli
  • Later stage: edema extends to distal third of leg with inflammation
  • Advanced: lipodermatosclerosis ("inverted champagne bottle" appearance)
  • Atrophie blanche: white stellate scars in supramalleolar region
  • Venous ulcers develop, especially on medial malleolus
  • Episodes of vesiculation may trigger autosensitization (ID reaction)

Treatment

  • Compression therapy (cornerstone of treatment)
  • Leg elevation and calf muscle exercises
  • Topical corticosteroids for acute inflammation
  • Emollients for skin barrier repair
  • Patch testing to identify contact allergens(if treatment resistant)
  • Avoid sensitizing topical agents (neomycin, lanolin)
  • Surgical intervention for severe venous insufficiency
  • Wound care for ulcers with appropriate dressings

8. Infectious Eczematous Dermatitis

Bacterial

Definition & Etiology

  • Eczematous reaction to a localized infection with purulent drainage
  • Most often due to Staphylococcus or Streptococcus species
  • Results from sensitization to bacterial antigens

Clinical Features

  • Pustules and vesicles spread from an infectious nidus
  • Advanced stages characterized by crusting, scaling, and oozing
  • In children: nares, ears, and face are most common sites
  • In adults: distal lower extremities most often affected
Memory Aid: "SPREAD" - Staph/Strep bacteria, Purulent drainage, Reaction to bacterial antigens, Eczematous lesions, Age-dependent distribution, Distal extremities in adults.

Treatment

  • Milder forms: open wet dressings, topical antibiotics
  • Topical corticosteroids to manage inflammation
  • Emollients to restore skin barrier
  • Severe disease: oral antibiotics targeted at causative bacteria
  • Oral corticosteroids sometimes needed for extensive disease

Differential Diagnosis

  • Eczema due to autosensitization (lesions develop at a distance from primary site)
  • HTLV-associated infective dermatitis (involves specific serology)
  • Impetigo (more discrete lesions, less eczematous reaction)
  • Contact dermatitis (exposure history, different distribution)

9. Diaper Dermatitis

Infantile

Types and Clinical Features

Type Key Features
Irritant Contact Dermatitis
  • Glazed erythema, erosions on convex surfaces
  • Spares skin folds
  • Due to prolonged exposure to urine/feces
Candidal Infection
  • Erythema with satellite pustules
  • Favors folds and genitalia
  • Often follows antibiotic use
Seborrheic Dermatitis
  • Salmon-colored to red patches
  • Favors folds
  • Other flexural sites and scalp often involved
Jacquet Erosive Dermatitis
  • "Punched out" erosions
  • Associated with chronic irritation, especially diarrhea
Atopic Dermatitis
  • Pruritic, erythematous patches and plaques
  • May involve extensor surfaces in infants
  • Often associated with personal or family history of atopy
  • relative sparing of diaper area
Allergic Contact Dermatitis
  • Well-demarcated erythema matching exposure pattern
  • Often from diaper components, wipes, or topical products
  • May spread beyond diaper area
Psoriasis
  • Well-demarcated erythematous plaques
  • Minimal scale in moist areas
  • Family history often present
Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis
  • Persistent purpuric papules
  • Resistant to conventional treatment
  • May have systemic involvement
Acrodermatitis Enteropathica
  • Erosive dermatitis around body orifices
  • Associated with zinc deficiency
  • Perioral and acral dermatitis often present
Kawasaki Disease
  • Erythematous rash in perineal region
  • Accompanied by fever, conjunctivitis, and lymphadenopathy
  • Desquamation of fingers and toes in later stages
Granuloma Gluteale Infantum
  • Reddish-purple nodules in diaper area
  • Associated with prolonged topical steroid use
  • Rare complication of diaper dermatitis
Congenital Syphilis
  • Copper-colored papulosquamous lesions
  • Involves palms and soles
  • Other systemic manifestations present

Pathogenesis

  • Fecal enzymes and alkaline environment from bacterial ureases
  • Breakdown of skin barrier from prolonged moisture
  • Secondary infection with Candida albicans
  • Friction between skin and diaper

Treatment

  • Frequent diaper changes using highly absorbent disposables
  • Protective barriers (zinc oxide, petrolatum)
  • Mild corticosteroids for acute inflammation
  • Antifungals for candidal infection

10. HTLV-Associated Infective Dermatitis

Rare

Definition

A rare childhood/adolescent chronic eczema associated with HTLV-1 (Human T-cell lymphotropic virus type I), a retrovirus that dysregulates the immune system.

Etiology & Pathogenesis

  • HTLV-1 retrovirus
  • Transmission routes:
    • Breastfeeding (primary route)
    • Sexual intercourse
    • Blood transfusion
    • Needle sharing
  • Immunosuppression → susceptibility to S. aureus, β-hemolytic streptococci, parasitosis (scabies, strongyloidiasis)
  • Genetic, socioeconomic, and nutritional factors contribute

Epidemiology

  • Endemic in sub-Saharan Africa, Caribbean, South Japan, South America, Iran
  • Only ~2% of infected children develop infective dermatitis
  • Usually appears after loss of maternal antibodies in infancy/early childhood

Clinical Features

  • Age: Early childhood
  • Sites involved (at least 2 of):
    • Scalp
    • Ears
    • Eyelid margins
    • Paranasal skin
    • Axillae
    • Groin
  • Exudative, crusty eczematous dermatosis
  • Chronic watery nasal discharge/crusting of anterior nares
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis
  • Generalized fine papular rash (sometimes)
  • Almost always infected with S. aureus or β-hemolytic streptococci
  • Rapidly responds to antibiotics but promptly relapses
  • Complications:
    • Corneal opacities
    • Progression to HTLV-1–associated adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (ATLL)
    • Tropical spastic paraparesis (HAM/TSP): progressive neurological disorder
    • Increased risk of developing HTLV-1 associated myelopathy

Diagnostic Criteria

Major ( 4 required, with HTLV-1 one of them):

  • Eczema in ≥2 areas (scalp, ears, eyelids, paranasal, axillae, groin)
  • Chronic watery nasal discharge/crusting
  • Chronic relapsing dermatitis that responds to antibiotics but relapses after stopping
  • onest early childhood
  • HTLV-1 seropositivity

Minor (supportive):

  • Positive cultures for S. aureus or β-hemolytic streptococci
  • Generalized fine papular rash
  • Anemia, elevated ESR
  • Hyperimmunoglobulinemia
  • Increased CD4+ and CD8+ counts, elevated CD4/CD8 ratio

Differential Diagnosis

  • Atopic dermatitis:
    • HTLV dermatitis: more exuberant, visibly infected, less pruritic
    • No personal/family history of atopy
    • Prominent nasal crusting, blepharoconjunctivitis
  • Seborrheic dermatitis (not associated with blepharoconjunctivitis)
  • Impetigo

Treatment

  • Antibiotics:
    • TMP-SMX, cephalexin, erythromycin
    • Quick improvement but frequent relapses
  • Topical therapies:
    • Antibiotics (including intranasal)
    • Corticosteroids
    • Bleach baths
Memory Aid: "VIRAL" - Virus HTLV-1 positive, Infected appearance, Relapses after antibiotics, Antibiotics temporarily effective, Lymphotropic virus association.

⚠️ Exam Traps & Tricks

  • HTLV-D vs Atopic Dermatitis: Less itchy, superinfected, +HTLV serology
  • Treatment response: Quick but non-curative
  • Not just dermatitis: Remember systemic complications
  • Diagnosis requires HTLV-1 serology (mandatory)

11. Pityriasis Alba

Hypopigmented

Clinical Features

  • Hypopigmented, slightly scaly patches
  • Common on face, upper arms, shoulders
  • More noticeable in darker skin phototypes
  • Often follows mild inflammation

Pathogenesis

  • Mild form of eczema with post-inflammatory hypopigmentation
  • Impaired transfer of melanosomes to keratinocytes
Memory Aid: "WHITE" - White patches, Hypopigmentation post-inflammation, In children common, Treated with mild corticosteroids, Evident in darker skin types.

Treatment

  • Emollients and mild topical corticosteroids
  • Sun protection
  • Typically self-resolving
