📊 Introduction & Key Statistics
90% of skin cancer deaths are due to melanoma despite being only a small percentage of skin cancers
Risk hierarchy: White male > White female > Black male > Black female (lowest risk)
Age pattern reversal: More common in females <40 years, males >40 years (same pattern as NMSC)

📈 Epidemiology Pearls

  • Incidence: ↑ high in Caucasians, low in dark-skinned people
  • Origin: 70% de novo, 30% from precursor
  • Recurrence: 90% of recurrences in first 5 years
  • Second primary: 3.5-4.5% risk of second cutaneous melanoma

⚠️ Critical Facts

Sunblock does NOT protect from melanoma and SCC
BCC: little to no effect from sunblock
Type of melanoma does NOT predict prognosis - all have same prognosis
🧬 Genetic Overview & Pathogenesis

🧬 Genetic Classification

Non-CSD (Chronic Sun Damage)

BRAF, NRAS (same as Congenital melanocytic nevus and NF)

CSD, Acral, Mucosal

KIT > BRAF, NRAS

BRAF can regulate MITF expression to ensure protein levels compatible with proliferation and survival of melanoma cells

🔄 Signaling Pathways

MAPK Pathway

Receptor KIT → phosphorylation → BRAF, MEK, ERK activation
→ bind to Cyclin D1 → phosphorylate Rb (Retinoblastoma)
→ release E2F from Rb-E2F → E2F works as transcription factor
P16 inhibits Cyclin D1, prevents Rb-E2F release
CSD, Acral, Mucosal: (20-40%) KIT, (15-20%) NRAS
Non-CSD: (60%) BRAF (B600V Type)

PI3K Pathway

Activated by KIT → phosphorylation PIP2→PIP3
→ activate AKT → inhibits apoptosis + accelerates cell growth via mTOR
WNT signaling: Can promote OR inhibit tumor growth. Inhibits β-Catenin (β-Catenin and APC mutations are rare in melanoma)

🛡️ Immune Pathways

MC1R: Activated by melanocortins (ACTH, α-MSH)
Defect in T Cell Signaling: CTLA-4 → inhibitory of CD28
Both CTLA-4 and PD1 inhibit CD28 on cytotoxic T-cells

Immune Evasion Mechanism

CD8+ cytotoxic cells need connection with APC
→ go to lymph node → activate more T-cells
CTLA-4 and PD-1 inhibit this process
Treatment: Anti-CTLA-4 and Anti-PD1 to allow self-defense
Melanoma produces IL-10 and TGF-β for immunoinhibitory effect → survival
CTLA4 + PD1 are immunoinhibitory → promote melanoma survival

🔍 Clinical Types of Melanoma
Important: Number of melanocytic nevi is the key risk factor for Superficial Spreading and Nodular melanoma. Skin type and hair color are important for Lentigo Maligna.
Superficial Spreading Melanoma (SSM) - 60% 50s
Most common type, youngest age group (50s)

📍 Location

Trunk - men
Legs - women
Upper back - men

📏 Characteristics

  • 50% associated with pre-existing lesions
  • SSM diameter ≤5 mm
  • Horizontal growth pattern initially
Nodular Melanoma - 2nd most common 6th decade
Thick melanomas (>3mm) were predominantly nodular type

📍 Location

Trunk
Head and neck

M > F

👥 Demographics

  • Older men
  • Mostly head and neck
  • Associated with fewer nevi
  • Vertical growth pattern
Lentigo Maligna Melanoma (LMM) - 7th decade
5% of lentigo maligna lesions progress to lentigo maligna melanoma
Here, skin type and hair color are important risk factors (vs. nevus count for SSM/Nodular)

📍 Location

Face (most common)
Cheeks and nose

Chronically sun-damaged areas

🔬 Dermoscopy Features

Circle in circle: Hyperpigmented follicular openings
Annular granular pattern: Irregular pigmented dots around follicles
Rhomboidal structures: Do not respect openings

Dermoscopy Criteria (89% sensitivity, 96% specificity)

Asymmetric pigmented follicular openings
Dark rhomboidal structures
Slate-gray dots
Slate-gray globules
Acral Lentiginous Melanoma (ALM) - 7th decade
70% of melanoma in Blacks, 45% in Asians. Usually diagnosed at advanced stage. M = F
Due to KIT activation → Treat with KIT inhibitor (Imatinib)
Exons: 11, 13, 17
Sole is most common location

🔬 Special Variants

Amelanotic Melanoma

All types can be amelanotic
No difference in diagnosis and treatment
No difference in prognosis
35% of mucosal melanomas are amelanotic

Spitzoid Melanoma

Symmetrical dermal nodule
Do not mature + atypical melanocytes
FISH and CGH to differentiate from Spitz nevus
P16: preserved in Spitz, lost in melanoma

Desmoplastic Melanoma

Sun-exposed areas
Associated with lentigo maligna
Mistaken as fibrosis or scar
Highly infiltrative but does not metastasize

Other Variants

Clear Cell Sarcoma: Distal extremities, adolescents, tendons. t(12;22)(q13;q12) EWS-ATF1 fusion

Malignant Blue Nevus: Scalp, >1cm, atypical spindle cells, necrosis. BAP-1 loss = poor prognosis

👁️ Ocular & Mucosal Melanoma

Ocular melanoma: Associated with GNAQ > GNA11 (same as nevus of Ota, blue nevus)
Divided into conjunctival and uveal
BAP-1 loss in uveal melanoma = increased metastasis risk and death
Mucosal melanoma: 35% amelanotic, KIT activating mutation, Treat with Imatinib
Occur near mucocutaneous junctions, ~1% of melanomas
Oral: hard palate most common

🤰 Special Situations

Melanoma and pregnancy: No increased risk, only darkening of melanocytic nevi during pregnancy (10% of women experience this)
High-risk melanoma in women: Wait 2 years after diagnosis before pregnancy (2/3 of recurrences occur within this period)
Childhood melanoma: Melanoma with spitzoid features is most common
🔬 Diagnosis & Investigation
"Little Red Riding Hood" sign → redness around mole = risk of melanoma
History of change in shape, size, and color is the MOST SENSITIVE clinical sign of melanoma

🔍 Clinical Diagnosis

ABCDE Criteria

A - Asymmetry
B - Border irregularity
C - Color variation
D - Diameter >6mm
E - Evolution (change)
Suspect melanoma if >5mm diameter

🔬 Dermoscopy (x10 magnification)

2-Step Dermoscopy Process

1. Determine if melanocytic in origin or not
2. Look for: pigment network, streaks, aggregated globules, homogeneous blue pigment, parallel pattern (acral lesions)

🎯 Melanoma-Specific Dermoscopy Patterns

Most common patterns in melanoma:
• Multicomponent pattern
• Asymmetric starburst pattern

🔬 Histopathology

Melanoma follows 2 growth patterns:
• Horizontal (radial) → NO risk of metastases
• Vertical → RISK of metastases

📏 Microstaging

Breslow depth: Measured from top of granular cell layer to deepest point of tumor

Key Histologic Features

Breslow depth (most important)
Ulceration
Mitosis/cm²
Surgical margins

🧪 Immunohistopathology

Marker Use Special Notes
S100 Most reliable in spindle cell pattern lesions Useful in all types except desmoplastic
HMB-45 (PMEL17/gp100) Stains melanosomes Standard melanoma marker
SOX-10 Helpful in desmoplastic melanoma When other markers fail

🧬 Molecular Analysis

Spitz Nevus vs. Spitzoid Melanoma

CGH (Comparative Genomic Hybridization)

20% of Spitz nevus show ↑ chromosome 11q → NOT in melanoma

FISH

Chromosome 6 differentiates benign nevus from melanoma

Loss of p16 → melanoma (vs Spitz nevus)

Spitzoid melanoma with loss of 9p21 → poor prognosis

Gene Expression Profiling (GEP): Applied to cutaneous melanomas to predict risk of metastasis

🧪 Laboratory Workup

Excisional biopsy with narrow 1-2mm margins is the preferred method for suspected lesions

Also: MIA (melanoma inhibitory activity)
S100A: Used in Spitz nevus

📊 TNM Staging & Prognosis
Staging depends on: thickness >1mm, ulceration, mitotic rate >1/mm², age (older), sex (male), site (trunk, head/neck = poor; extremities = good), number of lymph nodes, site of distant metastases

📏 T-Stage (Tumor - by 40x microscopy)

T-Stage Thickness Subcategory Criteria
Tis In situ - Melanoma in situ
T1 <1mm T1a No ulceration AND mitosis <1/mm²
T1b With ulceration OR mitosis ≥1/mm²
T2 1-2mm T2a No ulceration
T2b With ulceration
T3 2-4mm T3a No ulceration
T3b With ulceration
T4 >4mm T4a/T4b Same ulceration criteria
Special rule: If thickness 0.8-1mm (even without ulceration) = T1b

🔗 N-Stage (Regional Lymph Nodes)

N-Stage Number of Nodes Subcategory Details
N0 0 - No regional lymph node metastases
N1 1 node N1a Micrometastases
N1b Macrometastases
N2 2-3 nodes N2a Micrometastases
N2b Macrometastases
N2c In-transit/satellite metastases without nodal involvement
N3 4 or more - 4+ regional lymph nodes

🌍 M-Stage (Distant Metastases)

M-Stage Site LDH Status
M0 No distant metastases -
M1a Distant skin, subcutaneous, or nodal Normal LDH
M1b Lung metastases Normal LDH
M1c All other visceral sites ↑ Lactate Dehydrogenase
M1d CNS metastases Any LDH

📊 Overall Staging

Stage Groups

Stage 0: Melanoma in situ
Stage I: T1a (IA), T1b/T2a (IB) - Localized
Stage II: T2b (IIA), T3a/T3b/T4a (IIB), T4b (IIC) - Localized
Stage III: Any N1-N3 - Regional lymph nodes
Stage IV: M1 - Distant metastases
Quick reference: IA/T1a, IB/T1b+T2a, IIA/T2b, IV/M1, III/N1+

💊 Management & Treatment

🔪 Surgical Management by Stage

📏 Stage I & II - Re-excision Margins by Breslow Depth

Breslow Depth Surgical Margin Special Considerations
In situ 0.5cm (5mm) -
Lentigo maligna (face) 1cm (10mm) Use radiation + imiquimod
<1mm 1cm (10mm) -
1.01-2mm 2cm (20mm) Use Mohs for facial/acral lesions
>2mm 2cm (20mm) Use Mohs for facial/acral lesions
Local recurrence: Any recurrence within 2cm from scar, risk is 4%
Nowadays: Do SLNB (NOT ultrasound) if >1mm Breslow depth because 20% have lymph node metastases

🔍 Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy (SLNB)

SLNB Indications

Must be done in ALL melanomas >1mm
If positive → complete lymph node dissection
If palpable lymph nodes → direct dissection (already metastatic)
If you palpate lymph nodes during examination → Do dissection (means it already metastasized)
Positive lymph node = Stage III → WHOLE BODY PET-CT needed + S100B

🎯 Stage III - Regional Metastatic Disease

🔪 Management Options

Skin Metastases

Surgery: Primary option
Multiple on one limb: Melphalan infusion
Others: Cryotherapy, laser, intralesional IL-2, interferon, imiquimod
Skin satellite and in-transit metastases: Melanoma spread through lymph vessel before reaching lymph nodes
Treatment of choice: Surgical
Alternative: Isolated limb perfusion with melphalan, intralesional IL-2

💉 Adjuvant Therapy (Stage II, III)

Goal: Eliminate micrometastases in high-risk patients

📋 Interferon-α Dosage Schedules

Schedule Dose Frequency Duration Indication
Low-dose 3 million IU SC Days 1,3,5 every week 18 months Stage II-III
High-dose Initiation 20 million IU/m² IV Days 1-5 every week 4 weeks Stage III
High-dose Maintenance 10 million IU/m² SC Days 1,3,5 every week 11 months Stage III
Pegylated Initiation 6 mcg/kg SC Day 1 every week 8 weeks Stage III
Pegylated Maintenance 3 mcg/kg SC Day 1 every week Total 5 years Stage III
Side Effects: Flu-like symptoms, depression, suicide risk
Currently, Interferon-α and Pegylated Interferon are the only FDA-approved adjuvant therapies

🌍 Stage IV - Distant Metastatic Disease

Median survival: 9 months. ↑ LDH = poor prognosis

🔪 Treatment Modalities

Surgery

Palliative resection if feasible

Radiation Therapy

Treatment of choice for cerebral metastases
Whole brain radiotherapy + surgical resection (standard)
Other uses: pain control, spinal cord compression, bone metastases, local cutaneous control

💊 Systemic Therapy

Systemic therapy is the MAINSTAY treatment for Stage IV disease
🧪 Chemotherapy

DTIC (Dacarbazine)

Most widely used chemotherapy
FDA approved
~10-15% response rate
🛡️ Immunotherapy

High-dose IL-2

FDA approved: 600,000 IU/kg every 8 hours for 16 doses
High toxicity → needs admission
Low benefit overall

CTLA-4 Blockade

Activate T cells (CD8), activate CD28
Agents: Tremelimumab, Ipilimumab (FDA approved)
Side Effects: Autoimmune complications

Cancer Vaccines

Triple peptide: gp100, MART-1/Melan-A, tyrosinase HLA-A2 epitopes
Enhance T-cell production of IFN-γ

🦠 Oncolytic Virus Therapy

Talimogene laherparepvec (2015 FDA/EMA approved)
Genetically modified live oncolytic herpes virus
For melanoma metastatic to skin and lymph nodes
Injected intralesionally
Side Effects: Cellulitis
Contraindications: Immunosuppressed and pregnant patients

📊 Treatment Approach by Stage

Stage 1A (Thickness <1mm)

Nothing additional ± ultrasound lymph nodes

Stage 1B (Thickness ≥1mm)

Sentinel lymph node biopsy
If positive → lymph node dissection

Stage III (Positive lymph nodes)

WHOLE BODY PET-CT + S100B
Adjuvant treatment recommended: Ipilimumab and IFN-α (FDA approved)

Stage IV (Visceral involvement on PET-CT)

Check LDH levels
Surgery if feasible
Radiation for cerebral metastases
Systemic therapy (mainstay)

📈 Follow-up Protocol

90% of all recurrences occur during the first 5 years
Follow-up: 1-4 times per year for 2 years, then every 6-12 months for life
Significant risk of second primary cutaneous melanomas: 3.5-4.5%
🎯 Molecular Targeted Therapy & Immunotherapy

🧬 MAPK Pathway Targeting

🎯 BRAF Inhibitors

FDA Approved: Dabrafenib, Encorafenib, Vemurafenib
Target: Melanomas with BRAF V600E, V600D, V600K mutations
Dosing: Vemurafenib 960mg
Side Effects: ↑ UVA sensitivity (vemurafenib), SCCs and keratoacanthomas (25%)
BRAF + MEK inhibitors → decreased SCC risk (combination reduces side effects)

🎯 MEK Inhibitors

FDA Approved: Binimetinib, Cobimetinib, Trametinib
Used: Combined with BRAF for better response and lower side effects
Alone: When NRAS mutation detected

🎯 c-KIT Inhibitors

Agents: Imatinib, Nilotinib, Dasatinib
Good for: Acral melanoma and mucosal melanoma
Target: KIT mutations (exons 11, 13, 17)

🛡️ Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

🔗 Anti-CTLA-4 Antibodies

Agents: Ipilimumab (FDA approved), Tremelimumab
Mechanism: Block CTLA-4 to activate T cells

🔗 Anti-PD-1 Antibodies

Agents: Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab (FDA approved), Pidilizumab
More effective than Anti-CTLA-4 when used alone

🔗 Anti-PD-L1 Antibodies

Agents: Atezolizumab, Avelumab, Durvalumab
Approved for other cancers (lung, bladder, Merkel cell)
Anti-CTLA-4 and Anti-PD-1 are considered FIRST-LINE therapy in Stage IV patients who lack activating BRAF mutation
Good response indicator: Development of leukoderma at melanoma site or other skin areas

📊 Treatment Selection Algorithm

Stage IV Treatment Selection

If BRAF mutation → BRAF + MEK inhibitors
If NRAS mutation → MEK inhibitors alone
If KIT mutation (acral/mucosal) → c-KIT inhibitors
If no targetable mutation → Checkpoint inhibitors (Anti-PD-1 preferred)

⚠️ Risk Factors Summary

Genetic Factors

  • Family history of cutaneous melanoma
  • Lightly pigmented skin
  • Tendency to burn, inability to tan
  • Red hair color
  • DNA repair defects (xeroderma pigmentosum)

Environmental Factors

  • Intense intermittent sun exposure
  • Chronic sun exposure
  • Residence in equatorial latitudes
  • PUVA (possible)
  • Tanning bed use, especially under 35 years
  • Iatrogenic or acquired immunosuppression

📊 Phenotypic Risk Factors

Melanocytic Nevi Risk

100+ MN: ~8-10-fold ↑ risk
5+ AMN: ~4-6-fold ↑ risk
Multiple SL: ~3-4-fold ↑ risk
Relative risks are multiplicative!
Example: >100 MN + >5 AMN + multiple SL = 10 × 5 × 3 = 150-fold relative risk
Personal history of cutaneous melanoma significantly increases risk of second primary

🔬 Histopathologic Diagnosis Criteria

Architectural Pattern

  • Asymmetry
  • Poor circumscription of intraepidermal melanocytic component
  • Silhouette of tumor base is uneven (except nevoid melanoma)
  • No maturation of melanocytes with progressive descent
  • Nests not equidistant from one another
  • Nests vary in size and shape
  • Some nests become confluent
  • Scatter of melanocytes above dermal-epidermal junction
  • Solitary units predominate over nests within epidermis
  • Melanocytes in nests are not cohesive
  • Melanocytes extend down adnexal epithelium
  • Sheets of melanocytes within dermis
  • Large nests at base of lesions

Cytomorphology & Other Features

Cytomorphology
  • Atypical melanocytes (pleomorphic nuclei)
  • Mitotic figures
  • Necrotic melanocytes
Other Features
  • Signs of regression
  • Actinic elastosis
  • "Dusty" melanin within tumor cells
  • Non-uniform melanin distribution
  • Plasma cells at base of lesion

🧬 Molecular Diagnostics Comparison

Test Detects Advantages Disadvantages
CGH Chromosomal gains/losses Genome-wide screening without prior knowledge No mutations, small gains/deletions, balanced translocations
FISH Deletions, amplifications, translocations Individual cell evaluation, works with low neoplastic cell % No mutations, limited chromosomal loci (4-6), poor reproducibility
GEP Gene expression levels (20-30 genes) May predict metastasis risk, distinguish benign from malignant No genomic aberration info, 80-90% sensitivity/specificity
GEP currently used to predict metastatic risk in uveal melanomas
