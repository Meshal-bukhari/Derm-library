Lipoid Proteinosis (Hyalinosis Cutis et Mucosae, Urbach–Wiethe)

Rare autosomal recessive disorder due to ECM1 mutations. Features evolve over life; remember the disease chronologically.

Phase / Category Key Clinical Features High-Yield Exam Clues
Infancy
  • Weak/hoarse cry — classic first sign.
  • Cause: hyaline infiltration of the vocal cords.
  • Hoarseness in an infant board favorite
Early Childhood
  • Vesicles, pustules, hemorrhagic crusts on face & distal extremities.
  • Heals with pitted “pock-like” acneiform scars.
  • History of vesiculopustular eruptions → residual pitted scars.
Later Life
  • Diffuse thickened, waxy, yellow skin.
  • Moniliform blepharosis
    • Beaded, waxy papules along eyelid margins (“string of beads”).
  • Verrucous, cobblestone plaques on friction sites (elbows, knees).
  • Alopecia (patchy or diffuse).
  • Mucosa: woody, bound-down tongue; impaired tongue protrusion.
  • Moniliform blepharosis = pathognomonic cutaneous sign.
  • “Cannot protrude tongue” due to infiltration.
Systemic / Radiologic
  • CNS: epilepsy; neuropsychiatric disorders (variable).
  • Radiology (CT/MRI): bilateral, symmetric, bean-shaped amygdala calcifications (temporal lobes).
  • Head CT with amygdala calcifications = diagnostic gift don’t miss

Tip: Anchor recall to Infant hoarseness → Early vesiculopustular scars → Eyelid beads + woody tongue → Amygdala calcifications.
