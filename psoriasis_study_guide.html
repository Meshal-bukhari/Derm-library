Overview & Epidemiology

+
Psoriasis Definition: Chronic immune mediated disease
Population Prevalence: 2% of the population
Bimodal Age Distribution: 20-30s and 50-60s
Early Onset: ~75% of patients, the onset is before the age of 40 years
Comorbidities:
  • Increased risk of metabolic syndrome and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease
  • 20-30% will develop psoriatic arthritis

Immunological Cascade

Step 1: Stressed keratinocytes → release of Self DNA/RNA → forms a complex with LL37 (cathelicidin) → activates TLR9 on pDC
Step 2: Activated pDCs produce large amounts of IFN-α
Step 3: IFN-α + cytokines from KCs (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) → activates dDCs
Step 4: Activated dDCs move to the lymph nodes → produce IL-12 and IL-23
Step 5: IL-12 and IL-23 → drive naïve T cells into a Th1, Th17, and Th22 cells
Step 6: These T cells migrate into the dermis
Step 7:
  • Th1 produce IFN-γ and TNF-α → more inflammation
  • Th17 produce IL-17 and TNF-α → stimulate keratinocyte proliferation and inflammation
  • Th22 produce IL-22 → more T-cell recruitment and acanthosis
Summary for Biologics: IL-12 and IL-23 → Th17 → IL-17 and TNF-α (all biologics work on this pathway)
IL-22 level correlate with disease activity (MCQ)
IL-22 is responsible for acanthosis (MCQ)
IL-17 is responsible for pustules in pustular psoriasis (MCQ)

+

Most common form overall in adults
Most common form in children as well, followed by guttate (MCQ)
Genitalia involvement: Up to 45%
Important: BSA alone doesn't reflect the severity of the disease, and induration + scale need to be incorporated (PASI, see below)

More commonly seen in children compared to adults
Preceded by: Group A streptococcal pharyngitis

Psoriatic Arthritis

+
20-30% of psoriasis patients develop arthritis
5 subtypes exist
Mono-and asymmetric oligo arthritis is the most common (MCQ)
DIP is the most common site (MCQ)
Associated with: Severe cutaneous psoriasis, nail, scalp, inverse, high CRP
  • Morning stiffness lasting >1hr
  • HLA-B27 +ve in 50%
  • Screening by PEST score, diagnosis CASPAR criteria
Poor prognostic factors:
  • Early age
  • Female gender
  • Multiple joints
  • HLA-B27
Radiological findings: "PETT"
PETT Radiological Findings:
  • Pencil in a cup deformity
  • Erosions
  • Tissue swelling
  • Tuft resorption
Type Clinical Findings
Mono- and asymmetric oligoarthritis
(most common type)		 • Inflammation of the DIP and PIP joints of the hands and feet
• Involvement of the PIP or both the DIP and PIP joints of a single digit can result in the classic "sausage" digit
• MCP joint involvement unusual (unlike RA)
• May be accompanied by inflammation of larger joints
Arthritis of the distal interphalangeal joints • Exclusive involvement of the DIP joints
• Classic but uncommon presentation
• The joints may become fixed in a flexed position
Rheumatoid arthritis-like presentation
(difficult to distinguish from RA clinically*)		 • Symmetric polyarthritis that involves small and medium-sized joints, in particular the PIP, MCP, wrist, ankle and elbow
• Most patients are seronegative for RF
Arthritis mutilans**
(least common type)		 • Severe, rapidly progressive joint inflammation that results in destruction of the joints and permanent deformity
• Digits become shorter, wider and softer to palpation because of osteolysis and a telescoping phenomenon
Spondylitis and sacroiliitis
(also have peripheral joint involvement)		 • Spondylitis resembles that seen in ankylosing spondylitis, with axial arthritis as well as involvement of the knees and sacroiliac joints
• Patients are often HLA-B27-positive and may have associated inflammatory bowel disease and/or uveitis
*Whether some seropositive patients have an overlap of the two disorders is possible.
**Also occurs in multicentric reticulohistiocytosis.

☀️ Phototherapy (Click to expand)

Phototherapy is a cornerstone in the management of moderate to severe psoriasis, especially when topical agents are impractical or ineffective.

I. Ultraviolet B (UVB) Phototherapy
  • Narrowband UVB (NB-UVB)
    • Wavelength: 311 nm
    • Optimal irradiation currently available (superior to Broadband UVB)
    • Indications: Moderate to severe psoriasis; phototherapy of choice for adolescents
    • Efficacy: ~68% clearance (meta-analysis; slightly below PUVA)
  • Broadband UVB (BBUVB)
    • Older form, largely superseded by NB-UVB
    • Considered less effective, second-choice UVB modality (Excpet for Guttate psoriasis)
    • 🗺️ UVB in psoriasis flowchart
II. PUVA (Psoralen + Ultraviolet A Photochemotherapy)
  • Mechanism: Psoralen (oral/topical) + UVA exposure
  • Efficacy: Highest patient clearance (~70%) among classic systemic and phototherapies
  • Key Limitation: Seldom used due to potential for cumulative carcinogenicity
  • Cancer Risk:
    • >200 PUVA treatments = significantly increased risk of Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
    • Controversial: possible increased risk of cutaneous melanoma
🗺️ PUVA in psoriasis flowchart
III. Targeted Phototherapy
  • Agent: Monochromatic 308 nm light (excimer laser or equivalent)
  • Niche: Targets individual psoriatic plaques ("sniper rifle" for recalcitrant plaques; not for widespread disease)
Indications and Contraindications (Table 8.9)
Indications:
  • Moderate to severe psoriasis (monotherapy or combination)
Contraindications:
  • High Intrinsic Cancer Risk (Absolute/Strong Relative)
    • Genetic disorders with photosensitivity/cancer risk (e.g., xeroderma pigmentosum)
    • History of skin cancer (esp. melanoma/SCC)
    • High cumulative PUVA treatments (>150-200)
    • Previous arsenic exposure or ionizing irradiation
  • Increased Photosensitivity (Relative)
    • Photosensitive dermatoses (e.g., lupus, polymorphic light eruption)
    • Concurrent unavoidable phototoxic medications
    • Skin Type I (requires careful dosing)
    • Vitiligo (may need dose adjustment)
  • Critical Drug Interactions (Absolute)
    • Concurrent Cyclosporine (dramatically increases SCC risk)
    • Cyclosporine use after prior PUVA also increases SCC risk
  • Patient-Specific Factors (Relative)
    • PUVA-specific: Pregnancy/lactation, reproductive age without contraception, impaired liver function, cataracts
    • General: Poor compliance, seizure disorder (risk of injury in phototherapy unit)
Ingram regimen: Anthralin + phototherapy (MCQ)
Goekerman regimen: Coal tar + phototherapy (MCQ)
No need to wear goggles when face is treated, just close eyes (MCQ)
Decrease dose by 25% when using photosensitizing drug (MCQ)

💊 Acitretin (Click to expand)

Indications

• Severe psoriasis that cannot be managed by topical treatments or photo(chemo)therapy
• Monotherapy is indicated for erythrodermic or pustular psoriasis
• Combination therapy is indicated for chronic plaque psoriasis

Contraindications

• Severe liver dysfunction*
• Severe kidney dysfunction (elimination reduced)*
• Pregnancy or lactation*
• Women of childbearing potential who cannot guarantee adequate contraception during and up to 3 years following discontinuation of acitretin*
• Hyperlipidemia, especially hypertriglyceridemia, that cannot be controlled*
• Excessive alcohol intake*
• Unreliable patient*
• Concomitant medications that interfere with retinoid bioavailability or whose metabolism is altered
• Concomitant hepatotoxic drugs, e.g. methotrexate
• Poorly controlled diabetes mellitus
• History of pancreatitis
• Use of contact lenses
• Atherosclerosis

Acitretin - Evaluation Prior to and During Treatment

Pre-acitretin screening:
  • History to exclude contraindications
  • Complete blood count
  • Liver function tests (AST, ALT, GGT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin)
  • Serum triglycerides, cholesterol, HDL
  • Glucose
  • Serum creatinine
  • Pregnancy test
  • Consider spinal X-ray (often initially performed during the first 3 months of therapy if long-term treatment is anticipated)
Evaluation during acitretin treatment:
  • Monitor mucocutaneous side effects
  • Serum triglycerides, cholesterol/HDL, and liver function tests (every month for the first 2 months then every 2-3 months)
  • Serum creatinine (elderly patients or patients with mild to moderate renal dysfunction)
  • Monitor for development of hyperostosis by history (twice yearly) and by X-ray of spine (e.g. once yearly or every other year in patients receiving long-term treatment)*
  • Pregnancy test (twice yearly throughout treatment)*
*Absolute contraindications.
†Requires dose adjustment and careful monitoring, e.g. phenytoin competes for plasma protein binding.
‡Reserved for treatment-resistant patients.
*Frequency varies amongst clinicians.
†Contraindicated in patients receiving acitretin.

🧬 Biologics Indications & Contraindications (Click to expand)

General Indications

• Patients with moderate to severe psoriasis, eligible for a systemic treatment
• Patients with psoriatic arthritis, particularly those who have failed other disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs)

Restricted Indication

• Patients with moderate to severe psoriasis who are not candidates for topical treatments, photo(chemo)therapy, or classic systemic treatments because of insufficient efficacy or contraindications

Contraindications

The exact contraindications vary by agent; some listed below are relative, while others are absolute:

• Significant viral, bacterial or fungal infection, including active Salmonella or dimorphic fungal infection*
• Increased risk for developing sepsis*
• Active tuberculosis*
• Allergic reaction to the biologic agent*
• Selective for TNF-α inhibitors: ANA+ (especially if high titer) or autoimmune connective tissue disease, blood dyscrasias, congestive heart failure (NYHA grade III or IV), or demyelination disorders (the latter also if in first-degree relative)*
• Selective for ustekinumab: BCG vaccination within the past 12 months (mode of action expected to increase susceptibility to mycobacterial infections)
• Selective for secukinumab, ixekizumab, and brodalumab: Active Crohn disease (may cause exacerbation of bowel disease)
• History of hepatitis B viral (HBV) infection**
• History of hepatitis C viral infection (risk of activation)***
• Immunosuppressed patient
• Pregnancy (TNF-α inhibitors, ustekinumab and secukinumab are category B; ixekizumab was released after 2015)
• Breastfeeding
• Malignancy within the past 5 years (does not include adequately treated single cutaneous squamous or basal cell carcinoma)
• Excessive chronic sun exposure or photo(chemo)therapy
Guttate, pustular, or erythrodermic psoriasis are not established indications, but there is anecdotal evidence for efficacy and safety.
*Absolute contraindications.
**If HBsAg-positive, measure HBV DNA and treat with antiviral agent (e.g. tenofovir, entecavir, telbivudine)) prior to immunosuppressive therapy. Monitor for reactivation in those with anti-HBs or -HBc antibodies who are HBsAg-negative.
***Need to monitor liver function tests and viral load.

Face; groin, axilla, and other body folds • First-line: mild topical corticosteroids
• Second-line: topical calcineurin inhibitors or tacalcitol/calcitriol in combination with a mild topical corticosteroid
Nail • Differentiate proximal versus distal nail unit pathology due to nail matrix versus nail bed pathology, respectively; consider coexisting onychomycosis
• For distal nail unit pathology: vitamin D3 analogues topically (limited evidence)
• For proximal pathology: intralesional corticosteroids and/or systemic antipsoriatic therapies
