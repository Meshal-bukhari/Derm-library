Grover Disease

Transient Acantholytic Dermatosis: The Itchy Truncal Mimicker

🔎 Clinical Snapshot: At a Glance

  • What is it? An acquired, pruritic, papulovesicular eruption.
  • Who gets it? Primarily White men > 30 years old. (M:F ratio 1.8-7:1)
  • What does it look like? Discrete, red, scaly papules and papulovesicles.
  • Where is it? Primarily on the trunk (especially the back). Spares palms, soles, genitals.
  • Key Symptom: Usually intensely pruritic, but can be asymptomatic.
  • The Course: Can be self-resolving, wax and wane for years, or persist.
ICD10CM: L11.1 - Transient acantholytic dermatosis [Grover]
SNOMEDCT: 17796002 - Transient acantholytic dermatosis

🔥 Triggers & Associations

Common Triggers

  • ☀️ Excessive sun exposure
  • 🥵 Heat and sweating (Miliaria-like)
  • 🌵 Dry skin (Xerosis) - linked to winter predominance
  • 🛌 Prolonged bed rest (e.g., hospitalized patients)
  • ☢️ Ionizing radiation

Associated Conditions

  • Maligancies
  • Renal failure (acute & chronic)
  • Atopic dermatitis
  • Transplantation

👀 Clinical Presentation & Diagnostic Pearls

Look For

  • Primary Lesions: Discrete red papules and papulovesicles on the trunk.
  • Secondary Lesions: May be excoriated, crusted, or eroded due to intense pruritus.
  • Less Common: Pustules or vesicles may be seen.
  • Distribution: Primarily truncal. Proximal extremities and neck can be affected.
  • Negative Finding: Palms, soles, and genitals are spared.

💡 Diagnostic Pearl: Impact of Skin Color

This is a key clinical point. The presentation varies:

  • In lighter skin, papules are pink or red.
  • In darker skin, papules may appear violaceous or brown.

💡 Diagnostic Pearl: Dermoscopy

Dermoscopic findings can be a clue: look for brown, star-like, central patterns with linear vessels surrounded by white halos.

🤔 Differential Diagnosis & Pitfalls

Grover's is a "great mimicker." Organize your DDx by category to avoid being tricked.

Common Inflammatory

  • Papular Eczema / Atopic Dermatitis
  • Pityriasis Rosea
  • Seborrheic Dermatitis
  • Pityriasis Lichenoides Chronica
  • Irritant Contact Dermatitis
  • Urticaria
  • Secondary Syphilis

Follicular / Vesicular

  • Bacterial Folliculitis
  • Pityrosporum Folliculitis
  • Acne Vulgaris
  • Miliaria Rubra
  • Nonbullous Impetigo
  • Cutaneous Candidiasis
  • Scabies / Arthropod Bites

🚨 Acantholytic Imposters (HIGH YIELD)

  • Pemphigus Foliaceus
  • Pemphigus Vulgaris
  • Darier Disease
  • Hailey-Hailey Disease
  • Galli-Galli Disease

🔬 Best Tests: The Biopsy Story

A skin biopsy confirms the diagnosis. The key finding is focal acantholysis. However, the histologic pattern is variable and can mimic other serious diseases.

🚨 BOARD ALERT: Histology is the Key... and the Trap!

The histologic findings can resemble other major acantholytic disorders. Clinical correlation is essential. The pathologist needs the clinical picture to make the right call.

5 Main Histologic Patterns of Acantholysis

  • Pemphigus vulgaris-like: Suprabasilar acantholysis. (Most common)
  • Darier disease-like: Focal suprabasilar acantholysis with dyskeratosis. (Most common)
  • Pemphigus foliaceus-like: Acantholysis in the upper epidermis.
  • Hailey-Hailey disease-like: Full-thickness acantholysis ("dilapidated brick wall").
  • Spongiotic: Acantholysis combined with spongiosis.

Other findings: Mild superficial perivascular infiltrate with lymphocytes, histiocytes, and scattered eosinophils. Hyperkeratosis and parakeratosis can be present.

💡 Key Distinguishing Feature

Immunofluorescent studies are NEGATIVE. This is crucial for differentiating Grover's from true autoimmune blistering diseases like Pemphigus, which will have positive DIF.

✅ Management Pearls & Therapy

The goal is symptom control, as the disease is benign and there is no cure. Many asymptomatic patients desire no treatment. Spontaneous remission can occur.

1Patient Counseling & Lifestyle (Foundation)

  • Avoid triggers: excessive heat and sweating.
  • Gentle skin care to prevent xerosis (dry skin).
  • Use of emollients and anti-pruritic lotions (pramoxine, menthol).

2First-Line: Topical Corticosteroids

Mid- to high-potency topical corticosteroids are first-line for pruritus. Use creams over ointments (less occlusive).

  • High Potency (Class 1-2): Clobetasol, Halobetasol, Fluocinonide.
  • Mid Potency (Class 3-4): Triamcinolone, Mometasone, Fluocinolone.
  • Instructions: Apply BID for limited periods. Avoid face, folds, genitals.

3Adjunctive & Second-Line Therapy

  • Vitamin D Analogs: Calcipotriene cream, alone or rotated with steroids (e.g., calcipotriene on weekdays, steroids on weekends).
  • Antihistamines: Oral antihistamines (fexofenadine, hydroxyzine) for pruritus control.

4Severe or Recalcitrant Disease (Systemic Options)

Note: No controlled trials exist for these, based on case reports/series.

  • Retinoids: Isotretinoin (0.5-1 mg/kg/day) or Acitretin (0.5 mg/kg/day).
  • Systemic Corticosteroids: Prednisone (e.g., 10-60 mg/day) with a taper.
  • Phototherapy: Psoralen plus UVA (PUVA).
  • Other reported options: Vitamin A, Dupilumab.
