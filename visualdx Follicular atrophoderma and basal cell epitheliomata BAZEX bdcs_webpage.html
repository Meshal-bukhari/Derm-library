Bazex-Dupré-Christol Syndrome

Follicular Atrophoderma and Basal Cell Epitheliomata
0%

Saudi Board Exam High-Yield Facts: BDCS is an X-linked dominant disorder with the classic triad of basal cell carcinoma, follicular atrophoderma, and hypotrichosis. The onset of these signs is not simultaneous, and there is significant variable expression both inter- and intrafamilially.

Syndrome Overview

Definition & Epidemiology

Follicular atrophoderma and basal cell epitheliomata, also known as Bazex-Dupré-Christol syndrome (BDCS), is an exceedingly rare genodermatosis characterized by the triad of basal cell carcinoma (BCC), follicular atrophoderma, and hypotrichosis.

  • First described in 1964
  • Fewer than 200 cases reported
  • No sex predilection
  • Most cases in patients of European descent

Memory Aid: "BFH" for BDCS

Basal cell carcinoma
Follicular atrophoderma
Hypotrichosis

Genetics

BDCS is an X-linked dominant disorder caused by mutations in an unknown gene on an 11.4 Mb interval on chromosome Xq25-27.1. Two sporadic cases have also been documented in the literature.

The condition is hypothesized to be a disorder of the hair follicle.

When evaluating a patient with multiple BCCs at a young age, especially with concomitant hair abnormalities, always consider BDCS as a diagnosis, even if the classic triad is not fully present initially.

Clinical Manifestations

Basal Cell Carcinoma
Manifest in 2nd-3rd decade, aggressive course
Follicular Atrophoderma
Follicularly based dells devoid of hair
Hypotrichosis
Typically congenital, improves with age

For the Saudi Board Exam: Remember that the diagnosis of BDCS can be challenging because the onset of signs is not simultaneous, each sign is not specific to the disorder if it presents alone, and there is significant variable expression both inter- and intrafamilially.

Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)
  • Manifest during second and third decade of life
  • Onset in first decade has been reported
  • Typically localized to sun-exposed areas, particularly the face
  • Aggressive course with common relapses
  • Appearance depends on BCC subtype:
    • Nodular
    • Infiltrating
    • Pigmented
    • Superficial
Follicular Atrophoderma
  • Due to poor follicle unit development
  • Subsequent dilatation and deepening of follicular ostia
  • Present at birth or develops early in life
  • Manifests as follicularly based dells devoid of hair
  • Locations:
    • Typically on extensor surfaces of upper and lower extremities
    • Particularly dorsa of hands, elbows, and knees
    • Any hair-bearing area can be involved
  • Classically described as "ice-pick markings"
Hypotrichosis
  • Typically congenital
  • Occurs in 85% of cases
  • Improves with age
  • Involvement patterns:
    • Usually widespread
    • Can be limited to scalp, eyebrows, and eyelashes
  • Hair shaft abnormalities:
    • Pili bifurcati (hair shafts that split into two)
    • Pili torti (twisted hair)
    • Trichorrhexis nodosa (hair shaft breakage with node-like swellings)
Less Common Manifestations
  • Milia
  • Hypohidrosis
  • Facial hyperpigmentation
  • Trichoepitheliomas

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Pearls

The triad of basal cell carcinoma, follicular atrophoderma, and hypotrichosis is classic for BDCS. However, diagnosis can be challenging because:

  • The onset of these signs is not simultaneous
  • Each sign is not specific to the disorder if it presents alone
  • There is significant variable expression at inter- and intrafamilial levels

Thus, careful observation for development of signs of BDCS over time may be necessary to make the diagnosis.

In the exam, you may be presented with a case featuring a young patient with multiple BCCs and either hypotrichosis or follicular atrophoderma. Consider BDCS in your differential, even if the full triad is not yet present.

Best Tests

BDCS is a clinical diagnosis. Skin biopsy should be used to identify BCCs.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Rombo syndrome Features vermiculate atrophoderma, milia, trichoepitheliomas, BCCs, hypotrichosis, and peripheral vasodilation
Basal cell nevus syndrome (Gorlin syndrome) Multiple BCCs, odontogenic keratocysts, palmar/plantar pits, ectopic calcifications, skeletal anomalies
Schöpf-Schulz-Passarge syndrome Palmoplantar keratoderma, eyelid cysts, hypodontia, hypotrichosis, and susceptibility to skin tumors
Generalized basaloid follicular hamartoma Multiple skin-colored papules distributed symmetrically on the face, scalp, and trunk
Oley syndrome Hypotrichosis, hypohidrosis, and palmoplantar hyperkeratosis
X-linked dominant chondrodysplasia punctata Skeletal abnormalities, cataracts, ichthyosis, and hair abnormalities

Management

Management Pearls
  • A consultation with a dermatologist is appropriate for diagnosis and management
  • A consultation with genetics is also appropriate
  • Screening for BCCs by a dermatologist should be initiated during the first decade of life
  • BCC screening should be continued 1-2 times per year
  • Advise sun avoidance and sun-protection measures:
    • Using sunscreen
    • Wearing protective clothing

Early and regular screening for BCCs is critical in BDCS patients, starting in the first decade of life, as tumors can appear early and follow an aggressive course.

Therapy

There is no therapy for the underlying genetic defect.

Treatment for all BCCs should be individualized for the patient, taking into account:

  • Site
  • Size
  • Histologic subtype
  • Cosmesis
  • Wound healing
  • Cost
  • Other circumstances

Treatment options include:

  • Electrodessication and curettage
  • Excision
  • Cryosurgery
  • Radiation
  • Mohs surgery
  • Oral smoothened inhibitors

Key Points for Saudi Board Exam

High-Yield Summary
  1. BDCS is characterized by the triad of basal cell carcinoma, follicular atrophoderma, and hypotrichosis
  2. It is an X-linked dominant disorder with mutations on chromosome Xq25-27.1
  3. BCCs typically appear in the second to third decade but can occur in the first decade
  4. Follicular atrophoderma manifests as ice-pick markings devoid of hair
  5. Hypotrichosis is congenital in 85% of cases and improves with age
  6. The onset of signs is not simultaneous, and diagnosis requires careful observation over time
  7. Treatment focuses on BCC management and sun protection
  8. Screening should begin in the first decade and continue 1-2 times yearly
  9. There is no therapy for the underlying genetic defect
  10. Differential diagnosis includes Rombo syndrome, Gorlin syndrome, and others
