🧪 Retinoids Mastery Guide

Comprehensive Review for Saudi Board Dermatology Exam

🎯 Core Concepts

Essential Facts for Board Exam

  • Definition: Structural and functional analogues of vitamin A
  • Key Receptors: RARs (α, β, γ) and RXRs (α, β, γ)
  • Natural Retinoids: Tretinoin (all-trans RA), Isotretinoin (13-cis RA), Alitretinoin (9-cis RA)
  • Most Important Side Effect: TERATOGENICITY (all systemic retinoids)

Vitamin A Metabolism Pathway

Remember the Conversion Sequence:

Retinyl Esters → Retinol → Retinaldehyde → Retinoic Acid

"Esters Reach Really Amazing" (E-R-R-A)

Mechanism of Action

Nuclear Receptor Binding & Gene Transcription
  1. Ligand Binding: Retinoids bind to nuclear receptors (RARs/RXRs)
  2. Dimerization: RAR-RXR heterodimers or RXR-RXR homodimers form
  3. DNA Binding: Dimers bind to hormone response elements (RAREs)
  4. Transcription: Co-activators recruited → gene transcription modulated
  5. Effects: Cellular differentiation, proliferation, apoptosis regulated

📊 Four Generations of Retinoids

1st Generation

Type: Non-aromatic

Members:

  • Tretinoin (all-trans RA)
  • Isotretinoin (13-cis RA)
  • Alitretinoin (9-cis RA)

Key: Natural retinoids

2nd Generation

Type: Mono-aromatic

Members:

  • Acitretin
  • Etretinate (discontinued)
  • Motretinide

Key: Better for psoriasis

3rd Generation

Type: Polyaromatic (arotinoids)

Members:

  • Adapalene:
    • Exhibits selective affinity for RARs
    • Higher affinity for RAR-β and RAR-γ than RAR-α
    • In epidermis (keratinocytes):
    • RAR-β is not expressed
    • RAR-γ is the primary target receptor
  • Tazarotene
  • Bexarotene

Key: Receptor-selective

4th Generation

Type: Highly selective

Members:

  • Trifarotene
  • Seletinoid G
  • Tamibarotene

Key: Increased selectivity

Remember for Exams:

• Isotretinoin has NO clear receptor affinity but is the ONLY retinoid that suppresses sebum

• Acitretin activates RARs despite poor binding (paradox!)

• Bexarotene is RXR-selective → causes hypothyroidism

🏥 Clinical Pearls by Condition

Acne Treatment Strategy

Isotretinoin for Acne:

  • Standard dose: 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
  • Cumulative dose target: 120-150 mg/kg
  • Lower cumulative dose (70 mg/kg) may be effective
  • Lag period: 1-3 months before improvement
  • ~33% require second course

Initial flare possible in first 3-4 weeks - start low dose!

Psoriasis Treatment

Acitretin for Psoriasis:

  • Best for: Pustular psoriasis (1st line!)
  • Plaque psoriasis: 30% clear, 50% improve
  • Starting dose: 10 mg/day (avoid initial worsening)
  • Maintenance: 25-50 mg/day
  • Re-PUVA: Acitretin + PUVA = synergistic
CTCL Treatment

Bexarotene for CTCL:

  • Starting dose: 150 mg/m²/day
  • Target dose: 300 mg/m²/day
  • Response rates: ~50% in advanced disease
  • ALWAYS give with fenofibrate + levothyroxine!

⚠️ Universal Adverse Effects of Systemic Retinoids

Category Shared Effects
I. Mucocutaneous Effects
(Most Universal & Shared)
  • Cheilitis (earliest and most frequent)
  • Xerosis (dry skin)
  • Desquamation/peeling (palms, soles, face)
  • Mucosal dryness:
    • Oral → dryness
    • Nasal → epistaxis
    • Ocular → dry eye
  • Skin fragility
  • Photosensitivity
  • Palmoplantar peeling
  • Retinoid dermatitis (erythema, peeling)
  • Granulation tissue with pyogenic granuloma-like appearance:
    • Periungual sites
    • Acne cyst sites
  • Telogen effluvium (diffuse hair thinning)
  • Nail fragility, onycholysis, paronychia
  • Sticky skin sensation (palms/soles)
  • Staphylococcus aureus colonization → secondary infections*
II. Laboratory Abnormalities
(Universally Observed)
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • ↑ Cholesterol (total, LDL, VLDL)
  • ↓ HDL
  • ↑ Liver enzymes:
    • ALT, AST
    • Also ALP, LDH, bilirubin
  • Seen in 20–50% of patients depending on agent, but shared across all retinoids
III. Ocular Effects
  • Dry eyes (xerophthalmia)
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis
  • Reduced night vision (nyctalopia)
  • Photophobia, glare sensitivity, blurred vision
  • Common due to ↓ Meibomian secretion & inhibition of visual cycle enzymes
IV. Musculoskeletal Effects
  • Muscle pain (myalgia), cramps
  • Elevated creatine kinase (CK)
  • Bone pain (without imaging findings)
  • Hyperostosis (DISH-like changes)
  • Anterior vertebral ligament calcification
  • Extraspinal ligament/tendon calcifications
  • Osteophyte formation
  • Premature epiphyseal closure (rare; with prolonged high-dose use)
  • Pathophysiology tied to retinoid impact on osteoblast/osteoclast activity & long‐term remodeling
V. CNS & Psychiatric Effects
  • Headache
  • Rare pseudotumor cerebri (especially with tetracyclines)
  • Fatigue, irritability
  • Mood changes (depression, suicidal ideation)
  • Not causally proven; data in both directions exists
  • Visual disturbances (from pseudotumor cerebri or ocular dryness)
VI. GI & Systemic Effects
  • Anorexia, nausea, abdominal pain
  • Fatigue, lethargy
  • Dry lips, mouth, nose → altered taste/smell, bleeding
  • Rare pancreatitis (from severe hypertriglyceridemia)
  • Increased susceptibility to Staph aureus infection (cutaneous)
VII. Teratogenicity
(Most Severe Risk)
  • All systemic retinoids are potent teratogens
  • Fetal malformations:
    • Craniofacial defects:
      • Microtia/anotia
      • Cleft palate
    • CNS anomalies:
      • Hydrocephalus
      • Microcephaly
    • Cardiac defects
    • Thymic & parathyroid hypoplasia
    • Limb anomalies
    • Genital/anorectal atresia
  • Mandatory contraception:
    • Acitretin: 3 years
    • Isotretinoin, Alitretinoin, Bexarotene: 1 month
VIII. Bone & Connective Tissue
  • Osteoporosis (rare, with long-term use)
  • Periosteal thickening
  • DISH-like axial hyperostosis
  • Ligament & tendon calcification
  • Shared due to retinoid effects on bone remodeling

Drug-Specific Side Effects

Isotretinoin

  • significantly suppress sebum production via seboatrophy
  • Depression controversy
  • Acne fulminans-like presentation, cysts evolving into pyogenic granuloma-like lesions
  • Hyperuricemia → gout
  • Pseudotumor cerebri (rare)
  • S. aureus colonization

Acitretin

  • Palmoplantar peeling
  • Re-esterification → etretinate
  • 3-year contraception!

Bexarotene

  • central Hypothyroidism (40%!)
  • Severe Hypertriglyceridemia (80%)
  • Leukopenia (30%)
  • Milder mucocutaneous

Bexarotene Protocol (MUST KNOW):

Start BEFORE bexarotene:

  • Day -7: Fenofibrate 145-200 mg daily
  • Day 0: Levothyroxine 0.05 mg daily
  • Monitor free T4 (not TSH!) - central hypothyroidism

👥 Special Populations

Pediatric Considerations

Key Points:

  • Isotretinoin: Safe in adolescents, monitor growth
  • Concern: Premature epiphyseal closure (rare)
  • Required: Only with high doses (>1 mg/kg) for >4-6 years
  • No routine X-rays needed for standard acne treatment

Pregnancy Categories (Historical)

All Systemic Retinoids = Category X

Topical Retinoids:

  • Tretinoin, Adapalene: Category C
  • Tazarotene, Bexarotene: Category X

Renal/Hepatic Impairment

Dosing Adjustments
  • Renal disease: No dose adjustment needed (isotretinoin safe in hemodialysis)
  • Hepatic disease: Use with caution, monitor LFTs closely
  • Avoid: Concurrent hepatotoxic drugs

🔍 Controversies & Updates

Isotretinoin & Depression

  • Current Evidence: No causal link established
  • Studies show: Depression often IMPROVES with treatment
  • Still monitor for psychiatric symptoms
  • Document: Pre-existing psychiatric history

Isotretinoin & IBD

  • Meta-analysis: No increased risk of IBD
  • Confounders: Severe acne itself, antibiotic use
  • Recommendation: Can use, but document discussion
Surgery & Isotretinoin - Updated Guidelines

Current Recommendations:

  • Most procedures: NO need to delay
  • Safe: Minor surgery, superficial procedures
  • Delay 6-12 months:
    • Fully ablative laser resurfacing
    • Mechanical dermabrasion
    • Deep chemical peels

🎓 Board-Style Practice Points

Q1: Which retinoid requires monitoring of free T4?

Answer: Bexarotene

Explanation: Causes central hypothyroidism by suppressing TSH β-subunit. Monitor free T4, not TSH!

Q2: Best retinoid for pustular psoriasis?

Answer: Acitretin

Explanation: First-line for all forms of pustular psoriasis (palmoplantar, generalized von Zumbusch)

Q3: Only retinoid that suppresses sebum production?

Answer: Isotretinoin

Explanation: Despite having no clear receptor affinity! Mechanism involves sebocyte apoptosis.

Q4: Which combination increases pseudotumor cerebri risk?

Answer: Isotretinoin + Tetracyclines

Explanation: Both can cause intracranial hypertension independently

Ichthyosis Management

Key Points:

  • Best retinoid: Acitretin > Isotretinoin
  • ARCI: Excellent response
  • X-linked: Good response (but mild disease)
  • Strategy: Intermittent courses due to lifelong nature
  • Monitoring: Growth in children, bones if long-term

Lupus Erythematosus

Retinoids in LE:

  • Effective for: Hypertrophic LE, chronic cutaneous LE
  • Dose: 0.2-1.0 mg/kg/day (isotretinoin or acitretin)
  • Comparable to: Hydroxychloroquine efficacy
  • Limitation: High recurrence after stopping

🏥 Clinical Scenarios

Scenario 1: Acne patient wants laser hair removal

Answer: Safe to proceed!

Explanation: Updated guidelines - no need to delay laser hair removal, IPL, or fractional lasers. Only delay fully ablative procedures.

Scenario 2: Psoriasis patient drinks alcohol occasionally

Answer: Avoid acitretin, use isotretinoin instead

Explanation: Alcohol + acitretin → etretinate (stored in fat, 120-day half-life). For women of childbearing age, this extends contraception to 3 years!

Scenario 3: CTCL patient with triglycerides 600 mg/dL on bexarotene

Answer: Continue bexarotene, optimize lipid management

Explanation: Add/increase fenofibrate dose. Only stop if TG >800 mg/dL. Most patients need lipid-lowering drugs with bexarotene.
