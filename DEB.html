Dystrophic Epidermolysis Bullosa (DEB)

Quick Summary

  • Common Gene: All forms of Dystrophic EB (DEB) are caused by mutations in the COL7A1 gene, which encodes Type VII collagen, the primary component of anchoring fibrils.
  • Level of Cleavage: The defining feature of DEB is blister formation in the sublamina densa, below the basement membrane. This deep split leads to the hallmark features of scarring, milia, and nail dystrophy.
  • Dominant vs. Recessive:
    • Dominant DEB (DDEB): Generally milder. Caused by a dominant-negative missense mutation. Anchoring fibrils are present but may be reduced or abnormal.
    • Recessive DEB (RDEB): Ranges from intermediate to severe. Caused by biallelic loss-of-function mutations. Anchoring fibrils are severely reduced or completely absent.
  • Key Complication: Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) is the leading cause of death in RDEB patients after adolescence, arising in chronic non-healing wounds.

Core Knowledge Structure

1. Pathophysiology: The COL7A1 Spectrum

Gene: COL7A1

Protein: Type VII Collagen

Function: Forms anchoring fibrils that secure the lamina densa of the basement membrane to the underlying papillary dermis.

Pathophysiologic Cascade

DDEB (Dominant-Negative)

  • Mutation: Typically a missense mutation substituting another amino acid for glycine in the triple-helical domain.
  • Effect: One faulty collagen chain disrupts the assembly of the entire trimer, acting like a "poison peptide."
  • Result (TEM): Anchoring fibrils are present but may be reduced in number or have an abnormal appearance. The split is sublamina densa.

RDEB (Loss-of-Function)

  • Mutation: Compound heterozygous or homozygous mutations, often premature stop codons that lead to truncated, non-functional protein.
  • Effect: Little to no functional Type VII collagen is produced.
  • Result (TEM): Anchoring fibrils are rudimentary, sparse, or completely absent. The epidermis-dermis connection is extremely weak.

2. Clinical Features & Variants

I. Dominant Dystrophic EB (DDEB)

  • Inheritance: Autosomal Dominant (AD)
  • Gene: COL7A1 (Dominant-negative mutation)
  • General Presentation: Milder than RDEB. Characterized by lifelong blistering with trauma, leading to atrophic scarring, milia formation, and nail dystrophy/loss. Extracutaneous involvement is minimal.

Unique DDEB Variants:

DDEB, Localized (formerly Acral or Pretibial): Disease activity is confined to specific areas like the shins or acral sites (hands and feet), presenting as blisters, erosions, and atrophic plaques after minor trauma.

DDEB, Pruriginosa: Intense, intractable pruritus is the defining symptom, leading to chronic scratching that forms pruritic papules, nodules, and lichenified plaques, most commonly on the shins.

DDEB, Self-improving (formerly Bullous Dermolysis of the Newborn): Blistering is severe at birth but spontaneously improves or resolves within the first 1-2 years of life. This is due to a temporary defect in the transport of Type VII collagen to the dermal-epidermal junction.

II. Recessive Dystrophic EB (RDEB)

  • Inheritance: Autosomal Recessive (AR)
  • Gene: COL7A1 (Biallelic loss-of-function mutation)
  • General Presentation: A spectrum of severity, generally more severe than DDEB, with significant extracutaneous complications.

Unique RDEB Variants:

RDEB, Severe (formerly Hallopeau-Siemens): The most devastating form.

  • Cutaneous: Widespread blistering leads to extensive scarring and hallmark pseudosyndactyly ("mitten" deformities).
  • Extracutaneous: Severe systemic involvement including microstomia, esophageal strictures, corneal scarring, anemia, growth failure, and a high risk of aggressive SCC (90% cumulative risk by age 55).

RDEB, Inversa: Disease activity is most severe in intertriginous areas (axillae, groin, neck) and the trunk. Mucosal involvement is significant.

RDEB, Centripetalis: Blistering begins acrally in childhood and slowly progresses centripetally (towards the trunk) over many years.

RDEB, Pruriginosa & Self-improving: These variants have recessive forms that mirror the clinical presentation of their dominant counterparts but with an AR inheritance pattern.
