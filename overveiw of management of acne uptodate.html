🎯 Acne Vulgaris - Saudi Board Review

Comprehensive Clinical Guide for Board Certification

🎯 Overview & Treatment Principles

🔥 High-Yield Board Points

  • 4 Key Pathogenic Factors: Follicular hyperkeratinization, sebum production, C. acnes proliferation, inflammation
  • Only curative therapy: Oral isotretinoin
  • Antibiotic resistance prevention: Always combine with benzoyl peroxide
  • Maintenance therapy: Topical retinoids are preferred

💎 Clinical Pearl

Treatment Timeline: Minimum 2-3 months to assess efficacy. Initial response = reduction (not clearance) of lesions. Set expectations early!

Pathogenesis Overview

🔬 Four Pillars of Acne Pathogenesis

  1. Follicular Hyperproliferation & Abnormal Desquamation
    • Targets: Retinoids, azelaic acid, salicylic acid
  2. Increased Sebum Production
    • Targets: Isotretinoin, oral contraceptives, spironolactone, clascoterone
  3. C. acnes Proliferation
    • Targets: Benzoyl peroxide, antibiotics, azelaic acid
  4. Inflammation
    • Targets: Isotretinoin, tetracyclines, retinoids, azelaic acid, dapsone

🧠 Memory Aid: Treatment Principles

"BEAM" Approach

  • Benzoyl peroxide - Always with antibiotics
  • Expectations - 2-3 months minimum
  • Adherence - Key to success
  • Maintenance - Retinoids preferred

General Treatment Guidelines (2024 AAD)

🎯 Board Algorithm

Step 1: Assess severity & complications

Step 2: Comedonal only → Retinoid monotherapy

Step 3: Inflammatory → Retinoid + Antimicrobial

Step 4: Moderate-Severe → Consider systemic therapy

Step 5: Maintenance → Retinoid preferred

🔍 Pretreatment Assessment & Classification

🎯 Essential Assessment Components

  • Lesion types: Comedones vs inflammatory papules/pustules/nodules
  • Severity: Distribution, degree of involvement
  • Complications: PIH, PIE, scarring, psychological distress
  • Contributing factors: Comedogenic products, medications, hormonal disorders

Severity Classification

🟢 Mild Acne Vulgaris

  • Scattered, small (<5mm) comedones or inflammatory lesions
  • Limited skin involvement (one body area or few lesions)
  • No nodules
  • No scarring
  • No near-confluent involvement

🟡 Moderate Acne Vulgaris

  • Visually prominent acne
  • Many comedones or inflammatory lesions
  • Multiple body area involvement
  • May have occasional nodules

🔴 Severe Acne Vulgaris

  • Extensive nodular lesions
  • Associated scarring
  • Multiple body areas affected
  • Significant psychological impact

💎 Board Pearl: "Upgrade" Criteria

Even mild acne should be treated as moderate-severe if there is:

  • Scarring
  • Disfiguring PIH/PIE
  • Significant psychological distress

Patient Counseling Essentials

🧠 Counseling "TIME" Framework

  • Time - 2-3 months minimum for assessment
  • Improvement - Reduction, not complete clearance initially
  • Modifications - Regimen adjustments are normal
  • Expectations - Long-term maintenance usually needed

🧴 Skin Care Guidelines (Click to expand)

  • Gentle cleansers: pH 5.5-7 (syndets) vs soap pH 9-10
  • Avoid aggressive scrubbing: Can worsen inflammation
  • Noncomedogenic products: Though labeling isn't regulated
  • No picking: Increases scarring risk

🍎 Diet Considerations (Click to expand)

No routine dietary restrictions recommended - Limited evidence for milk and high-glycemic diets. Consider nutritional counseling if patient pursues restrictions.

🟢 Mild Acne Management

🎯 First-Line Approach

  • Comedonal only: Topical retinoid monotherapy
  • Papulopustular/Mixed: Retinoid + Antimicrobial(s)
  • Gold standard: Retinoid + Benzoyl peroxide

Topical Retinoids

💊 Topical Retinoids ▼

💎 Retinoid Selection Pearl

Adapalene 0.1% gel = Only OTC retinoid in US. All retinoids have similar efficacy - choose based on tolerability, cost, and vehicle preference.

Retinoid Concentrations Key Features
Tretinoin 0.025%, 0.05%, 0.1% Photolabile, avoid with BP simultaneously
Adapalene 0.1%, 0.3% Light stable, less irritating, OTC 0.1%
Tazarotene 0.05%, 0.1% Most potent, short-contact option available
Trifarotene 0.005% RAR-γ selective, effective for truncal acne

⚠️ Contraindications

  • Pregnancy: Not recommended (especially tazarotene - Category X)
  • Fish allergy: Avoid micronized tretinoin 0.05% gel

🧠 Application Pearls

  • Timing: Once daily, traditionally at night
  • Amount: Pea-sized for entire face
  • Technique: Apply to dry skin, avoid simultaneous BP with tretinoin
  • Titration: Start every other night for sensitive skin/skin of color

Benzoyl Peroxide

🧪 Benzoyl Peroxide ▼

💎 BP Board Pearls

  • 2.5% as effective as higher concentrations with less irritation
  • No resistance reported to C. acnes
  • Bleaching: Warn patients about fabric/hair bleaching

Mechanism & Efficacy

  • Antibacterial: Oxidizing agent against C. acnes
  • Comedolytic: Mild keratolytic effect
  • Onset: Visible improvement in 3 weeks, maximum at 8-12 weeks

⚠️ Safety Alert

FDA Warning: Test on small area first 3 days - rare severe hypersensitivity reactions reported

Sample Regimen

📋 Mild Papulopustular Acne Regimen

Morning:

  • Gentle cleanser OR benzoyl peroxide cleanser
  • Benzoyl peroxide gel (if not using cleanser)

Evening:

  • Gentle cleanser
  • Topical retinoid (entire face)

🔴 Moderate to Severe Acne Management

🎯 Definition & Criteria

  • Visually prominent: Many comedones, inflamed papules/pustules
  • Large lesions: Nodules >5mm diameter
  • Multiple body areas involved
  • Associated scarring
  • Psychological burden (even if appears mild)

🎯 Systemic Therapy Decision Tree

Immediate Isotretinoin if:

  • Extensive nodular acne
  • Prominent scarring
  • Significant psychological distress

Alternative Systemic Options:

  • Papulopustular/Nodular → Tetracyclines, hormonal Rx
  • Comedonal only → Hormonal Rx, isotretinoin

Topical Therapy in Moderate-Severe Disease

💎 Combination Therapy Pearl

Except for isotretinoin monotherapy, always combine systemic + topical therapy. Minimum approach: Retinoid + Benzoyl Peroxide

Alternative Topical Agents

🔬 Alternative Topical Therapies ▼

Topical Dapsone

  • Concentrations: 5% (twice daily) or 7.5% (once daily)
  • Best for: Inflammatory papulopustular lesions
  • Efficacy: 48-58% reduction in inflammatory lesions

Important: Yellow-orange discoloration if applied with BP simultaneously. Not contraindicated in sulfa allergy (sulfone ≠ sulfonamide)

Topical Clascoterone

  • Mechanism: Androgen receptor inhibitor
  • Dose: 1% cream twice daily
  • Age: Approved ≥12 years
  • Efficacy: 18-20% treatment success vs 7-9% placebo

Azelaic Acid

  • Triple action: Antimicrobial, comedolytic, anti-inflammatory
  • Bonus: Improves PIH (tyrosinase inhibition)
  • Forms: 20% cream, 15% gel
  • Pregnancy: Safe option

💊 Systemic Medications

Oral Isotretinoin

🏆 Oral Isotretinoin - The Gold Standard ▼

🎯 Board High-Yield Facts

  • Only curative acne therapy - can induce long-term remission
  • Targets all 4 pathogenic factors
  • Cumulative dose: 120-150 mg/kg over ~20 weeks
  • iPLEDGE program mandatory in US

🧠 Isotretinoin "IMPACTS" All Pathways

  • Inhibits sebum production
  • Microcomedone prevention
  • Propionibacterium (C. acnes) reduction
  • Anti-inflammatory effects
  • Curative potential
  • Teratogenic risk
  • Side effects multiple

🚫 Absolute Contraindications

  • Pregnancy - Category X teratogen
  • Inability to comply with pregnancy prevention
  • Inability to understand risks

⚠️ Common Side Effects

  • Mucocutaneous: Dry skin, lips, eyes, nosebleeds
  • Laboratory: Elevated triglycerides, LFTs
  • Musculoskeletal: Myalgias, arthralgias
  • Ophthalmologic: Dry eyes, night vision changes

🚨 Severe Acne Flare Prevention

Acne Fulminans/Conglobata: Start low dose (0.1-0.5 mg/kg) + systemic corticosteroids to prevent dangerous flares

Oral Antibiotics

Hormonal Therapies

⚕️ Hormonal Therapies (Females Only) ▼

🎯 Mechanism Overview

Goal: Reduce androgen action on pilosebaceous units

  • ↓ Sebum production (30-50% reduction with spironolactone)
  • ↑ SHBG (reduces free androgens)
  • Suppression of ovarian androgens

Oral Contraceptives

FDA-Approved for Acne:
  1. Ethinyl estradiol/norethindrone (20/30/35 mcg + 1mg)
  2. Ethinyl estradiol/norgestimate (35 mcg + 180/215/250 mcg)
  3. Ethinyl estradiol/drospirenone (20 mcg + 3mg)
Antiandrogenic Progestins (Better for acne):
  • Drospirenone - Also has aldosterone antagonism
  • Cyproterone acetate - Not available in US
  • Dienogest

🚫 OCP Contraindications

  • Thromboembolism history
  • Thrombophilic disorders
  • Pregnancy
  • Hepatic disease
  • Migraine with aura

Spironolactone

  • Dose: 50-100mg daily (start 25-50mg)
  • Mechanism: Androgen receptor blocker + ↓ androgen synthesis
  • Onset: 3-6 months for full effect
  • Efficacy: 66% achieve ≥90% improvement

💎 Spironolactone Monitoring Pearl

Hyperkalemia monitoring:

  • Healthy women <45 years: No routine monitoring needed
  • Age ≥45, comorbidities, high-risk medications: Monitor K+
  • Risk: 0.72% in acne patients (same as baseline)

⚠️ Spironolactone Side Effects

  • Menstrual: Irregular bleeding (prevented by OCPs)
  • Breast: Tenderness
  • GI: Nausea, anorexia
  • CV: Orthostatic hypotension
  • CNS: Dizziness, headache, fatigue
  • Pregnancy: Theoretical feminization of male fetus

👥 Special Populations

Pregnancy Considerations

🚫 Contraindicated in Pregnancy

  • Oral isotretinoin - Category X teratogen
  • Topical tazarotene - Category X
  • Other topical retinoids - Not recommended
  • Oral tetracyclines - Tooth discoloration
  • Topical minocycline - Limited data

💎 Pregnancy-Safe Options

  • Topical: Erythromycin, clindamycin, azelaic acid
  • Limited areas: Benzoyl peroxide (some experts consider safe)
  • Oral: Erythromycin (if systemic therapy needed)

Pediatric Considerations

👶 Ages 1-7 Years

  • Workup needed: Evaluate for hyperandrogenism
  • Assessment: Physical exam for precocious puberty
  • Testing: Bone age, endocrine labs if indicated

🧒 Ages 7-12 Years (Preadolescent)

  • Normal occurrence from adrenarche
  • Treatment: Similar to adults with exceptions
  • Avoid: Tetracyclines age <8, hormonal therapy until 1 year post-menarche
  • Isotretinoin: Weight-based dosing appropriate for severe cases

Skin of Color

💎 Special Considerations (Phototypes IV-VI)

  • Higher PIH risk from both acne and treatments
  • Slow retinoid titration: Start every other night, lower concentrations
  • Short-contact therapy: BP washes vs leave-on gels
  • Gentle formulations: Creams/lotions over gels when possible

Maintenance of Response

🎯 Long-term Management

  • Acne therapies are suppressive, not curative (except isotretinoin)
  • Retinoids preferred for maintenance
  • Transition timing: After 3-6 months stable improvement
  • Some patients need combination maintenance

📋 Maintenance Strategy

Standard Approach:

  1. Achieve stable improvement (3-6 months)
  2. Transition to retinoid monotherapy
  3. If insufficient → Add BP intermittently
  4. Avoid long-term antibiotics

Management of Complications

🎭 PIH, PIE, and Scarring ▼

Post-inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH)

Management Approach:
  1. Photoprotection: Daily sunscreen essential
  2. Topical retinoids: Accelerate resolution
  3. Azelaic acid: Alternative if retinoid intolerant
  4. Hydroquinone: Gold standard for individual lesions
  5. Chemical peels: Glycolic/salicylic acid

⚠️ Hydroquinone Caution

Rare risk: Exogenous ochronosis (grayish discoloration). No human carcinogenicity reported despite rodent studies.

Post-inflammatory Erythema (PIE)

Common in phototypes I-III. Limited treatment data:

  • Pulsed dye laser
  • Fractional microneedling RF
  • Topical 5% tranexamic acid
  • Intense pulsed light

Scarring Prevention & Treatment

💎 Prevention is Key

Early aggressive treatment for patients with evidence of scarring. Consider systemic therapy early, similar to moderate-severe acne approach.

Severe Variants

🚨 Acne Fulminans

  • Presentation: Abrupt inflammatory nodules, friable plaques, erosions, ulcers
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, malaise, bone pain, arthralgia
  • Treatment: Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg + low-dose isotretinoin (0.1 mg/kg)
  • Timing: Steroids 2-4 weeks before isotretinoin

🚨 Acne Conglobata

  • Presentation: Severe nodular acne with drainage, sinus tracts, severe scarring
  • Treatment: Isotretinoin (low initial dose) + systemic corticosteroids
  • Alternative: TNF-α inhibitors (case reports)

📚 Quick Reference & Board Pearls

  • Comedonal: Retinoid monotherapy
  • Inflammatory: Retinoid + BP ± antibiotic
  • Moderate-Severe: Add systemic therapy

⚠️ Key Contraindications

  • Age <8: Tetracyclines
  • Fish allergy: Micronized tretinoin gel
  • Thromboembolism: OCPs

🧩 Board Exam Practice Questions

Click to test your knowledge with high-yield scenarios!

Question 1:

A 16-year-old female presents with moderate inflammatory acne on face and upper back. She is sexually active and desires contraception. What is the most appropriate first-line systemic therapy?

Correct Answer: B

For a sexually active female desiring contraception, an oral contraceptive pill addresses both acne and contraceptive needs. While other options are effective, the OCP provides dual benefit and is appropriate first-line therapy for this scenario.

Question 2:

A pregnant patient in her first trimester presents with worsening acne. Which treatment is most appropriate?

Correct Answer: C

Azelaic acid is safe in pregnancy. Tretinoin and tazarotene are not recommended (tazarotene is Category X), and doxycycline can cause tooth discoloration in developing fetus.
