Goltz Syndrome (Focal Dermal Hypoplasia)

Your High-Yield Board Review PowerPage for FDH

🚨 At a Glance: What is Goltz Syndrome?

Focal Dermal Hypoplasia (FDH), or Goltz syndrome, is a rare X-linked dominant disorder. It primarily affects females. Sporadic cases in males occur due to post-zygotic mutations.

Think: Lines of Blaschko + Multisystem Involvement (Skin, Bones, Eyes primarily).

Key Visual Cues (from source images): Whorled configuration Hypoplastic phalanges Limb hypoplasia Skin atrophy Skin ulceration Aplasia cutis congenita Syndactyly Coloboma

👶 Clinical Presentation: Neonate & Infant

🖐️ Key Cutaneous Signs (Follow Blaschko Lines!)

  • Linear, streaky cutaneous atrophy with telangiectasias (trunk & extremities).
  • Ulcers at sites of congenital skin absence, healing with atrophy.
  • Soft red-yellow nodules (fat herniations) along Blaschko lines.
  • Aplasia cutis congenita (can be present, rare).
  • Absent or dystrophic nails.

Later Cutaneous Features:

  • Hypo- and hyperpigmentation with fat herniation.
  • Fleshy and vascular papillomas (mucosal, perioral, perigenital, intertriginous areas).
  • Sparse hair.

🦴 Skeletal Abnormalities

  • Syndactyly (most common).
  • Polydactyly, oligodactyly (often with lobster-claw deformity).
  • Asymmetry of trunk and limbs (Limb hypoplasia).
  • X-ray (older patients): Vertical striations in metaphysis of long bones (Osteopathia striata).

👁️ Ocular Abnormalities

  • Coloboma (iris, retina, optic nerve).
  • Microphthalmia, anophthalmia.
  • Strabismus.
  • Aniridia.

🧠 Other Potential Findings

  • Intellectual disability (can be present).
  • Dental anomalies (implying need for dental specialist).
  • Central nervous system anomalies.

💡 Diagnostic Pearls & Key Tests

Classic Clue: Cutaneous lesions following Blaschko lines are CLASSIC for FDH.

Diagnostic Pearls

  • Osteopathia striata on X-ray is a characteristic bony finding, but generally found in older patients, not neonates.
  • Vascular lesions around mucocutaneous junctions are usually not obvious in the neonatal period.

🔬 Best Tests

  • Skin Biopsy: Shows a decreased to absent dermis with a normal overlying epidermis. Adipose cells extend up to the epidermis (fat herniation).
  • Radiography (X-ray): Reveals osteopathia striata in long bones (especially metaphyses).

🔄 Differential Diagnosis & Pitfalls

Be ready to differentiate Goltz from these conditions:

  • Incontinentia pigmenti (also X-linked, Blaschko lines; but different stages, skin biopsy distinct)
  • Rothmund-Thomson syndrome (poikiloderma, skeletal, cataracts, but AR, photosensitivity)
  • Conradi-Hünermann syndrome (chondrodysplasia punctata, ichthyosis, cataracts)
  • Aplasia cutis congenita (isolated ACC vs. syndromic feature in Goltz)
  • Nevus lipomatosus superficialis (fat herniation, but localized, not usually linear/Blaschkoid in the same way, no systemic features)
  • Encephalocraniocutaneous lipomatosis (ipsilateral neurocutaneous findings, lipomas)
  • Ectrodactyly-ectodermal dysplasia-clefting (EEC) syndrome (key triad, AD)

Board Pitfall: Don't confuse the Blaschko lines of Goltz with Incontinentia Pigmenti without considering other features. Goltz has fat herniations and dermal hypoplasia; IP has distinct evolving stages (vesicular, verrucous, hyperpigmented, hypopigmented/atrophic).

🛠️ Management & Prognosis

Management Pearls

  • Treatment is supportive.
  • Requires a multidisciplinary team:
    • Dermatologist
    • Ophthalmologist
    • Dentist
    • Orthopedic surgeon

Therapy

Supportive care tailored to the patient's specific anomalies.

Prognosis

  • Patients generally have a normal lifespan.
  • May be handicapped by skeletal deformities.
  • Skin, eye, teeth, CNS, and skeletal anomalies can have psychosocial consequences.
