Graft-versus-Host Disease (GVHD) - Quick Review

\n\n
\n

Quick Summary Box

\n

Core Concept: GVHD is an immune-mediated disorder where donor T cells attack recipient tissues after allogeneic HSCT. The skin is the most commonly affected organ.

\n
    \n
  • Acute GVHD (aGVHD): Morbilliform exanthem, 4–6 weeks post-HSCT. Targets skin, liver, gut. Stage IV resembles TEN → high mortality.
    • \n
  • Chronic GVHD (cGVHD): Autoimmune-like, polymorphous. Mimics LP, LS, morphea, eosinophilic fasciitis.
    • \n
  • Key Management Shift: Steroid-refractory disease → aGVHD: Ruxolitinib; cGVHD: Ruxolitinib, Ibrutinib, Belumosudil.
    • \n
  • Differential: Differentiate from engraftment syndrome (earlier onset ~10–12 days, fever + pulmonary edema, steroid-responsive).
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

1. Definition & Classification

\n

GVHD = multi-organ immune disorder after HSCT. Traditional timing (<100d acute, >100d chronic) now less rigid; NIH criteria focus on clinical features.

\n
    \n
  • aGVHD: Classic acute form.
    • \n
  • Delayed aGVHD: Acute features after day 100.
    • \n
  • cGVHD: Distinct chronic features.
    • \n
  • Overlap: Acute + chronic simultaneously.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

2. Epidemiology

\n
    \n
  • Worldwide HSCT > 35,000 annually.
    • \n
  • GVHD: 40% in HLA-identical, 60–70% in mismatched.
    • \n
  • Post-transplant cyclophosphamide reduces incidence/severity.
    • \n
  • Risk factors: HLA mismatch, older recipient, unrelated donor, multiparous female donor → male, peripheral blood stem cell source, myeloablative regimen.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

3. Pathophysiology

\n

aGVHD cascade:

\n
    \n
  • Conditioning damage: Chemo/radiation → cytokine release, APC activation.
    • \n
  • Donor T-cell activation: APCs present host antigens → donor T cell proliferation.
    • \n
  • Effector phase: Donor CTLs, NK, cytokines (TNF, IFN-γ, IL-1) → tissue attack (skin, liver, gut).
    • \n
\n

cGVHD:

\n

Autoimmune-like, involving T cells, B cells (BAFF ↑), impaired Tregs, fibrosis via PDGFR antibodies. Autoantibodies common but nonspecific.

\n
\n\n
\n

4. Clinical Features

\n

Acute GVHD:

\n
    \n
  • Skin: Acral morbilliform exanthem. Stages I–IV → Stage IV = TEN-like, fatal.
    • \n
  • Gut: Diarrhea, nausea, ileus.
    • \n
  • Liver: Cholestasis, ↑bilirubin.
    • \n
\n\n

Chronic GVHD:

\n

Polymorphous. Diagnostic skin lesions:

\n
    \n
  • Lichen planus-like
    • \n
  • Lichen sclerosus-like
    • \n
  • Morphea-like
    • \n
  • Scleroderma-like
    • \n
  • Eosinophilic fasciitis-like
    • \n
\n

+ mucosal, eye, lung, liver, musculoskeletal involvement.

\n
\n\n
\n

5. Investigations

\n
    \n
  • Biopsy aGVHD: Vacuolar change, keratinocyte necrosis.
    • \n
  • Biopsy cGVHD: Lichenoid vs sclerotic.
    • \n
  • MRI: Fascial involvement.
    • \n
  • Labs: Autoantibodies, eosinophilia (cGVHD).
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

6. Management

\n

aGVHD:

\n
    \n
  • Mild: Topical steroids.
    • \n
  • Severe: Systemic steroids.
    • \n
  • Steroid-refractory: Ruxolitinib (preferred).
    • \n
\n\n

cGVHD:

\n
    \n
  • Localized: Topicals, phototherapy.
    • \n
  • Severe: Systemic steroids.
    • \n
  • Steroid-refractory: Ruxolitinib, Ibrutinib, Belumosudil.
    • \n
  • Others: ECP, sirolimus, imatinib, rituximab.
    • \n
  • Supportive: PT, sun protection, skin cancer screening, ocular/oral/genital care.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

7. Prognosis

\n
    \n
  • aGVHD: Stage IV → very high mortality.
    • \n
  • cGVHD: Chronic morbidity, infections, secondary cancers, steroid complications.
    • \n
