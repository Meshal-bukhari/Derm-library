Introduction to Vesiculopustular Eruptions in Neonates

Vesicles, pustules, bullae, erosions, and ulcerations are common presentations in the newborn period and infancy. Accurate and rapid diagnosis is crucial, as the differential includes life-threatening infections alongside benign, self-limited conditions.

A systematic approach, including bedside tests and appropriate cultures/biopsies, is essential for evaluation and management.

When approaching a neonate with vesiculopustular eruptions:

  1. Consider the timing of onset (at birth vs. days later)
  2. Evaluate the morphology and distribution of lesions
  3. Perform appropriate bedside tests (Wright/Giemsa, Gram stain, KOH)
  4. Assess for signs of systemic illness
  5. Consider both infectious and non-infectious causes

  • Congenital Candidiasis: Widespread, involves palms/soles

Common Benign Conditions

Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum (ETN)

Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum (ETN)
Onset Usually 24-48 hours after birth (rarely at birth, can be up to 1-2 weeks)
Morphology Erythematous macules, small papules (1-2mm), vesicles, pustules, and wheals; often described as "flea bite-like" with a central papule/pustule and surrounding erythematous flare
Cell Infiltrate Primarily eosinophils (confirmed by Wright/Giemsa stain of pustule contents)
Distribution Trunk common, also face, proximal limbs, buttocks. Almost always spares palms and soles
Epidemiology Affects ~50% of full-term neonates (>2500g), rare in premature infants
Investigations Usually clinical dx. Wright/Giemsa stain confirms eosinophils
Treatment None required. Reassurance

Remember ETN with: E-T-N

  • Eosinophils in pustules
  • Two days after birth (24-48h onset)
  • Never on palms and soles

ETN must be differentiated from TNPM: remember that ETN shows eosinophils on Wright stain, appears 24-48 hours after birth, has prominent erythema, and spares palms and soles.

Transient Neonatal Pustular Melanosis (TNPM)

Transient Neonatal Pustular Melanosis (TNPM)
Synonyms Transient neonatal pustulosis, Lentigines neonatorum
Onset Present at birth
Morphology Three stages:
  1. Superficial vesiculopustules (2-10mm) without significant erythema
  2. Ruptured pustules leave fine collarettes of scale
  3. Residual hyperpigmented macules that persist for weeks to months
Cell Infiltrate Primarily neutrophils, occasional eosinophils (Wright/Giemsa stain)
Distribution Forehead, chin, nape of neck, lower back, buttocks, shins common. Can involve palms and soles
Epidemiology More common in infants with darker skin phototypes (~5% of Black newborns)
Investigations Usually clinical dx. Wright/Giemsa stain shows neutrophils
Treatment None required. Reassurance

Remember TNPM with: T-N-P-M

  • There at birth (present at birth)
  • Neutrophils in pustules
  • Palms and soles can be involved
  • Melanin hyperpigmentation follows

TNPM is most commonly confused with ETN. Key distinguishing features are onset at birth (vs. 24-48h for ETN), neutrophils (vs. eosinophils in ETN), minimal erythema (vs. prominent in ETN), and residual hyperpigmentation (vs. none in ETN).

Miliaria

Miliaria
Synonyms Heat rash, dew drops (crystallina), prickly heat (rubra)
Pathophysiology Obstruction of eccrine sweat ducts. Associated with overheating, swaddling, fever, occlusion
Variants Miliaria Crystallina: Superficial obstruction (stratum corneum). Onset birth/early infancy. Tiny (1-2mm), clear, fragile, non-inflammatory vesicles ("dew drops")

Miliaria Rubra: Deeper obstruction (spinous layer). Onset usually ≥1 week. Small erythematous papules, papulovesicles, pustules
Cell Infiltrate M. crystallina: minimal inflammation. M. rubra: variable inflammatory cells (not prominent eosinophils)
Distribution Forehead, neck, upper trunk, occluded areas
Treatment Cooling measures, avoid overheating/occlusion. Resolves spontaneously

Remember the types of Miliaria with: C-R-P

  • Crystallina: Clear vesicles, Clear of inflammation
  • Rubra: Red papules, Reactive inflammation
  • Profunda: rare in infants, Profound (deeper) obstruction

Distinguish Miliaria from ETN/TNPM by association with overheating, lack of specific cell type on Wright/Giemsa stain, and improvement with cooling. Miliaria crystallina appears as clear "dew drops" without inflammation.

Neonatal Cephalic Pustulosis (NCP)

Neonatal Cephalic Pustulosis (NCP)
Synonym Historically "Neonatal Acne" (misnomer)
Onset Typically ~5 days to 3 weeks of life
Morphology Inflammatory papules and pustules on an erythematous base. No comedones (key difference from infantile acne)
Cell Infiltrate Giemsa stain may show neutrophils and yeast forms (Malassezia spp.)
Distribution Cheeks most common; also forehead, chin, eyelids, less often neck, upper chest, scalp
Pathogenesis Inflammatory reaction possibly related to Malassezia spp. colonization
Course Resolves spontaneously over weeks to months (by 3 months). No scarring
Treatment Usually not necessary. Topical imidazole or low-potency hydrocortisone sometimes used
NCP vs. Infantile Acne
NCP
  • Onset: 5 days to 3 weeks
  • NO comedones
  • Possible Malassezia association
  • Resolves by 3 months
  • No scarring
Infantile Acne
  • Onset: 6 weeks to 1 year
  • Comedones present
  • Hormonal influence
  • Can last 1-2 years
  • Potential scarring

Remember NCP vs. Infantile Acne with: COMED

  • COmedones - absent in NCP, present in infantile acne
  • Malassezia - associated with NCP
  • Early onset - NCP (days to weeks), Infantile acne (months)
  • Duration - NCP (short), Infantile acne (longer)

Sucking Blisters

Sucking Blisters
Onset Present at birth
Morphology Intact flaccid bulla or, more commonly, a solitary erosion or ulceration on a non-inflamed base. Linear or round, 5-15mm
Pathogenesis Vigorous sucking on the affected area in utero
Distribution Radial forearm, wrist, hands, fingers most common; occasionally foot. Unilateral or bilateral
Complications None; isolated benign finding
Treatment Supportive care; heals spontaneously in days to weeks

Remember Sucking Blisters with: SUCK

  • Solitary lesion
  • Unilateral (typically)
  • Common on wrists/hands
  • Known to heal spontaneously

Differentiate sucking blisters from other blistering disorders by their characteristic location (distal upper extremity), solitary nature, presence at birth, and lack of inflammation.

Infectious Conditions

Infectious vesiculopustular eruptions in neonates often require immediate intervention and have systemic implications. Always consider HSV, bacterial sepsis, and congenital/neonatal candidiasis in the differential diagnosis of vesicopustular eruptions in neonates.

Cutaneous Candidiasis

Congenital vs. Neonatal Candidiasis
Congenital Candidiasis
  • Acquired in utero (ascending infection)
  • Onset at birth or first few days (up to day 6)
  • Widespread eruption - erythematous papules, vesiculopustules, diffuse erythema, fine scaling
  • Palms and soles often involved
  • Diaper area and oral mucosa usually spared
  • Higher systemic risk, especially if premature
Neonatal Candidiasis
  • Acquired during delivery or postnatally
  • Onset ≥1 week of age
  • Beefy red patches with satellite papules/pustules
  • Distribution: Diaper area, intertriginous zones, face
  • Oral thrush often present
  • Usually benign in healthy term infants

"Invasive Fungal Dermatitis"

Seen in very-low-birth-weight (VLBW) premature newborns (often <1000g) with cutaneous candidiasis:

  • Widespread "burn-like" erythema with desquamation and erosions
  • High risk of systemic dissemination
  • Treatment: Systemic antifungal therapy required (parenteral Amphotericin B)

Remember Candidiasis types with: C-N-I

  • Congenital: Completely at birth, Covers widely, Capable of systemic spread
  • Neonatal: Now at 1 week+, Nappies (diaper) affected, Nice prognosis
  • Invasive: Immature babies (<1000g), Intense ("burn-like") appearance, IV antifungals required

High-yield for the board: VLBW premature infants (<1000g) with congenital candidiasis require immediate systemic antifungal therapy (Amphotericin B) regardless of clinical appearance, as they have high risk of systemic dissemination.

Bacterial Infections

Organism Onset Morphology Key Features Diagnosis
Staphylococcus aureus Few days to weeks+ Pustules, flaccid bullae, erosions with collarettes, crusts Favors diaper, periumbilical areas, intertriginous zones Gram stain (GPC in clusters), culture
Group A Streptococcal (GAS) Few days to weeks+ Pustules, honey-colored crusts, fiery red intertriginous erythema Any region, umbilical stump, skin folds Gram stain (GPC in chains), rapid antigen, culture
Group B Streptococcal (GBS) Birth to first few days Vesicles, bullae, erosions, pustules, crusts Any body region. High risk of systemic disease Gram stain (GPC in chains), culture
Listeria monocytogenes Birth to first few hours Hemorrhagic pustules, petechiae Associated with sepsis, respiratory distress; maternal fever history Gram stain (Gram+ rods), bacterial culture
Pseudomonas aeruginosa Days to weeks+ Pustules, hemorrhagic bullae, necrotic ulcerations (ecthyma gangrenosum) Any region, esp. anogenital area Gram stain (Gram- rods), culture

Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)

  • Exfoliative toxins from S. aureus target desmoglein-1
  • Tender erythema → flaccid bullae → widespread peeling in sheets
  • Nikolsky sign positive
  • Periorificial and intertriginous accentuation
  • Mucous membranes usually spared (unlike TEN)
  • Treatment: Systemic anti-staphylococcal antibiotics

Remember bacterial causes with: GLSP

  • G-roup B Strep: Generalized distribution, early onset, high sepsis risk
  • Listeria: Like hemorrhagic pustules, Long rods on Gram stain
  • Staph: Skin erosions with scale collarettes, clusters on Gram stain
  • Pseudomonas: Purple-black necrotic lesions (ecthyma gangrenosum)

Critically important: GBS and Listeria infections in neonates have high risk of sepsis and meningitis. Hemorrhagic pustules, respiratory distress, or temperature instability are red flags warranting immediate systemic antibiotics.

Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Infection

Neonatal vs. Intrauterine HSV
Neonatal HSV
  • Acquired perinatally
  • Onset birth to 2 weeks (usually after 5 days)
  • Grouped vesicles or pustules on an erythematous base
  • Distribution: Any region, scalp and torso favored
  • High risk of CNS and disseminated disease
Intrauterine HSV
  • Acquired in utero
  • Onset at birth
  • Vesicles, pustules, widespread erosions, scars, areas of aplasia cutis
  • Any body region
  • Associated with low birth weight, microcephaly, chorioretinitis

HSV Diagnosis and Management

  • Tzanck smear: Multinucleated giant cells (less sensitive)
  • PCR: Most sensitive and preferred method
  • CSF PCR mandatory if HSV infection suspected
  • Treatment: Systemic antiviral therapy (Acyclovir) required immediately upon suspicion

Remember HSV features with: VESICLE

  • Vesicles clustered in groups
  • Erythematous base
  • Scalp/torso common sites
  • Intrauterine form present at birth
  • CSF PCR always needed
  • Life-threatening if untreated
  • Emergent Acyclovir required

Critical board point: Any vesicular eruption in a neonate requires consideration of HSV. CSF PCR is mandatory to exclude CNS involvement. Acyclovir should be started immediately upon suspicion, before confirmation.

Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV) Infections

Type Onset Morphology Distribution Cause
Neonatal Varicella Birth to 2 weeks Vesicles on erythematous base ("dew drop on a rose petal") Generalized Maternal primary varicella 7 days before to 2 days after delivery
Herpes Zoster (Shingles) Typically ≥2 weeks Vesicles on erythematous base Dermatomal pattern Prior neonatal varicella or maternal infection during pregnancy
Congenital Varicella Syndrome At birth Erosions, ulcers, scarring, aplasia cutis congenita Often extremities, may follow dermatomal pattern Maternal varicella in 1st or early 2nd trimester

Remember VZV types with: NZC

  • Neonatal Varicella: Generalized vesicles, high risk if mom infected 7d before to 2d after delivery
  • Zoster: Zone of dermatome, reactivation of prior infection
  • Congenital Syndrome: Cicatricial (scarring) lesions, Caused by 1st trimester infection

Congenital Varicella Syndrome presents with scarring and limb abnormalities at birth, whereas Neonatal Varicella presents with typical vesicular eruption after birth. The timing of maternal infection is critical (1st trimester vs. peripartum).

Scabies

Scabies
Onset Typically ≥3-4 weeks of age (incubation period)
Morphology Papules, nodules (often reddish-brown), crusted areas, vesicles, pustules, burrows (linear or S-shaped). Intense pruritus
Distribution Any region including head, neck, palms, soles (unlike older children/adults). Favors axillae, groin, wrists
Investigations Mineral oil preparation (scraping from burrow/papule) to identify mites, eggs, or scybala (feces). Dermoscopy can help visualize burrows/mites
Treatment Treat infant and all household contacts simultaneously with permethrin 5% cream
Complications Post-scabetic trigger for Acropustulosis of Infancy (AI)
Scabies vs. Acropustulosis of Infancy (AI)
Scabies
  • Onset: ≥3-4 weeks (incubation period)
  • Burrows may be visible
  • Mites/eggs/feces on scraping
  • Family members often affected
  • Responds to permethrin treatment
Acropustulosis of Infancy
  • Onset: Usually 3-6 months (can be earlier)
  • Recurring crops of vesicles/pustules
  • No mites on scraping
  • Often occurs after treated scabies
  • Responds to potent topical steroids

Remember Scabies features with: MITES

  • Mineral oil prep diagnostic
  • Intense pruritus
  • Treat all household contacts
  • Everywhere distribution in infants (including head)
  • S-shaped burrows pathognomonic

Key board point: Always perform mineral oil scraping to exclude scabies in infants with acral pustules/vesicles. Scabies in infants can affect any body region including head, neck, palms and soles (unlike in adults).

Uncommon/Rare Non-Infectious Conditions
Acropustulosis of Infancy (AI)

Onset Classic form usually 3-6 months (occasionally birth to weeks). Post-scabetic form follows scabies infestation.

Morphology Intensely pruritic vesicles and pustules recurring in crops (lasting 7-14 days, recurring every 3-5 weeks).

Cell Infiltrate Neutrophils primarily, +/- eosinophils (smear/biopsy).

Distribution Hands and feet (palms, soles, dorsal aspects, lateral digits); occasionally scalp, trunk, proximal extremities.

Investigations Clinical dx, rule out scabies (mineral oil prep essential). Skin biopsy supportive (subcorneal pustule with neutrophils).

Treatment: Symptomatic: potent topical corticosteroids, oral antihistamines.

Board Alert: Most important differential is scabies - always perform mineral oil prep to exclude active infestation.

Eosinophilic Pustular Folliculitis (EPF)

Onset Mean 6 months (range birth to 14 months).

Morphology Recurrent crops of pruritic follicular papules, pustules, vesicles on erythematous base, often evolving to hemorrhagic crusts. Scarring possible.

Cell Infiltrate Eosinophils (smear/biopsy). Histology shows dense eosinophilic infiltrate, often centered on hair follicles.

Distribution Scalp is the most common site; also face (esp. brows), less often trunk, extremities.

Investigations Clinical suspicion, smear/biopsy confirms eosinophils. Peripheral eosinophilia often present.

Treatment: Symptomatic: mid-potency topical steroids, topical tacrolimus, oral antihistamines. Oral dapsone/antibiotics for severe cases.

Board Alert: Can be histologically identical to neonatal AD-HIES rash. Consider HIES if persistent/severe or later systemic features develop.

Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH)

Onset Birth or first few weeks.

Morphology Highly variable: crusted papules, vesiculopustules, nodules, erosions, ulcers, petechiae, purpura, atrophy, milia, hemangioma-like lesions. Can be solitary or widespread.

Cell Infiltrate Biopsy shows Langerhans cells (large, ovoid, "coffee bean" nuclei) staining positive for S100, CD1a, CD207 (Langerin).

Distribution Any site, including palms/soles. Favors flexural sites, scalp.

Investigations Skin biopsy is diagnostic. Mandatory systemic workup: Full exam, CBC diff, LFTs, Chem panel, Urine osmolality, Abd US, Skeletal survey, CXR.

Course: Purely cutaneous form often resolves spontaneously ("Congenital Self-Healing Reticulohistiocytosis"), but requires monitoring for potential systemic involvement later.

Board Alert: All cases require biopsy with IHC (S100/CD1a/CD207) and complete systemic workup, even if clinically skin-limited.

Incontinentia Pigmenti (IP)

Onset Usually within first 2 weeks (vesicular stage).

Morphology Follows Lines of Blaschko in 4 stages:

  1. Vesicular: Linear vesicles/bullae on erythematous base
  2. Verrucous: Hyperkeratotic linear papules/plaques
  3. Hyperpigmented: Swirled/linear grey-brown macules
  4. Hypopigmented/Atrophic: Subtle linear streaks

Cell Infiltrate Stage 1 Biopsy: Eosinophilic spongiosis, scattered necrotic keratinocytes, dermal eosinophils.

Distribution Lesions follow Blaschko lines.

Investigations Clinical dx often possible. Skin biopsy (Stage 1) characteristic. Genetic testing available (IKBKG/NEMO gene). Ophthalmology consult mandatory.

Genetics: X-linked dominant, usually lethal in males.

Board Alert: Associated with dental, ocular, and CNS abnormalities. Ophthalmology screening is essential due to risk of blindness from retinal vascular issues.

AD Hyper-IgE Syndrome (AD-HIES)

Onset Neonatal rash (papulopustular/vesicular) in 70-80%, often at birth or first month (mean 7 days).

Morphology Papulopustules, vesicles, often crusted. Biopsy shows eosinophils, intraepidermal vesicles, eosinophilic folliculitis (similar to EPF).

Cell Infiltrate Eosinophils, similar to EPF histologically.

Distribution Favors face, scalp, neck, shoulders; also axillae, diaper area.

Investigations Clinical suspicion, STAT3 mutation analysis. IgE/Eosinophil count (may be normal early).

Later Features: "Cold" abscesses, chronic eczematous dermatitis, recurrent pneumonias (pneumatoceles), osteopenia, retained primary teeth.

Board Alert: Neonatal rash of HIES can be histologically identical to EPF. Consider HIES if persistent/severe or later systemic features develop.

Vesiculopustular Eruption of TMD in Down Syndrome

Onset First few days to weeks of life.

Morphology Vesicles and pustules. Pathergy common (sites of minor trauma, adhesive dressings).

Cell Infiltrate Skin biopsy shows spongiotic vesiculopustules with infiltrate containing immature myeloid cells. Peripheral blood also shows leukocytosis with immature myeloid cells.

Distribution Starts on face, often spreads to trunk and extremities.

Investigations Clinical context (Down syndrome or features), CBC with diff (leukocytosis, blasts), Skin biopsy. Karyotype if mosaicism suspected.

Association: Neonates with Trisomy 21 (or mosaicism) who have Transient Myeloproliferative Disorder (congenital leukemoid reaction due to somatic GATA1 mutations).

Board Alert: Consider in any neonate with unexplained vesiculopustular eruption and leukocytosis, even without obvious Down syndrome phenotype. Increased risk of developing myeloid leukemia in first few years of life.

Congenital Erosive and Vesicular Dermatosis (CEVD)

Onset Present at birth.

Morphology Widespread erosions, vesicles, crusting, "scalded skin" appearance (up to 75% BSA). Heals over 1-2 months leaving characteristic supple, reticulated scarring.

Distribution Generalized, but often relative sparing of face, palms, and soles.

Investigations Diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out infections (esp. HSV) and genetic blistering disorders. Skin biopsy is non-specific.

Associations: Almost always occurs in premature infants. Later sequelae can include scarring alopecia, nail hypoplasia, hypohidrosis, neurodevelopmental abnormalities.

Board Alert: Must exclude intrauterine HSV infection, which can mimic CEVD clinically. Perform HSV PCR. The distinctive pattern of healing with reticulated scarring but relative sparing of face/palms/soles helps differentiate.

Bullous/Erosive Diseases
Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)

Onset Few days to weeks (rarely congenital).

Morphology Tender erythematous patches → fragile, flaccid bullae → widespread superficial erosions and peeling in sheets ("scalded" appearance). Nikolsky sign positive.

Distribution Generalized, often with periorificial (mouth, eyes) and intertriginous accentuation. Mucous membranes usually spared (unlike TEN).

Investigations Clinical diagnosis often. Skin biopsy shows split high in epidermis (granular layer). Bacterial culture positive only from primary site of infection (toxin-mediated).

Pathogenesis: Exfoliative toxins (ET-A, ET-B) from a localized S. aureus infection target desmoglein-1, causing intraepidermal split at the granular layer.

Treatment: Systemic anti-staphylococcal antibiotics, supportive care (wound care, fluid management).

Board Alert: Differentiate from TEN by sparing of mucous membranes and superficial split (granular layer in SSSS vs. subepidermal in TEN).

Epidermolysis Bullosa (EB)

Onset Usually at birth or first few days/weeks.

Morphology Mechanically induced blisters and erosions due to skin fragility. Severity and associated features (scarring, milia, nail dystrophy, mucosal involvement) depend on the specific type and subtype.

Distribution Varies with type; often affects trauma-prone sites (hands, feet, elbows, knees).

Investigations Skin biopsy for immunofluorescence antigen mapping and/or electron microscopy on an induced blister. Genetic testing confirms subtype and mutation.

Types: Simplex (intraepidermal), Junctional (lamina lucida), Dystrophic (below lamina densa), Kindler (mixed).

Board Alert: The level of split on IF mapping or EM is diagnostic for classifying EB type. Specific mutations correlate with phenotypic severity and associated features.

Epidermolytic Ichthyosis (EI)

Onset At birth.

Morphology Generalized erythroderma with blistering and erosions, leading to superficial peeling. Over time, blistering decreases and hyperkeratosis develops (often corrugated/verrucous, esp. in flexures).

Distribution Generalized.

Investigations Skin biopsy shows characteristic epidermolytic hyperkeratosis (vacuolar degeneration of suprabasal keratinocytes, coarse keratohyalin granules). Genetic testing confirms KRT1 or KRT10 mutation.

Genetics: Autosomal dominant, mutations in KRT1 or KRT10.

Board Alert: In neonatal period, can be confused with SSSS or congenital candidiasis. The histologic finding of epidermolytic hyperkeratosis is diagnostic.

Other Causes of Erosions/Ulcerations
  • Iatrogenic: Fetal scalp electrode marks, adhesive tape stripping, pressure sores, irritant dermatitis (e.g., chlorhexidine - geometric erosions)
  • Aplasia Cutis Congenita (ACC): Localized absence of skin at birth, typically on scalp vertex. Can be membranous or ulcerated/scarred
  • Ulcerated Infantile Hemangioma: Often presents as ulcer before hemangioma proliferates
  • Neonatal Lupus Erythematosus (NLE): Can present with erosions/ulcers, often on face, associated with maternal anti-Ro/La antibodies
  • Pyoderma Gangrenosum (PG): Rare in neonates, undermined ulcers, often requires workup for underlying disease
  • Acrodermatitis Enteropathica: Zinc deficiency, erosive dermatitis in periorificial and acral distribution

Board Alert: Always consider iatrogenic causes, especially in NICU setting. Geometric patterns suggest external causes. Location can be key - ACC typically on scalp, umbilical cord abnormalities with erosions may suggest EB.

Diagnostic Clues and Bedside Tests

Algorithm Approach to Vesiculopustular Eruptions

Start with bedside tests based on primary morphology (vesicle/pustule):

1. Tzanck Smear

Quick bedside test for viral infections (HSV, VZV). Scrape base of fresh vesicle, stain with Wright-Giemsa or Giemsa stain.

  • Positive: Multinucleated giant cells (HSV/VZV)
  • Negative: Does not rule out viral infection

2. KOH Preparation

For suspected fungal infections (candidiasis, tinea).

  • Positive: Budding yeast, pseudohyphae (Candida) or hyphae (dermatophytes)

3. Gram Stain

For bacterial infections.

  • Gram-positive cocci: S. aureus, GBS
  • Gram-negative rods: Pseudomonas, E. coli

4. Mineral Oil Preparation

For scabies. Scrape burrow or papule, examine under microscope.

  • Positive: Mites, eggs, or fecal pellets (scybala)

5. Tzanck Smear vs. KOH vs. Gram Stain

Test Best For Results
Tzanck Viral infections (HSV, VZV) Multinucleated giant cells
KOH Fungal infections (Candida, tinea) Hyphae, pseudohyphae, spores
Gram Stain Bacterial infections Gram-positive/negative organisms

Board Tip: Always perform a Tzanck smear in any neonate with vesiculopustular lesions to rapidly evaluate for HSV, which requires immediate treatment. Negative Tzanck does not rule out HSV - if clinical suspicion is high, start acyclovir and send PCR.
