Gout

Definition & Epidemiology

Gout is a metabolic disease in which needle-like crystals of monosodium urate (ionized form of uric acid) are deposited in tissue.

  • Most common form of crystal-induced arthritis
  • Typically affects men in their 40s-50s
  • Male to female ratio is 9:1
  • Risk factors: Obesity, alcohol consumption, renal disease, and diuretic use
Pathogenesis
  • Uric acid is the end product of purine catabolism
  • When body fluids become supersaturated with uric acid, crystallization occurs
  • Urate crystals activate the NLRP3 (cryopyrin) inflammasome
  • This leads to production of IL-1, triggering inflammation
  • When neutrophils phagocytose urate crystals, it leads to neutrophil leakage of lysosomes → vicious cycle of inflammation

Clinical Features: The Four Stages

Stage 1: Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia

High uric acid levels without symptoms

Stage 2: Acute Gouty Arthritis

  • Severe sudden joint pain
  • Tenderness, erythema, warmth, and swelling
  • Most commonly affects the big toe (podagra)
  • May be polyarticular
  • Fever and systemic symptoms may occur
  • As swelling subsides, violaceous hue + desquamation appear

Stage 3: Intercritical Gout

Intervals between attacks

Progressively shorter duration with time

Stage 4: Chronic Tophaceous Gout

  • Monosodium urate deposits known as "tophi"
  • Appears ~10 years after onset of gout
  • Less than 10% of patients develop tophi
  • Firm dermal or subcutaneous papules/nodules
  • Skin-colored, erythematous, or yellow-white
  • May have smooth or multilobulated contours
  • Surface may ulcerate and drain fluid or chalky material
  • Common locations: skin overlying joints and ear helix
Extracutaneous Manifestations
  • Uric acid nephrolithiasis - acute renal failure
  • Most commonly seen in the setting of tumor lysis syndrome
Diagnosis
  • Typically made during an acute attack of arthritis
  • Joint fluid aspiration → urate crystals
  • When examined by polarized light microscopy, urate crystals exhibit negative birefringence (change color from yellow to blue)
  • 24hr urine collection - detect risk of nephrolithiasis
  • X-ray → "punched-out" erosions with sclerotic "overhanging" margins, but no osteophytes

Histopathology

  • Biopsy specimen needs to be placed in an ethanol-based fixative (Carnoy's fluid)
  • Deposits of amorphous material within the dermis and subcutis
  • These deposits contain needle-like clefts (which represent dissolved urate crystals)
  • Surrounded by granulomatous inflammation
  • Secondary calcification or ossification can be seen
  • Upon staining with 20% silver nitrate solution, the crystals appear black
  • De Galantha stain → brown-black
Treatment

For acute gout:

  • NSAIDs (indomethacin 50 mg TID) or colchicine
  • Intra-articular or systemic steroids

For chronic gout:

  • Allopurinol and febuxostat
  • Protein and alcohol avoidance
  • IL-1 blockers

In tumor lysis syndrome:

  • IV rasburicase

Goal: Maintenance of serum uric acid ≤6 mg/dl

Pseudogout

  • Clinically indistinguishable from gout
  • Caused by deposition of calcium pyrophosphate (CPPD) crystals
  • Associated with osteoarthritis (most common), hyperparathyroidism, and hemochromatosis
  • On polarized microscopy, exhibits positive birefringence (in contrast to gout's negative birefringence)

Lipoid Proteinosis

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive

Mutation: ECM-1 (extracellular matrix protein 1)

Deposition of hyaline-like material in the skin, larynx, oral mucosa, and CNS

In CNS involvement, sickle-shaped/bean-shaped calcification within the amygdalae is seen on imaging

Clinical Features
  • The first clinical finding is a weak cry
  • The most common finding is hoarseness

Cutaneous lesions appear within the first 2 years of life, in stages:

First Stage

  • Vesicles and hemorrhagic crusts on the skin of the face and extremities
  • Resolves with icepick-like scars

Second Stage

  • The skin becomes diffusely thickened and develops a yellow hue
  • Papules, plaques, and nodules may appear anywhere
  • Involvement of the eyelid margin is highly testable
  • Verrucous lesions may develop on the elbows, knees, and hands
  • Alopecia of the eyebrows and eyelashes
  • Corneal ulcers may occur due to infiltration of the eyelids
  • Tongue involvement leads to macroglossia
  • Involvement of the frenulum leads to restricted motion of the tongue → inability to protrude the tongue
  • Recurrent parotitis can occur due to occlusion of the salivary duct

Other Manifestations

  • Dental → tooth hyperplasia or aplasia, typically lose their teeth at an early stage
  • Neurologic manifestations include seizures, learning disability, amygdala dysfunction, and intracerebral hemorrhage
Pathology
  • Amorphous, eosinophilic mostly acellular hyaline material vertically oriented in the papillary dermis
  • The hyaline surrounds the adnexal structures and contains collagen (type II and IV) and laminin
  • PAS-positive and diastase-resistant
Treatment
  • No curative treatment available
  • Steroids have some beneficial effects
  • Plastic surgery and dermabrasion have been successfully performed
  • Acitretin, D-penicillamine, and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) have shown limited benefits in some reports

Colloid Millium

Types & Etiology

Four subtypes: adult, juvenile, pigmented, and nodular

  • Adult colloid milium is the most common type
  • Occurs in middle-aged whites with significant sun exposure
  • May be associated with petrochemicals exposure
  • Juvenile type develops before puberty, and AD/AR forms exist
  • Pigmented type is associated with topical hydroquinone or phenols
Pathogenesis
  • Exact mechanism unknown
  • Sun exposure plays a role in adult type
  • Photo-induced damage to elastic fibers is the theory, with colloid being the final product of degenerated elastin
Clinical Features
  • Multiple, dome-shaped translucent to yellow or yellow-brown papules
  • Found on chronically sun-exposed sites
  • Face (especially periorbital), ears, posterolateral neck, and the dorsal hands
  • With minor trauma, lesions can become hemorrhagic
  • In pigmented type, grouped gray-brown papules are present on the face
  • In nodular type, skin-colored to yellowish nodules or plaques develop, often on the face
Pathology
  • Nodules composed of homogeneous eosinophilic colloid material in the papillary dermis
  • Clefts and fissures within the nodules
  • Grenz zone is often present, as well as solar elastosis
  • Positive for crystal violet, Congo red and thioflavin T (amyloidosis-like)
  • In adult type, negative pan-cytokeratin stain is helpful in distinguishing from amyloidosis (positive in juvenile type)
Treatment

Local destructive methods are the primary treatment approach

Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS)

A group of inherited disorders characterized by deficiencies of enzymes involved in the catabolism of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs).

General Features
  • Most are autosomal recessive
  • Excessive amounts of GAG (mainly Dermatan sulfate & Heparan sulfate) are found in different tissues
  • Leads to coarse facies, intellectual disability, hepatosplenomegaly, skeletal abnormalities, and corneal clouding
  • Cutaneous features are typically non-specific
  • Hunter syndrome (XLR, IDS) has characteristic pebbled ivory-colored plaques between the scapulae
Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnostic Tests

  • Examination of urine, peripheral leukocytes, and dermal fibroblasts for excessive GAGs

Treatment

  • Supportive care
  • Enzyme replacement therapy
  • Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT)
