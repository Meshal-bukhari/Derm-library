Ichthyoses & Erythrokeratodermas

High-Yield Board Review for Saudi Board Exam

Quick Definition & Core Concepts

Ichthyoses and erythrokeratodermas are disorders of cornification in which abnormal differentiation and desquamation of the epidermis result in a defective cutaneous barrier.

  • Ichthyoses: Characterized by scaling of the skin in a widespread distribution (from Greek "ichthy" meaning fish)
  • Erythrokeratodermas: Feature discrete areas of erythema and hyperkeratosis, usually without substantial scale

Most important diagnostic factors: (1) determine timing of onset (birth vs. later), (2) assess for multisystem involvement, and (3) evaluate the quality/distribution of scale, presence of erythroderma, blistering, and adnexal abnormalities.

Diagnostic Approach to Ichthyosis

Key History Elements

  • Prenatal/birth history: Prenatal diagnoses (e.g., heart defects), prolonged labor (suggestive of steroid sulfatase deficiency), prematurity (characteristic of harlequin ichthyosis)
  • Consanguinity: Makes autosomal recessive ichthyosis more likely
  • Family history: Epidermal nevi, ichthyosis, or erythrokeratoderma

Physical Examination Findings

  • Types of skin findings: Collodion membrane, bullae/erosions, erythroderma, peeling, diffuse scaling, circumscribed areas of erythema/hyperkeratosis
  • Distribution patterns: Generalized, unilateral, following lines of Blaschko, regional
  • Adnexal evaluation: Scalp hair, eyebrows, eyelashes (including trichoscopy); nails; mucous membranes
  • Systemic findings: Microcephaly, dysmorphic facies, asymmetry, contractures, hepatosplenomegaly

Basic Laboratory Evaluation

  • Complete blood count
  • Electrolytes
  • Hepatic panel
  • Immunoglobulin levels (including IgE)
  • Peripheral blood smear to evaluate for vacuoles in leukocytes (suggests neutral lipid storage disease or multiple sulfatase deficiency)

Additional Assessments

  • Hearing screen (auditory brainstem response or oto-acoustic emission test)
  • Ophthalmologic examination
  • X-rays to evaluate epiphyses (especially if ichthyosiform lesions are unilateral or in a mosaic distribution pattern)

Genetic Testing Approach

Preferred approach: A multigene panel is preferable over single gene testing if the diagnosis is not evident based on clinical and laboratory findings

Alternative Testing Methods

  • FISH and microarray are alternatives if suspect steroid sulfatase deficiency
  • Following resolution of collodion membrane (if present), immunohistochemical staining of skin biopsy for transglutaminase-1, LEKTI, or filaggrin

Histopathologic Evaluation

  • Timing: Consider skin biopsy particularly if bullae/erosions present (to assess for epidermolysis)
  • Ichthyosis vulgaris: Look for diminished granular layer (more evident in severe forms)
  • Characteristic histopathologic features: Can help recognize specific disorders, such as epidermolytic ichthyosis and ichthyosis hystrix Curth-Macklin

Special Stains & Processing

  • Lipid assessment: Lipid antibodies (e.g., adipophilin) or stains (e.g., oil red O, Sudan III) to detect cytoplasmic lipid droplets in paraffin-embedded or fresh frozen skin sections for neutral lipid storage disease
  • Cell cultures: Fibroblast or keratinocyte cultures of skin biopsy specimen to measure FALDH activity or check for elevated free fatty alcohols if suspect Sjögren-Larsson syndrome
  • Electron microscopy: Using ruthenium tetroxide fixation to assess for abnormalities in the epidermal lipid system

Hair Abnormalities

  • "Tiger tail" hair: Trichothiodystrophy (pathognomonic)
  • Trichorrhexis invaginata: Netherton syndrome (pathognomonic)
  • Hypotrichosis: Multiple conditions including KID syndrome, Netherton syndrome, trichothiodystrophy and many other
  • Trichorrhexis nodosa: Netherton syndrome, Trichothiodystrophy

  • Peeling skin: Netherton syndrome, Peeling skin syndrome
  • Hystrix-like hyperkeratosis: Epidermolytic ichthyosis, Ichthyosis hystrix

  • Bullae/blisters: Epidermolytic ichthyosis, Superficial epidermolytic ichthyosis

Common Ichthyoses You MUST Know

Ichthyosis Vulgaris

Gene: FLG (Filaggrin)

Inheritance: Autosomal semi-dominant

Incidence: 1:100-1:1750 (Most common disorder of cornification)

Onset: Infancy/childhood

Key features: Fine, adherent scales on extremities and trunk with sparing of flexures; larger scales on lower legs; hyperlinear palms/soles, furrowed heels

Associated: Keratosis pilaris, atopic dermatitis (25-50% of patients)

Diagnosis: Clinical; histology shows diminished/absent stratum granulosum

Steroid Sulfatase Deficiency (X-linked recessive ichthyosis)

Gene: STS (deleted in ~90% of patients)

Inheritance: X-linked recessive

Incidence: 1:2000-1:9500 boys

Onset: Infancy

Key features: Fine to large, dark, adherent scales of extremities, trunk, neck, and lateral face; sparing of skin folds

Associated: Corneal opacities; cryptorchidism → risk of testicular cancer; female carriers may have prolonged labor

Diagnosis: FISH or microarray to identify gene deletion; increased plasma cholesterol sulfate

Lamellar Ichthyosis/CIE Spectrum

Genes: TGM1, ABCA12, CYP4F22, and others (11 genes total)

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive

Incidence: LI: 1:200,000-1:300,000; CIE: 1:100,000-1:200,000

Onset: Birth (frequently collodion membrane)

LI features: Large, thick plate-like brown scales; absent or mild erythroderma

CIE features: Fine, white scales; erythroderma

Associated: Heat intolerance; scarring alopecia (variable)

Diagnosis: Clinical; transglutaminase-1 immunostaining; genetic testing

Harlequin Ichthyosis

Gene: ABCA12

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive

Onset: Birth

Key features: Very thick, yellow-brown plates of scale that tightly encase the neonate; large, deep, bright red fissures

Associated: Premature delivery; often neonatal death due to sepsis or respiratory insufficiency; extreme ectropion, eclabium, and ear deformities

Outcome: Survivors develop severe CIE-like phenotype

Diagnosis: Clinical appearance is diagnostic

Other Important Forms

Epidermolytic Ichthyosis

Genes: KRT1, KRT10

Inheritance: Autosomal dominant (rarely recessive)

Onset: Birth

Key features: At birth: erythroderma, blistering, erosions. Later: hyperkeratosis with cobblestone appearance, ridging of the flexures

Associated: Frequent skin infections, malodor, gait abnormalities

Diagnosis: Clinical; histopathology shows epidermolysis, hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, and vacuolization

Netherton Syndrome

Gene: SPINK5 (serine protease inhibitor)

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive

Onset: Birth/infancy

Key features: Congenital erythroderma and peeling; two phenotypes: ichthyosis linearis circumflexa and CIE-like; pruritus

Associated: Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair - pathognomonic); highly elevated serum IgE; failure to thrive; recurrent infections; food allergies

Diagnosis: Clinical; trichoscopy; hair shaft analysis

Sjögren-Larsson Syndrome

Gene: ALDH3A2 (Fatty Aldehyde Dehydrogenase)

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive

Onset: Birth

Key features: Fine to plate-like scaling or non-scaling hyperkeratosis; favors abdomen, neck, flexures; lichenification; pruritus

Associated: Spastic di- and tetraplegia; perifoveal glistening white dots (pathognomonic); intellectual disability

Diagnosis: FALDH activity assay in cultured fibroblasts; genetic testing

Erythrokeratodermas

Genes: GJB3, GJB4, LOR and others

Inheritance: Usually autosomal dominant

Onset: Infancy/childhood

Key features: Well-demarcated, red hyperkeratotic plaques (vs. ichthyosis with more diffuse scaling)

Types:

  • Erythrokeratoderma variabilis: Migratory, figurate erythematous plaques + fixed hyperkeratotic plaques
  • Progressive symmetric erythrokeratoderma: Fixed, symmetric plaques over knees, elbows, extremities

Associated: Palmoplantar keratoderma common

The Collodion Baby

Collodion baby is not a specific diagnosis but a phenotype at birth shared by several congenital ichthyoses. About 10% of collodion babies have a milder "self-healing" phenotype called self-improving collodion ichthyosis.

Clinical Presentation

  • Often born prematurely with increased perinatal morbidity and mortality
  • Covered with a taut, shiny, transparent membrane (thickened stratum corneum) resembling plastic wrap
  • May have ectropion, eclabium, and hypoplasia of nasal and auricular cartilage
  • Sucking and pulmonary ventilation may be impaired (dehydration, malnutrition, hypoxia, pneumonia)
  • After birth, membrane dries, cracks, and breaks → fissures → water loss → electrolyte imbalance
  • Fissures allow entry of microorganisms → infection → potential sepsis
  • Circular bands of hardened skin can lead to vascular constriction and distal edema

Clinical Course

  • Within 2-4 weeks, membrane peels off in sheets and transition to underlying disease phenotype
  • Most commonly evolves into congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma or lamellar ichthyosis
  • In self-improving collodion ichthyosis (10% of cases): desquamation leaves normal skin or minimal findings (xerosis, fine scaling, palmar hyperlinearity, mild PPK, red cheeks, hypohidrosis)

Management of Collodion Baby

Major risks: thermoinstability, hypernatremic dehydration, skin infections, sepsis, and pneumonia due to restricted ventilation and aspiration of amniotic fluid containing scales.

Key Management Principles

  • Place in controlled environment (humidified incubator)
  • Carefully monitor fluid and electrolyte balance
  • Facilitate gradual desquamation with wet compresses and light emollients to increase skin elasticity and pliability
  • DO NOT manually remove the collodion membrane or use topical keratolytic agents (increased risk of infections and percutaneous absorption)
  • Watch for signs of infection and treat promptly
  • Support nutrition and hydration

Acquired Ichthyosis

Always consider underlying diseases when ichthyosis presents later in life. The most concerning cause is malignancy, particularly lymphoma.

Interactive Quiz

1. A 5-month-old presents with erythroderma, peeling skin, and severe pruritus. Trichoscopy reveals bamboo-like hair shafts. What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • A. Netherton syndrome
  • B. Lamellar ichthyosis
  • C. Sjögren-Larsson syndrome
  • D. Congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma

Correct Answer: A. Netherton syndrome

The combination of erythroderma, peeling skin, pruritus, and trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair) is classic for Netherton syndrome. This autosomal recessive condition is caused by mutations in SPINK5 gene encoding a serine protease inhibitor.

2. A newborn presents as a collodion baby. After membrane shedding, the infant develops large, plate-like dark scales without significant erythema. The most likely diagnosis is:
  • A. Harlequin ichthyosis
  • B. Congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma
  • C. Lamellar ichthyosis
  • D. Netherton syndrome

Correct Answer: C. Lamellar ichthyosis

Large, plate-like dark scales without significant erythema following collodion baby presentation is classic for lamellar ichthyosis. In contrast, CIE would present with fine white scales and erythroderma, while Netherton syndrome would have characteristic hair shaft abnormalities.

3. A 3-year-old boy presents with dark scales on the trunk, neck, and extremities with sparing of the skin folds. His mother reports a prolonged labor. What is the most likely diagnosis?
  • A. Ichthyosis vulgaris
  • B. X-linked ichthyosis (steroid sulfatase deficiency)
  • C. Lamellar ichthyosis
  • D. Epidermolytic ichthyosis

Correct Answer: B. X-linked ichthyosis (steroid sulfatase deficiency)

X-linked ichthyosis affects males and presents with dark, adherent scales with sparing of the skin folds. The history of prolonged labor is a clue, as female carriers often experience prolonged labor when carrying affected boys due to inadequate cervical dilatation from placental steroid sulfatase deficiency.

Flashcards - Hover to Reveal Answers

What gene mutation is responsible for ichthyosis vulgaris?

Filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations are responsible for ichthyosis vulgaris. These mutations lead to reduced or absent filaggrin protein, resulting in diminished stratum granulosum on histology.

What are the pathognomonic findings in Sjögren-Larsson syndrome?

Perifoveal glistening white dots in the retina are pathognomonic for Sjögren-Larsson syndrome. This is accompanied by the clinical triad of ichthyosis, spastic di/tetraplegia, and intellectual disability.

What distinguishes erythrokeratodermas from ichthyoses?

Erythrokeratodermas feature discrete areas of erythema and hyperkeratosis, usually without substantial scale, while ichthyoses are characterized by scaling of the skin in a widespread distribution.
