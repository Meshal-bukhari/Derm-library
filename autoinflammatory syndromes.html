Autoinflammatory Disorders: Comprehensive Review

Source: Fitzpatrick's Dermatology Chapter 39 | For: Saudi Dermatology Board Exam Preparation

Key Concept: Autoinflammatory disorders are caused by dysregulation of the innate immune system, presenting with recurrent sterile inflammation. Unlike autoimmune conditions, they lack high autoantibody titers and antigen-specific T cells.

Overview of Autoinflammatory Disorders

Autoinflammatory disorders are characterized by:

  • Dysregulation of the innate immune system
  • Sterile inflammation (mimicking infection)
  • Common symptoms: fever, skin lesions, joint pain
  • IL-1 plays a prominent role in many monogenic forms
  • Typically presenting in childhood (sometimes neonatally)
  • Inherited in autosomal-recessive or autosomal-dominant patterns (with many sporadic cases)
  • Characteristic skin eruptions: neutrophilic urticarial lesions, pustulosis, cellulitis-like lesions, erythematous and maculopapular eruptions
Cryopyrin-Associated Periodic Syndromes (CAPS)

Genetics: Autosomal dominant disorder caused by gain-of-function mutations in NLRP3 gene (encoding cryopyrin/NLRP3)

Pathophysiology: Gain-of-function mutations in NLRP3 lead to inflammasome activation and abnormal IL-1β secretion

Spectrum includes three phenotypes of increasing severity:

  1. Familial Cold Autoinflammatory Syndrome (FCAS): Mildest form
  2. Muckle-Wells Syndrome (MWS): Intermediate severity
  3. Chronic Infantile Neurologic Cutaneous and Articular Syndrome (CINCA)/Neonatal-Onset Multisystem Inflammatory Disease (NOMID): Most severe form
Urticarial skin lesions Recurrent fever Joint pain Cold-induced in FCAS Neurologic involvement in CINCA/NOMID

Clinical Features:

  • Skin: Urticarial skin lesions (typically non-pruritic or mildly pruritic, often first sign of disease)
  • FCAS:
    • Cold-induced episodes lasting <24 hours
    • Fever, urticarial lesions, arthralgia, conjunctivitis
    • Typically begins in infancy/early childhood
  • MWS:
    • Episodes lasting 24-48 hours or subcontinuous
    • Progressive sensorineural hearing loss
    • Risk of amyloidosis
  • CINCA/NOMID:
    • Continuous symptoms with exacerbations
    • Present at birth with diffuse erythema and fever
    • Chronic aseptic meningitis
    • Brain atrophy and cognitive impairment
    • Sensory neural hearing loss
    • Elevated intracranial pressure causing papilledema
    • Characteristic epiphyseal overgrowth of long bones

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical features + family history (except CINCA/NOMID which is usually sporadic)
  • Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR, SAA)
  • Leukocytosis (especially neutrophilia)
  • Skin biopsy: Neutrophilic perivascular infiltrate in superficial and mid-dermis without vasculitis
  • Genetic testing for NLRP3 mutations (negative results don't exclude diagnosis due to possible mosaicism)

Treatment:

IL-1 antagonists are highly effective and standard of care:

  • Anakinra (Kineret): Daily subcutaneous injections
  • Rilonacept (Arcalyst): Weekly subcutaneous injections
  • Canakinumab (Ilaris): Every 8 weeks subcutaneous injection
Schnitzler Syndrome

Genetics: Sporadic acquired autoinflammatory disorder; some patients have mosaicism of NLRP3 mutations

Epidemiology: Rare, late-onset (typically around 50 years or older)

Urticarial skin lesions IgM gammopathy Recurrent fever Bone/joint pain

Clinical Features:

  • Strong similarities to CAPS
  • Recurrent urticarial skin lesions (usually first sign, can precede other symptoms by years)
  • Nonpruritic or mildly pruritic lesions, unresponsive to antihistamines
  • Intermittent fever (can exceed 40°C)
  • Bone pain, arthralgias
  • Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
  • Key distinguishing feature: Monoclonal IgM gammopathy (mostly IgMκ)

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical, laboratory, and radiologic findings
  • Presence of monoclonal IgM gammopathy
  • Skin biopsy: Neutrophilic infiltrate without vasculitis (similar to CAPS)
  • Dramatic response to IL-1 antagonists supports diagnosis

Prognosis:

10-20% develop lymphoproliferative disorders (e.g., Waldenström macroglobulinemia, B-cell lymphoma)

Treatment:

  • IL-1 antagonists (anakinra, canakinumab) induce complete remission in >90% of cases
  • Symptoms recur rapidly upon withdrawal of anakinra
Deficiency of the IL-1 Receptor Antagonist (DIRA)

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in IL1RN gene

Pathophysiology: Mutations lead to complete absence or dysfunction of IL-1Ra, resulting in unopposed IL-1 signaling

Pustular skin eruption Oral mucosal lesions Joint swelling Bone inflammation Perinatal/early onset

Clinical Features:

  • Perinatal to several months of age onset
  • Pustular skin eruption (ranging from isolated pustules to generalized pustulosis or ichthyosiform lesions)
  • Oral mucosal vesicular lesions or aphthous ulcers
  • Nail changes (onychomadesis, pits)
  • Painful joint swelling
  • Characteristic bone findings: balloon-like widening of anterior rib ends, periosteal elevation, multifocal osteolytic lesions
  • High fever is NOT typical (unlike many other autoinflammatory disorders)

Histopathology:

  • Intraepidermal neutrophils and neutrophilic pustules
  • Marked papillary dermal edema
  • Intense neutrophilic infiltrate throughout dermis and superficial subcutis

Diagnosis:

  • Often misdiagnosed as infectious osteomyelitis
  • Negative bone/blood cultures and unresponsiveness to antibiotics
  • Genetic testing to identify IL1RN mutations

Treatment:

  • Recombinant IL-1Ra (anakinra) - dramatic clinical improvement
  • Lifelong treatment required
Deficiency of the IL-36 Receptor Antagonist (DITRA)

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in IL36RN gene

Pathophysiology: Lack of IL-36Ra leads to aberrant IL-36 signaling and IL-8 overproduction in keratinocytes, resulting in neutrophil recruitment

Generalized pustular psoriasis Recurrent flares High fever Childhood or adult onset

Clinical Features:

  • Recurrent sudden-onset flares of generalized erythematopustular skin eruption
  • High fever (40-42°C)
  • Neutrophilia and elevated hepatic acute-phase proteins
  • Triggers: infections, medications, menstruation, pregnancy, withdrawal of retinoid therapy
  • No joint involvement
  • Nail dystrophy possible
  • Can be life-threatening due to systemic inflammation

Histopathology:

Neutrophil infiltration in upper epidermis forming spongiform pustules of Kogoj with acanthosis

Diagnosis:

  • Genetic testing revealing IL36RN mutations in GPP patients
  • More frequent in GPP without psoriasis vulgaris

Treatment:

Optimal treatment not established; IL-1 and IL-17 antagonists reported to have efficacy

CARD14-Mediated Pustular Psoriasis (CAMPS)/Psoriasis 2 (PSORS2)

Genetics: Autosomal dominant disorder caused by gain-of-function mutations in CARD14

Pathophysiology: Mutations lead to aberrant MAPK activity and NF-κB signaling, resulting in overproduction of IL-8 and IL-36γ

Various psoriasis phenotypes Plaque psoriasis Pustular psoriasis

Clinical Features:

Various psoriasis phenotypes including:

  • Generalized pustular psoriasis
  • Plaque psoriasis
  • Wide spectrum of clinical severity

Treatment:

One case report of successful treatment with IL-12/IL-23 antagonist ustekinumab

Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF)

Genetics: Most common autoinflammatory disorder; mostly autosomal recessive (less common autosomal dominant) caused by mutations in MEFV gene encoding pyrin

Pathophysiology: Mutations lead to pyrin-inflammasome activation and abnormal IL-1β secretion

Epidemiology: Highest rates in Mediterranean populations (Armenian, Sephardic-Jewish, Arabic, Turkish); 80% diagnosed before 10 years of age

Recurrent febrile attacks (1-3 days) Erysipelas-like skin lesions Peritonitis Pleuritis Synovitis

Clinical Features:

  • Recurrent short febrile attacks lasting 1-3 days
  • Erysipelas-like skin lesions: well-demarcated, warm, tender, swollen, red skin lesions (lower legs, feet, ankles)
  • Peritonitis, pleuritis, synovitis
  • Patients completely well between attacks
  • Triggers: cold exposure, fatigue, emotional stress, menstruation
  • In infants <2 years: fever alone may be initial symptom

Histopathology:

Edema of superficial dermis and perivascular/interstitial neutrophilic infiltrate without vasculitis

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical findings: recurrent fever episodes with erysipelas-like skin lesions and abdominal/pleural/joint pain (1-3 days)
  • Elevated inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP, SAA)
  • Genetic testing for MEFV mutations (not all patients have identifiable mutations)

Complications:

Amyloidosis (leading to renal failure) - major cause of mortality

Treatment:

  • First-line: Colchicine - prevents attacks and amyloidosis development
  • Second-line: IL-1 antagonists (e.g., canakinumab) for colchicine-resistant or intolerant patients
Hyperimmunoglobulinemia D with Periodic Fever Syndrome (HIDS)/Mevalonate Kinase Deficiency (MKD)

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in MVK gene encoding mevalonate kinase

Pathophysiology: Reduced mevalonate kinase enzyme activity (1-10% of normal) leads to IL-1β overproduction

Epidemiology: Higher incidence in Netherlands (1 in 350 carry recessive gene) and northern France; symptoms typically begin in first year of life

Recurrent fever episodes (3-7 days) Widespread erythematous macules/papules Abdominal pain Lymphadenopathy Oral aphthous ulcers

Clinical Features:

  • Recurrent fever episodes lasting 3-7 days (every 4-8 weeks, but intervals vary)
  • Abdominal pain, diarrhea, vomiting
  • Headache, joint pain
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Skin lesions in >80% of patients during attacks:
    • Most common: widespread erythematous macules and papules
    • Also: urticaria, annular erythema, erythematous nodules, petechiae
    • Acral lesions more frequent than truncal
  • Oral aphthous ulcers (with/without genital ulceration) in 50% of patients
  • Flares triggered by immunizations, trauma, physical/emotional stress

Diagnosis:

  • Elevated ESR, CRP, neutrophilic leukocytosis during attacks
  • Elevated serum IgD level (though ~20% can have normal levels)
  • 80% have increased IgA levels
  • Genetic testing for MVK mutations

Mevalonic aciduria is a severe form of MKD (enzyme activity <1%) with neurologic impairment, growth retardation and early death

Treatment:

  • NSAIDs: Reduce fever severity/duration
  • Corticosteroids: At attack onset for NSAID-refractory cases
  • IL-1 antagonist (canakinumab): FDA-approved since 2016
  • Colchicine is not effective
Tumor Necrosis Factor Receptor-Associated Periodic Syndrome (TRAPS)

Genetics: Autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in TNFRSF1A gene encoding TNF receptor-1

Epidemiology: Second most common inherited periodic fever syndrome; seen in almost all ethnic groups; most present before age 10

Long-lasting fever episodes (3 weeks) Migratory myalgia Migratory erythematous rash Periorbital edema Serositis

Clinical Features:

  • Recurrent high-grade fever with chills, typically lasting ~3 weeks
  • Episodes may occur spontaneously or be triggered by infection, minor injury, stress, exercise
  • Abdominal pain with nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (sterile peritonitis)
  • Characteristic migratory myalgia (monocytic fasciitis) moving from torso to distal limbs
  • Headaches, serositis, monoarticular arthritis, conjunctivitis
  • Various skin lesions:
    • Erythematous macules/papules
    • Erythematous patches
    • Edematous/urticarial plaques
    • Periorbital edema with conjunctivitis
    • Often migratory and associated with underlying myalgia

Histopathology:

Nonspecific perivascular dermal infiltrate of lymphocytes and monocytes

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical findings: long-lasting fever attacks with abdominal pain and migratory muscle pain with overlying skin lesions
  • Elevated inflammatory markers
  • Serum levels of soluble TNF receptor superfamily 1A may be low
  • Genetic testing for TNFRSF1A mutations

Complications:

10-15% develop amyloidosis (risk of renal failure)

Treatment:

  • NSAIDs: Symptom relief
  • IL-1 antagonists (anakinra, canakinumab): Most effective; canakinumab FDA-approved since 2016
  • TNF-α inhibitor (etanercept): Can be effective but may trigger severe inflammatory episodes in some patients
Haploinsufficiency of A20 (HA20)

Genetics: Autosomal dominant disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in TNFAIP3 gene encoding A20/TNFAIP3

Pathophysiology: Mutant A20/TNFAIP3 acts through haploinsufficiency, leading to increased NF-κB-dependent proinflammatory cytokines

Behçet-like disease Bipolar ulceration Early-onset Autoantibodies

Clinical Features:

  • Symptoms start in childhood or early adulthood
  • Clinically resembles Behçet's disease:
    • Fevers
    • Bipolar ulceration (oral and genital)
    • Skin lesions: erythematous papules, pseudofolliculitis, erythema nodosum-like lesions, pathergy
    • Uveitis
    • Polyarthritis
    • May develop retinal vasculitis, GI ulcers, CNS vasculitis
  • Autoantibodies may be present (ANA, anti-RNP, anti-dsDNA, lupus anticoagulant)

Diagnosis:

Based on clinical findings and genetic testing for TNFAIP3 mutations

Treatment:

  • Colchicine (effective in some patients)
  • TNF-α antagonists and IL-1R antagonists with variable efficacy
Otulipenia/OTULIN-related Autoinflammatory Syndrome (ORAS)

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in OTULIN

Pathophysiology: Mutant OTULIN leads to increased linear ubiquitination and elevated NF-κB-dependent proinflammatory transcripts

Early-onset severe inflammation Relapsing panniculitis Lipodystrophy Neutrophilic dermatitis

Clinical Features:

  • Onset within days to months after birth
  • Severe systemic inflammation
  • Prolonged fever
  • Relapsing nodular panniculitis with neutrophil infiltrate
  • Erythematous and pustular rash
  • Arthralgia, diarrhea
  • Progressive lipodystrophy
  • Developmental delay

Histopathology:

Neutrophilic dermatitis and panniculitis; one patient showed septal panniculitis with vasculitis

Treatment:

  • TNF-α inhibitor: Good response reported in 3 patients
  • Systemic steroids and anakinra: Variable or no efficacy
Chronic Atypical Neutrophilic Dermatosis with Lipodystrophy and Elevated Temperature (CANDLE)/Proteasome-Associated Autoinflammatory Syndrome (PRAAS)

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in PSMB8 gene; digenic inheritance with additional mutations possible

Pathophysiology: Mutations affect proteasome assembly and maturation, leading to accumulation of ubiquitinated proteins and increased type I interferon signaling

Recurrent fever Pernio-like eruptions Nodular erythema Lipomuscular atrophy Clubbed fingers

Clinical Features:

  • Development during first year of life
  • Recurrent fever
  • Pernio-like and nodular erythema-like eruptions lasting days to weeks
  • Long, clubbed fingers and toes with joint contractures
  • Progressive lipomuscular atrophy (face and upper limbs)
  • Variable features: periorbital erythema/edema, myositis, short stature, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, basal ganglia calcification

Laboratory Findings:

  • Chronic anemia
  • Elevated CRP and ESR
  • Variable hypergammaglobulinemia
  • Autoimmune features may be present

Histopathology:

Dense dermal infiltration of mononuclear cells, histiocytes, eosinophils, and neutrophils in perivascular and interstitial areas; no leukocytoclastic vasculitis

Treatment:

No effective treatment established; systemic steroids provide temporary improvement but lesions return after tapering

Blau Syndrome (BS)/Early-Onset Sarcoidosis (EOS)

Genetics: BS is autosomal dominant; EOS is sporadic; both caused by gain-of-function mutations in NOD2 gene

Granulomatous dermatitis Arthritis Uveitis Onset before age 4

Clinical Features:

  • Classic triad: granulomatous dermatitis, arthritis, and uveitis
  • Granulomatous dermatitis (typically first sign, before age 4):
    • Asymptomatic, slightly scaly, discrete
    • Yellowish to brown-red flat-topped papules
    • Present on trunk and extremities
  • Arthritis (usually in first decade):
    • Chronic, symmetrical, mostly painless polyarthritis
    • Intraarticular synovitis and tenosynovitis
    • Preserved joint range of motion
  • Ocular:
    • Insidious granulomatous iridocyclitis and posterior uveitis
    • Can develop into panuveitis
    • Photophobia, blurred vision, ocular pain
    • Usually bilateral
    • Significant visual loss in 20-30%

Histopathology:

Noncaseating granulomas composed of PAS-positive histiocytes and multinucleated giant cells in upper dermis

Diagnosis:

  • Demonstration of noncaseating granulomas in biopsies
  • Genetic testing for NOD2 mutations
  • Absence of bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (differentiating from adult sarcoidosis)

Treatment:

  • Low-dose corticosteroids during quiescent stage
  • TNF antagonists often effective for uveitis and arthritis
  • Methotrexate can suppress arthritis and allow corticosteroid tapering
  • IL-1 antagonists: variable results
Pyogenic Arthritis, Pyoderma Gangrenosum, and Acne (PAPA) Syndrome

Genetics: Autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in PSTPIP1 gene

Pathophysiology: PSTPIP1 interacts with pyrin; mutant PSTPIP1 may have increased binding to pyrin, leading to IL-1β overproduction via inflammasome activation

Sterile pyogenic arthritis Pyoderma gangrenosum Severe nodulocystic acne

Clinical Features:

  • Sterile pyogenic arthritis:
    • Begins in first decade of life
    • Progressively destructive
    • Episodes begin unprovoked or after minor trauma
    • Neutrophil-rich purulent synovial inflammation
    • May present with fever during episodes
  • Cutaneous symptoms (develop at puberty):
    • Pyoderma gangrenosum: poorly healing ulcers with undermined edges
    • Severe nodulocystic acne (can cause scarring if untreated)

Treatment:

  • Severe arthritis: drainage of synovial fluid, intraarticular steroid injections
  • High-dose corticosteroids (effective but often worsen acne)
  • TNF-α antagonist (etanercept)
  • IL-1Ra (anakinra) - reported effective in some patients
Majeed Syndrome

Genetics: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in LPIN2 gene encoding lipin-2

Chronic recurrent multifocal osteomyelitis (CRMO) Congenital dyserythropoietic anemia (CDA) Sweet syndrome-like neutrophilic dermatosis

Clinical Features:

  • CRMO:
    • Begins in infancy or early childhood
    • Recurrent episodes of pain and joint swelling
    • Can lead to short stature and joint contractures
  • CDA:
    • Hypochromic, microcytic anemia during first year of life
    • Severity ranges from mild to transfusion dependent
  • Neutrophilic dermatosis:
    • Resembles Sweet syndrome
    • Erythematous plaques, psoriasis, pustulosis

Histopathology:

Dermal neutrophilic infiltration with papillary dermal edema (similar to Sweet syndrome) without vasculitis

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical findings and genetic testing for LPIN2 mutations
  • Radiographs: osteolytic lesions with surrounding sclerosis
  • Bone biopsies to exclude infectious osteomyelitis
  • Sterile bone and blood cultures

Treatment:

  • CRMO: NSAIDs, corticosteroids if unresponsive
  • IL-1 blockade (anakinra, canakinumab): dramatic response reported in two patients
  • CDA: red blood cell transfusion if indicated
  • Neutrophilic dermatosis: short course of oral corticosteroids
Synovitis, Acne, Pustulosis, Hyperostosis, and Osteitis (SAPHO) Syndrome

Genetics: Multifactorial (genetic, environmental, immunologic); mostly sporadic but some familial cases

Epidemiology: Typically presents in middle-aged adults but can occur at any age; annual prevalence ~1 in 10,000

Palmoplantar pustulosis (PPP) Severe acne Oligoarthritis Osteitis (anterior chest wall)

Clinical Features:

  • Neutrophilic skin lesions and osteoarticular manifestations (usually appear within 2 years of each other)
  • Skin manifestations:
    • Most frequent: PPP
    • Also: severe acne (acne conglobata, acne fulminans, hidradenitis suppurativa)
    • Less frequent: pustular psoriasis, psoriasis vulgaris
  • Osteoarticular manifestations:
    • Oligoarthritis: sternocostal, sternoclavicular joints, sacroiliac joints, knees, ankles
    • Osteitis: anterior chest wall (sternum, clavicle, ribs)
    • Pain, tenderness, sometimes swelling over affected areas

Diagnosis:

Based on clinical, radiologic, and histologic findings (challenging as not all symptoms present simultaneously)

Prognosis:

Usually chronic with alternating remission/relapse; generally favorable prognosis

Treatment:

  • NSAIDs: First-line treatment
  • Intraarticular/systemic corticosteroids: Transiently effective
  • Skin therapies: Topical corticosteroids, PUVA for PPP/pustular psoriasis
  • Antibiotics: For severe acne (limited efficacy)
  • Bisphosphonates: Sometimes very effective for bone lesions
  • TNF-α antagonists: For refractory cases
  • IL-1Ra (anakinra): Promising in case reports

Tricky Areas and Common Exam Pitfalls

  1. Distinguishing between fever patterns:
    • FCAS: <24 hours, cold-induced
    • MWS: 24-48 hours or subcontinuous
    • CINCA/NOMID: Continuous with exacerbations
    • FMF: 1-3 days
    • HIDS/MKD: 3-7 days
    • TRAPS: 1-4 weeks (longest duration)
  2. Schnitzler syndrome vs. CAPS:
    • Both have urticarial lesions and neutrophilic histology
    • Key difference: IgM monoclonal gammopathy in Schnitzler
    • Age of onset: Schnitzler (~50y), CAPS (childhood)
  3. DIRA vs. infectious osteomyelitis:
    • Both have bone inflammation
    • DIRA: negative cultures, unresponsive to antibiotics
    • DIRA: pustular skin eruption, no high fever
  4. Autoinflammatory diseases with oral/genital ulcers:
    • HIDS/MKD: 50% have oral aphthous ulcers ± genital ulceration
    • HA20: Behçet-like with bipolar ulceration
    • TRAPS: Can have aphthous ulcers
  5. FMF vs. TRAPS:
    • Both have periodic fever and abdominal pain
    • Duration: FMF (1-3 days) vs. TRAPS (3 weeks)
    • Skin: Erysipelas-like (FMF) vs. migratory painful rash (TRAPS)
    • Response to colchicine: FMF (excellent) vs. TRAPS (poor)
  6. Response to biologics can help diagnose:
    • Dramatic response to IL-1 antagonists: CAPS, Schnitzler, DIRA
    • Dramatic response to TNF-α inhibitors: Otulipenia/ORAS
    • Response to colchicine: FMF (not effective in HIDS or other disorders)
  7. Disorders with pustulosis:
    • DIRA: Pustular eruption without high fever
    • DITRA: Generalized pustular psoriasis with high fever
    • CAMPS/PSORS2: Variable psoriasis phenotypes including pustular
    • SAPHO: PPP is most common cutaneous manifestation
  8. Diseases resembling Behçet's disease:
    • HA20: Early-onset Behçet-like, positive autoantibodies
    • HIDS/MKD: Can have oral/genital ulcers but has other distinctive features
  9. Be careful with skin manifestations interpretation:
    • Urticarial skin lesions in CAPS/Schnitzler don't respond to antihistamines
    • Erysipelas-like skin lesions in FMF are not infectious
    • Same genetic disease can have different cutaneous phenotypes (e.g., CAMPS)
  10. HIDS/MKD lab findings can be tricky:
    • ~20% of HIDS/MKD patients have normal IgD levels
    • 80% have increased IgA levels
    • Elevated IgD alone is not specific for HIDS
