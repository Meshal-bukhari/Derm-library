Deep Dive into Plant Allergic Contact Dermatitis (Type IV Hypersensitivity)

The Basics of Plant ACD

Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD) from plants is a Type IV (delayed) hypersensitivity reaction. It requires prior sensitization to a plant allergen (hapten). The reaction typically occurs hours to days after re-exposure in a sensitized individual.

Two plant families dominate plant ACD:

  • Anacardiaceae: The most frequent cause, primarily via Urushiol.
  • Asteraceae (Compositae): The second major cause, primarily via Sesquiterpene Lactones (SQLs).

I. Anacardiaceae Family Dermatitis (The Urushiol Group)

Causes more plant ACD than all other families combined.

Key Allergen: Urushiol

Composition
An oleoresin mix of catechols and resorcinols with long (C15/C17) alkyl side chains. Unsaturation (double bonds) in the side chain dramatically increases allergenicity.
Mechanism
Acts as a hapten, binding skin proteins. Requires plant damage to be released (except late fall). Very stable; persists on fomites, in smoke.

Important Allergenic Anacardiaceae:

  • Toxicodendron spp.:
    • T. radicans (Eastern Poison Ivy - vine), T. rydbergii (Western Poison Ivy - shrub)
    • T. pubescens (Eastern Poison Oak - shrub), T. diversilobum (Western Poison Oak - shrub/vine)
    • T. vernix (Poison Sumac - shrub/tree in wet areas)
  • Anacardium occidentale (Cashew Nut Tree): Allergen in nut shell oil (CNSO). Note: Shell oil also has irritant phenolic components causing immediate vesication.
  • Mangifera indica (Mango Tree): Allergen (resorcinols) in leaves, bark, stem, fruit skin. Pulp is usually safe regarding ACD.
  • Schinus terebinthifolius (Brazilian Pepper Tree): Common in Florida; allergen in sap, crushed berries.
  • Toxicodendron verniciflua (Japanese Lacquer Tree): Urushiol in sap used for lacquer; polymerized form remains allergenic.
  • Others: Metopium toxiferum (Poisonwood), Semecarpus anacardium (Marking Nut), etc.

Cross-Reactors (Outside Anacardiaceae):

  • Ginkgo biloba (Ginkgo Tree): Allergen (ginkgolic acid) in outer seed pulp/covering only. Leaves/kernel likely only irritant.
  • Grevillea spp. (e.g., Silk Oak): Allergen is pentadecylresorcinol (structurally related, less allergenic).

Clinical Features:

  • Onset: Typically 1-2 days post-exposure (range hours to weeks for primary sensitization). Peaks 1-14 days.
  • Morphology: Intensely pruritic, erythematous eruption. Classic linear streaks of papules progressing to vesicles/bullae (from brushing).
  • "Black-Spot" Dermatitis: Oxidized urushiol stains skin black within lesion; acts as both ICD and ACD. Stain permanent on clothing (retains allergenicity).
  • Distribution: Any exposed site. Transfer via fomites (clothes, tools, pets), sawdust, smoke (can cause severe respiratory/ocular issues).
  • No Spread from Blister Fluid: Fluid itself is non-allergenic. Apparent spread is due to varying absorption/reaction times.
  • Complications: Erythema Multiforme-like reactions (~2 weeks later), rare nephritis, other systemic rashes.
  • Course: Untreated lasts 2-3 weeks. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation common.

Diagnosis & Treatment:

  • Diagnosis: Clinical presentation & history. Patch testing not standard for urushiol.
  • Decontamination: Prompt (<30 min ideal), thorough washing (3x) with soap (dish soap good), warm water, unidirectional strokes.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Cool compresses, Burow's soaks, calamine. Avoid topical sensitizers (antihistamines, benzocaine).
  • Topical Steroids: Potent/superpotent useful ONLY if applied very early.
  • Systemic Corticosteroids: Highly effective for moderate/severe cases. Crucial point: Requires gradual taper over 2-3 weeks (e.g., Prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day) to prevent rebound. Short packs (6-day) are INADEQUATE. IM depot steroid is an option.
  • Antihistamines (Oral): Primarily for sedation; itch is not histamine-driven.
  • Prevention: Avoidance! Protective clothing. Use heavy-duty VINYL gloves (rubber is permeable). Wash contaminated items carefully.

II. Asteraceae (Compositae) Family Dermatitis (The SQL Group)

Second major cause of plant ACD. Includes weeds, ornamentals, vegetables.

Key Allergen: Sesquiterpene Lactones (SQLs)

Composition
C15 compounds with sesquiterpene + lactone ring. >4000 types exist. Found in leaves, stems, flowers, pollen (bound to airborne parts).
Mechanism
Act as haptens. Allergenicity varies.

Important Allergenic Asteraceae:

  • Parthenium hysterophorus (Congress Grass): Major global cause, esp. India. High SQL levels.
  • X Dendranthema cultivars (Chrysanthemums): Very common garden/florist cause, often from "dead-heading".
  • Ambrosia spp. (Ragweed): SQLs in plant parts (not the pollen protein Amb a 1 causing hayfever) cause ACD, often ABCD pattern.
  • Others: Arnica montana, Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew), Taraxacum officinale (Dandelion - ABCD from mowing), Helianthus annuus (Sunflower - ABCD from trichomes), Lactuca sativa (Lettuce), Cynara scolymus (Artichoke).

Cross-Reactors (Outside Asteraceae):

  • Frullania spp. (Liverworts): Cause "woodcutter's eczema" (SQLs) in Pacific NW.
  • Magnolia spp. (Magnolia)
  • Laurus nobilis (Bay Laurel leaves)

Clinical Features:

  • Variable Presentation: Can be typical eczematous ACD (e.g., Chrysanthemum handling).
  • Airborne Contact Dermatitis (ABCD): Common pattern (Parthenium, Ragweed, Dandelion). SQLs on airborne particles affect exposed skin (face, neck, V-chest, arms). Initially seasonal, becomes chronic/lichenified. Classic ABCD involves eyelids, melolabial folds, retroauricular areas (unlike photodermatitis which spares folds).
  • Photosensitivity / Chronic Actinic Dermatitis (CAD): SQLs are NOT photoallergens, but many SQL-allergic patients develop photosensitivity or CAD (up to 85% of CAD patients are Asteraceae-allergic). Mechanism might involve UV-induced neoantigens.
  • "Woodcutter's Eczema" (Frullania): Pseudo-photodistribution (worse in wet winter), involves upper eyelids, spares submental area.

Diagnosis & Treatment:

  • Diagnosis: History, clinical pattern (ABCD). Patch testing is key but CHALLENGING:
    • SQL Mix: High false negative rate (35-65%).
    • Compositae Mix: False negative rate >15%.
    • Recommended Combo: SQL mix + Compositae Mix II + Parthenolide detects ~96%.
    • Testing to suspect plant parts may be needed (use caution).
  • Treatment:
    • Avoidance is paramount.
    • Acute ACD: Similar to Anacardiaceae (steroids).
    • Chronic/ABCD/CAD: Difficult. May need PUVA, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil.

III. Other Plant Families Causing ACD

Alliaceae (Onion Family)

  • Allium sativum (Garlic): Frequent fingertip dermatitis in cooks (hyperkeratosis, fissures). Allergen: diallyl disulfide (also an irritant). Patch testing to whole garlic = irritant. Disposable gloves (vinyl, latex, nitrile) are NOT protective; use reusable domestic RUBBER gloves.

Alstroemeriaceae / Liliaceae (Lily/Tulip Families)

  • Tulipa spp. (Tulips): Cause "Tulip Fingers" (ACD + ICD). Fingertips, periungual. Allergen: Tulipalin A.
  • Alstroemeria spp. (Peruvian Lily): Common florist ACD. Fingertips (erythema, fissures, hyperkeratosis). Allergens: Tulipalin A & B. Allergen penetrates VINYL gloves; use NITRILE gloves.

Myrtaceae

  • Melaleuca alternifolia (Tea Tree Oil): Increasingly common ACD. Sensitizers often degradation products (light/heat/moisture exposure).

Primulaceae

  • Primula obconica (Primula): Common houseplant ACD. Allergen: Primin. Often from "dead-heading".

Botanical Products in Cosmetics/Topicals

  • Common source of ACD. Culprits include Tea Tree Oil, Feverfew (Asteraceae), Lichen acids. Asteraceae most common in herbal remedies.
  • Standard patch test screens often miss relevant botanical allergies. ROAT or testing to patient's products may be needed.

Key Takeaways for Board Exam Memorization

  • Top 2 ACD Families: Anacardiaceae (Toxicodendron, Mango skin, Cashew shell) & Asteraceae (Chrysanthemum, Parthenium, Ragweed parts).
  • Key Allergens:
    • Urushiol (Anacardiaceae): Catechol/Resorcinol mix. Cross-reacts: Ginkgo seed pulp, Grevillea. Stable on fomites/smoke.
    • Sesquiterpene Lactones (SQLs) (Asteraceae): Cause direct ACD & ABCD. Associated with CAD. Cross-reacts: Liverworts (Frullania), Magnolia.
    • Diallyl disulfide: Garlic (Allium sativum).
    • Tulipalin A: Tulips (Tulipa), Peruvian Lily (Alstroemeria).
    • Primin: Primrose (Primula obconica).
    • Tea Tree Oil Degradation Products: Melaleuca alternifolia.
  • Classic Clinical Patterns:
    • Linear Streaks: Urushiol (Poison Ivy/Oak).
    • Airborne Contact Dermatitis (ABCD): SQLs (Ragweed parts, Parthenium, Dandelion). Involves folds.
    • Fingertip Dermatitis: Garlic, Tulip, Alstroemeria.
    • Black-Spot Dermatitis: Urushiol (ICD + ACD).
    • Woodcutter's Eczema: Frullania (Liverwort) SQLs.
    • Association with CAD: SQLs (Asteraceae).
  • Treatment & Prevention Pearls:
    • Urushiol ACD Steroid Taper: Must be 2-3 WEEKS to prevent rebound.
    • Glove Specifics:
      • Urushiol (Poison Ivy): Heavy-Duty VINYL (Rubber permeable).
      • Alstroemeria (Tulipalin A): NITRILE (Vinyl permeable).
      • Garlic (Diallyl disulfide): Domestic RUBBER (Disposables permeable).
  • Patch Testing Caveats: Urushiol not standard. SQL/Compositae mixes have high false negatives. Garlic is irritant. Testing to specific items often needed.

Potential Areas of Confusion / Exam Pitfalls

  • ICD vs. ACD Overlap: Know plants causing both (Garlic, Tulips, Toxicodendron Black-Spot). Distinguish primary mechanism if asked.
  • ACD vs. PPD vs. Protoanemonin ICD:
    • ACD (Urushiol): No light needed. Can cause PIH later. Linear streaks common.
    • PPD: Requires UV light. Leaves marked hyperpigmentation. Can be linear/bizarre.
    • Protoanemonin ICD (Buttercup): No light needed. Linear vesicles. Leaves NO hyperpigmentation.
  • Urushiol Sources Beyond Toxicodendron: Remember Mango SKIN (not pulp), Cashew Nut Shell Oil, Ginkgo SEED PULP (not leaf), Lacquer.
  • SQL Patterns: Differentiate direct contact vs. ABCD (know distribution - involves folds) vs. CAD association (SQLs not photoallergens themselves).
  • Ragweed Confusion: Type I hayfever (pollen protein Amb a 1) vs. Type IV ACD/ABCD (SQLs in plant parts).
  • Patch Testing Limits: Standard screens insufficient for many botanicals/SQLs. Garlic = irritant.
  • Glove Permeability: MEMORIZE: Vinyl for Urushiol, Nitrile for Alstroemeria, Rubber for Garlic.
  • Steroid Taper Duration: The 2-3 week taper for systemic steroids in poison ivy is high-yield.
