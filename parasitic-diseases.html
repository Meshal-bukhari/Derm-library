A. Leishmaniasis Vector: Sandfly

+

Big Picture:

Chronic parasitic infection affecting skin, mucosa, and viscera. Leishmania organisms reside within phagolysosomes of mononuclear phagocytes.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Old World: L. major, L. tropica, L. aethiopica (oriental sore, Delhi boil)
  • New World: L. mexicana, L. braziliensis (chiclero ulcer, uta, espundia)
  • Sandfly Vector: Phlebotomus (Old World), Lutzomyia (New World)
  • Amastigotes: Intracellular form (diagnostic!)
  • Clinical Forms: Cutaneous, Mucocutaneous, Diffuse Cutaneous, Visceral

High-Yield Distinctions:

  • L. major: Zoonotic/rural, moist, early ulcerative, rapid
  • L. tropica: Anthroponotic/urban, dry, late ulcerative, chronic
  • L. braziliensis: Mucocutaneous (nasal septum, palate)
  • Visceral (Kala-Azar): Fever, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia

Diagnosis:

  • Gold Standard: Demonstration of amastigotes (biopsy, smear)
  • PCR: Most sensitive and specific
  • Culture: NNN media
  • Montenegro Skin Test: Delayed hypersensitivity. Positive in cutaneous and Mucocutaneous , 90% if done in lesions more than 3 months . Negative in diffuse and visceral

Treatment:

  • Pentavalent antimonials (sodium stibogluconate, meglumine antimoniate) or miltefosine. Miltefosine is oral, but teratogenic. pentavelant antimonial therapy related toxicity: ECG abnormalities
  • Visceral: Liposomal amphotericin B. best option in pregnancy
  • Simple cutaneous: May observe in endemic areas

B. Cutaneous Amebiasis GI Extension

+

Big Picture:

Usually gastrointestinal infection, but rare cutaneous involvement via extension or inoculation.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Painful ulcers: Often perianal/genital, with ragged edges, gray-white necrotic base
  • Trophozoites in tissue: Amebas with ingested RBCs are diagnostic

Diagnosis:

  • Wet mount or histology: Amebas with ingested RBCs
  • Skin biopsy will look for trophozoites, which appear as ovoid or round unicellular basophilic structures often surrounded by a clear halo.

Treatment:

  • Metronidazole

C. Balamuthia mandrillaris Free-living ameba

+

Big Picture:

Free-living ameba causing fatal granulomatous encephalitis, but can have preceding skin lesion.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Indurated plaque, central face: Slowly growing
  • Immunocompetent hosts: Unlike Acanthamoeba

Diagnosis:

  • Biopsy: Mixed granulomatous infiltrate, few amebas
  • PCR, specialized culture

Treatment:

  • Multiagent therapy (miltefosine plus others)
  • Poor prognosis

D. Trypanosomiasis Reduviid bug/Tsetse fly

+

Types:

1. Chagas Disease (American Trypanosomiasis)

  • Vector: Reduviid (kissing) bugs
  • Chagoma: Localized swelling at bite site
  • Romaña sign: Unilateral periorbital edema
  • Phases: Acute, Indeterminate, Chronic (heart, GI)

Diagnosis: Trypomastigotes in blood, PCR, Serology, Xenodiagnosis

Treatment: Benznidazole or nifurtimox

2. African Trypanosomiasis (Sleeping Sickness)

  • Vector: Tsetse flies
  • Trypanosomal chancre: Painful, indurated lesion
  • West African (T.b. gambiense): Chronic, neurologic
  • East African (T.b. rhodesiense): Acute, often fatal
  • Trypanids: Annular erythematous eruption

Diagnosis: Trypanosomes in skin, blood, lymph node, CSF

Treatment: Oral fexinidazole (first line for gambiense), NECT

E. Toxoplasmosis Cats

+

Big Picture:

Toxoplasma gondii, a coccidian parasite. Cats are definitive hosts. Infection can be acquired or congenital.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Usually asymptomatic in immunocompetent
  • Congenital: Chorioretinitis, deafness, intracranial calcifications
  • Cutaneous: Rare. Hemorrhagic/necrotic papules in congenital

Diagnosis:

  • Serology (IgM, IgG), PCR
  • Biopsy: Trophozoites within macrophages (not always seen)

Treatment:

  • Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid

A. Cutaneous Larva Migrans Animal hookworms

+

Big Picture:

Animal hookworm larvae penetrate skin and migrate within the epidermis.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Creeping eruption: Serpiginous, pruritic tracts (1-2 cm/day)
  • Self-limited: Larvae can't complete life cycle in humans

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical (biopsy rarely shows larva)

Treatment:

  • Ivermectin (single dose) or albendazole

B. Gnathostomiasis Raw freshwater fish

+

Big Picture:

Larvae of Gnathostoma nematodes. Acquired by eating raw freshwater fish.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Migratory panniculitis: Deep, tender, pruritic nodules that move
  • Superficial form: Mimics cutaneous larva migrans

Diagnosis:

  • History, eosinophilia
  • Biopsy (may see larva), Serology

Treatment:

  • Albendazole or ivermectin

C. Onchocerciasis (River Blindness) Black flies

+

Big Picture:

Onchocerca volvulus, transmitted by black flies. Primarily in Africa.

Key Buzzwords:

  • Onchocercomas: Subcutaneous nodules with adult worms
  • Microfilariae: In skin and eyes, cause blindness
  • "Leopard skin": Depigmentation (shins)
  • "Hanging groin": Lymphatic obstruction
  • Sowda: Lichenification onchocercal

Diagnosis:

  • Skin snip (microfilariae)
  • Slit-lamp exam (eyes)

Treatment:

  • Ivermectin (repeat dosing), Moxidectin
  • Doxycycline (targets Wolbachia endosymbionts)

D. Filariasis (Lymphatic) Mosquitoes

+

Big Picture:

Infection of lymphatics by filarial nematodes (Wuchereria bancrofti, Brugia malayi, B. timori).

Key Buzzwords:

  • Acute: Lymphangitis, orchitis
  • Chronic: Lymphedema, elephantiasis, hydroceles

Diagnosis:

  • Microfilariae in blood (nocturnal periodicity)
  • Circulating filarial antigen (for W. bancrofti)

Treatment:

  • Diethylcarbamazine (DEC) + Albendazole
  • Doxycycline (targets Wolbachia)

E. Schistosomiasis Snails

+

Big Picture:

Trematode (fluke) infection. S. haematobium (urinary), S. japonicum, S. mansoni (GI).

Key Buzzwords:

  • Cercarial dermatitis: Pruritic papules at penetration
  • Katayama fever: Acute reaction (fever, urticaria)
  • Chronic: Fibro-occlusive disease of liver, intestine, bladder
  • Swimmer's Itch: Transient dermatitis from animal schistosomes

Diagnosis:

  • Eggs in urine or feces
  • Biopsy, Serology

Treatment:

  • Praziquantel

F. Cysticercosis & Echinococcosis Tapeworm larvae

+

Cysticercosis:

  • Cause: Larval stage of Taenia solium (pork tapeworm)
  • Key features: Subcutaneous nodules, neurocysticercosis

Diagnosis: Imaging (calcified cysts), serology, biopsy

Treatment: Albendazole or praziquantel

Echinococcosis (Hydatid Disease):

  • Cause: Larval stage of Echinococcus granulosus
  • Key features: Hydatid cysts (liver and lungs most common)

Diagnosis: Imaging, serology

Treatment: Surgery, albendazole
