📚 INTRODUCTION & EPIDEMIOLOGY

👥 GENDER DISTRIBUTION

Acute urticaria: Men = Women

Chronic urticaria: Women (60%)

Delayed pressure urticaria: More in males

🎂 AGE DISTRIBUTION

Urticaria can occur in any age group

Chronic urticaria: More common in 4th and 5th decades

🧬 PATHOGENESIS

HLA Association: HLA DR4, DQ8 (chronic spontaneous urticaria) associated mainly with Th2 response
The primary effector cell of Urticaria is the mast cell, and histamine is the major mediator of pruritus and wheals.
Acute spontaneous urticaria: Type 1 hypersensitivity reaction
Chronic Urticaria: Mainly is idiopathic

🔬 MAST CELL DEGRANULATION MECHANISMS

❖ Classic Immediate Hypersensitivity:

Bound specific IgE by allergen then immunologic degranulating stimuli that act through the IgE receptor

Autoimmune: Autoantibodies against FcɛRI or IgE
IgE-dependent (allergic): latex gloves
Latex gloves - MCQ focus
Immune complex: Vasculitis
Kinin- and complement-dependent: C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency
Medications: PCN and cephalosporins (>TMP/SMX, and minocycline)

❖ Non-immunologic Stimuli:

Independent of the FcɛRI

Medications: Opiates, Aspirin, NSAID, ACEI, radiocontrast media, polymyxin B
Complement: C5a anaphylatoxin, Stem cell factor
Neuropeptides: Substance P, VIP
Vasoactive stimuli: Nettle stings
Mast cell Activators: PGD2, leukotrienes F,D,E4
Inhibitors: PGE2
Important Note: Increase anti thyroid antibodies → up to 25% (mainly with autoimmune urticaria)

🔍 DETAILED CLASSIFICATIONS & TYPES

A- SPONTANEOUS URTICARIAS

🔹 Acute Urticaria:

Causes (in order): (1) idiopathic > (2) infectious: URIs, UTI, GI > (3) drugs (b-lactams most common), (4) foods
Common in children with atopic dermatitis
📊 CAUSES OF ACUTE URTICARIA
50% Idiopathic
40% Upper respiratory tract infections
9% Drugs
1% Foods

🔹 Chronic Spontaneous Urticaria:

60% idiopathic Associated with Autoimmune disorder
Prognosis: 55% will resolve within 1 year, only 5% will persist after 5 years
📊 CAUSES OF CHRONIC URTICARIA
45% Idiopathic
20% Autoimmune
5% Pseudoallergic
5% Infection-related
25% Inducible
75% Spontaneous (non-physical, non-vasculitic) + 25% Inducible
Aspirin: aggravates pre-existing chronic urticaria in up to 30% of patients
Autoimmune represents those patients with functional autoantibodies against FcɛRI or the Fc portion of IgE.

B- INDUCIBLE URTICARIAS: Mechanical, Thermal, sweeting/stress

Key Features: Mechanical urticarias: May last more than 24 hours. Not associated with Atopy

🔧 1- DERMOGRAPHISM:

Can be simple or symptomatic

Immediate response within seconds/minutes from provocations
Simple immediate dermographism: Occurs in at least 5% of the normal population (non pruritic)
Symptomatic immediate: The most common type of physical urticaria (pruritic)
Darier's sign: A localized dermographic response after rubbing cutaneous lesions of mastocytosis

🔧 2- DELAYED PRESSURE URTICARIA (DPU):

Deep erythematous swellings at sites of sustained pressure to the skin. After a delay of 30 minutes to 12 hours And can last more than 24 hours.
📍 Sites frequently affected:
Waistline: After wearing tight clothing
Feet and sole: From tight shoes and after walking
Palms: From manual work
Genitalia: After intercourse
Systemic features: Such as malaise, "flu-like" symptoms, arthralgia

🔧 3- VIBRATORY ANGIOEDEMA:

Very rare form, lasting 30 minutes. Stimuli include jogging, and the use of vibrating machinery

🌡️ THERMAL URTICARIAS: Heat or Cold

🔥 1- Heat Contact Urticaria:

Within minutes of contact with heat source for 30 minutes, can present with systemic manifestations

❄️ 2- Cold Exposure:

Can be acquired or familial (NLRP3,NLRP12,PLCG2 gene mutation)
Primary cold contact urticaria: 95% of cold urticaria. Wheeling occurs minutes after re-warming the skin. May follow URTI, arthropod bites, HIV infection. (idiopathic)
Secondary cold contact urticaria: Cryoglobulinemia, cryofibrinogenemia, hepatitis B/C, lymphoproliferative disease, mononucleosis associated with other manifestations such as Raynaud's phenomenon or purpura, may last longer other types.
Reflex cold urticaria: Life threatening due generalized cooling of the body
Negative ice cube test in reflex cold urticaria and familial cold urticaria
⚠️ SAFETY WARNING: Pts should never swim alone as massive mediator release can cause hypotension

💦 SWEETING/STRESS URTICARIAS: Adrenergic / Cholinergic

🔹 Cholinergic Urticaria:

Any form of physical exertion with rising body temperature
Hot baths "Aquagenic urticaria"
Sudden emotional stress
Drinking alcohol, and eating spicy
Methacholine skin test → Injection of Methylene chloride
"Aquagenic urticaria" (seen in cystic fibrosis, Polycythemia vara, old xerotic patient and Hodgkin's)

🔹 Adrenergic Urticaria:

Distinguished from cholinergic urticaria by the presence of blanched vasoconstricted skin surrounding small pink wheel
Test: Intradermal norepinephrine injection

🤝 C- CONTACT URTICARIA

Immediate and transient localized swelling and redness that occurs on the skin after direct contact with an offending substance.
Immunological: IgE mediated such patient with atopic dermatitis.
Non-Immunological: Sorbic acid and benzoic acid in eye solutions, cinnamic aldehyde in cosmetics and Fragrances, eating Raw meat, fish, and vegetables
🍎 Food Contact Hypersensitivity Syndrome:

Itching and mild swelling of the mouth, tongue, and soft palate within minutes of eating fresh fruits and not cooked fruit. It usually occurs in hay-fever sufferer

📊 STAGING SYSTEM FOR CONTACT URTICARIA SYNDROME
Stage Manifestations
Stage 1 Localized urticaria (redness and swelling); dermatitis (eczema); nonspecific symptoms (itching, tingling, burning sensation)
Stage 2 Generalized urticaria
Stage 3 1. Bronchial asthma (wheezing); rhinitis, conjunctivitis
2. Oropharyngeal symptoms: (lip swelling, hoarseness, difficulty in swallowing);
3. GI symptoms: (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramps)
Stage 4 Anaphylactoid reactions (shock)
🎯 MAJOR DIFFERENTIALS

(1) URTICARIAL VASCULITIS

Lesions that resemble urticaria but have distinct characteristics

🔍 Key Distinguishing Features:

Last >24 h
Burn/hurt (rather than itchy)
Post inflammatory hyperpigmentation
Histology shows mild LCV (+/- eosinophils)

📊 Two Types: Both common in female

❖ Normocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis (NUV): 70%–80%

Skin-limited and idiopathic (self limited in 3 years)

❖ Hypocomplementemic vasculitis (HUV): 20%–30%

Highly associated with systemic disease "only in females"

🧬 Pathogenesis:

Immunocomplex vascular deposition
Hypocomplementemia vasculitis (antibody against C1q) = low C1q levels

⚡ Triggers: Its idiopathic disorders with triggers

Autoimmune disease: SLE (associated with HUV), Sjogren's
Infection: Viral Hepatitis B/C, EBV
Medications: NSAIDs, MTX, TNF-α inhibitors, Cimetidine, Fluoxetine, Potassium iodide
Malignancy: Leukemia/lymphoma, Gammopathies (IgM, IgG)
Others: Serum sickness
May have concomitant angioedema. Recurrent episodes lasting months to years
🏥 SYSTEMIC MANIFESTATIONS
System Manifestations
Musculoskeletal (50%) Arthralgias, myalgias
GI (15%–30%) Recurrent abdominal pain, diarrhea, N/V
Renal (20%–30%) Glomerulonephritis or interstitial nephritis
Pulmonary (20%) SOB, severe COPD (major prognostic finding), laryngeal edema
Ocular (10%) Uveitis, conjunctivitis, episcleritis
Constitutional symptom Fever, malaise, arthralgias, myalgias

🧪 Laboratory Testing:

HUV: (low) CH50, C3 and C4; anti-C1Q Ab (~100% of HUV patients) and high ESR
Check ANA given strong association of HUV with SLE (up to 50%)
DsDNA and Anti smith (+)

(2) SCHNITZLER'S SYNDROME

Chronic urticaria + IgM gammopathy + two of the following:
Periodic fever, arthralgia, bone pain (otosclerosis), high ESR, or high WBC
Neutrophilic infiltrate on histopathology
Treatment: Anakinra (IL1 antagonist)

(3) EPISODIC ANGIOEDEMA WITH EOSINOPHILIA (GLEICH SYNDROME)

A syndrome of episodic angioedema with hypereosinophilia, weight gain, and fever
High IL5

(4) SYSTEMIC CAPILLARY LEAK SYNDROME

Aka Clarkson syndrome
Episodic massive plasma exudation from blood vessels >> potential life-threatening hypotension, analogous to anaphylaxis
Angioedema maybe a feature
associated with an IgG paraproteinemia
Medications (in particular IL-2) >> can produce systemic capillary leak syndrome
🔬 CLINICAL APPROACH

📋 HISTORY

Lesion: Duration, size, frequency, distribution, pruritic or painful
Risk factor: Travel history, Medication, Allergy, Stress, Food, Type of Work, History of infections
Other: Quality of life, Previous therapy, Family history, Medical condition, Constitutional symptoms, systemic symptoms: Respiratory/GI/urinary

👨‍⚕️ EXAMINATION

1- Thorough General examination and local examination for the wheel and exclude if any sign of angioedema
2- Sign of systemic disease
3- Common for urticaria to have cleared by the time of consultations, so patient optimal to take photograph, testing for dermatographism is needed if no lesion is visible at the time of consultation.

🧪 SPECIAL TESTS

Delayed pressure urticaria: Apply 2.5 kg weight as pressure on the back or thigh for 20 minutes
Thermal induced urticaria:
1- Cold Ice cube for 5 minutes 4C (wheals from 30 seconds - 20 minutes) (negative in familial and reflex)
2- Hot Apply heated cylinder (temp increment up to 45C) for 30 seconds- 5 minutes
Aquagenic urticaria: Shower at body temperature
Cholinergic urticaria: Exercise on an over heated setting.

🔬 SCREENING TESTS

Acute spontaneous urticaria: No need for screening but positive (RAST) due to type 1 hypersensitivity reaction
Chronic spontaneous urticaria: CBC and ESR, thyroid function, IgE level
📊 Standard Laboratory Tests:
Complete blood cell (CBC) count with differential
Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and/or C-reactive protein (CRP)
Liver enzymes, and thyrotropin (TSH).

🔍 Key Points for Additional Testing:

Eosinophilia: Elevated in parasitic infection as intestinal parasitosis
High ESR and CRP: Urticarial vasculitis
Examination for Stool for ova and parasites: If patient have 1- gastrointestinal (GI) tract symptoms, 2- an elevated eosinophil count, 3- a positive travel history
ANA, Rheumatoid factor and C4 should be ordered: If ESR is high and there another rheumatological feature.
Thyroid antibody titer order in: Patients with urticaria unresponsive to antihistamines or steroids to exclude thyroid disorder
Hepatitis B and C: If suspected cryoglobulinemia or cold-induced urticaria and urticarial vasculitis.
C1-esterase Level: If Angioedema without wheel and low C4 to Exclude hereditary Angioedema and acquired C1 inhibitor deficiency.

🔬 SKIN BIOPSY

Skin biopsy is not routinely needed but should be performed with one following features:
Indicative of urticarial vasculitis as lesions persisting beyond 24 hours, painful rather than pruritic, accompanying petechial or purpuric characteristics, leave residual pigmentation upon resolution
An elevated CRP/ESR and/or systemic symptoms (arthralgias, fever).
Symptoms that are unresponsive to appropriate doses of antihistamines
💊 TREATMENT

🎯 FIRST STEP: AVOID TRIGGERS

⚠️ PRIORITY: If Angioedema is present: Securing Airway is top priority and the possibly of using adjuvant Epinephrine.
Soothing lotions (containing pramoxine and/or menthol)
May need to avoid: Aspirin, NSAIDs Opiates
Exclusion diets: low pseudoallergen diets may be helpful in some cases
Cold urticaria: cyproheptadine 4-8 mg TID.

💊 FIRST LINE: 2nd Second-generation H1 antihistamines

Only about 40% Respond. Should be taken daily even if not symptomatic
🤰 Pregnancy Guidelines:
Cetirizine and loratadine (category B) as preferred medication especially in the 2nd and 3rd trimester.
Diphenhydramine and chlorpheniramine: have the most evidence supporting their safety in pregnancy. Not preferred during breastfeeding -> infant sedeative effect
Chlorpheniramine: Used but should be avoided close to delivery and specially during breast feeding (sedative effects in the child)
Hydroxyzine: Contraindicated during breastfeeding due to reports of seizures in breastfed infants.

📈 2ND LINE: If not control within 2-4 weeks

Increase the dose of 2nd generation antihistamine up to 4 tablet

🇺🇸 UNITED STATES PRACTICE PARAMETERS FOR THE DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT OF CU

Begin treatment at a step appropriate for patient's level of severity and previous treatment history
At each level of the step-care approach, medication(s) should be assessed for patient tolerance and efficacy
Returning to the previous level of treatment is appropriate at any step, once consistent control of urticaria/angioedema is achieved
Step 1:
Monotherapy with second-generation antihistamine
Avoidance of triggers (NSAIDs) and relevant physical factors if physical urticaria/angioedema syndrome is present
Step 2:
One or more of the following:
- Dose advancement of second-generation antihistamine used in step 1
- Add another second-generation antihistamine
- Add H₂-antagonist
- Add leukotriene-modifying agents
- Add first-generation antihistamine to be taken at bedtime
Step 3:
Dose advancement of sedating first-generation antihistamine (hydroxyzine or doxepin) as tolerated
Step 4:
Add an alternative agent:
Omalizumab or cyclosporine
Other antiinflammatory agents, immunosuppressants, or biologics

🎈 ANGIOEDEMA

🔍 INTRODUCTION

📋 Causes:

Idiopathic (most common)
Physical stimuli (temperature, vibration)
Type I hypersensitivity reactions: Drugs other than ACE-I, Arthropod bite, Food allergy
Pseudo allergic: NSAID, Asprin, IV contrast
C1 inhibitor deficiency syndromes (HAE, AAE)
ACE-I induced angioedema
Ascher syndrome: Recurrent upper (and sometimes lower) lips, with euthyroid goiter, stretching of eyelid skin
Ascher syndrome - MCQ focus

🧬 Pathogenesis:

Similar to urticaria. Main mediator is histamine apart from Urticaria (HAE, AAE) and ACE-I induced angioedema (main is excess bradykinin)
HAE, AAE and ACE-I induced angioedema - main mediator is excess bradykinin
🔬 ANGIOEDEMA DISORDERS

🧬 C1 INHIBITOR DEFICIENCY SYNDROMES

Two types: Hereditary and acquired

🧬 Hereditary Angioedema (HAE):

Starts in the 1st to 2nd decade and more severe in adolescence
Three types (type 1 is the most common 85%), (Type 3 is more common in female, while other type similar between female and male)
🔬 Pathogenesis: All forms of HAE are autosomal dominant
Type 1 and 2: Mutation in C1 INH
Types I low C1 INH levels and type II has low C1 INH function (but normal levels)
Type I vs Type II C1 INH levels - Key MCQ point
Type 3: Due to an activating mutation in factor (FXII)

🔬 Acquired Angioedema (AAE):

Starts in middle age
Type I: Consumption of C1 INH
Type II: Inhibitory antibodies against C1 INH
Risk: B-cell lymphoproliferative disorders, plasma cell dyscrasias, or autoimmune CTDs

🧪 Investigations:

Acquired and hereditary forms result in high level of bradykinin
Screening test: C4 (low result)
Low C1q levels seen in acquired angioedema only
Low C1 INH levels: in types I only (helps distinguish between between type 2 which normal level)

💊 DRUG-INDUCED ANGIOEDEMA

Most common: ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril > captopril)
Risk is more with black, 77% occur within first 3 weeks, almost all within first year
ACEI block kinase II led to high level of bradykinin

🔹 Others causes (NOT bradykinin-induced):

PCN, cephalosporins, NSAIDs, radiocontrast media, and monoclonal antibodies (biologics)
🎯 ANGIOEDEMA APPROACH

🚨 GENERAL APPROACH

Similar to urticaria
Intensive support may be needed
Should carry epi-pen in case of angioedema in oropharynx

🔍 FOR C1 INH DEFICIENCY

❓ When to suspect:

Recurrent episodes of angioedema without urticaria or pruritus, lasting two to five days (without treatment).
Unexplained recurrent episodes of self-limited, colicky, abdominal pain especially in patients who also have had cutaneous angioedema.
Unexplained laryngeal edema (even a single episode)
A family history of angioedema

🔍 FOR ANGIOEDEMA WITHOUT WHEALS (ISOLATED ANGIOEDEMA)

Step 1: Serum C4 level
Step 2: Interpret C4 result
If C4 is LOW:
C1 inh protein level (quantitative)
C1 inh function (functional assay)
  • HAE Type I: LOW C1 inh level, LOW C1 inh function
  • HAE Type II: NORMAL C1 inh level, LOW C1 inh function
C1q level
  • Acquired C1 inh Deficiency: LOW C1q levels
  • HAE (Types I & II): NORMAL C1q levels
If C4 is NORMAL:
  • Drug-Induced Angioedema: Clinical diagnosis based on medication history
  • nC1-INH-HAE (formerly Type III): Genetic testing for mutations (F12, PLG, etc.)
  • Idiopathic Angioedema: Diagnosis of exclusion
  • Episodic Angioedema with Eosinophilia (Gleich Syndrome): Check CBC with differential for marked eosinophilia
  • Systemic Capillary Leak Syndrome (Clarkson Syndrome): Serum Protein Electrophoresis (SPEP) if clinically suspected

Management of HAE

Antihistamines, Corticosteroids, and Epinephrine are NOT effective for HAE attacks. Using them is a clinical error and a common board exam trap.

Sebetralstat plasma kallikrein inhibitor (FDA approved)
Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor-induced urticaria is believed to result from the inhibition of endogenous kininase and a subsequent increase in bradykinin.
💊 Therapeutic Targets:
Icatibant: A decapeptide, is a specific bradykinin B₂ receptor antagonist.
Ecallantide: A 60-amino acid recombinant protein, selectively inhibits kallikrein.
Off label, icatibant has been used to treat ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema.
🩺 C1 Inhibitor Products:
Two forms of C1 inh, one of which is administered intravenously and the other subcutaneously, are approved for prevention of attacks.
Lanadelumab: A human monoclonal antibody that inhibits plasma kallikrein, is under investigation for prevention of attacks with twice-monthly administration.
🧬 Alternative to C1 inh concentrate:
Icatibant and ecallantide have been approved for the emergency treatment of hereditary angioedema (HAE) as alternatives to C1 inh concentrate (derived from human plasma) or recombinant C1 inh (derived from milk of transgenic rabbits).

Duration Keys: Wheals <24h (except DPU), Angioedema 2-3 days, DPU 30min-12h delay lasting >24h
Mediators: Histamine (most), Bradykinin (HAE, AAE, ACE-I angioedema)
Prognosis: 55% CSU resolves in 1 year, only 5% persists after 5 years
Safety: Cold urticaria patients should never swim alone (massive mediator release → hypotension)
Treatment Pearls: Omalizumab 300mg monthly (Saudi/European 3rd line), Dapsone avoid in G6PD deficiency
