Muscles of Facial Expression

Comprehensive Study Guide for Saudi Board Exam

The muscles of facial expression are a key component of facial anatomy, essential for both functional and aesthetic considerations in surgical and clinical practice. This interactive study guide covers their origin, innervation, functions, and clinical implications.

Key Facts: All muscles of facial expression arise from the second branchial arch, are innervated by the facial nerve (CN VII), and are concentrated in the central facial region. They insert into overlying skin and other local muscle groups and contribute to the relaxed skin tension lines of this region.

Overview of Facial Muscles

Clinical Pearl

Fluidity of facial movements is orchestrated by the muscles' interaction with the SMAS (superficial musculoaponeurotic system). A surgeon must have detailed knowledge of these muscles and the potential consequences of disrupting their innervation.

The muscles of facial expression can be categorized by their innervation from different branches of the facial nerve:

Facial Nerve Branch Distribution

  • Temporal Branch: Frontalis, Corrugator supercilii, Upper portion of Orbicularis oculi, Auricular (anterior and superior)
  • Zygomatic Branch: Lower portion of Orbicularis oculi, Nasalis (alar portion), Procerus, Levator anguli oris, Zygomaticus major
  • Buccal Branch: Buccinator, Depressor septi nasi, Nasalis (transverse portion), Zygomaticus minor, Levator labii superioris, Risorius
  • Marginal Mandibular Branch: Orbicularis oris (with buccal), Depressor anguli oris, Depressor labii inferioris, Mentalis
  • Cervical Branch: Platysma (with marginal mandibular)

Mnemonic: "T-Z-B-M-C"

The 5 branches of facial nerve from superior to inferior:

Temporal → Zygomatic → Buccal → Marginal mandibular → Cervical

Major Muscle Groups

  1. Epicranius (Occipitofrontalis): Has anterior (frontalis) and posterior regions connected by galea aponeurotica
  2. Periorbital Muscles: Orbicularis oculi, Corrugator supercilii, Procerus
  3. Nasal Muscles: Nasalis (alar and transverse portions), Depressor septi nasi
  4. Oral Muscles: Orbicularis oris, Levator/Depressor groups, Zygomaticus, Buccinator
  5. Platysma: Covers the neck and extends to the lower face

Understanding the muscles of facial expression is crucial for:

  • Surgical Procedures: Facial trauma repair, cosmetic surgery, facial reconstruction
  • Neurological Assessment: Evaluating facial nerve function in conditions like Bell's palsy
  • Cosmetic Applications: Botox injections, fillers, and other aesthetic procedures
  • Pathological Identification: Recognizing abnormal facial movements in various disorders

Clinical Pearl

Due to a high degree of anastomoses, damage to the zygomatic or buccal branches is often temporary and less debilitating than a similar injury to the temporal branch.

Muscles by Region

Upper Face/Scalp Region

Epicranius (Occipitofrontalis)

Innervation: Temporal Branch Connected by galea aponeurotica

The epicranius or occipitofrontalis muscle of the scalp has an anterior and posterior region connected by the galea aponeurotica. Contraction of these muscles allows the skin to slide over the scalp. However, humans no longer have voluntary control of the posterior muscle group, which is innervated by the posterior auricular branch of the facial nerve.

Clinical Pearl

Midline vertical forehead skin tension lines occur due to variation in distance between the left and right frontalis bellies. Horizontal skin tension lines occur perpendicular to the frontalis contractile orientation.

Frontalis

Innervation: Temporal Branch Raises eyebrows, opens eyes wide Droopy eyebrow, inability to open eyes widely

The frontalis muscle, a member of the epicranius complex, begins at the anterior hairline and inserts into the forehead and eyebrow skin. It also connects with the procerus, orbicularis oculi, and corrugator supercilii muscles.

Periauricular muscles (Temporoparietalis)

Innervation: Temporal Branch & Posterior Auricular Draw back temporal skin

The small periauricular muscles, also referred to as the temporoparietalis group, arise from the superficial temporalis fascia and the lateral galea. They help draw back the temporal skin and are innervated by the posterior ramus of the temporal branch of the facial nerve.

Periorbital Region

Orbicularis Oculi

Innervation: Temporal (upper) & Zygomatic (lower) Eye closure, blinking, tear extraction Difficulty with eye closure, eyelid droop

The orbicularis oculi muscle complex is the major group that acts on the eyelid and periorbital skin. It inserts into the medial and lateral canthal tendons and encircles the eye region. Its palpebral portion has a preseptal component overlying the orbital septum and a pretarsal portion overlying the tarsal plate of the eyelid. The orbicularis oculi is intertwined with the procerus, corrugator supercilii, and frontalis muscles and attaches to the superficial temporalis fascia at its lateral border.

  • Orbital portion: Surrounds the orbit, responsible for tight closure
  • Palpebral portion: Covers the eyelids
    • Preseptal: Covers the orbital septum
    • Pretarsal: Overlies the tarsal plate of eyelid

Corrugator Supercilii

Innervation: Temporal Branch Draws brows medially & downward (scowl) Difficulty scowling/furrowing brow

The corrugator supercilii muscle is located over the medial upper orbital rim. It contributes to a "scowling" facial expression by drawing the eyebrows medially and downward. It interdigitates with and is covered by the frontalis and orbicularis oculi muscles. The vertical and oblique skin tension lines of the glabella are caused by contraction of this muscle.

Nasal Region

Procerus

Innervation: Zygomatic Branch Causes foreshortening of nose & "bunny" lines

The procerus muscle overlies the nasal bone and attaches to the nasal root skin. It is responsible for "bunny lines", i.e. skin tension lines exaggerated by wrinkling up the nose.

Nasalis

Innervation: Zygomatic (alar) & Buccal (transverse) Alar flaring & compression

The nasalis muscle has two portions: alar and transverse. The alar portion is innervated by the zygomatic branch, while the transverse portion is innervated by the buccal branch. The nasalis muscle courses across the nasal dorsum and facilitates alar flaring and compression.

Depressor Septi Nasi

Innervation: Buccal Branch Pulls columella down toward lip

The depressor septi nasi muscle, which lies deep to the orbicularis oris, pulls the columella down toward the lip and can form a transverse skin tension line across the philtrum.

Oral Region

Orbicularis Oris

Innervation: Buccal & Marginal Mandibular Pursing of lips, speech articulation Feeding difficulty, drooling, speech problems

The orbicularis oris muscle is unique in that it has no bony or cartilaginous attachment. It functions as a sphincter for the mouth, essential for pursing and puckering of lips, and plays a critical role in speech articulation, particularly for labial sounds (M, V, F, P, O).

Clinical Pearl

Damage to the orbicularis oris can result in significant functional deficits including difficulty with feeding, drooling, and muffled speech with poor articulation of labial sounds.

Zygomaticus Major & Minor

Innervation: Zygomatic & Buccal Branches Main contributors to smile formation Difficulty smiling, lip seal issues

The zygomaticus major muscle travels from the zygoma downward and diagonally to the upper corner of the mouth, where it contributes to the nasolabial fold. Zygomaticus major and minor muscles are the main contributors to smile formation.

Buccinator

Innervation: Buccal Branch Prevents cheek overextension during chewing Food accumulation between teeth & cheek

The buccinator muscle constitutes a large area of the cheek as it courses from the posterior maxillary area to the upper medial surface of the mandible, where it interdigitates with the orbicularis oris. The facial vascular bundle crosses its surface. The parotid duct and transverse facial artery run parallel to each other as the duct pierces the buccinator to enter the mouth at the second maxillary molar.

The buccinator muscle is critically important for:

  • Preventing food accumulation in the vestibule during mastication
  • Providing resistance to intraoral pressure during blowing
  • Assisting in speech articulation and whistling
  • Serving as a surgical landmark, as the parotid duct penetrates it at the level of the second upper molar

Lip Elevator and Depressor Muscles

Complex network of agonist/antagonist muscles

An intricate network of interdigitating agonist and antagonist muscle groups contributes to lip movements and oral expression. Lip elevators, depressors, and retractors assist the sphincteric orbicularis oris muscle in lip movement.

Elevator Muscles:

  • Levator labii superioris: Elevates upper lip (Buccal branch)
  • Levator anguli oris: Elevates mouth angles (Zygomatic and buccal)
  • Zygomaticus minor: Elevates & retracts mouth angles (Buccal)

Depressor Muscles:

  • Depressor anguli oris (triangularis): Depresses & retracts lip (Marginal mandibular)
  • Depressor labii inferioris (quadratus): Depresses & retracts lip (Marginal mandibular)
  • Mentalis: Depresses & retracts lip, elevates chin, protrudes lower lip (Marginal mandibular)

The mentalis muscle has bellies with variable proximities to each other, and a patient with a chin "dimple" or "cleft chin" has a larger distance between mentalis muscle bellies. This is a normal anatomic variant.

Lower Face/Neck Region

Platysma

Innervation: Marginal Mandibular & Cervical Branches Minor role in retracting mouth angles

The platysma muscle runs from the superficial fascia of the chest across the anterior and lateral neck over the mandible to intercalate with the lower lip depressors and retractors. It is continuous with the superficial fascia or SMAS of the lower face. The platysma muscle provides only a thin protective cover to the anterior facial vascular bundle and mandibular branch of the trigeminal nerve.

Clinical Pearl - The Modiolus

The modiolus platform is formed by the convergence of fibers from the orbicularis oris and lip elevators and depressors. It is located 1 cm lateral to the mouth angle and accounts for cheek "dimples" in some patients. It works in synergy with the perioral muscles to facilitate speech enunciation.

SMAS & Fascial Planes

The superficial musculoaponeurotic system (SMAS) is a critical anatomical structure in facial surgery. It acts as a unifying infrastructure to the head and neck, creating an anatomic plane for identification and protection of key structures. Knowledge of neurovascular anatomy with respect to the superficial investing fascia promotes effective surgical dissection and closures that minimize functional compromise and maximize cosmesis.

Cross-Sectional Anatomy of Fascial Planes of the Upper Face

Key layers from superficial to deep:

  1. Epidermis
  2. Dermis
  3. Subcutaneous Fat
  4. SMAS (Superficial Musculoaponeurotic System)
    • Superficial leaflet
    • Deep leaflet - contains orbicularis oculi muscle
  5. Deep Temporalis Fascia
  6. Temporalis Muscle
  7. Periosteum

Important structures within these planes:

  • Temporal branch of facial nerve - lies between the SMAS and deep temporalis fascia
  • Superficial temporal artery - superficial to the SMAS

Surgical Pearl

Blunt dissection of the scalp SMAS separates the galea aponeurotica from the skull periosteum, the superficial temporalis fascia from the deep temporalis fascia, and the subfrontalis SMAS from the forehead periosteum. This is called the subgaleal plane, and it is the ideal avascular dissection plane for scalp surgery.

Surgical Considerations

  • Beware of the supraorbital/supratrochlear neurovascular bundles above the eyebrows when dissecting to the deep galea, as these can be damaged as they exit their respective foramina.
  • In the SMAS–parotid fascial plane of the cheek, the buccal and zygomatic branches of the facial nerve are endangered due to thin protective aponeurotic tissue in this area.
  • The superficial temporal vessels and auriculotemporal nerve course within the superficial temporalis fascia above the zygomatic arch, then assume a superficial plane in the subcutaneous fat.

Ideal Dissection Planes

Surgical undermining and dissection should maximize the horizontal planes provided by the superficial fascial network of the head and neck:

  • Undermining in the subcutaneous fat of the face below the subdermal plexus minimizes the risk to motor nerves, but remember that sensory branches and blood vessels course through this area.
  • There is sparse subcutaneous fat on the dorsal nose, forehead, upper lip, and eyelid.
  • With the exception of the forehead where dissection is usually in the subgaleal plane, the ideal dissection plane in these regions lies just superficial to the thin investing fascia (SMAS) covering the regional muscles of facial expression.

Mnemonic: "S-F-M-V-N"

For the layers in scalp (superficial to deep):

Skin → Fat → Muscle (galea aponeurotica) → Vessel layer (loose areolar tissue) → Normal periosteum

Clinical Applications

Facial Nerve Injury Patterns

Understanding the pattern of facial muscle deficits helps in localizing facial nerve injuries:

Temporal Branch Injury

Most significant functional impact
  • Flattening of horizontal forehead tension lines
  • Inability to raise eyebrow on affected side
  • May have difficulty with eye closure (though cross-innervation often prevents this)

Clinical Note: Damage to the temporal branch is often more debilitating than damage to other branches due to less cross-innervation.

Zygomatic Branch Injury

Often temporary due to anastomoses
  • Difficulty with tight eyelid closure
  • Lower eyelid droop
  • Complete loss uncommon due to cross-innervation by temporal branch

Buccal Branch Injury

Often temporary due to anastomoses
  • Asymmetric "crooked" smile at rest
  • Inability to whistle
  • Food accumulates between teeth & buccal mucosa with chewing

Marginal Mandibular Branch Injury

Affects lower face expression
  • Inability to smile - normal at rest, but asymmetric expression when smiling
  • Difficulty with lower lip movement

Surgical Relevance of Facial Muscles

Surgical Landmarks and High-Risk Zones

Several anatomical relationships are crucial for surgeons to recognize:

  • The temporal branch of facial nerve lies between the SMAS and deep temporalis fascia - vulnerable during forehead and temple procedures
  • Parotid duct pierces the buccinator to enter the mouth at the second maxillary molar - important landmark during facial procedures
  • The platysma muscle provides only a thin protective cover to the anterior facial vascular bundle and mandibular branch of the trigeminal nerve - requires careful attention during neck procedures

Cosmetic Implications

  • Relaxed Skin Tension Lines: Understanding the contractile orientation of facial muscles helps predict and account for skin tension lines during procedures
  • Age-Related Changes: With aging, support from the SMAS diminishes, leading to characteristic changes in facial appearance
  • Botulinum Toxin Targets: Knowledge of specific muscles and their actions guides precise neurotoxin placement for optimal cosmetic results

Anatomical Variants

Important variants to recognize:

  • Midline vertical forehead skin tension lines occur due to variation in distance between the left and right frontalis bellies
  • The mentalis muscle has bellies with variable proximities to each other - a patient with a chin "dimple" or "cleft chin" has a larger distance between mentalis muscle bellies (normal anatomic variant)
  • Cheek "dimples" represent the location of the modiolus platform (~1 cm lateral to mouth angle)
