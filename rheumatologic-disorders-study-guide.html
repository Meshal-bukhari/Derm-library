Overview: Autoinflammatory vs. Autoimmune Disorders

Autoinflammatory Disorders

Due to abnormalities in the innate immune system

  • Still disease
  • Inherited inflammatory syndromes
  • Characterized by recurrent fevers
  • Sterile neutrophilic inflammation
  • Frequently involves skin, joints, eyes, and serosae
  • Often due to overexpression of IL-1

Autoimmune Disorders

Due to abnormalities in the adaptive immunity and antibody production

  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Sjögren syndrome
  • Involves autoreactive antigen-specific T cells
  • Presence of characteristic autoantibodies
  • Lymphocytes play central role
  • Target-organ damage via specific immunity

Key Concept: The distinction between autoinflammatory (innate immune system) and autoimmune (adaptive immune system) is fundamental to understanding these diseases and their treatment approaches. While autoinflammatory conditions often respond to IL-1 blockade, autoimmune conditions typically require modulation of adaptive immunity.

Still disease is one of the 7 types of juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA), characterized by an evanescent, erythematous eruption that typically accompanies spiking fevers. It can also occur in adults (Adult-Onset Still Disease).

Systemic-Onset JIA (Still Disease)

  • Most common rheumatological disorder in children
  • Equal sex ratio (F:M is 1:1)
  • Possible LACC1 mutation
  • Evanescent eruption accompanying fever spikes
  • Joint involvement typically follows cutaneous manifestations

Adult-Onset Still Disease

  • Severe debilitating systemic illness
  • Often difficult to diagnose
  • Affects young adults
  • Preceded by sore throat and constitutional symptoms
  • Distinctive feature: carpal ankylosis

Adult-Onset Still Disease (AOSD) presents with a distinctive feature of carpal ankylosis, which results in minimal pain but a limited range of motion in the carpal joints.

Cutaneous Manifestations

The hallmark cutaneous finding is the "Resident's rash" - an evanescent, salmon-colored, non-pruritic eruption that accompanies fever spikes (occurs in late afternoon or early evening).

  • Present in up to 90% of patients
  • Begins prior to arthritis onset in 25% of patients
  • Predilection for axillae and waist
  • Linear lesions secondary to Koebner phenomenon are common

Systemic Manifestations

  • Fever: Daily high fevers (usually above 38.9°C) that typically occur in the late afternoon or early evening
  • Arthritis: Frequently polyarticular, most commonly involving the knees, ankles, hips, followed by small joints of hands
  • Other systemic features: Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, serositis

In AOSD, the skin eruption is asymptomatic and transient, occurring in association with fever spikes. It is most often macular with a characteristic salmon-pink color, favors sites of pressure, and can exhibit the Koebner phenomenon.

Pathogenesis

Still disease and AOSD are characterized by activation of the innate immune system with upregulation of IL-1 playing a critical role in the pathogenesis (due to inflammasome activation).

  • IL-1 pathways:
    • IL-1 → expression of leukocyte adhesion molecules by endothelial cells → perivascular infiltrate of neutrophils and monocytes
    • IL-1 expression → fever
  • IL-6 pathways:
    • IL-6 → reduced osteoblast and increased osteoblast activity, and increase CRP
  • Activated neutrophils and macrophages produce TNF-α, IL-1, and IL-6

The dysregulated production of IL-1 and IL-6 explains the therapeutic effectiveness of IL-1 antagonists (e.g., anakinra) and IL-6 inhibitors (e.g., tocilizumab) in treatment.

Laboratory Findings

Typical lab findings include:

  • Leukocytosis, granulocytosis, thrombocytosis
  • Elevated liver function tests
  • Polyclonal gammopathies
  • Markedly elevated ferritin (can be >4000 mg/ml in AOSD)
  • Elevated ESR/CRP

In AOSD, ferritin levels may correlate with disease activity; a low circulating level of glycosylated ferritin (fraction <20%) is characteristic.

Histopathology

The evanescent exanthem is characterized by a perivascular and interstitial neutrophil-dominant mixed infiltrate, a reaction pattern referred to as "neutrophilic urticarial dermatosis".

Diagnostic Criteria for Still Disease

  1. High episodic fevers (>38.9°C) daily for ≥ 2 weeks and documented to be quotidian for ≥ 3 days
  2. Plus one of the following features:
    • Transient evanescent, salmon pink, blanching eruption
    • Generalized lymphadenopathy
    • Hepatomegaly/splenomegaly
    • Serositis
  3. Symmetric polyarthritis > oligoarthritis

Complications

Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS) is a serious complication occurring in up to 10% of patients with Still disease and 15% of AOSD patients. It is characterized by:

  • Fever
  • Cytopenias
  • Liver dysfunction
  • Coagulopathy
  • Hypofibrinogenemia
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Very high serum levels of ferritin

Treatment: High-dose systemic steroids

Other complications include hepatitis, pericarditis, and rarely secondary amyloidosis.

The prognosis of Still disease/AOSD is variable. In ~40%-50% of patients, the arthritis resolves completely. However, approximately half have a chronic course with persistent arthritis and systemic complications.

Treatment

Treatment options for Still disease/AOSD include:

  • Mild symptoms: NSAIDs
  • Moderate-severe symptoms: Steroids + MTX
  • MAS: High dose systemic steroids
  • Biologics:
    • IL-1 inhibitors: Anakinra, canakinumab (based on IL-1 pathogenesis)
    • IL-6 inhibitors: Tocilizumab
  • Recalcitrant cases: HSCT (hematopoietic stem cell transplantation)

Symptoms that last for longer than 6 months indicate a poor prognosis. In AOSD, high ferritin is associated with chronic pattern of disease, recurrent flares, and poor prognosis.

What is the hallmark rash of Still disease, and when does it typically appear?

"Resident's rash" - evanescent, salmon-colored eruption that accompanies fever spikes in the late afternoon/evening. It favors the axillae and waist and exhibits Koebnerization.

Relapsing Polychondritis (RP)

Overview

  • Uncommon inflammatory disorder that primarily affects cartilaginous structures
  • Most common in Caucasians
  • Men and women are equally affected
  • Age: 20-60 years

Pathogenesis

  • Positive association with HLA-DR4
  • Immunologic reaction against type II collagen
  • Antibodies identified in 30%-50% of cases
  • Antibodies against types IX and XI collagen as well

Clinical Features

Key features include:

  • Auricular chondritis: Erythema, swelling and pain of the cartilaginous portion of the auricle, sparing the earlobe (most prominent feature)
  • Nasal chondritis: Can result in a saddle nose deformity
  • Respiratory tract involvement: The most serious complication - cough, hoarseness, dyspnea, wheezing, airway obstruction

Other features:

  • Joints: Episodic, migratory, asymmetric, non-erosive oligo- or polyarticular arthritis
  • Eyes: Conjunctivitis, scleritis, corneal ulcerations, uveitis, optic neuritis
  • Cutaneous manifestations: Vasculitis, aphthous ulcers, erythema elevatum diutinum, Sweet's syndrome, erythema nodosum

MAGIC syndrome = Mouth and genital ulcers with inflamed cartilages (Relapsing polychondritis + Behçet disease)

Patients with cutaneous small vessel vasculitis (CSSV) and aphthous ulcers have a higher risk of developing myelodysplastic syndrome.

Respiratory tract involvement is the most serious complication, with pneumonia being the #1 cause of mortality.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria for Relapsing Polychondritis

Diagnosis is based on:

  • Histologically confirmed chondritis in two of the following:
    • Auricular cartilage
    • Nasal cartilage
    • Laryngotracheal cartilage
  • OR one of them plus two or more of the following:
    • Ocular inflammation
    • Vestibular dysfunction
    • Hearing loss
    • Seronegative inflammatory arthritis

Treatment

  • Prednisone (0.5-1 mg/kg/day, or higher if systemic involvement)
  • Dapsone, colchicine, and NSAIDs can be used as adjunctive therapy
  • Other immunosuppressives (MTX most effective) and biologics

A comprehensive evaluation for systemic involvement, including a PET scan, should be performed prior to initiation of therapy, as systemic disease requires more aggressive treatment.

What are the 3 key features of Relapsing Polychondritis to recognize on exam?

1) Auricular chondritis with earlobe sparing
2) Nasal chondritis with saddle nose deformity
3) Respiratory tract involvement (most serious complication)

Sjögren syndrome (SS), also known as sicca syndrome, is an autoimmune disorder that affects secretory glands, particularly the lacrimal and salivary glands.

Epidemiology

  • Second most common autoimmune CTD after rheumatoid arthritis
  • Females 30-40s
  • F:M is 9:1
  • Males have a better prognosis (usually the opposite in autoimmune CTDs)

Classification

  • Primary: Occurs alone
  • Secondary: Occurs in association with other autoimmune diseases (SLE, RA, SSc)

SS patients have a 20x increased risk of Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (B-cell). The most common lymphoma is extranodal marginal zone B-cell lymphoma in the salivary gland.

Increased risk of neonatal lupus in pregnant women (especially if positive serologies).

Pathogenesis

Genetics

  • Germline abnormality of TNFAIP3 → ↑risk of antigen-driven B-cell lymphomas
  • Increased expression of Bcl-2 → ↑risk of marginal zone B-cell lymphoma

Triggers

  • EBV, coxsackievirus, HTLV-1, and HCV are possible triggers

Immune Response

Autoantibodies:

  • Anti-Ro/SSA, Anti-La/SSB are associated with:
    • ↑risk of subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE)
    • ↑risk of vasculitis
    • ↑risk of extraglandular disease
    • ↓age of onset
  • Anti-Fodrin → most sensitive and specific antibody

Other immunologic features: ↑TLR function, ↑Th17, ↑costimulatory molecules (CD40-CD40L)

Clinical Features

The most common cutaneous feature is xerosis.

The most important cutaneous feature is vasculitis → associated with systemic involvement, positive serologies, high ESR, high mortality, and risk of lymphoma.

Mucosal Manifestations

  • Xerostomia: Difficulty swallowing, oral thrush, dental caries, transient gland enlargement
  • Xerophthalmia: Keratoconjunctivitis sicca, photophobia, foreign body sensation

Other Cutaneous Manifestations

  • Purpura
  • Urticarial vasculitis
  • SCLE
  • Annular erythemas
  • Erythema nodosum
  • Amyloidosis
  • Raynaud's
  • Waldenstrom's hypergammaglobulinemic purpura

Internal Organs

  • Lungs
  • Kidneys
  • Bone marrow
  • Neurological involvement
  • Arthritis (common)

Diagnosis

ACR/EULAR 2016 Classification Criteria for Primary Sjögren's Syndrome

Score ≥4 required when the weight from the five criteria items below are summed:

  • Labial salivary gland with focal lymphocytic sialadenitis and focus score of ≥1 foci/4 mm²: 3 points
  • Anti-SSA/Ro-positive: 3 points
  • Ocular staining score ≥5 (or van Bijsterveld score ≥4) in at least one eye: 1 point
  • Schirmer's test ≤5 mm/5 min in at least one eye: 1 point
  • Unstimulated whole saliva flow rate ≤0.1 mL/min: 1 point

Minor salivary gland biopsy is one of the criteria for diagnosis: presence of one or more foci (50 or more lymphocytes) in 4 mm² of salivary gland tissue.

Prognosis

Bad prognostic factors:

  • Hypocomplementemia
  • Cryoglobulinemia
  • Vasculitis
  • Parotid enlargement
  • Lymphoproliferative diseases

Treatment

  • Symptomatic treatment: Emollients, eye drops, saliva substitutes
  • If systemic involvement/vasculitis: Systemic therapy
    • Systemic corticosteroids
    • Hydroxychloroquine
    • Steroid-sparing immunosuppressants (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil)
    • Rituximab in resistant cases

What are the 5 poor prognostic factors in Sjögren syndrome?

1) Hypocomplementemia
2) Cryoglobulinemia
3) Vasculitis
4) Parotid enlargement
5) Lymphoproliferative diseases

Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)

Overview

  • Overlapping features of multiple autoimmune CTDs
  • Strong female predominance (9:1)
  • Associated with HLA-DR4

Pathogenesis

Anti-U1RNP (high titers) antibodies thought to play pathogenic role

The function of U1RNP is to splice pre-mRNA into mRNA

  • Anti-hnRNP-A2 have been described
  • May be associated with EBV and cytomegalovirus infection → molecular mimicry

Clinical Features

  • Overlapping features of two or more of the following:
    • SLE
    • DM/PM
    • SSc
    • RA
  • Raynaud's phenomenon is the earliest and most common sign

Pulmonary artery hypertension is the most common cause of mortality

Diagnosis

  • Lacks anti-dsDNA, Smith antibodies, and hypocomplementemia - helps to differentiate from SLE
  • High titer of U1RNP antibody → hallmark

Treatment

Pathology and treatment depend on the clinical entity and manifestations present.

What is the hallmark antibody in MCTD, what is its function, and what is the most common cause of mortality?

High-titer anti-U1RNP antibody.
U1RNP functions to splice pre-mRNA into mRNA.
Pulmonary artery hypertension is the most common cause of mortality.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) affects ~2% of the population with a female predominance (F:M is 2-3:1).

Pathogenesis

  • Trigger: EBV and E. coli infections → ?molecular mimicry
  • Genetics: HLA-DR1 and -DR4, PTPN22 gene (also seen in SLE, Graves, and T1DM)
  • Immune response: Interplay between the innate and adaptive immunity, antibodies that target citrullinated proteins, increased TNF-α and IL-6

Cutaneous Manifestations

Rheumatoid Nodules

  • 20% of RA patients
  • Associated with high titer of RF
  • Firm semi-mobile papulonodules on pressure areas/extensors
  • May also occur in visceral organs

Accelerated Rheumatoid Nodulosis

Sudden appearance of multiple RNs following treatment with MTX, rapid tapering of steroids, or TNF-α inhibitors

Rheumatoid Vasculitis

  • Late complication of RA
  • Affects any vessel
  • Cutaneous features depend on the size of involved vessels
  • Associated with neuropathy, scleritis, alveolitis, carditis, intestinal ulcers, and proteinuria
  • Bad prognosis (mortality ~40%)
  • Treatment: Aggressive therapy with systemic steroids + cyclophosphamide ± plasmapheresis

Bywater's Lesions

  • Nailfold thromboses and purpuric papules on the distal digits
  • LCV on histopathology but not associated with systemic vasculitis

Felty Syndrome

  • Rare but severe (occurs in seropositive patients)
  • Triad of neutropenia, splenomegaly, and RA
  • Therapy-resistant leg ulcers (pretibial) → "PG-like"
  • Increased risk of systemic infections
  • Increased risk of lymphoma and leukemia
  • Treatment with G-CSF or splenectomy

Rheumatoid Neutrophilic Dermatitis

Resembles Sweet syndrome clinically and histologically, but no tenderness + no fever + seropositive for RF

When evaluating a patient with suspected rheumatoid vasculitis and negative/inconclusive cutaneous histologic findings, consider nerve conduction studies/electromyography (to detect mononeuritis multiplex), sural nerve biopsy, muscle biopsy, or angiogram.

  3. Nailfold thromboses of Bywater's lesions

Laboratory Findings

  • RF (sensitivity 80%, specificity 85%)
  • Anti-CCP (sensitivity 70%, specificity 95%)

Treatment

  • Articular disease: NSAIDs, low-dose systemic corticosteroids, disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis of medium/large vessels: Aggressive therapy with systemic steroids + cyclophosphamide ± plasmapheresis
  • Others: AZA, MMF, TNF-α inhibitors, Rituximab, cyclosporine

Which cutaneous manifestation of RA requires investigation for neuropathy?

Palpable purpura associated with rheumatoid vasculitis

Autoinflammatory Diseases

General Characteristics

  • Group of diseases due to aberrant activation of the innate immune system
  • Characterized by recurrent fevers and flares of multi-organ inflammation
  • Frequently involves the skin, joints, eyes, and serosae
  • Features sterile neutrophilic inflammation
  • Most are likely due to overexpression of IL-1
  • Improvement on anakinra, rilonacept, and canakinumab (Anti-IL-1) supports this theory

Histopathology

"Neutrophilic urticarial dermatosis" is a common reaction pattern seen in autoinflammatory disorders.

  • Perivascular + interstitial dermal mixed infiltrate, primarily neutrophilic
  • Similar pattern seen in Schnitzler syndrome, Still disease/AOSD

Familial Cold Autoinflammatory Syndrome (FCAS)

  • Attack length: Minutes-3 days
  • Cold-induced urticarial papules and plaques (extremities > trunk, face)
  • Arthralgia > myalgia

FCAS differs from acquired cold urticaria in that it has:

  • Negative ice-cube test (only with acquired cold urticaria)
  • Cutaneous and extracutaneous reactions are often delayed up to 6 hours following a cold challenge

Muckle-Wells Syndrome (MWS)

  • Attack length: 1-2 days
  • Urticarial papules and plaques (widespread on face, trunk and extremities)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss; headache
  • Abdominal pain, but rarely serositis
  • ~25% develop amyloidosis

Neonatal-Onset Multisystem Inflammatory Disease (NOMID)/Chronic Infantile Neurologic, Cutaneous, and Articular Syndrome (CINCA)

  • Attack pattern: Continuous + flares
  • Urticarial papules and plaques; occasional oral ulcers
  • Epiphyseal and patellar overgrowth, arthritis, deforming arthropathy
  • Sensorineural hearing loss; aseptic meningitis, seizures
  • Dysmorphic facies - frontal bossing, protruding eyes
  • Amyloidosis as late complication

Other Inherited Autoinflammatory Diseases

DIRA (Deficiency of IL-1 Receptor Antagonist)

  • Gene: IL1RN (IL-1 receptor antagonist)
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Age of onset: Neonatal
  • Cutaneous: Pustules within areas of erythema (resembles pustular psoriasis)
  • Systemic: Sterile osteolytic bone lesions
  • Treatment: IL-1 antagonists - often dramatic response

DITRA (Deficiency of IL-36 Receptor Antagonist)

  • Gene: IL36RN (IL-36 receptor antagonist)
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Age of onset: Childhood to adulthood; can flare with pregnancy
  • Cutaneous: Pustules within areas of erythema - resembles generalized pustular psoriasis
  • Treatment: Retinoids, IL-1 antagonists

PAPA Syndrome

  • Gene: PSTPIP1
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of onset: Childhood
  • Cutaneous: Pyoderma gangrenosum, pathergy, severe acne
  • Systemic: Recurrent, painful, sterile arthritis
  • Treatment: TNF inhibitors, IL-1 antagonists, corticosteroids

Blau Syndrome

  • Gene: NOD2/CARD15
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of onset: Childhood
  • Cutaneous: Numerous tiny papules, granulomatous infiltrate in dermis
  • Systemic: Uveitis, early-onset symmetric granulomatous polyarticular arthritis
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids, TNF inhibitors, MTX, CSA, IL-1 antagonists

ADA2 Deficiency

  • Gene: CECR1 (ADA2)
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Age of onset: Infancy to adulthood
  • Cutaneous: Livedo racemosa
  • Systemic: Childhood-onset polyarteritis nodosa, early-onset strokes, hepatosplenomegaly
  • Treatment: TNF inhibitors, Allogeneic HSCT

SAVI (STING-Associated Vasculopathy with onset in Infancy)

  • Gene: TMEM173 (STING)
  • Inheritance: AD
  • Age of onset: Early infancy
  • Cutaneous: Acral infarcts/ulcerations/gangrene, bone resorption with loss of digits, violaceous plaques
  • Systemic: Nasal septum perforation, interstitial lung disease
  • Treatment: JAK inhibitors (minimal response to corticosteroids, DMARDs)

CANDLE Syndrome

  • Gene: PSMB8
  • Inheritance: AR
  • Age of onset: Infancy
  • Cutaneous: Violaceous plaques that can be annular, pernio-like lesions, eyelid edema and erythema
  • Systemic: Partial lipomuscular atrophy, joint contractures, basal ganglia calcification
  • Treatment: JAK inhibitors

How can you differentiate Familial Cold Autoinflammatory Syndrome (FCAS) from acquired cold urticaria?

FCAS has a negative ice-cube test and typically shows delayed onset of cutaneous and extracutaneous reactions (up to 6 hours after cold exposure), while acquired cold urticaria has a positive ice-cube test and immediate reaction.
