POTENTIALLY LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY

Mucormycosis in Adult

A High-Yield Overview for Saudi Board Exam Preparation

Quick Reference & High-Yield Facts

🚨 BOARD EXAM HIGH-YIELD ALERT

Mucormycosis is a potentially fatal fungal infection caused by fungi in the order Mucorales. The most common causative genera are Rhizopus, Mucor, and Rhizomucor. It predominantly affects immunocompromised hosts and can present in various clinical forms with high mortality if not promptly treated.

📌 KEY FACTS TO REMEMBER

  • The 5 major clinical forms: rhino-orbital-cerebral, pulmonary, cutaneous, gastrointestinal, and disseminated
  • Major risk factors: uncontrolled diabetes mellitus, diabetic ketoacidosis, hematological malignancies, neutropenia, deferoxamine therapy, corticosteroid therapy
  • Hallmark of infection: vascular invasion and tissue necrosis
  • Black necrotic ulcers/eschars are classic physical findings
  • Management depends on: rapid diagnosis, correction of underlying risk factors, aggressive surgical debridement, and antifungal therapy

💡 MEMORY AID: "MUCOR" - Key Diagnostic Features

  • M - Massive tissue necrosis with black eschars
  • U - Unilateral symptoms (especially in rhinocerebral form)
  • C - Compromised hosts (immunosuppressed, diabetic)
  • O - Orbital involvement (proptosis, ophthalmoplegia) in rhinocerebral form
  • R - Rapid progression and high mortality if untreated
Causative Organisms

Most common: Rhizopus, Mucor, Rhizomucor

Less common: Absidia, Cunninghamella, Saksenaea, Apophysomyces

Definition & Pathophysiology

Mucormycosis is an infection caused by fungi in the order Mucorales. These fungi are ubiquitously distributed in decaying organic material such as vegetables, bread, fruits, and seeds. They grow and sporulate abundantly, and clinical infection typically occurs via inhalation of spores. Primary cutaneous infection can develop when spores are inoculated through abraded skin.

Key Pathophysiological Features

  • HIGH-YIELD The hallmark of infection is vascular invasion and tissue necrosis
  • Fungi can invade blood vessels causing thrombosis and subsequent tissue infarction
  • Angioinvasion leads to characteristic black necrotic lesions/eschars
  • Fungi appear as broad, non-septate hyphae with branches occurring at right angles (differentiate from Aspergillus which has septate hyphae with acute-angled branching)

Microscopic Identification Memory Aid

"Mucor takes RIGHT turns" - Mucor hyphae branch at right angles (90°)

"Aspergillus branches ACUTELY" - Aspergillus hyphae branch at acute angles (45°)

The 5 Major Types of Mucormycosis
1. Rhino-orbital-cerebral Disease

MOST COMMON FORM COMMON IN DIABETICS

Clinical Presentation:

  • Unilateral facial pain, headaches, fever
  • Nasal or sinus congestion and discharge
  • Black, necrotic ulcerations (usually involving the palate)
  • Orbital involvement: orbital cellulitis, proptosis, ophthalmoplegia
  • Multiple cranial nerve palsies (permanent defects in up to 70% of cases)
  • May present with central retinal artery occlusion (sudden blindness)

Complications: Spread to cavernous sinus, internal carotid artery, and brain

2. Pulmonary Disease

COMMON IN HEMATOLOGICAL MALIGNANCIES

Clinical Presentation:

  • Often insidious and minimally symptomatic in immunocompromised patients
  • May be found incidentally on chest CT
  • Isolated fevers unresponsive to antibiotics
  • Cough, dyspnea, and unremitting fever despite broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Hemoptysis (can be fatal)

Physical Examination: Often nonspecific or normal (can include tachypnea, crackles, decreased breath sounds, wheezing)

Imaging Findings:

  • CXR: Segmental consolidation (unilateral/bilateral, focal/multifocal), nodules, cavitation
  • CT: "Halo sign" (ground-glass opacity around nodule), enlarged hilar/mediastinal lymph nodes (10%), pleural effusions (20%)
3. Cutaneous Disease

Forms:

  • Primary: Associated with direct inoculation (trauma, burns, surgical wounds)
  • Secondary: Occurs from hematogenous spread
  • Intertriginous: Reported in immunocompromised hospitalized patients (skin folds without preexisting trauma)

Clinical Features:

  • Initial inflammation followed by necrosis
  • Black, necrotic ulcer with a violaceous border
  • Often occurs at sites of tissue injury, compound fractures, or stomas
  • Crepitus is NOT present (differentiating feature)
4. Gastrointestinal Disease

MOST COMMON IN NEWBORNS

Epidemiology: Mainly seen in extreme cases of malnutrition or immunosuppression

Pathogenesis: Believed to occur via direct ingestion of spores

Clinical Features:

  • Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding
  • Fever
  • Change in bowel habits

Pitfall: May be misdiagnosed as necrotizing enterocolitis in newborns

5. Disseminated Disease

Pathogenesis: Most commonly occurs following pulmonary mucormycosis, but can result from any subtype

Organ Involvement:

  1. Brain (most susceptible)
  2. Spleen
  3. Heart
  4. Skin
  5. Other organs

Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors

  • HIGH-YIELD Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus
  • HIGH-YIELD Diabetic ketoacidosis
  • Hematological malignancies
  • Neutropenia
  • Lymphomas
  • Deferoxamine therapy
  • Malnourishment
  • Immunosuppression due to solid organ and stem cell transplantation
  • Corticosteroid therapy

Other At-Risk Populations

  • Surgical patients
  • Burn patients
  • Trauma patients
  • Injection drug users
  • Newborn infants

HIV/AIDS Patients

Mucormycosis is rare in HIV patients but when it occurs:

  • Can occur without typical predisposing factors
  • More common in intravenous drug users
  • Patients with prolonged and severe neutropenia
  • Individuals with very low CD4 counts
Special Epidemiological Settings
  • Natural Disasters: Epidemic mucormycosis has been associated with penetrating and nonpenetrating trauma from natural disasters (e.g., Joplin, Missouri tornado in 2011 - Apophysomyces trapeziformis species)
  • COVID-19: BOARD EXAM ALERT Post-COVID-associated mucormycosis (predominantly pulmonary) has been reported with increasing frequency, particularly on the Indian subcontinent

Drug that increase the risk

  • Deferoxamine - strongest association
  • Chelating agents
  • Corticosteroids
  • Ibrutinib
  • Monoclonal antibodies (alemtuzumab, blinatumomab, infliximab)
  • TNF-alpha inhibitors
  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors
Clinical Presentation & Diagnostic Findings

Look For

  • HIGH-YIELD Black, necrotic ulcer with a violaceous border - often at sites of tissue injury, compound fractures, or stomas (crepitus is not present)
  • Periorbital and facial edema, erythema, and pain
  • Proptosis and limitation of eye movement
  • Decreased vision, conjunctival chemosis, afferent pupillary defect, ophthalmoplegia
  • Central retinal artery occlusion (sudden onset of blindness)
  • Fever and mental status alteration
  • Adherent black eschar on palate, nasal turbinates, and/or septum in the rhino-orbital-cerebral form

Important Diagnostic Pearls

  • RED FLAG Persistent fever of unclear etiology in an at-risk patient (particularly with leukemia, lymphoma, or following bone marrow or solid organ transplant) warrants evaluation with chest CT
  • RED FLAG Cough, dyspnea, and hemoptysis in an at-risk patient despite broad-spectrum antibiotics should prompt search for invasive fungal infections
  • HIGH-YIELD Insidious presentation with subtle physical findings in immunocompromised patients - requires high index of suspicion
  • BOARD TIP Carefully survey oral cavity and palate, perform prompt biopsy and culture of any suspicious lesions

Histopathological Hallmark - Must Know!

The hallmark of infection is vascular invasion and tissue necrosis demonstrated on histopathological specimens. All suspicious lesions should be biopsied (not swabbed) and specimens sent to both pathology and microbiology.

Laboratory and Diagnostic Testing

  • KOH preparation: Fungal hyphae can be demonstrated with potassium hydroxide (KOH) and hematoxylin and eosin staining, or chlorazol black E stain
  • Histopathology: Hyphae can also be seen under Grocott's methenamine-silver or periodic acid-Schiff staining
  • Culture: Diagnostic yield of culturing sputum, respiratory secretions, and blood is low
  • Important note: Alert the microbiology laboratory to the possibility of mucormycosis for proper processing of tissue samples
  • Surrogate serum markers: (1,3)-beta-d-glucan, aspergillus antigen, serum cryptococcal antigen, and serum and urine histoplasma antigens

Pulmonary Differential Diagnosis

The differential of a lung nodule in an immunocompromised host includes:

  • Nonstandard presentations of common bacteria:
    • Staphylococcus aureus
    • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
    • Legionella micdadei
  • Other bacterial infections:
    • Nocardiosis
    • Actinomycosis
  • Other fungal infections:
    • HIGH-YIELD Aspergillus
    • Cryptococcus
    • Endemic fungi:
      • Histoplasmosis
      • Blastomycosis
      • Coccidioidomycosis
  • Noninfectious etiologies:
    • Neoplasms
    • Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disease (PTLD)

Similar Clinical Manifestations

The clinical manifestations of mucormycosis are similar to those of other invasive fungi:

  • Aspergillus - thinner, septate hyphae with frequent, acute-angled branching
  • Fusarium - septate hyphae
  • Pseudallescheria spp. - septate hyphae

Mucor morphology: Broad, non-septate hyphae with branches occurring at right angles; rarely, septa may be seen

Orbital/Rhinocerebral Differential Diagnosis

  • Orbital cellulitis
  • Preseptal cellulitis
  • Cutaneous anthrax

Cutaneous Differential Diagnosis

  • Ecthyma gangrenosum (in immunocompromised patients)
  • Cutaneous leishmaniasis
  • Tularemia
  • Candida sepsis

Gastrointestinal Differential Diagnosis

  • HIGH-YIELD Necrotizing enterocolitis (in a newborn)
Mucor Aspergillus

Mucor Characteristics

  • Microscopy: Broad, non-septate hyphae
  • Branching pattern: Right-angle (90°) branching
  • Tissue invasion: Marked angioinvasion with thrombosis and tissue necrosis
  • Risk factors: Primarily diabetes, ketoacidosis, neutropenia
  • Treatment: Lipid formulation of amphotericin B + surgical debridement

Aspergillus Characteristics

  • Microscopy: Narrow, septate hyphae
  • Branching pattern: Acute-angle (45°) branching
  • Tissue invasion: Angioinvasion but less pronounced necrosis than Mucor
  • Risk factors: Primarily neutropenia, transplantation, prolonged steroid use
  • Treatment: Voriconazole is first-line (NOT effective against Mucor)

Mucormycosis vs. Aspergillosis Comparison

Feature Mucormycosis Aspergillosis
Hyphae structure Broad, non-septate (ribbon-like) Thin, septate
Branching angle Right angles (90°) Acute angles (45°)
Primary risk factor Diabetes/DKA Neutropenia
First-line treatment Lipid amphotericin B Voriconazole
Response to echinocandins Poor (may use as adjunct) Good (as salvage or combination)

Management & Treatment

4 Critical Management Factors

  1. Rapidity of diagnosis
    Early recognition and diagnosis are essential for survival
  2. Correction of underlying risk factor
    Treatment of acidosis, reduction in immunosuppression, glycemic control
  3. Aggressive surgical debridement
    Essential for source control; may require repeated surgical exploration
  4. Institution of antifungal therapy
    Early administration of appropriate antifungal agents

Key Management Pearls

  • HIGH-YIELD Empiric therapy is warranted pending the results of diagnostic tests
  • Consultation with an infectious disease specialist is highly recommended
  • Surgery is often necessary for control, although it can be highly morbid

Treatment Regimens

Primary Regimen
  • Lipid complex amphotericin B 5 (up to 10) mg/kg/day intravenously (IV), or
  • Liposomal amphotericin B 5 mg/kg/day IV
Alternative Regimen
  • Isavuconazole 372 mg IV every 8 hours for a total of 6 doses followed by 372 mg IV daily
  • Posaconazole 400 mg by mouth twice daily (take with a meal), or
  • Amphotericin B 1 mg/kg/day IV
Step-down Regimen

Following lipid formulation of amphotericin B:

  • Posaconazole (delayed release tablet) 300 mg once daily (take with a meal)
  • Isavuconazole 372 mg by mouth once daily

Treatment Duration

Treatment should be continued until:

  • Resolution of clinical signs and symptoms
  • Resolution of radiographic indicators of active disease
  • Resolution of underlying immunosuppression, if possible

Treatment can last weeks to months and can be lifelong in the immunocompromised.

Echinocandin Controversy

The addition of an echinocandin to amphotericin B can be considered but is controversial. Some studies suggest benefit in rhino-orbital-cerebral disease, but overall evidence is mixed.

Question 5: Treatment

The first-line antifungal agent for treatment of mucormycosis is:

  • A. Liposomal amphotericin B
  • B. Voriconazole
  • C. Fluconazole
  • D. Caspofungin

Correct! Lipid formulations of amphotericin B are the first-line treatment for mucormycosis. Voriconazole is ineffective against Mucorales. Posaconazole or isavuconazole may be used as step-down therapy or in cases where amphotericin B is contraindicated.
