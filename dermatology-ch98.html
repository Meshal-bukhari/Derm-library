Hypertrophic Scars and Keloids

Core Concept: Both conditions result from abnormal wound healing with excessive collagen production by fibroblasts due to an imbalance between collagen synthesis and degradation.

Epidemiology

  • Others > Caucasians
  • Hypertrophic scars > keloids in frequency
  • Most common in 10-30 years of age

Pathogenesis

  • Fetal life: Healing occurs without scarring (regenerative healing)
  • Adult life: Healing occurs in three consecutive steps:
    1. Inflammatory phase: Aims to contain injury and prevent infection
    2. Proliferative phase: Formation of granulation tissue
    3. Remodeling phase: Collagen synthesis and degradation
  • In keloids and hypertrophic scars, collagen synthesis is markedly increased in relation to collagen degradation
  • High levels of TGF-β → leads to fibrosis
  • Fundamentally an imbalance between collagen production and collagen degradation

Syndromes associated with spontaneous keloids:

  • Goeminne syndrome
  • Bethlem myopathy syndrome
  • Rubinstein-Taybi syndrome

Clinical Features

  • Smooth firm nodules and plaques
  • May be pruritic or painful
  • Develop within a few weeks to months after wounding
  • Hypertrophic scars flatten within 1-2 years; keloids do not
  • Favor sites with increased skin tensions (upper trunk, shoulder, upper outer arm)
  • Keloids like the ear, despite minimal skin tension

Pathology

Hypertrophic Scars

  • Increased fibroblasts and density of collagen fibers within dermis
  • Collagen fibers oriented parallel to the skin surface
  • Flattened epidermis
  • Fibrotic papillary dermis
  • Elastic fibers diminished or absent
  • Increased dermal blood vessels oriented vertically

Keloids

  • Strikingly thick, glassy, homogeneous collagen bundles
  • Collagen fibers oriented haphazardly throughout dermis (keloidal collagen)
  • Epidermis not involved
  • Papillary dermis not involved
  • Fibroblasts not increased
  • Increased elastic fibers within deep dermis

What is the key histopathological difference in collagen arrangement between hypertrophic scars and keloids?

Hypertrophic scars have fine, wavy collagen oriented parallel to the epidermis, while keloids have thick, glassy collagen bundles oriented haphazardly/randomly throughout the dermis (keloidal collagen).

Treatment

  • First-line therapy: Intralesional triamcinolone (10-40 mg/ml)
  • Clobetasol propionate 0.05% cream under silicone dressing occlusion (for needle-phobic patients)
  • Intralesional 5-fluorouracil (can be combined with steroids)
  • Intralesional Bleomycin (Level 2):
    Mechanism: Inhibits fibroblast proliferation.
    Use: Comparable improvement to triamcinolone; consider in patients at risk for steroid-induced hypopigmentation.
    Dose: 1.5 IU/ml, multiple puncture, max 2ml/cm², 10 IU/session, monthly.
  • PDL "Can start day of suture removal, repeated every 3-4 weeks. Used for postsurgical scar reduction." and cryotherapy can also be combined with steroids
  • Scar revision (risk of recurrence)
  • Radiation therapy (last option)

Dupuytren Disease

Core Concept: A fibromatosis affecting the palmar and digital fascia leading to flexion contractures.

Epidemiology

  • Common in Caucasians
  • Rare in dark skin types
  • Most common in older men
  • Associated with other fibromatoses (see chapter 116)
  • Also associated with diabetes, alcoholism, and cigarette smoking
  • Vemurafenib may cause worsening or even new-onset disease

Pathogenesis

  • Fibrotic cytokines (TGF-β1/2) are involved
  • Leads to thickening of palmar and digital fascia

Clinical Features

  • Usually presents late
  • Flexion contractures of the affected digits
  • Ring finger is most commonly affected
  • Disease progression:
    1. Begins as a nodule in the fascia of the palm
    2. Grows to produce a cord
    3. Cord contracts → flexion contracture

Pathology

  • Proliferation of myofibroblasts which align along lines of tension
  • Smooth muscle actin (SMA) positive
  • Thick collagen

Treatment

  • Fasciectomy
  • Collagenase injection
Which finger is most commonly affected in Dupuytren disease?

The ring finger is most commonly affected in Dupuytren disease.

Cutis Vertices Gyrata

Core Concept: Overgrowth of the scalp producing symmetric gyrate or cerebriform folding of the skin.

Clinical Features

  • Overgrowth of the scalp
  • Progresses to make symmetric gyrate or cerebriform folding of the skin
  • Soft to spongy on palpation
  • Affects the crown and vertex
  • Hair loss in the folds, but not the furrows
  • Can be isolated or associated with neurological or ophthalmological manifestations

Treatment

  • Asymptomatic, requires no treatment
  • Surgical excision is occasionally performed

Hyaline Fibromatosis Syndromes

MCQ FOCUS

Includes juvenile hyaline fibromatosis and infantile systemic hyalinosis, which are on a spectrum with mutation in anthrax toxin receptor 2 (ANTXR2) and autosomal recessive inheritance.

Core Concept: Rare genodermatoses characterized by hyaline accumulation in the dermis, with a spectrum of severity.

General Characteristics

  • Includes juvenile hyaline fibromatosis and infantile systemic hyalinosis
  • Rare genodermatoses
  • Characterized by hyaline accumulation in the dermis
  • They have the same mutation
  • They are on a spectrum
MCQ FOCUS

Genetics:

  • Mutation in anthrax toxin receptor 2 (ANTXR2)
  • Autosomal recessive inheritance

Clinical Features

  • Firm papules and nodules
  • Typical case is on the perinasal area (Board exam focus)
  • Scalp, ear, neck, and hands can be involved
  • Severe physical limitations due to contractures

Pathology

  • Ill-defined nodules of hyaline collagen
  • Periodic acid–Schiff (PAS) positive

Treatment

  • Limited options
  • Surgical excision of nodules and gingival hypertrophy
  • Release of contractures can be performed
  • Lesions often recur
  • Physiotherapy

Disorders Characterized by Increased Elastic Tissue Fibers

Note: This section presents a comprehensive table of disorders characterized by increased elastic tissue fibers. Refer to the book for complete descriptions of each entity.

Key disorders included in the table:

Disorder Clinical Features Histopathologic Features Comments
Late-onset focal dermal elastosis Multiple 1-3 mm yellow papules that may coalesce. Favors the neck, axillae, groin, antecubital and popliteal fossae. Increased normal-appearing elastic fibers in the mid and deep reticular dermis. Lacks the systemic features of PXE (see Ch. 97).
Linear focal elastosis (elastotic striae) Multiple yellow, palpable, striae-like bands of the lumbosacral region. Focal increase in number of elongated or fragmented elastic fibers (thickened > thinned) within the mid dermis; the ends of the elastic fibers may be split with a "paintbrush" appearance. Early lesions may have inflammation and elastolysis. May represent an excessive regenerative process of elastic fibers, perhaps to repair striae distensae (see Ch. 99).
Elastoderma Localized areas of lax, pendulous skin; neck, trunk, arm. Increased normal- and abnormal-appearing elastic fibers throughout the dermis and subcutis. Lacks the systemic features of PXE (see Ch. 97).
Elastofibroma dorsi Large mass in the deep subcutaneous tissue of the infrascapular area. Arises within the fascia. Increased elastic fibers with a globular and beaded morphology admixed with plump collagen bundles, fibroblasts, and mature fat cells (Fig. 98.11). Favors older women > men and Japanese. Possibly related to chronic mechanical stress.
Elastoma (nevus elasticus) Firm, skin-colored to yellowish papule(s) which may be clustered. Favors the trunk. Accumulation of thick, tortuous, branching elastic fibers within the reticular dermis. Type of connective tissue nevus (see Ch. 116). Associated with Buschke-Ollendorf syndrome.
Elastosis perforans serpiginosa Annular and polycyclic plaques composed of keratotic papules. Favors the neck, face, antecubital fossae and other flexural areas. Transepidermal or perifollicular channels that contain fragments of elastotic material. Increased number of coarse elastic fibers in the superficial dermis. No secondary calcium deposition on elastic fibers. Associated with penicillamine (lumpy-bumpy fibers*). Down syndrome and connective tissue disorders (e.g. Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome; see Ch. 96).
Pseudoxanthoma elasticum (PXE) Yellowish skin papules and "cobblestoned" plaques; redundant folds. Favors flexural sites, especially the neck and axillae, and periumbilical region. Distorted and fragmented elastic fibers in mid and deep reticular dermis. Calcium deposition on altered elastic fibers. Angioid streaks, GI hemorrhage, atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (see Ch. 97).
Actinic or solar elastosis (including cutis rhomboidalis nuchae, solar elastotic bands, elastotic nodules of the ear) Yellow to yellow-white, thickened, coarsely wrinkled skin that favors the lateral forehead, malar region, posterolateral neck, mid extensor arms. Bands – forearms. Nodules – antihelix > helix. Bluish-grey elastotic material composed of degenerated elastic fibers within the upper and mid dermis. In more advanced phases, the elastotic material may aggregate into dense amorphous masses ("actinic elastoma"). Photodamage may be accelerated by medications, e.g. voriconazole (see Ch. 87).
Favré-Racouchot syndrome (nodular elastosis with cysts and comedones) Bilateral, clustered, open > closed comedones within thickened skin. Favors malar prominence and lateral periorbital region. Severe solar elastosis with follicular distention and cystic structures filled with compact keratin. See Ch. 87.
Acrokeratoelastoidosis Bands of waxy or translucent papules and plaques. Favors ulnar side of thumb and radial side of forefinger. Shallow depression of the epidermis. Usually a decrease in thin and partially fragmented elastic fibers in the mid and deep reticular dermis. In some cases, elastic fibers are increased, coarse, and aggregated. Also an inherited form that can involve non-sun-exposed sites of the hands and/or feet (see Ch. 58).
Elastoma of Dubreuilh Yellow to yellow-brown papules or plaques. Favor forehead, cheeks, lateral neck. Amorphous masses composed of aggregated elastotic material in the upper dermis. See Ch. 87.
