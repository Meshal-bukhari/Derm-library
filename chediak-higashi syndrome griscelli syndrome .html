CHS vs Griscelli Syndrome (GS) – Quick Review

Feature Chédiak-Higashi Syndrome (CHS) Griscelli Syndrome Type 1 (GS1) Griscelli Syndrome Type 2 (GS2) Griscelli Syndrome Type 3 (GS3)
Inheritance AR (LYST gene) AR (MYO5A) AR (RAB27A) AR (MLPH)
Pathogenesis Defective vesicle trafficking → giant granules in multiple cell types Defective melanosome transport + neurologic dysfunction Defective vesicle docking → cytotoxic granule release failure Defective melanosome transport (pigment only)
Clinical Features
Hair Silvery metallic sheen Silvery/metallic hair
Skin Pigmentation Diffuse pigment dilution ± paradoxical hyperpigmentation Diffuse pigment dilution
Infections Recurrent pyogenic (Staph, Strep, etc.) No immunodeficiency Recurrent infections, HLH risk No infections
Bleeding Diathesis Mild (petechiae, bruising) Absent Absent Absent
Neurologic Symptoms Progressive neurologic decline (gait, dementia) Present from infancy (e.g., seizures, delay) Secondary only (if HLH develops) Absent
Accelerated Phase (HLH) Yes (most fatal feature) No Yes (life-threatening) No
Key Pathology & Labs
Hair Microscopy Small, regularly spaced melanin clumps Large, irregularly distributed melanin clumps
Peripheral Smear GIANT granules in neutrophils NORMAL neutrophils
Skin Biopsy Giant melanosomes in melanocytes "Stuffed" melanocytes
Immunologic Abnormalities ↓ NK function, neutropenia None ↓ cytotoxic T-cell function, hypogammaglobulinemia None
Diagnostic Traps & Differentiators
CHS vs GS2 Both present with silvery hair and infections.
Deciding factor: Peripheral smear → Giant granules = CHS, Normal = GS2.
Bleeding and ocular findings → CHS. Absent in GS.
GS1 vs GS2 GS1: Primary neurologic symptoms.
GS2: Neurologic symptoms only if HLH develops.
Key: Look for infections, pancytopenia → GS2
Gene Association Recall CHS → LYST
GS1 → MYO5A
GS2 → RAB27A
GS3 → MLPH
