Tufted Angioma

(Angioblastoma of Nakagawa)

Core Synopsis

A rare vascular tumor characterized by histologic "tufted" capillary infiltration of the dermis, admixed with dilated lymphatic vessels. Theorized to have a lymphatic origin.

15% Present at Birth
50% By Age 5
> Slight Male Predominance

Natural History: Initial growth period, then stabilizes. Usually persists, but may spontaneously regress (partially or completely) in some patients.

Clinical Presentation "Look For"

  • Solitary, dusky red-violaceous plaque or nodule.
  • Predilection: Trunk & extremities.
  • Often poorly defined and indurated.
  • Associated: Nodularity (angiomatous papules), hypertrichosis, hyperhidrosis.
  • Pain: Initially painless, becomes painful in a significant proportion.
Impact of Skin Color: Erythema may be harder to see in darker skin (appears hyperpigmented/duskier). Lighter skin: pink, red, or maroon.

KASABACH-MERRITT PHENOMENON (KMP) ALERT!

A potentially life-threatening complication associated with Tufted Angioma (and Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma).

KMP is characterized by:

  • Enlarging vascular lesion.
  • Profound thrombocytopenia.
  • Consumptive coagulopathy.
  • Often, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.
Board Pearl / Clinical Red Flag: Worsening pain in a tufted angioma may signify platelet consumption and herald the onset of KMP. Act fast!

Clinical Patterns Described

  1. Tufted angioma without complications.
  2. Tufted angioma complicated with chronic coagulopathy WITHOUT thrombocytopenia.
  3. Tufted angioma associated with Kasabach-Merritt Phenomenon (KMP).

Diagnostic Pearls

  • Tufted angiomas are generally darker and more indurated compared with infantile hemangiomas.
  • They are often painful (a key differentiator from many infantile hemangiomas).
  • Worsening pain: Suspect KMP! (Reiterated for emphasis)

Best Tests: Laboratory Workup

Due to common association with coagulopathy, initial labs are crucial:

  • CBC with platelet count
  • Fibrinogen
  • D-dimer
  • Prothrombin Time (PT)
  • Activated Prothrombin Time (aPTT)
  • International Normalized Ratio (INR)

Best Tests: Biopsy & Histology

Biopsy Caution: Perform in an OR with blood products readily available due to high risk of associated coagulopathy.

Definitive diagnosis. Key features:

  • Small, round lobules of capillaries in mid/deep dermis.
  • "Cannonball" or "tufted" distribution of these lobules.
  • Surrounding fibrosis in the dermis.
  • Characteristic crescent-like shapes (slit-like vascular spaces) often noted within the round lobules of densely packed capillaries.

Immunohistochemistry (IHC) - CRITICAL FOR DIFFERENTIATION:

  • Lymphatic Markers (D2-40, POX-1): POSITIVE in both Tufted Angioma & Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma (KHE).
  • Vascular Marker (GLUT-1):
    • Tufted Angioma: NEGATIVE
    • Infantile Hemangioma: POSITIVE (Key differentiator!)

Note: Tufted angiomas can be difficult to distinguish histologically from KHE. Clinical correlation and full IHC panel are vital.

Best Tests: Imaging

MRI: To evaluate the extent of the tufted angioma and to help differentiate from other tumors.

Differential Diagnosis & Pitfalls

Infantile Hemangioma
Asymptomatic and soft; rapidly grows before a slow involution; NO associated coagulopathy. GLUT-1 POSITIVE.
Vascular Malformation (e.g., AVM, PWS, KTS)
Present at birth, grow proportionally with child, do not involute. Different histology. KTS has limb overgrowth & varicosities.
Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma (KHE)
Histologically very similar (D2-40+, POX-1+). Also associated with KMP. Often more aggressive/infiltrative. GLUT-1 NEGATIVE. Distinction can be challenging.
Congenital Hemangioma (RICH/NICH)
Fully formed at birth. RICH involutes rapidly, NICH persists. GLUT-1 NEGATIVE.
Infantile Myofibromatosis
Firm, fibrous tumor. Different histology (spindle cells).
Congenital Dermatofibrosarcoma Protuberans
Firm plaque, locally aggressive. Different histology (storiform pattern, CD34+).
Hemangiopericytoma (now part of Solitary Fibrous Tumor spectrum)
Rare in infancy. Different histology (staghorn vessels, spindle cells).

Management & Therapy

Management Pearls:

  • Grows slowly (months to a decade) then stabilizes. Lateral extension typical (vs. vertical in Infantile Hemangiomas).
  • KMP Event: Prompt referral to Pediatric Hematologist. Surgical consult (Pediatric/Vascular surgeon) if amenable to excision.

Therapy: No consensus. Guided by symptomatology, thrombotic complications, patient age, lesion size/location.

Tufted Angioma WITHOUT KMP:
  • Low-dose aspirin
  • Sirolimus
  • Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL)
  • Surgical removal (depending on location/size)
  • Observation (for asymptomatic lesions in non-vital areas)
Tufted Angioma WITH KMP:
  • Serious condition.
  • Typically requires hospital admission for monitoring and treatment.
  • Multidisciplinary approach (Heme/Onc, Derm, Surgery).
