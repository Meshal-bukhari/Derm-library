Introduction to Pemphigus

Definition: Pemphigus is a group of chronic autoimmune blistering diseases of the skin and mucous membranes characterized by intraepidermal acantholysis due to loss of cell-cell adhesion between keratinocytes, caused by autoantibodies directed against cell surface components.

The term "pemphigus" stems from the Greek word pemphix, meaning blister or bubble. These are potentially life-threatening conditions that, before the advent of corticosteroids, had a high mortality rate.

For board exams, remember that pemphigus is characterized by intraepidermal blistering, which distinguishes it from other autoimmune bullous diseases like bullous pemphigoid (subepidermal blistering).

The hallmark of pemphigus is the finding of IgG autoantibodies against the cell surface of keratinocytes. The functional inhibition of desmogleins, which play an important role in cell-cell adhesion, by these autoantibodies results in blister formation.

Epidemiology

  • Affects men and women equally
  • Mean age of onset: 50-60 years (though can occur at any age)
  • Incidence: 0.76-5 new cases per million per year globally
  • Higher incidence (16-32 cases per million per year) in Jewish ancestry, particularly Ashkenazi Jews
  • In most countries, pemphigus vulgaris is more common than pemphigus foliaceus
  • Regional variations exist (e.g., in Brazil, pemphigus foliaceus is much more common)

Pathogenesis of Pemphigus

Pathogenic Autoantibodies

The primary pathogenic mechanism in pemphigus involves IgG autoantibodies targeting cell surface components of keratinocytes, specifically desmogleins, which are cadherin-type cell adhesion molecules found in desmosomes.

Remember: Compelling evidence shows that these autoantibodies are directly pathogenic - if they're removed from patient sera by immunoadsorption, the sera no longer cause blisters. When IgG fractions from patients with pemphigus are injected into neonatal mice, they develop blisters resembling human disease.

Desmogleins and Desmosomal Structure

Desmogleins are transmembrane glycoproteins of the cadherin family that form part of desmosomes, which are cell-cell adhesion structures that link the intermediate filament cytoskeleton to the cell membrane. There are four isoforms (Dsg1-4), with Dsg1 and Dsg3 being the primary targets in pemphigus.

Desmoglein Compensation Theory - Key Points:

  • Distribution of Dsg1 vs Dsg3:
    • In skin: Dsg1 is expressed throughout epidermis (more in superficial layers); Dsg3 is restricted to lower epidermis (basal/parabasal)
    • In mucosa: Both Dsg1 and Dsg3 are present, but Dsg1 expression is much lower than Dsg3
  • Pemphigus foliaceus (anti-Dsg1 only):
    • Superficial epidermal blisters in skin (where only Dsg1 is present)
    • No mucosal lesions (Dsg3 compensates for compromised Dsg1)
  • Mucosal-dominant PV (anti-Dsg3 only):
    • No or limited skin lesions (Dsg1 compensates for compromised Dsg3)
    • Mucosal erosions (low Dsg1 cannot compensate for loss of Dsg3)
  • Mucocutaneous PV (anti-Dsg1 and anti-Dsg3):
    • Extensive skin AND mucosal involvement (no compensation possible)

This theory also explains why neonates born to mothers with pemphigus vulgaris develop blisters but those born to mothers with pemphigus foliaceus rarely do: neonatal epidermis expresses Dsg3 throughout all layers (similar to mucosa), so anti-Dsg1 antibodies alone cannot cause significant blistering.

Mechanisms of Blister Formation

Two primary mechanisms contribute to blister formation in pemphigus:

  1. Direct inhibition: Autoantibodies sterically hinder desmoglein-desmoglein interactions between keratinocytes, especially when they target the functionally important N-terminal domains.
  2. Signal transduction: Antibody binding triggers intracellular signaling leading to internalization/endocytosis of desmogleins and desmosomal disassembly.

Clinical Features

Pemphigus Vulgaris

  • Oral involvement: Present in virtually all cases - painful erosions; intact blisters rare. "Although scattered or extensive erosions may be seen anywhere in the oral cavity, the most common sites are the buccal and palatine mucosae.
  • Cutaneous lesions: Flaccid, thin-walled, easily ruptured blisters
  • Predilection sites: Scalp, face, chest, back, axillae, groin, umbilicus
  • Nikolsky sign: Positive (lateral pressure causes skin slippage)
  • Special features: Lesions that heal leave hyperpigmentation without scarring

Pemphigus Foliaceus

  • Oral involvement: Absent (critical differential point)
  • Cutaneous lesions: Scaly, crusted cutaneous erosions on erythematous base
  • Predilection sites: Seborrheic distribution (face, scalp, upper trunk)
  • Nikolsky sign: Positive
  • Special features: May stay localized for years or rapidly progress to exfoliative erythroderma

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus

  • Oral involvement: Severe, intractable stomatitis affecting all surfaces of oropharynx and extending onto vermilion lip
  • Cutaneous lesions: Polymorphic - may resemble PV, bullous pemphigoid, erythema multiforme, or lichenoid eruptions
  • Associated with: Underlying neoplasms (lymphoma, CLL, Castleman disease, thymoma, sarcoma. Non-Hodgkin lymphoma and chronic lymphocytic leukemia together account for two-thirds of patient)
  • Special features: Severe pseudomembranous conjunctivitis; may develop bronchiolitis obliterans (potentially fatal)

IgA Pemphigus

  • Oral involvement: Rare
  • Cutaneous lesions: Flaccid vesicles or pustules; annular or circinate pattern
  • Predilection sites: Axilla and groin, trunk, proximal extremities
  • Special features: Pruritus often significant; two types: subcorneal pustular dermatosis type and intraepidermal neutrophilic type

Other Clinical Variants

Pemphigus Vegetans

A rare vegetative variant of pemphigus vulgaris characterized by:

  • Initial flaccid blisters that become erosions and then form fungoid vegetations or papillomatous proliferations
  • Predilection for intertriginous areas, scalp, and face
  • Two subtypes: severe Neumann type and mild Hallopeau type
  • Histopathology:
    • Epidermis: Shows features of pemphigus vulgaris (suprabasilar acantholysis) combined with significant reactive changes like papillomatosis, acanthosis, and the characteristic intraepidermal microabscesses filled with eosinophils.
    • Dermis: Displays an intense inflammatory infiltrate, which is notably rich in eosinophils.

Pemphigus Erythematosus (Senear-Usher Syndrome)

A localized variant of pemphigus foliaceus characterized by:

  • Scaly and crusted lesions primarily in the malar region
  • May have immunologic features of both lupus erythematosus and pemphigus

Herpetiform Pemphigus

Usually a variant of pemphigus foliaceus characterized by:

  • Erythematous urticarial plaques and tense vesicles in a herpetiform arrangement
  • Eosinophilic spongiosis and subcorneal pustules with minimal acantholysis
  • May evolve into typical pemphigus foliaceus or pemphigus vulgaris

A positive Nikolsky sign is a hallmark of pemphigus, but it is not specific. It can also be seen in other conditions like toxic epidermal necrolysis, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, bullous impetigo, and staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome.

Unusual Clinical Presentations of Pemphigus Vulgaris

  • Isolated crusted plaque on face or scalp
  • Paronychia and/or onychomadesis
  • Foot ulcers
  • Dyshidrotic eczema or pompholyx-like lesions
  • Macroglossia
  • Gingival hyperplasia

Histopathology

Pemphigus Vulgaris

  • Intraepidermal blister due to acantholysis
  • Split occurs suprabasally (just above basal cell layer)
  • "Row of tombstones" appearance of basal cells
  • Acantholytic keratinocytes in blister cavity
  • Acantholytic process may involve hair follicles
  • Dermal papillary outline usually maintained
  • Moderate perivascular mononuclear infiltrate with eosinophils

Pemphigus Foliaceus

  • Intraepidermal acantholysis in upper epidermis
  • Split occurs within or adjacent to the granular layer
  • Acantholytic cells often almond- or spindle-shaped
  • May see only a "missing" horny layer with few acantholytic cells
  • Histologically indistinguishable from staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome and bullous impetigo

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus

  • Unique combination of PV-like, erythema multiforme-like, and lichen planus-like features
  • Suprabasilar acantholysis
  • Individual keratinocyte necrosis
  • Interface dermatitis (basal cell liquefactive degeneration)
  • Lymphocytes within the epidermis
  • Dense band-like lymphocytic infiltrate in the upper dermis
  • Eosinophils are rare

IgA Pemphigus

  • Intraepidermal pustule or vesicle
  • Pustules contain predominantly neutrophils
  • Acantholysis usually not prominent
  • SPD type: subcorneal pustules in the upper epidermis
  • IEN type: suprabasilar pustules involving the lower or entire epidermis

For board exams, understand that both pemphigus foliaceus and staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) show superficial acantholysis because they both target Dsg1 (SSSS via exfoliative toxin). The distinction is made by immunofluorescence (positive in PF, negative in SSSS).

Diagnostic Methods

Direct Immunofluorescence (DIF)

  • Purpose: Detect in vivo bound autoantibodies in patient's skin
  • Specimen: Perilesional skin or mucosa
  • Findings in pemphigus: IgG deposition on cell surfaces of keratinocytes ("chicken wire" or "honeycomb" pattern)
  • Sensitivity: Nearly 100% in active disease
  • Special patterns:
    • PV and PF: IgG (±C3) on keratinocyte cell surfaces
    • Paraneoplastic pemphigus: IgG and C3 on cell surfaces AND along BMZ
    • IgA pemphigus: IgA (not IgG) on keratinocyte cell surfaces

Indirect Immunofluorescence (IIF)

  • Purpose: Detect circulating autoantibodies in patient's serum
  • Substrate: Varies by disease (see table below)
  • Finding in pemphigus: Circulating anti-epithelial cell surface IgG
  • Sensitivity: 80-90% in active disease
  • Titer correlation: Often correlates with disease activity; useful for monitoring treatment response
Type of Pemphigus Recommended IIF Substrate
Pemphigus vulgaris Monkey esophagus (for anti-Dsg3)
Pemphigus foliaceus Human skin or guinea pig esophagus (for anti-Dsg1)
Paraneoplastic pemphigus Monkey/guinea pig esophagus (for anti-Dsg) AND rat bladder (for anti-plakin)

ELISA

  • Purpose: Detect antigen-specific autoantibodies
  • Method: Tests serum on plates pre-coated with recombinant Dsg1/Dsg3
  • Value: Distinguishes PV from PF serologically; monitors disease activity
  • Pattern in PF: Anti-Dsg1 positive, anti-Dsg3 negative
  • Pattern in Mucosal PV: Anti-Dsg3 positive, anti-Dsg1 negative
  • Pattern in Mucocutaneous PV: Both anti-Dsg1 and anti-Dsg3 positive

Other Diagnostic Tests

  • Tzanck smear: Rapid visualization of acantholytic cells (preliminary only)
  • Immunoprecipitation: Detects conformational epitopes; requires radioisotopes
  • Immunoblotting: Detects linear epitopes; easier to perform than immunoprecipitation
  • CLEIA: Chemiluminescent enzyme immunoassay; more sensitive and rapid than ELISA

Severity Assessment Scores in Pemphigus

Scoring Systems

  • PDAI (Pemphigus Disease Area Index): Allows standardized assessment of disease extent and response to treatment. Used for objective measurement of pemphigus severity. Ranges from 0-263, with a score of
    • 0-14: Moderate disease
    • 15-44: Significant disease
    • 45 or more: Extensive disease

    The PDAI score is crucial for determining treatment approach. Memorize these thresholds as they dictate first-line therapy choices.

  • ABSI (Autoimmune Bullous Skin Disorder Intensity Score): Allows standardized assessment of disease extent and response to treatment. Provides objective criteria for monitoring disease progression.

Clinical Assessment Parameters

  • Traditional Clinical Markers:
    • Number of new blisters per day
    • Rate of healing of new lesions
    • Circulating autoantibody titers (IIF or ELISA)
    Used to gauge prednisone dosing adjustments.

Treatment of Pemphigus

Before the advent of systemic corticosteroids, pemphigus vulgaris was often fatal. The primary goal of therapy is to reduce autoantibody production, not just to suppress local inflammation.

Treatment Guidelines

For Moderate Disease (PDAI 14 or less)

  • First Line:
    • Oral steroids 0.5mg/kg + Dapsone/Tetracycline
  • Second Line:
    • Azathioprine
    • Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF)
  • For Recalcitrant Cases:
    • Rituximab

For Significant or Extensive Disease (PDAI 15 or more)

  • First Line:
    • Rituximab + Oral Steroids
    • Rituximab dose: 1g 2 weeks apart, then 0.5-1g q6-12 months
    • Steroids dose:
      • For significant disease: 0.5mg/kg tapered over 3 months
      • For extensive disease: 1mg/kg tapered over 6 months
  • Second Line:
    • Other steroid sparing agents:
    • Azathioprine
    • Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF)
    • IVIG
    • Cyclophosphamide
    • Plasmapheresis
  • Third Line:
    • Dapsone
    • Tetracyclines
    • Methotrexate (MTX)
    • Cyclosporine

Treatment Ladder for Pemphigus Vulgaris

First-Line Therapy Level 1 Evidence
  • Rituximab: 375 mg/m² once weekly for 4 weeks OR 1g initially then at two weeks (may need repeated every 3-6 months)
  • Systemic corticosteroids: Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day as initial dose (usually 60 mg/day)

Note: Rituximab is now FDA-approved as first-line therapy for moderate-to-severe pemphigus.

Adjuvant Immunosuppressive Agents Level 1-2 Evidence

Used in combination with systemic corticosteroids for steroid-sparing effect:

  • Azathioprine: 2-4 mg/kg/day (usually 100-300 mg/day)
  • Mycophenolate mofetil: 2-3 g/day
  • Cyclophosphamide: 1-3 mg/kg/day orally or 500-1000 mg/m² IV every 4 weeks
Additional Systemic Options Level 2-3 Evidence
  • Pulse methylprednisolone: 1 g/day over 2-3 hours for 3-5 consecutive days
  • High-dose IVIg: 400 mg/kg/day for 5 consecutive days (may need monthly repetition)
  • Plasmapheresis: 1-2 times per week at onset
  • Methotrexate: 7.5-20 mg/week
  • Extracorporeal photopheresis: 2 days per month
Topical and Intralesional Therapy Adjunctive Treatment
  • Topical corticosteroids: Class I for localized persistent sites
  • Topical antibiotics: For secondary infections
  • Topical immunomodulators: Cyclosporine, tacrolimus
  • Intralesional corticosteroids
  • Intralesional rituximab: 5 mg/cm²

Several recent studies have shown that rituximab should be considered early in the course of disease rather than just for patients with recalcitrant disease. A 2017 randomized controlled trial showed that first-line rituximab plus short-term prednisone was more effective than prednisone alone for treatment of pemphigus.

Treatment of Other Pemphigus Types

Pemphigus Foliaceus

Treatment approach is similar to pemphigus vulgaris for widespread disease. For localized disease, superpotent topical corticosteroids may be sufficient. Dapsone can be used when neutrophils are histologically dominant.

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus

  • For benign tumors (thymomas, localized Castleman disease): surgical excision
  • For malignant neoplasms: tumor-specific chemotherapy or targeted therapy
  • Cutaneous lesions respond more rapidly to therapy than stomatitis, which is generally refractory
  • Overall prognosis is poor due to resistant nature

IgA Pemphigus

  • First line: Dapsone (response usually within 24-48 hours)
  • Alternatives: Sulfapyridine, acitretin
  • Refractory cases: Low to medium-dose prednisone, photochemotherapy (PUVA), colchicine

When complete remission is achieved with combined immunosuppressive and corticosteroid therapy, maintain the immunosuppressive dose while tapering prednisone. When a dose of 5-10 mg/day of prednisone is reached, careful taper then discontinuation of the immunosuppressive drug can be attempted.

Quick Quiz

Question 1: A 45-year-old woman presents with painful oral erosions for 3 months. She has recently developed flaccid blisters on her trunk and scalp. Histopathology shows suprabasilar acantholysis. Which autoantibody pattern would you expect?

A. Anti-Dsg1 IgG only
B. Anti-Dsg3 IgG only
C. Both anti-Dsg1 and anti-Dsg3 IgG
D. Anti-Dsg1 IgA only

The correct answer is C. This patient has mucocutaneous pemphigus vulgaris with both oral and skin involvement. According to the desmoglein compensation theory, patients with this form have antibodies against both Dsg1 and Dsg3. If she had only anti-Dsg3 antibodies, she would have predominantly mucosal disease with minimal skin involvement.

Question 2: Which histopathological feature best distinguishes pemphigus vulgaris from pemphigus foliaceus?

A. Presence of acantholysis
B. Level of intraepidermal split
C. Presence of eosinophils in the dermis
D. Positive direct immunofluorescence

The correct answer is B. Both diseases show acantholysis and positive DIF, but the level of the split is different: suprabasilar in pemphigus vulgaris vs. subcorneal/intragranular in pemphigus foliaceus.

Question 3: A 60-year-old man with chronic lymphocytic leukemia presents with severe stomatitis extending onto the vermilion lip, conjunctival erosions, and polymorphic cutaneous lesions including tense blisters on his palms. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Pemphigus vulgaris
B. Bullous pemphigoid
C. Mucous membrane pemphigoid
D. Paraneoplastic pemphigus

The correct answer is D. This clinical presentation is classic for paraneoplastic pemphigus: severe stomatitis extending onto the vermilion lip, conjunctival involvement, polymorphic cutaneous lesions, palm/sole involvement, and association with a lymphoproliferative disorder (CLL in this case).

Question 4: A patient with pemphigus foliaceus has autoantibodies against which of the following?

A. Desmoglein 1
B. Desmoglein 3
C. Both Desmoglein 1 and 3
D. Plakin family proteins

The correct answer is A. Pemphigus foliaceus is characterized by autoantibodies against Desmoglein 1 only. This explains the superficial epidermal blisters and lack of mucosal involvement according to the desmoglein compensation theory.

Question 5: A patient with widespread crusted erosions has no mucosal involvement. Tzanck smear shows acantholytic cells, and the histopathology reveals an intraepidermal split in the upper epidermis. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Pemphigus vulgaris
B. Pemphigus foliaceus
C. Bullous pemphigoid
D. Dermatitis herpetiformis

The correct answer is B. The clinical presentation (crusted erosions, no mucosal involvement) and histopathology (split in upper epidermis) are classic for pemphigus foliaceus. Pemphigus vulgaris has mucosal involvement and a lower intraepidermal split. Bullous pemphigoid and dermatitis herpetiformis have subepidermal splits.

Explain the Desmoglein Compensation Theory

When one desmoglein is targeted by autoantibodies, the other can compensate as long as they are coexpressed. The distribution pattern of Dsg1 and Dsg3 in skin vs. mucosa explains the clinical findings in different pemphigus types.
