🩺 Acquired Acrodermatitis Enteropathica

Saudi Board Dermatology Exam Preparation

📖 Synopsis & Pathophysiology ▼

🎯 Core Definition

Acquired acrodermatitis enteropathica (AAE) is an acquired condition that occurs as a result of zinc deficiency. In AAE, skin manifestations may be accompanied by alopecia and diarrhea, but this classic triad is present in only 20% of cases.

🔬 Clinical Spectrum Based on Severity

Mild to Moderate Deficiency: The clinical picture is that of thin, scaly plaques that may resemble psoriasis.

Severe Deficiency: It will manifest as a periorificial, acral, and intertriginous dermatitis that has eczematous features, but may also be vesiculobullous or even pustular. The eruption tends to be symmetrical.

🧬 Pathophysiology

It appears that zinc is required for the orderly proliferation of keratinocytes. Additionally, low tissue zinc is associated with the overproduction of inflammatory cytokines and reactive mediators.

📊 Demographics

All age groups
No sex predilection

🎯 Classic Triad

Only 20% have:
Skin + Alopecia + Diarrhea

🏆 Board Tip: Remember that the classic triad is rare (20%)! Don't rule out AAE if only skin lesions are present.
⚠️ Risk Factors & Causes ▼

🍽️ Dietary Causes

Decreased absorption of zinc is associated with inadequate dietary intake due to:

  • Low caloric diets
  • Anorexia nervosa
  • Selective eating
  • Vegetarian diets
  • Diets high in phytates that bind zinc (typical of cereal grain-based diets common to parts of the Middle East and North Africa)
  • Total parenteral nutrition (TPN) with inadequate zinc supplement is a known cause of deficiency

🏥 Medical Conditions

Malabsorption related to:

  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Celiac sprue
  • Cystic fibrosis
  • Numerous bariatric procedures

Other conditions:

  • Chronic diarrhea
  • Chronic liver disease
  • Renal disease (nephrotic syndrome)

💊 Medications Associated with Zinc Deficiency

  • Proton pump inhibitors (decrease absorption)
  • Loop diuretics (increase loss)
  • Some angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors
  • Calcium channel blockers
🧠 Memory Aid: "PPI + Loops + ACE = Zinc Goes Missing"
🎯 Differential Alert: Identical clinical findings may result from hereditary acrodermatitis enteropathica, a rare autosomal recessive disease caused by abnormal zinc absorption due to a transport deficiency across the small intestine.
👁️ Look For: Clinical Presentation ▼

🔍 Primary Lesions

Sharply demarcated, scaly plaques in the intertriginous, periorificial, and acral regions. Scales may be adherent and shiny, or peeling off. Transient bullae, erosions, and crusting may be seen. The fingers and toes may show similar lesions.

🌈 Impact of Skin Color on Clinical Presentation

In lighter skin colors: plaques may appear any shade of pink or red.

In darker skin colors: erythema is subtler and plaques may appear pink, maroon, or brown. Adherent scales may appear dark brown.

🔗 Associated Manifestations

  • Chronic diarrhea
  • Nail dystrophy and paronychia
  • Nonscarring alopecia
  • Glossitis
  • Stomatitis
  • Failure to thrive
🎯 Location Memory: "PAI" - Periorificial, Acral, Intertriginous
🏆 High-Yield: The combination of periorificial + acral + intertriginous distribution with risk factors should make you think AAE immediately!
💎 Diagnostic Pearls ▼

🔮 When to Suspect AAE

Suspect AAE in cases of therapy-resistant intertrigo, or a periorificial or acral exfoliating, scaly, or bullous eruption, when a history of risk factors or associations is present.

🧠 Pearl: "Therapy-Resistant + PAI Distribution + Risk Factors = Think Zinc!"

🧪 Best Tests ▼

🩸 Primary Testing

Measure serum zinc. Levels should be low, but serum levels do not necessarily reflect cellular zinc status; therefore, skin lesions may be present in patients with normal serum zinc.

📊 Advanced Testing Considerations

  • Serum levels of zinc are depressed in chronic inflammatory states, so measurement of the erythrocyte zinc concentration may be more useful.
  • Alkaline phosphatase is a zinc-dependent enzyme; therefore, even when the serum zinc level is normal, a low alkaline phosphatase level may indicate zinc deficiency.
  • Most zinc is albumin bound, so zinc levels may be reduced in patients who have hypoalbuminemia.

🔬 Additional Testing

Skin biopsy, bacterial and mycologic cultures, and select laboratory tests (glucagon level, niacin and tryptophan levels, amino acid assay, liver function tests) can be used to both rule out other causes and to confirm associated conditions.

🏆 Board Alert: Normal serum zinc doesn't rule out zinc deficiency! Consider erythrocyte zinc or low alkaline phosphatase as clues.
💡 Management Pearls ▼

⚡ Response to Treatment

Dramatic improvement of the rash and associated symptoms parallels the increase in serum zinc levels. Clinical improvement occurs within days to weeks - often before significant changes in serum zinc occur.

🔍 Comprehensive Approach

In addition to supplementation, investigate underlying causes and risk factors (if not already evident by history) such as alcohol use disorder, inadequate caloric intake, and digestive disease.

🎯 Management Memory: "Supplement + Investigate + Monitor"
💊 Therapy ▼

🎯 Treatment Protocol

Supplement with elemental zinc 0.5-1 mg/kg body weight per day. Address underlying cause(s).

Supplement until serum zinc normalizes (determined by checking serum levels every 3-6 months).

🏆 Board Essential: Remember the dose - 0.5-1 mg/kg/day of ELEMENTAL zinc, and monitor every 3-6 months!
