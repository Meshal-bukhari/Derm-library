STARTS Mnemonic — Detailed Breakdown

S — Simple closure | T — Tissue importation | A — Advancement | R — Rotation | T — Transposition | S — Skin grafts

Board-style, high-yield surgical repair notes

1. Simple Side-to-Side Closure

Core Concept Excise defect as an ellipse and close directly.

Mechanism in detail

  • Undermine surrounding skin to allow stretch and tension redistribution.
  • Tension vector is perpendicular to the ellipse long axis.
  • Length:width ratio ~3–4:1 to avoid puckering; end triangles = Burow's triangles.

When to choose it

First choice for most small–medium defects when adjacent skin laxity is adequate and closure lines can be hidden in relaxed skin tension lines (RSTLs).

When to AVOID it (Exam Trap):
  • Do NOT pick simple closure if resulting tension will distort a free margin (eyelid, nostril rim, vermilion) or cross a cosmetic boundary — escalate to a flap.

2. Advancement Flaps

Core Concept Tissue is slid forward into the defect without rotation.

Analogy

Like sliding a door or pulling a drawer straight out.

Mechanism in detail

  • Incise three sides of a rectangular/square flap, undermine, and pull forward.
  • Success depends on intrinsic elasticity of the flap tissue; tension vector is parallel to flap movement.

When to choose it

Ideal near structures you want to keep straight (eyebrow, lip) because linear movement avoids distorting horizontal features. Also used to relocate Burow's triangles away from sensitive areas.

High-Yield Examples: A-to-T flap, H-flap.

3. Rotation Flaps

Core Concept Semicircular flap pivots around a fixed point to cover adjacent defect.

Analogy

Like a swinging gate or windshield wiper.

Mechanism in detail

  • Curvilinear (arcing) incision creates a flap that can swing into place; wider arc = easier movement.
  • Tension concentrates at the pivot point; relies on skin elasticity to accommodate movement.

When to choose it

Excellent for large triangular defects on curved areas (cheek, scalp). Incisions can be hidden along cosmetic subunit boundaries.

High-Yield Examples: Dorsal nasal (Rieger) flap; Mustardé cheek flap.

4. Transposition Flaps

Core Concept Flap lifted and pivoted over a bridge of intact skin into nearby defect — trades laxity from one area to another.

Analogy

Like leapfrog — the flap "jumps" over normal skin.

Mechanism in detail

  • Conceptually distinct: does not rely on elasticity of the flap itself but recruits laxity from an adjacent reservoir.
  • Principle is similar to Z-plasty — reorients tissue to lengthen in one direction while shortening another, useful for tight closures.

When to choose it

Workhorse for defects in tight, inelastic skin where a nearby pocket of loose tissue is available (e.g., nasal tip repaired using upper lateral sidewall laxity).

High-Yield Examples: Rhombic flap, Bilobed flap (classic nasal tip technique).

5. Tissue Importation Flaps

Core Concept Bring tissue from a distant donor site (pedicled) when local tissue is insufficient.

Analogy

Like building a temporary bridge from one county to another.

Mechanism in detail

  • Pedicle of well-vascularized tissue is raised and transferred to the defect; pedicle provides blood supply while bridging intact skin.
  • Usually requires second-stage surgery (≈3 weeks) to divide the pedicle.

When to choose it

For large, deep, or complex defects where local tissue is absent or destroyed — e.g., subtotal nasal reconstruction or when cartilage grafts need robust vascularized cover.

High-Yield Examples: Paramedian forehead flap, Nasolabial interpolation flap.

6. Skin Grafts

Core Concept Free piece of skin (epidermis ± dermis) detached from donor site and placed on defect, relying on wound bed for revascularization.

Analogy

Like laying a patch of sod on bare earth.

Mechanism in detail

  • No intrinsic blood supply — survives initially by plasmatic imbibition, then by revascularization from the bed.
  • Differentiate full-thickness vs split-thickness grafts based on dermal content and requirements.

When to choose it

Broad, shallow defects where a flap would be infeasible or would cause excessive distortion; also used for internal nasal lining in complex repairs.

When to AVOID it (Exam Trap):
  • Grafts WILL NOT take on poorly vascularized beds (bare bone, bare cartilage, tendon) — require healthy, bleeding base.
  • Cosmetic match (color/texture/contour) is often inferior to a well-executed flap.

Quick Mnemonics & Exam Tips

  • STARTS — Simple, Tissue importation, Advancement, Rotation, Transposition, Skin graft (use as checklist from least to most complex).
  • Pick simple closure whenever possible — escalate only when tension will distort free margins or cross cosmetic units.
  • Use advancement/rotation to preserve linear features (brows, lips) and to hide incisions in subunits.
  • Use transposition when local elasticity is poor but a nearby lax reservoir exists.
  • Reserve tissue importation and staged pedicles for very large/subtotal reconstructions (expect second stage).
  • Remember: grafts need a healthy bed — if bed is cartilage/bone/tendon, think flap or cartilage graft + flap.
