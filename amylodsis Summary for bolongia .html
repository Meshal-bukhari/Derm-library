Amyloidosis Defined:

A group of diseases characterized by abnormal extracellular deposition of amyloid, a fibrillar proteinaceous material with a characteristic cross-β-pleated sheet configuration, within tissues.

Core Amyloid Properties

Major Components

  • The major component of all amyloid deposits is the fibril protein
  • Amyloid P component (derived from serum amyloid P protein or SAP) is a universal constituent of all types of amyloid
  • Other components include glycosaminoglycans and apoE lipoprotein

Physical Properties

  • Cross-β-pleated sheet configuration (all types)
  • 7-10nm wide non-branching, non-anastomosing fibrils on EM
  • All amyloid types share these physical properties regardless of origin

💡 HIGH-YIELD: Amyloid Staining Properties

  • Congo red stain: orange-red color under standard light microscopy
  • Characteristic apple-green birefringence with Congo red stain under polarized light
  • Other useful stains: crystal violet, Thioflavin T, Sirius red, Dylon stain
  • KMnO₄ test: AA amyloid (but not AL) loses its affinity for Congo red after exposure to potassium permanganate
  • Immunohistochemical stains (e.g., for keratin, light chains) help identify amyloid type
  • Tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) is the preferred method for precise protein identification including AL, ATTR, and ALect2

🧠 Amyloid Type Mnemonic: "AAAAA"

  • AL: Antibody Light chain → Primary systemic amyloidosis & nodular cutaneous amyloidosis
  • AA: Acute phase proteins → Secondary systemic amyloidosis (inflammation/infection) & autoinflammatory disorders (FMF, Muckle-Wells)
  • ATTR: A Transthyretin protein → Wild-type ATTR associated with senile systemic/cardiac amyloidosis
  • AKer: A Keratin protein → Primary cutaneous macular & lichen amyloidosis
  • Aβ2M: A β2-Microglobulin protein → Hemodialysis-associated amyloidosis
Primary Localized Cutaneous Amyloidosis

Lichen amyloidosis is the most common form of primary cutaneous amyloidosis

Macular Amyloidosis

  • Hyperpigmented macules in rippled pattern
  • Common sites: upper back (especially scapular)
  • Often pruritic
  • More common in women
  • Clinical overlap with notalgia paresthetica

Lichen Amyloidosis

  • Persistent, pruritic, hyperpigmented papules
  • Predilection for shins and extensor surfaces
  • Often in rippled or ridged pattern
  • May coalesce into plaques
  • Initially unilateral, can become bilateral

Biphasic Amyloidosis

Features of both macular and lichen amyloidosis in the same patient (or even same lesion)

Nodular Amyloidosis

  • Single or multiple waxy nodules/plaques
  • Common on trunk and extremities
  • Composed of AL amyloid (immunoglobulin light chains)
  • ~7% risk of progression to systemic involvement (much less than previously quoted 50%)

💡 HIGH-YIELD: Associations of Macular/Lichen Amyloidosis

  • MEN type 2A (Sipple syndrome) - associated with lichen amyloidosis with early onset (often before age 10)
  • All patients with cutaneous amyloidosis in MEN2A have pathogenic variant in codon 634 of RET proto-oncogene
  • Notalgia paresthetica - significant clinical and histological overlap
  • Autoimmune connective tissue disorders (e.g., systemic sclerosis, SLE, dermatomyositis)
  • IL-31 receptor-associated amyloidosis (PLCA1 and PLCA2)

Primary Systemic Amyloidosis (AL type)

AL amyloidosis is a life-threatening manifestation of an underlying plasma cell dyscrasia. The fibrils are composed of AL protein consisting of immunoglobulin light chains, usually λ-type (75%-80%).

💡 HIGH-YIELD: Classic Presentation

The presence of macroglossia together with carpal tunnel syndrome is a classic presentation and should trigger investigation for amyloidosis

Mucocutaneous Manifestations

Skin Findings (present in ~25% of cases)

  • Waxy, translucent, or purpuric papules, nodules, and plaques
  • Smooth, waxy infiltration with papules on palms and fingertips
  • Small papules on face, neck, scalp, anogenital region
  • Diffuse infiltration causing sclerodermoid appearance
  • Hemorrhagic bullae resembling porphyria cutanea tarda
  • Nail dystrophy resembling lichen planus

Mucosal Findings

  • Rubbery swellings or infiltration of the mucosa
  • Macroglossia (uniformly enlarged, firm tongue)
  • Hemorrhagic papules, plaques, and blisters on tongue
  • Xerostomia due to salivary gland infiltration

Periorbital Purpura ("Raccoon Eyes")

Characteristic purpura in periorbital distribution following minor trauma (coughing, Valsalva maneuver, proctoscopy)

Caused by amyloid infiltration of vessel walls leading to increased fragility

Can also present as "pinch purpura" anywhere on the body

Systemic Manifestations

Renal

  • Proteinuria (albuminuria)
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Edema (nephrotic syndrome)

Cardiac

  • Restrictive cardiomyopathy
  • Congestive heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
  • Dyspnea, hepatomegaly, edema

Neurologic

  • Sensory neuropathy (bilateral, symmetrical)
  • Autonomic dysfunction
  • Postural hypotension
  • Impotence
  • GI motility disturbances
Diagnosis of Primary Systemic Amyloidosis
Diagnostic Approach
Tissue Biopsy
  • Biopsy of specific mucocutaneous lesions, if present
  • Fine needle aspiration of abdominal subcutaneous fat (preferred method when no specific skin/mucosal lesions)
  • Rectal mucosal biopsies (less preferred due to bleeding risk)
  • Gingival or tongue biopsies
  • Bone marrow biopsy
Laboratory Tests
  • Serum protein electrophoresis
  • Urine protein electrophoresis
  • Immunofixation electrophoresis (serum and urine)
  • Serum free light chain assay
  • Complete blood count
  • Renal and liver function tests
  • Cardiac markers (NT-proBNP, troponin)
Imaging
  • Serum Amyloid P (SAP) component scintigraphy - used primarily outside US for localizing amyloid deposits and monitoring disease
  • Echocardiogram
  • Cardiac MRI
Other Studies
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • Bone marrow plasma cell assessment (percentage, clonality via flow cytometry, morphology)

💡 HIGH-YIELD: Prognosis & Treatment

  • Cardiac and renal involvement are major negative prognostic factors
  • Without therapy, prognosis is poor, especially with cardiac involvement
  • Treatment strategies parallel those for multiple myeloma
  • FDA-approved: cyclophosphamide, bortezomib, dexamethasone (CyborD) plus daratumumab
  • High-dose melphalan followed by autologous stem cell transplantation for eligible patients
  • Supportive measures according to affected organs
Secondary Systemic Amyloidosis (AA type)

Secondary systemic amyloidosis occurs as a complication of severe chronic inflammatory diseases. It is characterized by deposition of AA protein (derived from an acute phase protein synthesized by the liver).

Associated Conditions

  • Chronic infections (tuberculosis, lepromatous leprosy)
  • Chronic inflammatory diseases:
    • Rheumatoid arthritis
    • Ankylosing spondylitis
    • Hidradenitis suppurativa
    • Psoriasis (generalized or pustular)
    • Autoimmune connective tissue disorders
  • Autoinflammatory syndromes:
    • Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF)
    • Muckle-Wells syndrome (associated with AA amyloid)
    • TNF receptor-associated periodic syndrome (TRAPS)

Key Features

  • Primarily affects kidneys, liver, spleen, adrenals, heart
  • Cutaneous lesions are rare
  • Amyloid detectable in subcutaneous fat aspirates
  • Treatment involves addressing the underlying disease
  • TNF inhibitors can be beneficial for underlying inflammatory disorders
  • Eprodisate may slow renal function loss
  • Colchicine is the treatment for Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF) and prevents secondary AA amyloidosis
Other Important Amyloidosis Types Hemodialysis-Associated Amyloidosis

Pathogenesis

  • Results from decreased excretion of β2-microglobulin
  • Aβ2M accumulates because it's not readily filtered through dialysis membranes
  • Improved dialysis membranes have decreased incidence

Clinical Features

  • Primarily musculoskeletal manifestations:
    • Carpal tunnel syndrome
    • Bone cysts
    • Destructive spondylo-arthropathy
  • Occasional subcutaneous nodules
ATTR Amyloidosis

Wild-type ATTR Amyloidosis

  • Also known as senile systemic amyloidosis or senile cardiac amyloidosis
  • Deposition of wild-type transthyretin in cardiac tissue
  • Predominantly affects the elderly
  • Treatment with tafamidis meglumine FDA-approved

Hereditary ATTR Amyloidosis

  • Due to mutations in transthyretin gene
  • Presents with neurologic or cardiac dysfunction
  • Skin rarely involved
  • Treatments: liver transplantation, tafamidis, patisiran, inotersen

Treatment Options for Macular & Lichen Amyloidosis

  • Breaking the itch-scratch cycle is essential
  • First-line:
    • Potent topical corticosteroids (with/without occlusion)
    • Mild keratolytics (salicylic acid) - especially for lichen amyloidosis
    • Occlusive dressings (hydrocolloid, zinc oxide-impregnated)
  • Second-line:
    • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
    • Intralesional corticosteroids
    • UVB phototherapy
    • PUVA phototherapy
  • Third-line:
    • Systemic retinoids (acitretin, alitretinoin)
    • Dermabrasion
    • CO₂ or erbium:YAG laser therapy
    • Low-dose cyclophosphamide
    • Dupilumab (case reports)

Treatment Options for Nodular Amyloidosis

  • Surgical excision
  • Cryotherapy
  • Electrodesiccation
  • CO₂ laser
  • Local recurrences are common
  • Cyclophosphamide may be used in some cases

Treatment of Systemic Involvement

Primary systemic amyloidosis (AL type):

  • Historically: Melphalan and corticosteroids
  • Current: Cyclophosphamide, bortezomib, dexamethasone plus daratumumab
  • For eligible patients: High-dose melphalan with autologous stem cell transplantation
Secondary Cutaneous Amyloidosis

Secondary cutaneous amyloidosis refers to amyloid deposits that are clinically inapparent but detectable histopathologically within various skin lesions.

Common Associations

  • Seborrheic keratoses
  • Basal cell carcinomas
  • Dermatofibromas
  • Intradermal melanocytic nevi
  • Adnexal tumors
  • Bowen disease
  • Porokeratosis
  • Following PUVA therapy

Notable Examples

  • Hypotrichosis simplex of the scalp:
    • Autosomal dominant disorder
    • Progressive loss of scalp hair by third decade
    • Caused by variants in gene encoding corneodesmosin
    • Amyloid deposits around hair follicles and papillary dermis
Pathogenesis of Amyloid Formation

General Mechanisms

  • Begins with soluble precursor proteins
  • Proteins undergo conformational changes
  • These changes lead to:
    • Aggregation
    • Polymerization
    • Fibril formation
    • Extracellular tissue deposition as insoluble amyloid
  • Specific mechanisms vary by amyloid type

Type-Specific Mechanisms

  • Primary Systemic (AL): Amino acid substitutions in variable region of immunoglobulin light chains destabilize them
  • ATTR: Mutations in transthyretin gene alter protein stability and increase amyloidogenicity
  • Primary Cutaneous (AKer):
    • Fibrillar theory: Keratinocyte tonofilaments degenerate, pass into dermis, modified by histiocytes/fibroblasts
    • Alternative theory: Precursor proteins secreted at dermoepidermal junction by basal keratinocytes
    • Small fiber neuropathy may relate to associated pruritus

Self-Assessment Questions
1. What is the major fibrillar protein component common to all amyloid deposits?

The major component of all amyloid deposits is the fibril protein. Despite different precursors, all amyloid fibrils share a common cross-β-pleated sheet configuration.

2. Which staining pattern is characteristic of amyloid on Congo red stain under polarized light?

Apple-green birefringence is the characteristic appearance of amyloid when stained with Congo red and viewed under polarized light.

3. What amyloid protein type is associated with primary cutaneous macular and lichen amyloidosis?

AKer (keratin-derived) amyloid protein is associated with primary cutaneous macular and lichen amyloidosis.

4. What distinguishes AA amyloid from AL amyloid in laboratory testing?

AA amyloid loses its affinity for Congo red after exposure to potassium permanganate (KMnO₄), while AL amyloid retains its staining.

5. What is the classic clinical presentation that should trigger investigation for primary systemic amyloidosis?

The combination of macroglossia (enlarged tongue) and carpal tunnel syndrome is a classic presentation of primary systemic amyloidosis.

6. What is the approximate risk of progression to systemic disease for primary cutaneous nodular amyloidosis?

Approximately 7% of patients with primary cutaneous nodular amyloidosis progress to systemic involvement (much less than the previously quoted 50%).

7. What is the preferred diagnostic procedure for suspected systemic amyloidosis when specific skin/mucosal lesions are absent?

Fine needle aspiration of abdominal subcutaneous fat is the preferred diagnostic procedure due to its safety and relatively high sensitivity.

8. Which medication is used to prevent secondary AA amyloidosis in patients with Familial Mediterranean Fever?

Colchicine is the treatment of choice for Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF) and helps prevent the development of secondary AA amyloidosis.

Final Clinical Pearls

  • Always consider amyloidosis in patients with unexplained macroglossia
  • Periorbital purpura after Valsalva is nearly pathognomonic for systemic amyloidosis
  • Pruritic rippled hyperpigmentation on the back should raise suspicion for macular amyloidosis
  • Biopsy results should be correlated with clinical findings, as histology alone may not differentiate between types
  • Tandem mass spectrometry is now the gold standard for amyloid typing in complex cases
