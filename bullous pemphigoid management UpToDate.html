🎯 Introduction & Core Concepts

Bullous Pemphigoid (BP) is an autoimmune cutaneous blistering disease characterized by autoantibody deposition at the epithelial basement membrane zone. It most frequently affects older adults.

Classic Presentation: Generalized, pruritic, urticarial plaques and tense, subepithelial blisters.

Mainstays of Initial Treatment:

  • Topical corticosteroids
  • Systemic corticosteroids
  • Doxycycline

Additional immunomodulatory therapies are often added to minimize corticosteroid adverse effects or augment improvement.

🏆 Treatment Goals

Treatment is generally recommended as BP can cause discomfort, risk of infection, and negatively impact quality of life.

  • ✔️ Decrease blister formation and pruritus
  • ✔️ Promote healing of blisters and erosions
  • ✔️ Improve quality of life
These goals are achieved by attenuating autoantibody production and antibody-mediated inflammation while minimizing drug-induced adverse effects. Careful skin care and specialist referrals are also key.

🧐 Pretreatment Assessment: Critical First Steps

💊 Identifying Drug-Induced Bullous Pemphigoid
Key Concept: Drug-induced BP mimics classic BP (clinically, histopathologically, immunopathologically) and can develop months to over a year after drug ingestion. It likely accounts for a small proportion of cases.

Strongest Associations:

  • Gliptins (e.g., Sitagliptin, Linagliptin, Vildagliptin)
  • Programmed cell death protein 1/programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-1/PD-L1) inhibitors (e.g., Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab)
  • Loop diuretics (e.g., Furosemide)
  • Penicillins

Patient Evaluation for Drug-Induced BP:
  • Thorough medication history is paramount!
  • Clues suggesting drug-induced BP (vs. classic BP):
    • Younger patient age
    • Bullae on normal-appearing skin
    • Histopathologic findings: Intraepidermal vesicles, necrotic keratinocytes, or thrombus formation (Classic BP is subepidermal)
  • Similar to classic BP, pruritus and eczematous lesions may precede blistering.
⚙️ Drug Management in Suspected Drug-Induced BP
  • Promptly discontinue suspected drug whenever feasible.
  • Patients typically still require medical therapy (topical/systemic corticosteroids) initially.
  • Prognosis: Many respond rapidly to drug cessation + medical Rx (remission within 6 weeks, then taper). Some develop a chronic course like classic BP.
Gliptin Cessation - A Point of Nuance: Data is mixed.
  • One retrospective study (108 pts): No difference in time to control or relapse rates whether gliptin ceased or continued.
  • Another retrospective study (40 pts): Higher complete remission at 3 months if gliptin ceased (65% vs 5%).
Further study needed.
🤝 Indications for Referral: Multidisciplinary Care

Managed by a dermatologist ideally, in conjunction with the primary care clinician.

Refer to specialists for known or suspected mucosal involvement:

  • 👁️ Ophthalmology: Prompt referral for ocular signs (itching/burning eyes, visual changes).
  • 🩺 Gastroenterology: For dysphagia (esophageal involvement).
  • 🗣️ Otolaryngology (ENT): For laryngeal, pharyngeal, or nasal signs (hoarseness, dysphagia, nasal obstruction).

🗺️ Approach to Management: The Strategic Blueprint

Overall Philosophy: Immunosuppressants and anti-inflammatory meds are mainstays. Treatment may last years.
🚨 CRITICAL POINT: Much morbidity/mortality stems from patient comorbidities + treatment adverse effects, not direct disease effects.
➡️ Therefore, treatment should be conservative (minimal medication for remission, careful consideration of drug toxicity).
1️⃣ Selection of Initial Treatment: Key Choices

Typically one of the following as primary therapy:

  1. High-potency topical corticosteroid (e.g., Clobetasol propionate)
  2. Oral corticosteroid (e.g., Prednisone or Prednisolone)
  3. Doxycycline

Factors influencing choice: Feasibility, risk of AEs, relative efficacy, patient preference.

💡 Topical Clobetasol Propionate (Twice Daily):
  • Highly effective.
  • Lower mortality & severe complications vs. oral prednisone (1 mg/kg/day) in extensive BP. (Jullien et al. trial)
  • Challenge: Adherence, especially in older adults or those needing caregiver assistance for extensive application. Often leads to choosing oral options.

For patients where topical monotherapy isn't used, choosing between Prednisone and Doxycycline:

  • Doxycycline:
    • May be less likely for rapid remission vs. prednisone.
    • More favorable side effect profile. ✅
    • Authors' preference for milder presentations (e.g., localized BP, few new bullae/day).
      • Often add topical corticosteroid to affected areas with doxycycline.
    • Alternatives: Tetracycline, Minocycline. Sometimes given with Nicotinamide (active Vitamin B3, anti-inflammatory).
  • Prednisone:
    • Often considered for extensive disease (widespread, many new bullae/day).
    • However, some experts consider a trial of oral doxycycline reasonable even for extensive disease due to its favorable side effect profile.

Limited data: Initial methotrexate may also be effective as primary Rx.

2️⃣ Incorporation of Corticosteroid-Sparing Therapy
Rationale: Bullous Pemphigoid is chronic. Systemic corticosteroids have multiple AEs.
Goal: Facilitate corticosteroid tapering.

Common Agents: Doxycycline, Dapsone, Methotrexate, Mycophenolate, Azathioprine.

Timing:

  • Often added to oral corticosteroid regimen after achieving disease control.
  • OR, started simultaneously with oral corticosteroid (due to delayed onset of effect of sparing agents).
⚠️ Immunosuppressive Sparing Agents (Azathioprine, Mycophenolate, Methotrexate):
  • Often reserved for patients with extensive BP.
  • Decision to add requires careful risk-benefit analysis due to potential serious AEs.
  • In some older adults, risks of adding these may exceed maintaining low-dose prednisone (e.g., ≤5 mg/day).
3️⃣ Refractory Disease: When Standard Tx Fails

Options include biologic therapies:

  • Rituximab
  • Omalizumab
  • Dupilumab
  • Intravenous Immune Globulin (IVIG)

Typically used when:

  • Failure to respond to standard therapy.
  • Morbidity from systemic corticosteroid therapy occurs.
  • Tapering prednisone to <10 mg/day is not feasible despite corticosteroid-sparing therapies.

📊 Assessing Response to Therapy

Primary Method: Clinical Assessment

  • Presence of BP skin lesions.
  • Occurrence of new lesion development or expansion.
  • Rate of healing of existing lesions.
  • Presence of pruritus.
  • Current medication regimen.
  • For resolved lesions/pruritus: duration of resolution.
Adjunct: Serum Antibody Levels
  • BP180 (BPAG2) antibody levels usually correlate with clinical activity.
  • Marked decreases in BP180 and lesser decreases in BP230 (BPAG1) Ab levels detected soon after starting topical steroids.
  • Limited data: Association between BP180 Ab levels and relapse risk.
  • (See "Clinical features and diagnosis..." for ELISA details)

For Clinical Studies & Specialized Clinics:
- Precise descriptors of response are recommended (e.g., 2012 international expert panel definitions).
- Bullous Pemphigoid Disease Area Index (BPDAI): Validated score used to quantify response. BPDAI activity score correlates well with BP180 antibody titer.

📉 Treatment Tapering and Cessation

Guided by clinical assessment. Key milestone:

"End of the consolidation phase" (Supports initiating tapering):
  • No new lesions or pruritic symptoms for at least two weeks.
  • Most (approx. 80%) of established lesions have healed.

Role of Serum BP180 Antibody Levels in Tapering (Adjunctive):

  • Data for best approach is lacking, but used in practice.
  • Approaches: Awaiting substantial reductions from pre-treatment levels to support tapering; following trends to predict exacerbations.
  • Needs more study for informing therapy changes.

Tapering Process:

  • Generally slowly (e.g., 5 mg prednisone reduction every 2-4 weeks).
  • Close clinical follow-up.
  • Continue tapering if: 80% lesions healed, <3 lesions healing spontaneously within 1 week, no pruritus.
  • Awaiting completely blister-free skin may prolong treatment and increase AE risk (esp. with oral corticosteroids).
🏁 Discontinuation of Treatment

Attempt once patients are in complete remission (absence of new/established lesions or pruritus) on minimal therapy for at least two months.

Examples of Minimal Therapy Doses:

  • Clobetasol propionate: ≤20 g per week
  • Prednisone: ≤0.1 mg/kg per day
  • Azathioprine: ≤0.7 mg/kg per day (normal TPMT)
  • Mycophenolate mofetil: ≤500 mg per day
  • Mycophenolic acid: ≤360 mg per day
  • Methotrexate: ≤5 mg per week
  • Dapsone: ≤50 mg per day
  • Doxycycline: 100 mg twice daily (Note: This is full therapeutic dose in some contexts, but considered minimal for maintenance here)
🔄 Managing Recurrences During Tapering/Post-Cessation
  • Mild Disease Activity Recurrence (e.g., <3 new lesions within one month):
    • Return to the preceding lowest effective treatment dose.
  • Greater Than Mild Disease Activity Recurrence (e.g., ≥3 lesions/month not healing in 1 week, extension of lesions, daily pruritus):
    • Return to dose that preceded the last effective treatment dose.
    • If this fails or patient presented with extensive disease: Return to initial effective dose.
    • Add a corticosteroid-sparing systemic agent (if not already on one).
    • Change the corticosteroid-sparing agent (if already on one).

⚠️ Determining Treatment Failure

Definition:

  • Continued development of non-transient lesions.
  • Continued extension of existing lesions.
  • Failure of existing lesions to begin to heal.
  • Continued pruritus despite an adequate course of treatment.

BP tends to respond quickly to appropriate therapeutic doses, usually within one month.

Examples of Points After Which Treatment Failure May Be Assumed:

Drug Criteria for Failure
Clobetasol propionate 40 g per day for four weeks
Prednisone 0.75 mg/kg per day for at least three weeks (with or without other therapies)
Azathioprine 2.5 mg/kg per day for four weeks (normal TPMT)
Mycophenolate mofetil 40 mg/kg per day for four weeks (normal renal function)
Methotrexate 15 mg per week for four weeks (>60 kg, no major renal impairment)
Dapsone 1.5 mg/kg per day for four weeks
Tetracyclines Full therapeutic dosing for four weeks

🩹 General Skin & Oral Care

🧴 General Skin Care

Best approach for blistered skin is not established. Authors' experience suggests:

  • 💧 Daily rupturing of tense blisters seems to reduce lateral extension.
  • Instructions for Patients/Caregivers:
    1. Cleanse blisters with alcohol prior to rupturing (sterile needle/blade).
    2. Puncture at most gravitationally dependent area to facilitate drainage.
    3. Leave overlying epithelial layer (blister roof) in place (acts as natural wound dressing).
    4. Cover open erosions with nonstick dressings (reduces pain, infection risk).
🚨 Superinfection is a significant concern! Especially with numerous lesions and systemic immunosuppressants.
Educate on warning signs: purulent drainage, worsening redness, or pain for prompt diagnosis/treatment.
👄 Oral Involvement

Occurs in 10-30% of BP patients (usually oral erosions).

Can often be managed with topical corticosteroid therapy (similar to mucous membrane pemphigoid).

Authors' experience: Mucous membrane involvement frequently improves during systemic therapy for cutaneous disease.
