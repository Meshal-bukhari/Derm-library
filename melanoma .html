GENETIC ABERRATIONS IN MUCOCUTANEOUS MELANOMAS
Pathogenesis involves four pathways signaling: MAPK, PI3K, WNT, MC1R-MITF
MAPK Pathway (Major pathway)

CKIT → GRP2 and SOS2 → activation of NRAS → activation of BRAF → MEK → ERK → activation of cyclin and cyclin dependent kinase → arresting retinoblastoma and releasing E2F for activation

MAPK pathway mnemonic: "Can't Get Really Big Men Eating Regularly" (CKIT → GRP2 → NRAS → BRAF → MEK → ERK → Regulation)

RISK FACTORS & EPIDEMIOLOGY
Epidemiology: More common in men • More in middle age (vs old in other non melanotic skin cancer) • Fair skin type (north Europe and north America) • Lightly pigmented skin

Genetic Risk Factors

Family history of cutaneous melanoma
Tendency to burn, inability to tan
Red hair color
DNA repair defects (e.g. xeroderma pigmentosum)
Familial melanoma genes: CDKN2A (P16,P14raf), BAP1, CDK4, SLC45A2, TYR mutation

Environmental Risk Factors

Intense intermittent sun exposure: superficial spread melanoma, and nodular melanoma
Chronic sun exposure: lentigo maligna melanoma
Residence in equatorial latitudes
PUVA (possible)
Tanning bed use, especially under the age of 35 years
Iatrogenic or acquired immunosuppression
Intense Intermittent sun-exposed regions → superficial spread and nodular melanoma
Chronic sun exposure → lentigo maligna melanoma
Sunburn in children and young adolescent (not elderly) → sunscreen for children, will help not old people
UVB believed to be the stronger risk factor
Phenotypic Expressions of Gene/Environment Interactions

Melanocytic Nevi and Solar Lentigines:

Increased total number of acquired melanocytic nevi (MN)
>100 MN, relative risk ~ 8-10-fold increased
Atypical melanocytic nevi (AMN)
>5 AMN, relative risk ~ 4-6-fold increased
Multiple solar lentigines (SL)
Multiple SL, relative risk ~ 3-4-fold increased
Relative risks (RR) are multiplicative: e.g. a person with >100 MN + >5 AMN + multiple SL has a relative risk ~ 10 × 5 × 3 = 150-fold

Atypical Melanocytic Nevi Criteria

Based upon the presence of at least three of the following criteria:

  1. Diameter ≥5 mm
  2. Ill-defined borders
  3. An irregular margin
  4. Varying shades within the lesion
  5. The simultaneous presence of papular and macular components
More than 5 Atypical nevi considered a risk
Atypical mole syndrome = 500 risk of melanoma
Common melanocytic nevi: >100 in one location (risk of superficial spread and nodular melanoma) 8-10 fold
Congenital melanocytic nevi: greater than 40 cm, satellite nevi, posterior axis (paraspinal, head, and neck regions). Melanoma arising within CMN is associated with NRAS mutation
Pregnancy considerations:
• High-risk melanoma, it may be reasonable to wait 2 years after diagnosis before becoming pregnant again (risk of recurrence)
• In pregnancy there is no risk of primary melanoma (only darkening due melanocyte activation)

APPROACH & DIAGNOSIS

History

Personal/Family History:
• Personal history/family history of melanoma
• Immunosuppression, transplant, HIV
• Genetic disorder (XP)
• UV light exposure, childhood sun burn, PUVA

Lesion Changes:
• Change in size, color, number, duration
• Ulcerate (most important)

Signs of Metastasis:
• Systemic symptoms: weight loss, fatigue, headache or cough
• Pain (any metastases)
• Convulsion (brain metastases)
• Instabilities (bone metastases)

Examination

Full examination:
• The scalp, external ocular/conjunctivae
• Oral mucosa, groin, buttocks
• Palms/soles/web

Lymph node:
• Primary echelon nodal basin(s)
• Regional skin and lymph-node ultrasonography

Lesion characteristics:
• Border, margin, size, color
ABCDE Criteria for Melanoma
A: Asymmetry
B: Border irregularity
C: Color variation
D: Diameter >5 mm
E: Evolution
Special signs:
• Ugly duckling sign: pigmented lesion that is different from other pigmented lesions
• Little Red Riding Hood sign
• EFG rule: Elevated, Firm, or Growing lesion - amelanotic or nodular melanoma
Dermoscopy: Increased screening sensitivity from 75% with examination to 90%

HISTOPATHOLOGY
CRITERIA FOR THE HISTOPATHOLOGIC DIAGNOSIS OF MELANOMA
Features: asymmetric, poorly circumscribed, nests of melanocytes within the epidermis that are not equidistant from one another, vary in size and shape

Architectural Pattern

• Asymmetry
• Poor circumscription of intraepidermal melanocytic component
• Silhouette of tumor base is uneven (except in nevoid melanoma)
• No maturation of melanocytes with progressive descent into the dermis
• Similar findings are present in the adnexal epithelium of folliculo-sebaceous units and eccrine duct
• Nests of melanocytes within the epidermis are not equidistant from one another
• Nests of melanocytes vary in size and shape
• Some nests of melanocytes become confluent
• Scatter of melanocytes above the dermal-epidermal junction
• Melanocytes arranged as solitary units predominate over nests within the epidermis
• Melanocytes in some nests are not cohesive
• Melanocytes extend down adnexal epithelium
• Sheets of melanocytes within the dermis
• Nests at the base of lesions are occasionally large

Cytomorphology

• Atypical melanocytes (with pleomorphic nuclei)
• Mitotic figures
• Necrotic melanocytes

Other Features

• Signs of regression
• Actinic elastosis
• "Dusty" melanin within tumor cells
• Melanin is not distributed in a uniform fashion
• Plasma cells at the base of the lesion
Location: Dermal-epidermal junction extended to upper epidermis
• If no dermis involvement = in situ
• If dermis involvement = invasive
NODULAR MELANOMA HISTOLOGY
• Vertical involvement (little epidermal involvement)
• Well-circumscribed tumor primarily within the dermis
LENTIGO MALIGNA MELANOMA HISTOLOGY
• Little pagetoid spread
• Solitary atypical more than nest
• More in adnexal structure (out root sheath)
• Solar elastosis
• Neurotropism
• Spindle cells (invasive component)
ACRAL LENTIGINOUS MELANOMA HISTOLOGY
• Proliferation of atypical melanocytes within the basal layer (most common) of a hyperplastic epidermis, sometimes pagetoid nest or solitary cell in cornified layer
• Melanomas in volar and subungual sites display strikingly dendritic melanocytes
Histologic Progression Patterns

Melanoma in situ

Increased number of melanocytes with atypical nuclei not only in the basal layer at all levels of the epidermis. (no dermal invasion)

Melanoma (superficial spread)

Pagetoid melanocytes spreading as solitary units and nests varying in size and shape are present throughout the entire epidermis. Neoplastic melanocytes extend into the dermis

STAGING (TNM SYSTEM)
Staging for melanoma: TNM
T: (1) Breslow depth, (2) histopathologic evidence of ulceration
N: (1) the number of metastatic lymph nodes; (2) clinically occult versus clinically detectable presence or absence of satellite, in-transit, and microsatellite metastases
M: (1) distant metastases, (2) positive LDH
Other Testing for Staging

Molecular Analysis

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH):
Can also assist in differentiating between benign and malignant melanocytic tumors, discriminate between benign nevi and melanoma with high sensitivity and specificity

Gene expression profiling (GEP):
Predict the risk of metastasis for cutaneous melanoma, May distinguish benign melanocytic nevi from cutaneous melanomas

Imaging & Biomarkers

Imaging:
PET scan (most sensitive for metastases), but sentinel lymph node (is more accurate and sensitive)

Serum markers for metastatic melanoma:
• S100B (most sensitive, most used and is risk factor for mortality)
• Melanoma inhibitory activity (MIA)

Treatment: Stage 1 and Stage 2

Wide local Surgical with safe margin:

More margin will not reduce risk of recurrence
  • In situ: 0.5 cm
  • Less than 1 mm: 1cm margin
  • 1-2 mm: 1-2 cm margin
  • More than 2mm: 2cm margin
Aim: avoid local recurrence (defined as any recurrence within 2 cm of the scar)
Risk of local recurrence is 4% (more with thick/ulcerated and head/neck and distal extremities) MCQ

Special Considerations:

  • Mohs surgery can be used in facial and acral area (to conserve the area and avoid unnecessary margin)
  • From Alomair file Approximately 20% of patients with a cutaneous melanoma > 1 mm in depth (need sentinel lymph node biopsy) MCQ
  • From bolognia: The "0.8-1.0 Rule" for SLNB:
    OFFER SLNB when:
    Thick melanomas: > 1.0 mm (high risk > 10%+)
    Medium melanomas: 0.8-1.0 mm (moderate risk ~5-10%)
    Thin but ulcerated: < 0.8 mm WITH ulceration (moderate risk ~5-10%)
    DON'T offer SLNB when:
    Melanoma in situ: No invasion = no metastasis risk
    Thin and clean: < 0.8 mm WITHOUT ulceration (low risk <5%)
    Memory Trick: Think "0.8 or ulceration" - if the melanoma is ≥0.8mm thick OR has ulceration (regardless of thickness), consider SLNB. The only exception is very thick melanomas >1.0mm always get it
Treatment: Regional Metastatic Melanoma (Stage III)

Lymph Node Management:

Metastatic spread of melanoma is primarily regional in about 70% of draining lymph nodes
  • First is sentinel lymph node biopsy
  • Serial sectioning using H&E stains combined with immunohistochemistry (e.g. MART-1/Melan-A, HMB45, S100)
  • If melanoma micro-metastasis is identified, then a complete regional lymph node dissection (Elective lymph node dissection)
  • Complete LN excision is the standard therapy

Lymph node metastasis types:

Metastasis to lymph node can be micrometastasis, macrometastasis, or in transit metastasis

Skin satellite and in-transit metastases:

Occur in the lymphatic vessels more proximally relative to the primary cutaneous melanoma and toward the regional lymph node (up 2cm from original location)

Treatment of choice for skin metastases is surgical but multiple lesions on a limb, isolated limb perfusion with melphalan, with or without tumor necrosis factor (TNF), has palliative value

Other options: Cryotherapy, Laser, IL2

ADJUVANT THERAPY OPTIONS

Used in Target patients with resected high-risk stage II or III melanoma. Maybe stage IV.

1. Chemotherapy and immunotherapy

Using microbial agents such as bacille Calmette-Guérin (BCG) or Corynebacterium parvum

2. IFN-α (high-risk stage II or III)

SE: flu-like symptoms, neuropsychiatric (depression, suicidal intention), hematologic, and hepatic side effects
Major side effect with high dose infusion
Note: IFN-α (high infusion dose risk of rhabdomyolysis)

3. Ipilimumab (stage III)

10 mg/kg every 3 weeks for 4 doses then every 3 months for up to 3 years

Treatment: Metastatic Melanoma (Stage IV)
Kinase inhibitors, Immune checkpoint inhibitors, surgical, radiation
Note: chemotherapy is absolute but the best result with dacarbazine, Temozolomide (some efficacy in CNS Mets)

Kinase inhibitors

Rapid and high response rate but resistance is an issue. (BRAF, MEK, Tyrosine kinase inhibitors)

Immune checkpoint inhibitors

Lower response rate but the response is often long-lasting. (Anti-CTLA-4, Anti-PD-1, Anti-PD-L1 antibodies)

Surgery:

  • For isolated non-visceral metastasis and complete resection with free surgical margins
  • Isolated lung metastases, median 5-year survival can approach 30% after surgical treatment
  • Brain metastases, surgical resection alone may extend median survival to about 10 months

Radiation therapy: palliative

For unresectable disease or distant metastases in patients with incurable disease, pain control and stabilization of bones affected by metastases, spinal cord compression, brain metastases, and local control of cutaneous disease

Important note: Whole brain radiotherapy combined with surgical resection had been the mainstay for treating cerebral metastases. (palliative)

Pattern Recognition for Boards:
• Sun-exposed + elderly + slow-growing = Lentigo maligna
• Nail + dark-skinned + thumb involvement = ALM
• Amelanotic + nodular + rapid growth = Nodular melanoma
• Head/neck + scar-like + S100+ HMB45- = Desmoplastic

• Blue-black + >1cm + head/neck = Malignant blue nevus
• Hard palate + amelanotic + poor prognosis = Mucosal
• Tendons + adolescent + t(12;22) = Clear cell sarcoma
