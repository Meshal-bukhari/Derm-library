Overview of Amyloidosis

Amyloidosis is a term describing several disorders characterized by abnormal deposition of amyloid, a fibrillar proteinaceous material derived from endogenous protein products.

Classification:
  • Primary: Amyloid deposition is the major feature on pathology
  • Secondary: Amyloid is present with another disease, but not clinically significant
Primary Amyloidosis:
  • Cutaneous: Amyloid deposits near the site of synthesis
  • Systemic: Amyloid circulates and deposits in distant tissues

Pathogenesis of Amyloidosis

The major component of amyloid is fibril protein

All forms of amyloidosis contain amyloid P

~30 types of amyloid protein have been identified, each associated with specific amyloid diseases:

Amyloid Protein Associated Conditions
Aβ Alzheimer's disease
AA Hereditary periodic fever syndromes and secondary systemic amyloidosis
Aβ2M Hemodialysis
AKer Macular and lichen amyloidosis
AL Primary systemic amyloidosis and nodular amyloidosis
ATTR 1. Familial amyloid polyneuropathy (TTR mutations)
2. Familial amyloid cardiomyopathy (TTR mutations)
3. Wild-type ATTR amyloidosis/senile systemic amyloidosis/senile cardiac amyloidosis

Properties of Amyloid

Appearance

On H&E: Amorphous, eosinophilic fissured masses

On Congo red: Orange-red color on light microscopy, and green birefringence on polarized light

On Thioflavin T: Yellow-green fluorescence(under UV fluorescence microscopy)

Other Stains

  • Crystal violet
  • Methyl violet
  • PAS
  • Sirius red
  • Pagoda red
  • Dylon stain

AA loses its affinity to Congo red when exposed to potassium permanganate

Pankeratin stain (MNF116 stain)can be used to differentiate AKer from colloid milium

To determine the exact type of amyloid protein, tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) is used

Primary Cutaneous Amyloidosis

Macular Amyloidosis

  • Protein: AKer (keratinocyte-derived)
  • Clinical: Hyperpigmented patches in a confluent or rippled pattern
  • Location: Upper back (scapular area)
  • Symptoms: Often pruritic, may be asymptomatic
  • Pathology: Amyloid deposition restricted to upper dermis

Lichen Amyloidosis

  • Protein: AKer (keratinocyte-derived)
  • Frequency: Most common type of primary cutaneous amyloidosis
  • Clinical: Discrete firm scaly hyperpigmented or skin-colored papules coalescing into plaques with rippled edge
  • Location: Shins and extensors
  • Symptoms: Pruritic
  • Pathology: Amyloid deposition restricted to upper dermis

Nodular Amyloidosis

  • Protein: AL (light chain-derived)
  • Clinical: Single or multiple waxy nodules or infiltrated plaques
  • Location: Trunk and extremities
  • Associated with:
    • Sjögren syndrome
    • IgG monoclonal gammopathy (40%)
    • Progression to systemic amyloidosis (7%)
  • Pathology: Diffuse deposition involving dermis, subcutis, and blood vessels

Macular and lichen amyloidosis are associated with MENII A (Sipple syndrome) and notalgia paresthetica (RET oncogene)

Variants of Primary Cutaneous Amyloidosis
  • Anosacral: Pigmentation and lichenification, associated with amyloidosis elsewhere
  • Bullous: Associated with lichen and systemic amyloidosis
  • Dyschromic: Guttate leukoderma superimposed upon a background of lichen or macular amyloidosis
  • Familial AKer amyloidosis: OSMRβ and IL-31RA mutations, both encode for IL-31 receptor
  • Biphasic: Shows features of both lichen and macular amyloidosis, as they likely represent a spectrum

Differential Diagnosis

Macular

  • Notalgia paresthetica
  • Pityriasis versicolor
  • Ashy dermatosis
  • Atrophic lichen planus

Lichen

  • Lichen simplex chronicus
  • Hypertrophic lichen planus
  • Pretibial myxedema
  • Prurigo nodularis

Nodular

  • Primary systemic amyloidosis (must be excluded)
  • Lymphoma cutis
  • Pseudolymphoma
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Granuloma annulare

Treatment

Treatment of Macular and Lichen Amyloidosis

No specific treatment is curative. The goal is to break the itch cycle.

  • Avoid chronic friction, scratching, and rubbing
  • Topical steroids, calcineurin inhibitors, and keratolytics
  • Phototherapy, lasers (Er:YAG, CO2)
  • Acitretin
  • Low-dose cyclophosphamide
Treatment of Nodular Amyloidosis

Local destructive methods

Primary Systemic Amyloidosis

A cutaneous manifestation of underlying plasma cell dyscrasia. Patients usually do not fulfill the criteria for multiple myeloma.

Amyloid is AL protein, derived from light chains of immunoglobulin and associated with IgG lambda monoclonal antibodies

Clinical Features

General

  • Fatigue, weight loss
  • Paresthesias
  • Dyspnea
  • Syncopal attacks due to orthostatic hypotension

Mucosal

  • Soft rubbery swellings or infiltration of the mucosa
  • Macroglossia
  • Associated hemorrhagic component frequently seen
  • Xerostomia from salivary gland infiltration

Cutaneous

  • Occurs in 25% of patients
  • Petechiae, purpura, and ecchymoses following minor trauma
  • "Raccoon eyes" after coughing, Valsalva maneuver, or proctoscopy
  • "Pinch purpura" after pinching or rubbing the skin
  • Waxy, translucent or purpuric papules and nodules
  • Smooth tiny skin-colored papules on head, neck, and anogenital region
Rare Cutaneous Presentations
  • Diffuse sclerodermoid appearance
  • Cutis verticis gyrate-like involvement of the scalp
  • Bullous lesions resembling PCT or EBA
  • Nail dystrophy resembling LP
  • Acquired acral cutis laxa

Renal

Proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, peripheral edema

Cardiac

Restrictive cardiomyopathy and congestive heart failure

Neurological

Sensory and autonomic involvement

Classic presentation: Macroglossia + carpal tunnel syndrome warrants investigation

Diagnosis

Patient with suspected systemic amyloidosis

Full skin and mucosal examination

If specific mucocutaneous lesions ➡️ Biopsy
If no specific mucocutaneous lesions ➡️ Fine needle aspiration of abdominal fat

Protein electrophoresis and immunofixation for blood and urine + serum free light chains

Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy if labs are abnormal

SAP scintigraphy for locating amyloid deposits and monitoring disease

Pathology

  • Amyloid deposits in the dermis and subcutis
  • Often found around sweat glands and within blood vessel walls
  • Rarely, amyloid-producing plasma cells can be found adjacent to the amyloid deposits

Treatment and Prognosis

  • Without treatment, median survival is ~13 months
  • Cardiac and renal involvement are bad prognostic factors
  • Treatment is similar to multiple myeloma (melphalan and systemic corticosteroids)
  • High-dose melphalan followed by autologous peripheral blood stem cell transplantation in younger patients with cardiac involvement
  • Newer therapies: lenalidomide, pomalidomide, bortezomib, daratumumab in combination with systemic corticosteroids and melphalan for older patients with cardiac involvement

Secondary Amyloidosis

Secondary Cutaneous Amyloidosis

  • Mainly in skin tumors
  • Clinically insignificant
  • Found in:
    • Dermatofibromas
    • Intradermal melanocytic nevi
    • Seborrheic keratoses
    • Pilomatricomas
    • Trichoepitheliomas
    • Sweat gland tumors
    • Basal cell carcinomas
    • Bowen disease
    • Porokeratosis
  • Also reported following PUVA therapy

Secondary Systemic Amyloidosis

  • A complication of severe chronic inflammatory diseases
  • Infections or autoimmune disorders
  • AA protein, which is synthesized by the liver as an acute phase protein
  • Skin involvement is rare
  • Treatment: treat the underlying disease + Eprodisate may slow the loss of renal function

Other Types of Amyloidosis

Hemodialysis-Associated Amyloidosis

Decreased excretion of β2-microglobulin

Subcutaneous nodules may occur occasionally

Inherited Amyloidoses

Autoinflammatory Syndromes

  • AA protein in Muckle-Wells syndrome
  • Colchicine is the treatment of choice in familial Mediterranean fever

Sipple Syndrome (MEN2A)

  • RET, codon 634, autosomal dominant
  • Medullary carcinoma of the thyroid, pheochromocytomas, and hyperparathyroidism
  • Increased serum levels of calcitonin
  • Associated with notalgia paresthetica and AKer amyloidosis <10 years of age

Hypotrichosis Simplex of the Scalp

  • Autosomal dominant
  • Mutations in genes that encode corneodesmosin
  • Normal hair at birth followed by progressive hair loss
  • Deposits of the truncated protein accumulate as amorphous amyloid deposits around hair follicles

X-Linked Reticulate Pigmentary Disorder (Partington Amyloidosis)

  • X-linked disease with mutations in POLA1, on chromosome Xp22
  • Associated with activation of proinflammatory genes and increased type I interferon profile
  • Adult women: linear streaks of hyperpigmentation (skin-limited)
  • Male patients: reticulated mottled brown skin pigmentation + recurrent respiratory infections, corneal dystrophy, and photophobia
  • Amyloid deposits are keratin-positive

Self-Assessment Quiz

Question 1

A 46-year-old woman develops an asymptomatic, waxy, red-brown, solitary plaque on her right posterior thigh. A punch biopsy demonstrates large globular deposits of amorphous eosinophilic material in the deep dermis, as well as subcutaneous fat and blood vessels with prominent plasma cells. Congo red staining shows apple-green birefringence under polarized light. What is the most likely source of amyloid material?

Answer: C) Amyloid light chains from skin-homing plasma cells

Explanation: This is a case of nodular cutaneous amyloidosis, characterized by waxy plaques/nodules with amyloid deposits in the deep dermis, subcutis, and blood vessels. The amyloid in nodular amyloidosis is AL type, derived from immunoglobulin light chains produced by skin-homing plasma cells.

Question 2

An 82-year-old man presented with a 10-year history of an enlarging, asymptomatic plaque on his left foot. Physical examination found a 7.5- x 6.5-cm, firm, exophytic plantar mass. Incisional biopsy found verrucous epithelium with eosinophilic, hyaline material throughout the dermis with admixed plasma cells. Dermal deposits stained with Congo red exhibited apple-green birefringence under polarized light. In situ hybridization found a 5:1 ratio of λ:κ immunoglobulin light chain. Complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, serum/urine protein electrophoresis, serum immunoglobulin light chains, electrocardiogram, and abdominal fat pad biopsy were unremarkable. What is the diagnosis?

Answer: D) Nodular cutaneous amyloidosis

Explanation: The exophytic mass with eosinophilic, hyaline material throughout the dermis showing apple-green birefringence with Congo red staining under polarized light is characteristic of nodular amyloidosis. The presence of admixed plasma cells and predominance of λ light chains is consistent with AL amyloid. Importantly, the systemic workup was negative, ruling out systemic amyloidosis.

Clinical Pearls and Memory Aids

Remember Amyloid Types: "KLAUS"

  • Keratin → AKer → Macular & Lichen cutaneous amyloidosis
  • Light chains → AL → Nodular cutaneous & Primary systemic amyloidosis
  • Acute phase protein → AA → Secondary systemic amyloidosis
  • Uremia (dialysis) → Aβ2M → Hemodialysis-associated amyloidosis
  • Senile cardiac → ATTR → Senile cardiac amyloidosis

Congo Red Properties: "ORPG"

  • Orange-Red on light microscopy
  • Potassium permanganate makes AA lose Congo red affinity
  • Green birefringence under polarized light

Primary Systemic Clues: "RACCOONS MAP"

  • Raccoon eyes (periorbital purpura)
  • AL protein (light chains)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Cardiac involvement (bad prognosis)
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Orange-red on Congo red
  • Nodular skin lesions
  • SAP scintigraphy for monitoring
  • Macroglossia (classic)
  • Abdominal fat aspiration (diagnostic)
  • Purpura with minor trauma
