Definition & Classification

Panniculitis = inflammation of subcutaneous fat

  • Mixed

CLINICAL PEARL

When analyzing a case of panniculitis, follow these key steps:

  1. Pattern identification: Septal, lobular, or mixed?
  2. Specific findings: Look for lipophagic changes, fat sclerosis, necrosis (membranous fat necrosis), or needle-shaped septate
  3. Vasculitis assessment: Is there vasculitis? (Nodular vasculitis, PAN, calciphylaxis, or thrombophlebitis)
  4. Dermal changes: Is the dermis normal or not? (Granuloma, sclerotic septa)
  5. Cell types: What inflammatory cells predominate?

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For board exams, the distinction between septal and lobular panniculitis is crucial for diagnosis. Know the specific histological features that define each type!

Diagnostic Approach to Panniculitis

Step-by-Step Diagnostic Algorithm

Step 1: Determine the Pattern

Is the inflammation predominantly septal, lobular, or mixed?

Step 2: Check for Vasculitis

Is there evidence of vasculitis (crucial distinction)?

Step 3: Look for Special Features

  • Ghost-like fat cells: Pancreatic panniculitis (with basophilic calcium deposition)
  • Cystic spaces in fat: Cold panniculitis
  • Fat necrosis: Erythema induratum (basophilic), lupus panniculitis (latter with lymphoid follicle-like aggregates, lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate)
  • "Needle-shaped" clefts in fat: Subcutaneous fat necrosis, sclerema neonatorum, post-steroid
  • Swiss cheese appearance: Paraffinoma or sclerosing lipogranuloma
  • Widened septae with giant cells: Erythema nodosum
  • Dense neutrophilic inflammation: α-1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Lipomembranous changes: Lipodermatosclerosis
  • Chicken wire pattern (linear collapsed vessels): Myxoid liposarcoma

Step 4: Biopsy Recommendations

  1. Excisional wedge biopsy is necessary for adequate sampling
  2. Punch biopsies are to be discouraged and may be misleading
  3. Biopsy an active rather than a late-stage lesion
  4. Special stains for infectious organisms
  5. Examine specimen under polarized light for foreign material

Erythema Nodosum (EN)

SEPTAL PANNICULITIS

📌 CLINICAL PEARL

The most common form of panniculitis!

Epidemiology

  • More common among women
  • More frequently during 2nd-4th decade of life

Pathogenesis

Delayed hypersensitivity response to a variety of antigenic stimuli, including bacteria, viruses, and chemical agents (Th1 pattern - IFN-γ, IL-2)

I. Most Common Causes
Idiopathic the most common cause (1/3 to 1/2 of cases)
Infections (may account for 1/3+ cases)
  • Streptococcal (esp. upper respiratory; most common infectious cause)
  • Viral upper respiratory tract infections
  • Bacterial gastroenteritis:
    • Yersinia (more common)
    • Salmonella
    • Campylobacter
  • Coccidioidomycosis (EN = lower risk of dissemination)
Drugs
  • Hormonal: Estrogens, oral contraceptive pills
  • Antibiotics: Sulfonamides, penicillins
  • Halides: Bromides, iodides
  • Biologics & Targeted Therapies:
    • TNF inhibitors: Etanercept, Infliximab, Adalimumab
    • Immune checkpoint inhibitors: Ipilimumab, Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab
    • BRAF inhibitors: Vemurafenib, Dabrafenib
    • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors: Imatinib, Dasatinib, Ibrutinib
  • Other: G-CSF, NSAIDs, Abatacept, Carbamazepine, Leukotriene inhibitors, Proton pump inhibitors
Sarcoidosis 10%-20% of cases in some series
Löfgren syndrome: EN, hilar lymphadenopathy, fever, polyarthritis, uveitis
Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) Crohn disease (stronger association than UC)
II. Uncommon Causes
Infections
  • Bacteria: Brucella melitensis, Chlamydophila pneumoniae, Chlamydia trachomatis, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • Fungi: Histoplasma capsulatum
  • Viruses: Hepatitis B virus (and vaccine)
Neutrophilic Dermatoses Behçet disease (EN here resembles erythema induratum), Sweet syndrome, Acne fulminans (incl. isotretinoin-associated)
Pregnancy
III. Rare Causes
Granulomatous Mastitis
Pernicious Anemia
Diverticulitis
Infections
  • Bacteria: Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Neisseria meningitidis, Escherichia coli, Bartonella henselae, Bordetella pertussis
  • Spirochetes: Treponema pallidum (syphilis)
  • Fungi: Dermatophytes (kerion), Blastomyces dermatitidis
  • Viruses: HIV, COVID-19
  • Protozoa/Parasites: Giardiasis, Amoebiasis (abscesses)
Genetic Syndromes MonoMAC syndrome (GATA2 mutations)
Malignancy Acute myelogenous leukemia (AML), Hodgkin disease (may overlap with Sweet syndrome)
Lupus erythematosus (LE) EN is one of several forms of panniculitis reported in LE (distinct from specific lupus panniculitis)
Important Distinction Erythema nodosum leprosum (ENL) is a different disease (small vessel vasculitis, not true EN)

Clinical Features

  • Bilateral, tender erythematous nodules
  • Arises in crops and the most common sites are the shins (others: thighs and forearms +/- trunk, neck, and face)
  • Ulceration is NOT a feature (key differentiating point)
  • Systemic symptoms may occur: arthritis, arthralgia, fever, and malaise
  • Lesions usually last a few days or weeks and then slowly involute, without scar formation
  • May have bruise-like appearance when starting to resolve
  • Recurrence: 33%

Pathology

  • Septal panniculitis
  • NO vasculitis
  • Neutrophils may predominate in early lesions
  • Histiocytes in late lesions
  • Miescher Microgranulomas (Key Feature):
    • Can be found in early lesions
    • Considered a characteristic, if not pathognomonic, feature of EN.
    • Description: Small collections of cells (macrophages or "histiocytoid cells" [neutrophil precursors])
    • Location: Found within septa or at a septal-lobular interface.
    • Arrangement: Tend to surround neutrophils or small cleft-like spaces.

Investigations

  • Chest x-ray: Rule out infection (esp. TB, sarcoidosis) and malignancy
  • Tuberculin test or IGRA: Evaluate for latent or active TB
  • Viral hepatitis panel: Assess for HBV and other viral hepatitides
  • Antistreptolysin O (ASO) and/or anti-DNase B titers: Evaluate for recent streptococcal infection
  • If diarrhea: Stool for bacteria (e.g., Salmonella, Yersinia, Campylobacter) and ova/parasite culture, fecal WBC
  • Pregnancy test: In all females of childbearing age
  • Malignancy workup: If indicated by history and exam
  • Biopsy: From early lesion; deep incisional biopsy is preferred

Differential Diagnosis

  • Erythema Induratum
  • Pancreatic panniculitis (can ulcerate)
  • Infectious panniculitis
  • Polyarteritis nodosa
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency panniculitis

Treatment

  • Generally self-limited (6 weeks), recurrence in 33%
  • First-line: NSAIDs (Naproxen 250-500mg BID, Indomethacin 25-50mg Q8-12h)
  • Note: Avoid NSAIDs in inflammatory bowel disease
  • Second-line: Potassium iodide (SSKI), Colchicine, Anti-TNF agents, Hydroxychloroquine
  • Third-line: Systemic corticosteroids, Thalidomide, Cyclosporine, Dapsone

For the board exam, remember:

  • SSKI (saturated solution of potassium iodide) is an important treatment option

Erythema Induratum (EI)

MIXED PANNICULITIS (LOBULAR/SEPTAL)

Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

  • Female predominance
  • Mean age: 30-40 years
  • Type III, IV cell-mediated response to an antigenic stimulus

Etiology

  • TB (Most common): With TB called "EI of Bazin" - typically ulcerates, +/- annular. There is a report of erythema induratum being induced by a tuberculin skin test
  • Non-TB causes: Without TB called "nodular vasculitis"/"EI of Whitfield" (no ulcer)
    • Infectious agents: Nocardia, hepatitis C virus, pseudomonas
    • Drugs: Propylthiouracil (PTU), etanercept, certolizumab, chemotherapy for breast cancer
    • Others: Superficial thrombophlebitis, SLE, RA, hypothyroid, Addison's, Takayasu, IBD, CLL, APLS, hepatitis B

Clinical Features

  • Tender, erythematous to violaceous nodules and plaques
  • Most often develop on the lower legs, especially the calves (uni/bilateral)
  • May also affect thigh, buttocks, arm, and feet
  • Annular forms more common in TB-related cases
  • Ulceration can occur (unlike EN)
  • Lesions are persistent and tend to heal with scarring
  • Recurrent nature

Pathology

  • Lobular or mixed septal/lobular panniculitis
  • Vasculitis of medium vessels is a prominent feature
  • Inflammation is mixed:
    • Predominantly neutrophilic (early)
    • Lymphocytic or granulomatous (late)
  • Fat necrosis (basophilic) with coagulative or caseous appearance
  • Sometimes with palisading granulomas

Investigation

  • Biopsy for H&E and culture
  • CBC, ESR, CRP, LFT
  • TB QuantiFERON, PCR, CXR
  • LFT, Hepatitis panel
  • Lipase, amylase
  • Serology for CTD+ANCA
  • Doppler US

Differential Diagnosis

  • Erythema nodosum
  • Infection-induced panniculitis - more prominent neutrophilic component, granular basophilic necrosis, sweat gland necrosis
  • Lupus panniculitis - less granulomatous, prominent lymphoplasmacellular infiltrate, may show mucin deposits
  • PAN and thrombophlebitis - inflammation limited to the immediate perivascular zone
  • Perniosis - history of cold exposure, microscopic examination shows prominent involvement of dermal vessels with "fluffy edema" of walls

Treatment

  • Treatment directed at the underlying cause
  • Leg elevation, bed rest, compression
  • Multi-drug anti-TB therapy for M. tuberculosis
  • Antimicrobials for other infection-related cases
  • Discontinuation of inciting medications
  • NSAIDs, SSKI, corticosteroids, colchicine, doxycycline, antimalarials, MMF, gold

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

Key distinguishing features from Erythema Nodosum:

  • EI can ulcerate; EN never ulcerates
  • EI has vasculitis; EN does not
  • EI heals with scarring; EN resolves without scarring
  • EI is lobular/mixed panniculitis; EN is septal panniculitis
  • Know the distinction between EI of Bazin (TB-related) and nodular vasculitis/EI of Whitfield (non-TB)

Lipodermatosclerosis (LDS)

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Definition & Epidemiology

Progressive fibrotic process of the skin and subcutaneous fat induced by chronic venous insufficiency

  • Most patients are women over age 40

Pathogenesis

  • Abnormal regulation of angiogenesis:
    • ↑(VEGFR-1), negative regulator of VEGF-mediated angiogenesis
    • ↑(Ang-2)
  • Protein C and S deficiency
  • Risk factors: Obesity, DM, HTN, thyroid disorders, PVD, peripheral neuropathy
  • Associated with history of cellulitis, thrombophlebitis

Clinical Features

  • Favors the medial aspect of lower extremities above the malleolus
  • Left leg > Right leg (if right leg, ask about previous DVT)
  • Unilateral or bilateral; some involve lower abdomen, dependent areas
  • Acute phase: Pain, warmth, erythema, induration; clinical/radiological evidence of venous disease (pseudocellulitis appearance)
  • Chronic phase: Marked sclerosis of dermis and subcutis, resulting in red-brown induration sharply demarcated from adjacent normal skin; inverted wine bottle appearance
  • Hyperpigmentation due to hemosiderin deposition

📌 CLINICAL PEARL

Clinical clues suggesting LDS rather than cellulitis:

  • Persistence of a lesion
  • Association with stasis changes
  • Lack of response to antimicrobials
  • Positive venous studies

Pathology

  • A mixed septal and lobular panniculitis (classified as "Lobular and mixed septal-lobular panniculitis"). Appearance varies with stage:
  • Early Lesions / Acute Phase:
    • Lobular changes: Mid-lobular ischemic necrosis
    • Septal changes: Lymphocytic infiltrate in septa rimming fat lobules
    • Vascular changes: Variable capillary congestion & thrombosis; hemorrhage with hemosiderin deposition
    • Fibrin deposition: Pericapillary fibrin (may precede clinical changes; can be seen in clinically normal extremities of patients with healed venous ulcers)
  • Progressed / Chronic Phase / Advanced Lesions:
    • Septal changes (prominent): Septal thickening and marked hyaline sclerosis involving lipocytes
    • Lobular changes & inflammation: Lipophage formation; mixed inflammatory cell infiltrates (inflammation reduced in advanced lesions)
    • Membranocystic (lipomembranous) change – KEY FEATURE:
      • Common, especially in chronic lesions
      • Thickened, undulating membranes forming cysts and papillary configurations
      • Origin: Degenerated cell membranes of lipocytes/macrophages
      • Composition: Ceroid (oxidation product of unsaturated fatty acids)
      • Diagnostic value: When present, highly suggestive
    • Elastic fiber changes: Short, frayed (sometimes calcified) elastic fibers in subcutaneous septa (resembles pseudoxanthoma elasticum)
    • Myospherulosis: Foreign-body granulomatous reaction to lipid-containing material and blood (can be seen)
    • Dermal changes: Fibrosis, tortuous thick-walled veins, superficial and deep perivascular inflammation

📌 CLINICAL PEARL

DDx of lipomembranous changes:

  1. LDS
  2. LE, DM → panniculitis
  3. Liposarcoma
  4. EN
  5. Diabetic dermopathy

Diagnosis

  • Primarily clinical - based on findings and history
  • Biopsy is usually not warranted (50% of biopsy sites do not heal)
  • If biopsy needed: thin elliptical from margin of red, indurated lesion
  • Diagnostic studies: Duplex ultrasound, laser Doppler scanning, ultrasound indentometry, capillary microscopy, MRI

Treatment

  • Supportive:
    • Weight loss
    • Leg elevation and consistent compression therapy (30-40 mmHg) or (15-20 or 20-30) daily except bedtime
  • Medical:
    • IL CS (e.g., Triamcinolone 5-10 mg/cc)
    • Oxandrolone → anabolic steroids enhance fibrinolysis
    • Stanozolol (withdrawn due to side effects)
    • Ultrasound, Pentoxifylline
  • Surgical: Fasciotomy, Phlebectomy

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • "Inverted wine bottle" or "inverted champagne bottle" appearance is pathognomonic
  • LDS is often mistaken for cellulitis ("pseudocellulitis")
  • Biopsy is usually avoided due to poor healing
  • Lipomembranous changes are characteristic histologically
  • Always look for underlying venous insufficiency as the root cause

Pancreatic Panniculitis

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Definition & Epidemiology

Uncommon complication (2%) of pancreatic disease including acute and chronic pancreatitis, pancreatic cyst, congenital pancreatic duct abnormality, and pancreatic carcinoma (associated with 24% mortality)

Pathogenesis

  • Lipase > amylase and trypsin elevated levels detected in blood, urine, and skin lesions
  • Venous stasis may promote this process

Clinical Features

  • Erythematous, edematous, sometimes painful nodules arise singly or in crops; may migrate
  • Can become fluctuant and ulcerate, discharging an oily material
  • Location: lower legs (ankle, knees), periarticular; others: scalp, chest, abdomen, arms; face spared
  • Lesion has softer center and might discharge oily material
  • Heals with hyperpigmented depressed scars
  • Systemic features: fever, abdominal pain, inflammatory polyarthritis, ascites, pleural effusions

📌 CLINICAL PEARL

Panniculitis can precede pancreatic disease identification by 1-7 months! Consider this diagnosis in any unexplained panniculitis case.

Schmidt's Triad = Poor Prognosis

  • Pancreatic panniculitis (subcutaneous nodules)
  • Polyarthritis (mono, oligo)
  • Eosinophilia

Pathology

  • Lobular or mixed septal/lobular process
  • Saponification of fat with fat necrosis leading to basophilic granular or homogeneous calcium deposition and microcyst formation
  • Ghost cells: necrosed lipocytes with thickened wall surrounded by neutrophils (DDx: mucormycosis)
  • Often referred to as "neutrophilic panniculitis"
  • Fibrosis and lipoatrophy in late stages

Investigation

  • CBC & diff: ↑WBC, ↑EOS
  • Lipase (most important single - first to be +), amylase (2-3 days after eruption of skin lesions), trypsin
  • LFT, RFT
  • CXR → pleural effusion
  • Abdominal imaging: X-ray, US (ascites), CT (saponification of pancreatic head, ascites)
  • Bone x-ray: lytic areas in cortical bone near large joints
  • CA 19-9, CEA
  • Biopsy

Differential Diagnosis

  • Erythema nodosum
  • Erythema induratum
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
  • Infection-induced panniculitis

Treatment

  • Supportive: Compression and elevation
  • Treat underlying pancreatic disease
  • Octreotide: synthetic somatostatin-like polypeptide inhibits pancreatic enzyme production
  • Surgical: Referral to surgery; resection of pancreatic cancer may lead to regression of panniculitis

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • "Ghost cells" with basophilic granular calcium deposition are pathognomonic
  • Oily discharge from nodules is characteristic
  • Panniculitis may be the presenting sign of pancreatic disease
  • Schmidt's triad (panniculitis + polyarthritis + eosinophilia) indicates poor prognosis
  • Lipase is the single most important diagnostic test

Lupus Panniculitis (Lupus Profundus)

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Epidemiology

  • Usually adults, age 30-40 years (can affect children, neonates)
  • Female:male ratio 2:1 to 4:1
  • Occurs prior to other manifestations of LE in many cases
  • Coexistent with DLE in 33%, SLE in 10-15%
  • 2-3% of SLE patients will have lupus panniculitis
  • May have family history of autoimmune disease
  • If a patient has both SLE and lupus panniculitis, their SLE will be less severe
  • Average duration: 10 years

Pathogenesis

Autoimmune: T lymphocytes (TH1) and macrophages; TLR7, TLR9 involvement

Clinical Features

  • Tender subcutaneous nodules and plaques that may arise in crops
  • Predilection for face (children), upper arms, hips, trunk, shoulder (most common in DLE)
  • No distal extremities involvement (helps exclude EN, EI)
  • Chronic, relapsing clinical course
  • Usually eventuates in subcutaneous atrophy, which can be disfiguring
  • DLE has surface changes (not seen in pure lupus panniculitis)
  • Lesions can appear after trauma, injection, surgery

Investigations

  • Skin biopsy for H&E + DIF:
    • In DLE: older lesions are better
    • For panniculitis: take RECENT active indurated lesion from sun-protected area (lower limbs or trunk)
  • CBC & DIFF: leukopenia
  • ESR ↑
  • Complement levels: C3, C4 ↓
  • ANA +
  • Anti-dsDNA +/-
  • Anti-ssDNA, SSA, SSB, and chemistry panel
  • IHC: CD3,4,7,8 to rule out cutaneous T-cell lymphoma

Pathology

  • Predominantly lobular panniculitis
  • Mucin deposition; hyaline necrosis of lobules; lymphocytes and plasma cells, sometimes with prominent nuclear dust
  • Nodular lymphocytic aggregates or lymphoid follicles
  • Overlying epidermal or dermal changes of discoid LE (if present)
  • Positive lupus band in DIF:
    • Subacute lupus in basal keratinocytes
      • Special features Calcification and mucin deposition Vacuolar changes Cellular composition ++ plasma cells TCR-γ +: monoclonal DIF + lupus band - Epidermal/dermal features May have changes of lupus Usually confined to subcutis

Differential Diagnosis

  • SPTCL (critical differential)
  • CTD panniculitis
  • Erythema nodosum
  • Erythema induratum
  • Deep morphea
  • Traumatic panniculitis

Treatment

  • Antimalarials: produce improvement in most patients; may be required for several years
    • Onset of action: 4-8 weeks, optimal benefit may take 6 months
  • Systemic corticosteroids (initial phase)
  • Dapsone
  • Cyclophosphamide
  • Thalidomide
  • Mycophenolate mofetil
  • Methotrexate
  • Cyclosporine
  • IVIG
  • For DLE lesions: potent topical or intralesional corticosteroids

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Critical differential diagnosis: SPTCL (which requires different management)
  • No distal extremity involvement (helps distinguish from EN and EI)
  • Typical distribution: face, proximal extremities, trunk
  • Antimalarials are mainstay of treatment (unlike other panniculitides)
  • Coexisting SLE tends to be less severe if panniculitis is present

Panniculitis of Dermatomyositis

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Epidemiology & Overview

  • Uncommon manifestation of dermatomyositis
  • Usually incidental microscopic changes of panniculitis (without clinical lesions)
  • Can precede or follow other symptoms of DM
  • Indurated, painful nodules +/- ulceration
  • DM with panniculitis: Usually responds to therapy

Clinical Features

  • Location: Trunk and extremities
  • Indurated, painful nodules +/- ulceration, lipoatrophy
  • Sometimes seen with established disease, but rarely the initial manifestation
  • May be seen in histologic sections without any overlying clinical lesions
  • Partial or generalized lipoatrophy without preceding clinical lesions of panniculitis occurs in up to 1/4 of patients with juvenile DM
    • Often accompanied by increased abdominal fat
    • Associated with metabolic abnormalities: ↑TG and insulin resistance

Pathology

  • Lymphocytic and plasmacytic lobular or mixed lobuloseptal inflammation
  • Vasculitis
  • Membranocystic changes → resistant to therapy
  • Calcification
  • Vacuolar interface (hole > lymphocyte)

DM Panniculitis vs. Lupus Panniculitis

DM Panniculitis Lupus Panniculitis
Pattern Lobular Lobular
Calcification +++ -
Lipomembranocystic changes +++ +
Hyaline necrosis + +++

Differential Diagnosis

Lupus panniculitis - hyaline necrosis and lymphoid nodules; both can be poikilodermatous, but lupus is more likely to show appendageal involvement and is associated with positive BMZ fluorescence

Treatment

  • Responds well to treatment
  • Prednisone
  • Methotrexate, Azathioprine, Cyclosporine
  • IVIG
  • Variable response to hydroxychloroquine
  • Residual facial lipoatrophy can be treated with hyaluronic acid and poly-L-lactic acid dermal fillers

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Calcification is prominent histologically (+++)
  • Lipomembranocystic changes are characteristic and resistant to therapy
  • Panniculitis can precede other DM symptoms
  • Juvenile DM can present with lipoatrophy without clinical panniculitis

Morphea/Scleroderma Panniculitis

SEPTAL PANNICULITIS

Clinical Features

  • Both morphea and scleroderma (systemic sclerosis) can affect the subcutaneous fat
  • Primary site of involvement in deep morphea (morphea profunda)
  • Also occurs in variants:
    • Disabling pansclerotic morphea of childhood
    • Generalized morphea
    • Linear morphea
  • Location: trunk and extremities
  • Indurated and depressed plaques, some with overlying sclerodermoid changes
  • Not typically painful
  • Eosinophilic fasciitis can involve subcutaneous fat and fascia
    • Associated with eosinophilia, polyclonal gammopathy, and serologic abnormalities

Pathology

  • Septal panniculitis
  • Lymphocytes and plasma cells predominate (macrophages and eosinophils may be present)
  • In late stages: subcutis largely replaced by hyalinized connective tissue +/- lipoatrophy

Differential Diagnosis

Features that help separate morphea/scleroderma panniculitis from other septal forms:

  • Septal panniculitis
  • Lymphoplasmacytic predominance
  • Dermal and subcutaneous sclerosis

From related conditions:

  • Scleroderma: Sclerosis with lesser degrees of subcutaneous inflammation
  • Eosinophilic fasciitis: Predominance of fascial involvement with extension into subcutis

Treatment

  • Intralesional corticosteroids
  • Hydroxychloroquine

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Morphea panniculitis is a septal panniculitis (unlike many other types which are lobular)
  • Primary site of involvement in deep morphea (morphea profunda)
  • Distinguished by lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate and dermal/subcutaneous sclerosis
  • Hyalinized connective tissue replacement of subcutis in late stages

Subcutaneous Fat Necrosis of Newborn, Sclerema Neonatorum & Post-steroid Panniculitis

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Subcutaneous Fat Necrosis of the Newborn (SFNN)

Pathogenesis

  • Rare, self-limited panniculitis occurring in first few weeks of life (2-3 weeks)
  • Precipitating factors:
    • Maternal: Diabetes, cocaine use
    • Birth trauma (less likely as most affected infants delivered by C-section)
    • Hypothermia (including cooling blankets for hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy)
    • Perinatal hypoxemia
  • Increased saturated:unsaturated fatty acid ratio → more prone to crystallization, adipocyte injury, and granulomatous inflammation
  • Type B crystals (larger) → capable of eliciting granulomatous response

Clinical Features

  • Full-term infants in first month of life (2-3 weeks)
  • Localized, smooth, circumscribed, symmetrical, mobile, red to violaceous subcutaneous nodules or plaques
  • Common locations: cheeks, trunk, thighs, extremities, back, buttocks
  • May be painless or painful
  • Resolves spontaneously within 3-6 months, leaving normal skin or small pitted scars

Complications & Associations

  • Hypercalcemia (1-4 months after skin lesion onset)
    • Due to extrarenal production of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3 by activated macrophages
    • Can cause fever, eosinophilia, and persistent nephrocalcinosis
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Anemia

Pathology

  • Lobular panniculitis
  • Needle-shaped clefts within both lipocytes and giant cells
  • Neutrophils, lymphocytes, macrophages, and giant cells
  • Crystals are doubly refractile and stain with oil red O (due to triglycerides)
  • Eosinophilic granules sometimes within multinucleated giant cells

Management of Subcutaneous Fat Necrosis of the Newborn (SFNN):

  • General Approach & Prognosis: The prognosis is usually favorable, with most lesions resolving spontaneously. Supportive care is emphasized, as spontaneous resolution is common.
  • Specific Management Strategies:
    • Supportive Care: Primary management involves supportive care and monitoring, as most lesions resolve on their own.
    • Monitoring for Complications:
      • Hypercalcemia is a known complication, especially in higher birth weight infants, typically appearing within two months of lesion onset. Serial monitoring of calcium levels is recommended for at least two months after lesion resolution.
      • Thrombocytopenia is another potential complication, so monitoring platelet counts is advised.
    • Management of Hypercalcemia (if it develops):
      • Hydration
      • Dietary restriction of calcium and vitamin D
      • Calcium-wasting diuretics (e.g., furosemide)
      • Calcitonin
      • Bisphosphonates (e.g., etidronate, pamidronate)
      • Corticosteroids may help by interfering with vitamin D metabolism and reducing calcitriol production.
    • Management of Severe Skin Inflammation: Systemic corticosteroids may be indicated in severe cases to control inflammation. No specific dose or duration is provided.

Sclerema Neonatorum

Epidemiology

  • Affects premature, debilitated infants
  • Usually during the first week of life
  • Slight male predominance

Pathogenesis

  • Crystallization and hardening of fat (increased saturated:unsaturated fatty acid ratio)
  • Defective ability to mobilize fatty acids
  • Precipitating factors: Hypothermia, perinatal asphyxia, defective complement activity, dehydration

Clinical Features

  • Widespread, diffuse, rapid hardening of subcutaneous tissues
  • Firm, rigid skin over most of the body (can't pick up skin over lesions)
  • Cool, waxy, rigid, board-like skin with purple mottled discoloration
  • Sparing: genitalia, palms, and soles
  • Poor prognosis: low birth weight, hypothermia, hemorrhagic phenomena

Associations

  • Respiratory difficulties
  • Congestive heart failure
  • Intestinal obstruction
  • Diarrhea
  • Death (usually from septicemia) in ~75% of patients

Pathology

  • Lobular panniculitis
  • Inflammation usually sparse to absent
  • Needle-shaped clefts within lipocytes (rather than giant cells)
  • Later stage: septal thickened connective tissue bands

Post-steroid Panniculitis

Epidemiology

  • Rare complication of rapid systemic corticosteroid withdrawal
  • Children (ages 20 months to 14 years)
  • Occasionally adults

Pathogenesis

  • Occurs after rapid withdrawal of systemic corticosteroids (oral or IV)
  • With prednisone, cumulative dosages typically 2000-6000 mg (2-6 g)

Clinical Features

  • Lesions appear 1-40 days (usually ~10 days) after rapid corticosteroid withdrawal
  • Disappear spontaneously over months to a year
  • Firm red plaques on cheeks, arms, trunk
  • May be small/discrete or large/confluent
  • Pruritic, tender, or asymptomatic

Associations

Underlying conditions treated with systemic corticosteroids: leukemia, cerebral edema, nephrotic syndrome, secretory diarrhea, acute rheumatic fever, COPD, Sjögren syndrome

Pathology

  • Needle-shaped clefts, some with "starburst" pattern, within lipocytes or giant cells
  • Lobular panniculitis with lymphocytes, macrophages, multinucleated giant cells

For the board exam, remember:

  • All three conditions share needle-shaped clefts histologically
  • SFNN: monitor calcium for 6 months (risk of hypercalcemia)

  • Post-steroid: occurs after rapid corticosteroid withdrawal
  • SFNN: appears in full-term infants; Sclerema: in premature infants

Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency Panniculitis

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Epidemiology

  • Male = Female
  • Infancy to 8th decade (panniculitis typically at age 30-60 years)

Pathogenesis

  • Glycoprotein produced in liver; most abundant circulating serine protease inhibitor (serpin)
  • Gene: SERPINA1
  • Alleles: >120 based on mobility (M,S,Z - Medium, Slow, very slow)
  • MM: common, normal level (120-200 mg/dl)
  • SZ: mild-moderate deficiency
  • ZZ: severe deficiency
  • Acts on proteolytic enzymes to prevent tissue degradation
  • A1 antitrypsin inhibits: trypsin, elastase, collagenase, plasmin, thrombin

Systemic Associations

  • Chronic liver disease
  • Emphysema
  • Pancreatitis
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Vasculitis (C-ANCA)
  • Angioedema
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Histoplasmosis

Clinical Features

  • Erythematous-purpuric tender nodules or plaques
  • Early lesions resemble cellulitis or abscess
  • Ulceration with scarring
  • Oily discharge or drainage
  • Predilection: flanks, buttocks, thighs, lower trunk, proximal extremities
  • Trauma-induced in ~1/3 of cases; may occur postpartum
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, pleural effusions, pulmonary emboli, early-onset emphysema, hepatic fibrosis, cirrhosis, pancreatitis, angioedema
  • Prolonged course, resistant to immunosuppressive therapy
  • Heals with scarring and atrophy
  • Severe manifestations in those with profound deficiency (PiZZ)

Pathology

  • Neutrophilic panniculitis followed rapidly by necrosis and destruction of fat lobules
  • Neutrophils between collagen bundles in reticular dermis (early diagnostic clue)
  • Dissolution of dermal collagen with liquefactive necrosis
  • Separation of fat lobules from adjacent septa
  • Characteristic "skip areas" of normal fat adjacent to severe necrotizing panniculitis
  • Chronic inflammation and hemorrhage at periphery
  • May show vasculitis or thrombosis
  • In intermediate deficiency: lipophage and giant cell accumulation may be prominent

Differential Diagnosis

  • Clinical: Pyoderma gangrenosum - A1AT deficiency ulcers lack the necrotic, "undermined" borders
  • Infection-induced panniculitis - require tissue culture
  • Traumatic (factitial) panniculitis
  • Pancreatic panniculitis
  • Erythema induratum (nodular vasculitis)
  • Subcutaneous Sweet syndrome

Treatment

  • Ineffective therapies: corticosteroids, immunosuppressants, cytotoxic agents, colchicine, danazol, hydroxychloroquine
  • Effective options:
    • Doxycycline (200 mg BID) for 3 months - effective in mild cases
    • Dapsone (50-150 mg daily) - for mild cases; suppresses neutrophil migration
    • Various combinations: cyclophosphamide + dexamethasone; methylprednisolone + dapsone
    • Reduction of alcohol (hepatotoxin may precipitate hepatitis)
  • Most effective treatment: Alpha-1 antitrypsin replacement IV (60 mg/kg/week) for 3-7 weeks
    • Rapid improvement, often after 3 weekly doses
    • Recurrences possible when levels fall below 50 mg/dl
  • Other options: plasma exchange, liver transplantation
  • Autophagy-enhancing drugs (carbamazepine) - may reduce systemic manifestations including hepatic fibrosis

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • "Splaying of neutrophils" between collagen bundles in reticular dermis is an early diagnostic clue
  • "Skip areas" of normal fat adjacent to necrotizing panniculitis is characteristic
  • Most effective treatment is A1AT replacement (unlike other panniculitides)
  • Conventional immunosuppressants are ineffective
  • Doxycycline and dapsone are effective in mild cases

Traumatic Panniculitis

PREDOMINANTLY LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Overview

Extrinsic injury of varying types can produce panniculitis. There are four broad categories:

  1. Cold panniculitis (Haxthausen's disease)
  2. Sclerosing lipogranuloma
  3. Panniculitis due to other injectable substances or therapies
  4. Panniculitis due to blunt trauma

Cold Panniculitis

  • Primarily affects children
  • Locations: cheeks, chin
  • Females > males (if wearing tight clothing)
  • Typically occurs after prolonged cold exposure to cheeks/limbs
  • May be from ambient temperature, therapeutic cold application, or popsicles (Popsicle panniculitis)
  • Pathogenesis: crystallization of subcutaneous fat with subsequent inflammation
  • Related to higher ratio of saturated:unsaturated fatty acids in infants

Sclerosing Lipogranuloma

  • +/- migratory erythema, swelling, abscess, lymphangitis, fibrosis
  • Male genitalia commonly affected (injection of oily substances)
  • Also from vitamin E cream
  • Eosinophilic sclerosing lipogranuloma: Japanese patients, Y-shaped induration of scrotum
  • Patients often deny self-injection
  • Grease gun granuloma: accidental firing of grease gun used by mechanics

Injection-Related Panniculitis

  • Texier's disease: phytonadione (vitamin K) injection
  • Sclerotic lesion with lilac border
  • Buttocks & thighs
  • May resemble a "cowboy gunbelt and holster"

Blunt Trauma

  • Ecchymotic character
  • Arm/hands/shin
  • May have hypertrichosis
  • Due to breakdown of erythrocyte membranes

Factitial Panniculitis

  • Injections of oils: mineral oil (paraffin), camphor, cottonseed, sesame oils, medical grade silicone
  • Injection of milk, feces
  • Therapeutic injections: meperidine, morphine, pentazocine, phytonadione (vitamin K), glatiramer acetate, interferon-β, interleukin-2, vaccines
  • Tissue augmentation: bovine collagen, poly-L-lactic acid, mesotherapy

Pathology - Cold Panniculitis

  • Septal/lobular inflammation, especially at dermal-subcutaneous border
  • Lymphocytes, neutrophils, foamy macrophages, poorly developed granulomas
  • Mucin, adipocyte necrosis, microcysts
  • Needle-shaped clefts are NOT observed (unlike SFNN)

Pathology - Lipogranuloma

  • Granulomatous lobular panniculitis with marked fibrosis
  • Numerous round or oval vacuoles of various sizes in dermis and subcutis ("Swiss cheese" appearance)
  • Nodular aggregates of lymphocytes, plasma cells, eosinophils, macrophages, giant cells
  • Exogenous oils within vacuoles can be identified in frozen sections stained with oil red O

Pathology - Injection-Related Panniculitis

  • Central nidus of subcutaneous inflammation
  • Sometimes fibrosis or polarizing foreign material
  • Special features depending on substance:
    • Povidone: gray-blue material within macrophages
    • Vitamin K: sclerotic lesions
    • Cancer vaccines, heparin: numerous eosinophils
    • Aluminum salts: macrophages with grayish-violaceous granular cytoplasm

Differential Diagnosis

  • Cold panniculitis vs. SFNN: No needle-shaped clefts in cold panniculitis
  • Sclerosing lipogranuloma: large vacuoles in dermis, can be identified with special stains
  • Infected panniculitis: special stains and cultures for organisms
  • Traumatic sclerosing panniculitis can resemble morphea clinically

Treatment

  • Focus on removal of inciting stimulus
  • Eradication of any associated infection
  • Intralesional or systemic corticosteroids to control inflammation
  • Surgical excision for sclerosing lipogranuloma

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Cold panniculitis primarily affects children (Popsicle panniculitis)
  • "Swiss cheese" appearance in sclerosing lipogranuloma
  • Texier's disease: phytonadione (vitamin K) with distinctive lilac border
  • Patient denial of self-injection is common in factitial cases
  • Special stains can identify exogenous oils (oil red O)

Infection-Induced Panniculitis

MIXED PANNICULITIS

Overview

Panniculitis can result from:

  • A distant focus of infection (e.g., erythema nodosum)
  • Or be directly induced by an infectious agent

No gender or race predilection. Involvement of subcutis can result from direct inoculation or septicemia.

Causative Agents

Bacteria

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Group A Streptococcus
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Brucella melitensis
  • Nocardia asteroides
  • Tropheryma whipplei

Mycobacteria

  • M. tuberculosis
  • M. marinum
  • M. avium-intracellulare
  • M. chelonae
  • M. fortuitum
  • M. ulcerans

Other Microorganisms

  • Coxiella burnetii
  • Borrelia burgdorferi
  • Fungi: Blastomyces, Histoplasma, Microsporum, Candida, Cryptococcus, Aspergillus, Mucor, Fusarium
  • Helminths & protozoa: Nematodes (Gnathostoma), Trematodes (Fasciola, Schistosoma)

Pathology

  • Mixed septal/lobular panniculitis
  • Neutrophilic infiltration, vascular proliferation, hemorrhage
  • Necrosis involving lipocytes, inflammatory cells, and eccrine sweat coils
  • Special staining and culture studies provide definitive diagnosis
  • Q fever (Coxiella burnetii): "doughnut-like" granulomatous lobular panniculitis

Differential Diagnosis

  • Pancreatic panniculitis
  • Traumatic panniculitis
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency panniculitis
  • Erythema nodosum
  • Erythema induratum
  • Ghost cells: differentiate from mucormycosis and granular calcium deposits

Treatment

  • Antimicrobial therapy tailored to identified organism
  • Empirical broad-spectrum coverage in severely ill patients
  • Surgery may be indicated for isolated lesions caused by grain-forming fungi or bacteria (mycetoma, botryomycosis)

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Specific stains and cultures are essential for diagnosis
  • "Doughnut-like" granulomas suggestive of Q fever
  • Can result from distant focus or direct inoculation
  • Treatment must be targeted to specific organism
  • Ghost cells in infectious panniculitis vs. ghost cells in pancreatic panniculitis

Subcutaneous Panniculitis-like T-Cell Lymphoma (SPTCL)

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Overview

  • Less than 1% of all cutaneous T-cell lymphomas
  • Cytotoxic T-cell lymphoma characterized by primarily subcutaneous infiltrates
  • Small, medium-sized, or large pleomorphic T cells with α/β+ T-cell phenotype
  • Associated with many macrophages

Clinical Features

  • α/β T-cell phenotype (CD8+ T-cells)
  • Predilection for legs
  • Solitary or multiple nodules
  • May be associated with systemic symptoms (fever, hemophagocytic syndrome)
  • Solitary or multiple nodules or deeply seated plaques
  • Primary sites: extremities and trunk
  • Systemic symptoms in >50% of patients: fever, fatigue, weight loss
  • Cytopenias and elevated liver function tests are common
  • Rarely ulcerates (vs. primary cutaneous γ/δ T-cell lymphoma)

Pathology

  • Lobular panniculitis
  • Pleomorphic T cells with hyperchromatic nuclei admixed with many macrophages
  • Necrosis, karyorrhexis, and cytophagocytosis
  • Immunophenotype: TCR-α/β+, CD3+, CD4−, CD8+
  • Expression of cytotoxic proteins
  • Neoplastic T cells express βF1 and are negative for CD56
  • Characteristic finding: rimming of fat cells by neoplastic T-cells

Treatment

  • Conservative immunosuppressive regimens may be effective and should initially be considered:
    • Prednisone
    • Cyclosporine
  • For solitary lesions: local radiotherapy may be effective
  • More aggressive cases: doxorubicin-based chemotherapy

Prognosis

  • Generally favorable (>80% 5-year survival)
  • Much better than primary cutaneous gamma/delta T-cell lymphoma (<10% 5-year survival)

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • Critical differential diagnosis: Lupus panniculitis (most important distinction)
  • Characteristic "rimming" of adipocytes by neoplastic T cells
  • α/β T-cell phenotype vs γ/δ T-cell phenotype (different prognosis and treatment)
  • CD56 negative in SPTCL (vs CD56+ in PCGD-TCL)
  • Survival much better in SPTCL compared to PCGD-TCL

Cytophagic Histiocytic Panniculitis

LOBULAR PANNICULITIS

Definition & Overview

  • Subcutaneous nodules or plaques with histiocytic infiltrates containing erythrocytes, lymphocytes, and/or karyorrhectic debris (hemophagocytosis)
  • Macrophages with cytophagia: "bean bag" cells
  • Most cases associated with lymphoma (lymphoid cells within pannicula)
    • γ/δ T-cell lymphoma
    • EBV-associated extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma, nasal type

Pathogenesis

Mutations in genes (including perforin) can predispose to hemophagocytic syndrome

Clinical Features

  • Subcutaneous nodules with purpura
  • May be accompanied by systemic hemophagocytic syndrome

Pathology

  • Macrophages engaged in cytophagic activity
  • "Bean bag cells" containing phagocytosed erythrocytes and other cells

Treatment & Prognosis

Directed at underlying lymphoma or hemophagocytic syndrome

🔍 EXAM FOCUS

For the board exam, remember:

  • "Bean bag cells" are pathognomonic
  • Most cases represent subcutaneous manifestation of lymphoma
  • High index of suspicion for underlying hematolymphoid malignancy
  • Assess for systemic hemophagocytic syndrome

Unusual or Newly Described Forms of Panniculitis

Clinical features: Red nodules, sometimes with necrosis; may have drainage; legs and elsewhere

Pathology: Necrotizing and/or granulomatous panniculitis; calcium oxalate crystals in subcutaneous vessels; deposits of sodium urate crystals

Notes: Oxalosis: primary (inherited) or secondary (chronic renal failure); Urate deposition may be isolated cutaneous finding in gout

Clinical features: Sudden onset of painful erythematous nodules; soles > palms; self-limited

Pathology: Neutrophilic eccrine hidradenitis

Notes: Precipitated by trauma (mechanical, thermal); Mimicked by erythema nodosum, pseudomonas hot-foot syndrome, cold panniculitis

Clinical features: Erythematous nodules that may be painful; extremities; associated with leukemias (AML, hairy cell leukemia), treatment of acute promyelocytic leukemia with all-trans-retinoic acid, and, rarely, solid tumors

Pathology: Predominantly neutrophilic lobular panniculitis, without vasculitis; immature myeloid forms have been described

Notes: Must exclude other neutrophil-rich panniculitides (infection-induced, traumatic, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, pancreatic)

Notes: Triggered by non-tuberculous infections; underlying disorders include atopy, antiphospholipid antibody syndrome

  • Plasma cell panniculitis: Hyperpigmentation and induration; trunk and extremities; sclerosis of subcutaneous septa; plasma cells prominent component of infiltrate
  • Panniculitis of Lyme disease: Tender nodules or diffuse fasciitis; fever, chills, photophobia, polyarthritis; acute septal panniculitis (may contain eosinophils, plasma cells); lymphocytic vasculopathy
  • Fasciitis-panniculitis syndrome: Subcutaneous induration, sometimes with regional pain; chronic inflammation, accompanied by replacement of fat lobules by fibrous tissue; thickening of fascia

Step 2: Vasculitis?

Step 1: Determine the Pattern

  • Septal: EN, morphea, necrobiosis lipoidica
  • Lobular: EI, lupus panniculitis, pancreatic, α1-antitrypsin, fat necrosis of newborn
  • Mixed: Lipodermatosclerosis, some infective panniculitis

Step 2: Is Vasculitis Present?

  • With vasculitis: EI, PAN, some infectious cases
  • Without vasculitis: EN, lupus panniculitis, pancreatic panniculitis, α1-antitrypsin

Step 3: Look for Special Features

  • Ghost cells: Pancreatic panniculitis
  • Needle-shaped clefts: Subcutaneous fat necrosis, sclerema neonatorum, post-steroid
  • Lymphoid follicles: Lupus panniculitis
  • Lipomembranous changes: Lipodermatosclerosis
  • Rimming of adipocytes: SPTCL
  • Dense neutrophilic infiltration: α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Miescher's granulomas: Erythema nodosum

Step 4: Consider the Location

  • Shins: Erythema nodosum
  • Calves: Erythema induratum
  • Face/cheeks (adults): Lupus panniculitis
  • Face/cheeks (infants): Cold panniculitis, subcutaneous fat necrosis
  • Lower legs with venous insufficiency: Lipodermatosclerosis
  • Buttocks/thighs: α1-antitrypsin deficiency, pancreatic panniculitis

Step 5: Incorporate Clinical Information

  • Ulceration: Erythema induratum, infection, α1-antitrypsin
  • No ulceration: Erythema nodosum, lupus panniculitis
  • Oily discharge: Pancreatic panniculitis
  • Inverted bottle appearance: Lipodermatosclerosis
  • Recent steroid withdrawal: Post-steroid panniculitis
  • Abdominal symptoms: Consider pancreatic disease
  • Respiratory symptoms: Consider α1-antitrypsin (emphysema)
  • History of venous disease: Lipodermatosclerosis
