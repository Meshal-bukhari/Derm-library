Mycobacterial Diseases

High-Yield Board Review

Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

🧠 Memory Hooks

  • DR for TT: HLA-DR2 & HLA-DR3 associated with Tuberculoid Leprosy
  • DQ for LL: HLA-DQ1 associated with Lepromatous Leprosy
  • RD for PB: Rifampin + Dapsone for Paucibacillary Leprosy
  • RDC for MB: Rifampin + Dapsone + Clofazimine for Multibacillary
  • Th1 for TT: Strong Th1 response in Tuberculoid
  • Th2 for LL: Stronger Th2 response in Lepromatous

Classification & Immunology: The Spectrum

TT (Tuberculoid) Pole
  • Strong Cell-Mediated Immunity (CMI)
  • Robust Th1 response
  • Granulomas++
  • Few bacilli (paucibacillary)
  • Positive lepromin test (Mitsuda reaction)
  • HLA-DR2 & HLA-DR3 association
LL (Lepromatous) Pole
  • Weak Cell-Mediated Immunity
  • Th2 humoral response predominates
  • Lots of bacilli in macrophages (Virchow cells)
  • Negative lepromin test
  • HLA-DQ1 association

These are the in-between stages, reflecting varying levels of immunity:

  • BT: Closer to TT pole
  • BB: Middle of spectrum ("borderline borderline")
  • BL: Closer to LL pole

Clinical Features

Tuberculoid Leprosy (TT) Lepromatous Leprosy (LL)
  • Single or Few Lesions (1-3)
  • Annular Plaques
  • Hypopigmented
  • Anesthetic (key feature!)
  • Sharply Demarcated, Raised Borders
  • Alopecia within lesions
  • Multiple Lesions (numerous)
  • Diffuse Infiltration, symmetrical
  • Varied morphology (macules, papules, nodules)
  • Less Anesthesia (early)
  • Leonine Facies (lion-like appearance)

Thickened Nerves - Key sites to remember:

  • Ulnar nerve at the elbow
  • Common peroneal nerve at fibular head
  • Greater auricular nerve in neck
  • Posterior tibial nerve behind medial malleolus

Results in sensory and motor deficits along nerve distribution.

Treatment

Type Forms Duration Drugs
Paucibacillary (PB) TT, BT, indeterminate 6 months (WHO)
1 year (US)		 RD for PB
Rifampin (monthly) + Dapsone (daily)
Multibacillary (MB) LL, BL, BB 12 months (WHO)
2 years (US)		 RDC for MB
Rifampin + Dapsone + Clofazimine

📌 Household Contact Risk

  • ~20-25% increased risk for household contacts
  • WHO recommends single-dose Rifampin prophylaxis in endemic areas

Leprosy Reactions

Type 1 Reaction (Reversal)

Think: CMI "Upgrading"

  • Immunology: Increased Th1 response
  • Timing: Before, during, or after treatment
  • Clinical: Existing lesions become inflamed, edematous
  • Neuritis: Common - nerve pain, tenderness
  • Management: Steroids (prednisone)
Type 2 Reaction (ENL)

Think: Immune Complex Deposition

  • Immunology: Th2 response, immune complex formation
  • Timing: Common in LL and BL during MB treatment
  • Clinical: New painful erythematous nodules
  • Systemic: Fever, malaise, neuritis, iridocyclitis
  • Management: Thalidomide (primary), Steroids

Cutaneous Tuberculosis

🧠 Classification by Route

  • Re-infection (Exogenous): TBVC - "Warty TB" - direct inoculation in previously exposed
  • Endogenous: Lupus Vulgaris, Scrofuloderma
  • Primary Inoculation: Tuberculous Chancre in TB naïve individuals
  • Tuberculids: "Ide" hypersensitivity reactions

Clinical Presentations

  • Chronic progressive plaque
  • "Apple Jelly" Nodules (hallmark) - seen on diascopy
  • Head and neck predilection (face, nose, ears)
  • "Cold Abscess" - firm subcutaneous nodule becoming fluctuant
  • Multiple draining sinuses
  • Overlying infected nodes/bone (usually cervical)
  • Keloid scarring after healing
  • Verrucous (wart-like) hyperkeratotic plaque
  • Extremities (hands, feet, trauma sites)
  • "Prosector's wart" in pathologists
  • Good immunity = Paucibacillary
  • Painless papulonodule progressing to ulcer
  • Regional lymphadenopathy (forming a primary complex)
  • Rare, except in Asia

Papulonecrotic Tuberculid:

  • Symmetric dusky red papules on extensor surfaces
  • Central necrosis, may ulcerate

Erythema Induratum (of Bazin):

  • Subcutaneous nodules on calves
  • Nodules may ulcerate
  • Women predominantly affected

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Method Features
Histopathology
  • Granulomatous inflammation (hallmark)
  • Caseation necrosis (variable)
  • Epithelioid histiocytes & Langhans giant cells
AFB Stain
  • Often negative in paucibacillary forms (LV, tuberculids)
  • More likely positive in scrofuloderma, chancre
Mantoux & IGRA
  • Positive in most forms (except early chancre)
  • IGRA preferred in BCG-vaccinated individuals
Culture
  • Gold standard for definitive diagnosis
  • Slow growing - inform lab to culture for Mycobacteria
PCR
  • Rapid and sensitive
  • Useful when AFB/culture negative
  • Can be used on paraffin-embedded tissue

Treatment

📌 Standard Anti-TB Therapy (ATT)

Intensive Phase (2 months): HRZE - Isoniazid, Rifampin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol

Continuation Phase (4+ months): HR - Isoniazid, Rifampin

⚠️ Extended treatment (6-9 months) often needed for cutaneous TB

Non-Tuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM)

🧠 Common Species & Exposures

  • M. marinum: "Fish Tank Granuloma" - aquatic exposure
  • M. ulcerans: "Buruli Ulcer" - endemic water exposure
  • RGM: M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus - post-procedural, medical devices

Clinical Presentations

Species Presentation Key Features
M. marinum Fish Tank Granuloma
  • Sporotrichoid pattern (ascending lesions along lymphatics)
  • Extremities (often hands)
  • Aquarium or swimming pool exposure
M. ulcerans Buruli Ulcer
  • Painless, deep, undermined ulcer
  • Starts as nodule
  • Mycolactone toxin causes tissue destruction
  • Endemic in Africa, Australia, Asia
RGM
(M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus)		 Post-procedural infections
  • Post-trauma or procedure history
  • Pustules, abscesses, nodules
  • Surgery, injection, tattoo sites
  • M. fortuitum in nail salon exposures

Diagnosis

  • Consider NTM in non-healing granulomatous lesions with relevant exposure
  • Histopathology: Granulomas, neutrophilic abscesses with RGM
  • AFB Stain: Often positive, but not always
  • Culture on specialized media: Essential for species ID and susceptibility
  • Important: Inform lab you suspect NTM! Different growth requirements
  • PCR & 16S rRNA Sequencing: Rapid identification

Treatment

📌 Macrolide-Based Regimens

Foundation: Clarithromycin or Azithromycin

Combination with:

  • Rifabutin, Ethambutol
  • Quinolones (Moxifloxacin, Ciprofloxacin)
  • Amikacin, Linezolid, Doxycycline, TMP-SMX

Other considerations:

  • Surgical excision for localized lesions
  • Prolonged treatment (3-6+ months)
  • Susceptibility testing essential for RGM

Anti-Tuberculosis Drug Side Effects

🧠 First-Line TB Drugs: HRZE

  • H: Isoniazid (INH)
  • R: Rifampin (RIF)
  • Z: Pyrazinamide (PZA)
  • E: Ethambutol (EMB)

Key Side Effects by Drug

Drug Dermatologic Systemic Monitoring
Isoniazid (INH)
  • Rash
  • SJS/TEN (rare)
  • Peripheral Neuropathy
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • LFTs
  • Neurological exam
  • Prevent neuropathy with Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
Rifampin (RIF)
  • Rash
  • Orange discoloration of secretions
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Hepatic enzyme induction
  • Drug interactions
  • LFTs
  • Warn patients about orange secretions
  • Check drug interactions
Pyrazinamide (PZA)
  • Photosensitivity
  • Rash
  • Hyperuricemia (gout)
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • LFTs
  • Uric acid (if gout history)
  • Sun protection
Ethambutol (EMB)
  • Rash
  • Optic Neuritis
  • Color blindness (red-green)
  • Decreased visual acuity
  • Regular vision testing
  • Color vision testing
  • Stop if vision changes

Managing Drug Side Effects

Drug-Induced Hepatitis

Symptoms: RUQ pain, nausea, vomiting, jaundice, fatigue

Labs: Elevated LFTs (AST, ALT, bilirubin)

Management:

  1. Stop all hepatotoxic drugs
  2. After LFTs normalize, reintroduce one by one:
    1. Start with Rifampin
    2. Then add INH
    3. Then add Ethambutol
    4. PZA often avoided if severe hepatitis

⚠️ Serious Reactions Requiring Immediate Drug Discontinuation

  • SJS/TEN
  • Severe hepatitis
  • Optic neuritis
  • Severe neuropathy

Always consult ID/Pulm specialist for management!
