Colloid Milium

A comprehensive study guide for dermatology board exam preparation

📋 Overview

⭐ High-Yield Board Facts

  • Rare degenerative condition related to sun exposure
  • Characterized by multiple domed, skin-colored papules on sun-exposed skin
  • Histologically: deposition of homogeneous eosinophilic colloid in papillary dermis
  • Four clinical variants: adult, juvenile, pigmented, and nodular
  • No specific medical treatment; physical modalities may help
[Clinical Image: Multiple domed, skin-colored papules on sun-exposed skin]

👨‍👦 Clinical Variants

Type Epidemiology Clinical Features Notes
Adult Most common; 4:1 male preponderance Waxy, translucent, amber or skin-colored, firm papules (1-5 mm) Most frequently encountered form
Juvenile Very rare; prepubertal onset Waxy, translucent, amber or skin-colored, firm papules (1-5 mm) Autosomal dominant or recessive inheritance; abnormal keratinocyte susceptibility to UV damage
Nodular Subset of adult type Nodules or plaques, skin-colored to yellow, larger (5-10 mm) Predominantly on face
Pigmented Subset of adult type Grouped gray-brown papules, usually on face Associated with hydroquinone exposure (exogenous ochronosis)

💡 Clinical Pearl

One diagnostic pearl is that gelatinous material can be expressed from papules using firm pressure.

🧪 Etiology & Pathophysiology

Primary Causes

  • Ultraviolet (UV) light exposure (primary factor)
  • History of severe sunburns
  • Keratinocyte susceptibility to UV damage (juvenile form)

Chemical Exposures

  • Petroleum products
  • Phenols (reported in oil refinery workers)
  • Hydroquinone (contributes to pigmented form via exogenous ochronosis)

📝 Board Exam Focus

Remember to ask about industrial exposures and hydroquinone use when presented with a case suspicious for colloid milium, particularly the pigmented variant.

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Distribution

Lesions occur in crops over sun-exposed areas, with predilection for:

  • Cheeks
  • Nose
  • Periorbital area
  • Ears
  • Neck
  • Dorsal hands
  • Forearms
  • Upper eyelids (can occur in isolation)

Natural History

Lesions tend to reach a peak within 3 years, after which few new papules occur. Important for board exams: lesions do not resolve spontaneously and occasionally may be pruritic.

🔬 Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on:

  • Characteristic appearance of waxy papules
  • Photodistribution
  • History of UV exposure or chemical exposure
  • Expression of gelatinous material with pressure

Histopathology

Biopsy will demonstrate fissured eosinophilic colloid masses in the papillary dermis - this is the pathognomonic finding!

[Histopathology Image: Eosinophilic colloid masses in papillary dermis]

⚖️ Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiating Features
Amyloidosis Different histopathology; amyloid stains with Congo red
Sarcoidosis Granulomatous inflammation on histology; systemic involvement
Sebaceous hyperplasia Central umbilication; yellowish color
Trichoepithelioma Skin-colored papules/nodules; follicular differentiation on histology
Syringomas Small, flesh-colored papules; ductal differentiation on histology
Tuberous sclerosis (adenoma sebaceum) Typically presents in late childhood/early adolescence; other features of TS
Favre-Racouchot disease More like open comedones on malar prominence and lateral ocular areas
Milia More scattered (nonconfluent), smaller, yellow to white papules

💊 Management

Key Board Concept: There is no specific medical treatment for colloid milium.

Prevention

  • Sun protection/avoidance (although no literature supports efficacy for existing lesions)
  • Avoid chemical exposures (petroleum, phenols, hydroquinone)

Treatment Options

Physical Methods

  • Dermabrasion
  • Cryotherapy
  • Diathermy

Laser & Light Therapies

  • Er:YAG laser
  • Fractionated CO₂ laser
  • Intense pulsed light (IPL)

Other Modalities

  • Photodynamic therapy with methyl aminolevulinate

💡 Special Consideration in Darker Skin

Hypopigmentation following cryotherapy is more prominent, frequent, and long-lasting in darker skin colors. Exercise caution with this modality and discuss this potential side effect with patients.

⚠️ Drug Reactions

Hydroquinone exposure has been associated with colloid milium, particularly the pigmented variant, through the mechanism of exogenous ochronosis.

📚 Board Exam Preparation

Self-Assessment Questions

Q1: What is the histopathological hallmark of colloid milium?

Fissured eosinophilic colloid masses in the papillary dermis.

Q2: Which chemical exposure is specifically associated with the pigmented variant of colloid milium?

Hydroquinone (via mechanism of exogenous ochronosis).

Q3: What is the male:female ratio in adult colloid milium?

4:1 male preponderance.

Q4: What is the natural course of colloid milium lesions?

Lesions reach peak within 3 years, after which few new papules appear. Importantly, they do not resolve spontaneously.

Q5: Which form of colloid milium is thought to have genetic inheritance patterns?

Juvenile colloid milium (autosomal dominant or recessive inheritance patterns).

⭐ High-Yield Summary

  1. 4 clinical variants: adult (most common), juvenile (rare, genetic), nodular, and pigmented
  2. Strong association with sun exposure; also petroleum, phenols, and hydroquinone
  3. Diagnostic pearl: gelatinous material expressible with pressure
  4. Histology: eosinophilic colloid masses in papillary dermis
  5. No medical treatment; physical and laser modalities with limited success
  6. Caution with cryotherapy in darker skin tones due to hypopigmentation risk
