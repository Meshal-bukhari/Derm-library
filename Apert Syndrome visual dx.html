Apert Syndrome: Dermatology Board Review

Contributors: Harold E. Cross MD, PhD, Lauren Patty Daskivich MD, MSHS (Source: VisualDx)

Apert Syndrome: Core Triad & Key Identifiers

Remember Apert by its most striking features often tested:

  • Acrocephaly (Craniosynostosis): Tower-shaped skull due to premature fusion of coronal sutures.
  • Midface Hypoplasia: Flattened midface appearance.
  • Syndactyly: Significant fusion of digits in hands ("mitten hands") and feet ("sock feet").
  • FGFR2 Gene Mutation: Exclusively caused by mutations in this gene. This is a CRITICAL distinction.
  • Chronic Hyperhidrosis: Often a universal cutaneous finding.

Key Visual Clues (VisualDx Snapshot)

  • Craniosynostosis
  • Flat nasal bridge
  • Hypertelorism
  • Chronic hyperhidrosis
  • Broad nail and/or distal digit
  • Syndactyly

The Big Picture: Understanding Apert Syndrome

Apert syndrome is a clinically heterogeneous skeletal disorder. It shares features with, and is allelic to, Crouzon, Pfeiffer, and Jackson-Weiss acrocephalopolysyndactyly syndromes. Clinical severity varies widely:

  • Some patients are mildly affected, appearing almost normal.
  • Others exhibit severe facial deformities.
  • Syndactyly can be present, sometimes to a marked degree.
  • Pre- and postaxial polydactyly may occur.

Progressive Synostosis is a Hallmark: This occurs in hands, cervical vertebrae, carpus, tarsus, and, critically, the skull (leading to acrocephaly). The midface is typically flattened. A small but significant number of individuals experience developmental delay and cognitive impairments.

Clinical Presentation: Spotting Apert Syndrome

A high index of suspicion is key. Combine these findings for a strong diagnostic suggestion:

Craniofacial Features – The "Apert Look"

  • Acrocephaly: The most striking feature. A tall, "tower-like" skull due to craniosynostosis.
  • High Forehead: Contributes to the characteristic skull shape.
  • Flattened Midface: Results in a retruded appearance of the central face.
  • Shallow Orbits: Leads to proptosis.

Ocular Manifestations – More Than Just Appearance

  • Proptosis (Exophthalmos): Bulging eyes due to shallow orbits. Can be severe.
    • Clinical Pearl: Significant proptosis can lead to periodic entrapment of eyelids behind the eyeballs, requiring manual replacement.
  • Apparent Hypertelorism: Eyes appear widely spaced.
  • Strabismus: Often a V-pattern exotropia (outward turning of eyes, worse on upgaze).
  • Downward-Slanting Palpebral Fissures.
  • Interrupted Eyebrows: Gaps or sparsity of hair in eyebrows.
  • Exposure Keratitis: Corneal inflammation due to poor eyelid closure over proptotic eyes.
  • Amblyopia: Present in nearly 20% of patients ("lazy eye").
  • Corneal Scarring & Optic Atrophy: Can result from chronic exposure or other complications.

Limb Anomalies – The Telltale Hands & Feet

  • Syndactyly: Common, but highly variable in severity. Often involves both skin and bone (synostosis).
    • "Mitten Hand" and "Sock Foot": Classic descriptors for severe syndactyly.
    • BOARD ALERT: Fusion of distal phalanges and synonychia (fused nails) can be present in the hands but is NEVER present in the feet.
  • Synpolydactyly: Syndactyly combined with polydactyly. Sometimes used for classification, but not always present.
  • Broad Nail and/or Distal Digit: Often seen, particularly of the thumb and great toe.

Other Skeletal & Systemic Findings

  • Cervical Spine Fusion: Present in ~25% of individuals, most commonly C2-C3.
    • Clinical Implication: Important for pre-anesthesia assessment.
  • Cleft Palate: May be present.

Neurological & Developmental Aspects

  • Brain Anomalies (on Imaging):
    • Ventriculomegaly
    • Defects of the corpus callosum
    • Partial absence of the septum pellucidum (Anomalies here seem to correlate with IQ)
    • Chiari I malformations
    • Hydrocephalus
    • Abnormalities of the semicircular canals
  • Cognitive Function:
    • Developmental delays are common.
    • Intellectual disability is found in a minority.
    • More than 39% of patients have a normal IQ. Other CNS defects (besides septum pellucidum) do not strongly correlate with IQ.
  • Neurosensory Hearing Loss: Sometimes present.

Cutaneous Findings – Key for Dermatologists!

  • Hyperhidrosis (Excessive Sweating): Probably present in ALL patients. A very high-yield point.
  • Oily Skin & Acneiform Lesions: With onset of adolescence, skin becomes excessively oily. Severe, recalcitrant acne may appear on the face, trunk, and upper arms.
  • Eyebrow Gaps (Sparsity of Hair): Apparent gaps where there is a lack of eyebrow hair (connects with "interrupted eyebrows" under ocular findings).

Diagnostic Pearls & Considerations

The combination of acrocephaly (facial features) and synpolydactyly (digital deformities) strongly suggests an acrocephalopolysyndactyly syndrome, like Apert.

However, classifying the specific type and syndrome is challenging due to:

  • Overlapping features among related syndromes.
  • Significant clinical variation even within Apert syndrome.

No Unequivocal Diagnostic Criteria: Diagnosis relies heavily on clinical pattern recognition, supported by genetic testing.

Differential Diagnosis & Pitfalls: Distinguishing Apert

Craniosynostosis is a feature of over 150 heritable conditions. The specific pattern of synostoses combined with hand/foot deformities narrows the field to acrocephalopolysyndactyly syndromes.

The Major Challenge: Discerning which specific type of acrocephalopolysyndactyly a patient has. Members of this group (Apert, Crouzon, Pfeiffer, Jackson-Weiss) are allelic and have overlapping features.

Key Distinctions – Focus on Genetics!

  • Apert Syndrome: Caused SOLELY by mutations in FGFR2. Diagnostic certainty for Apert syndrome is achieved when mutations are found in FGFR2.
  • Crouzon, Pfeiffer, Jackson-Weiss Syndromes:
    • Also allelic with Apert and *can* have FGFR2 mutations.
    • However, these conditions can *also* result from mutations in other genes (e.g., FGFR1, FGFR3), allowing for genetic differentiation.

Comparing Specific Syndromes:

Syndrome Key Differentiating Features from Apert Common Gene(s)
Apert Syndrome Severe symmetric syndactyly (osseous/cutaneous "mitten hand/sock foot"), acne, hyperhidrosis. Intellectual disability more common than in Crouzon. FGFR2 (exclusive)
Crouzon Syndrome Craniosynostosis, midface hypoplasia, proptosis. Typically normal hands and feet. Intelligence usually normal. FGFR2, (rarely FGFR3 - Crouzonodermoskeletal)
Pfeiffer Syndrome Craniosynostosis, broad, medially deviated thumbs and great toes, variable syndactyly (usually milder than Apert). Three clinical subtypes with varying severity. FGFR1, FGFR2
Jackson-Weiss Syndrome Craniosynostosis, midface hypoplasia, foot anomalies (broad great toes, tarsal synostosis). Hand anomalies less severe or absent. Intelligence usually normal. FGFR2
Saethre-Chotzen Syndrome Coronal synostosis (often asymmetric leading to plagiocephaly), ptosis, low frontal hairline, small ears, mild syndactyly (2-3 fingers). Often short stature, radioulnar synostosis, hallux valgus (C). TWIST1 (most common), FGFR2, FGFR3 (rare)
Carpenter Syndrome Craniosynostosis (cloverleaf or turribrachycephaly), polysyndactyly (preaxial in feet, postaxial in hands), obesity, congenital heart disease, intellectual disability. Autosomal Recessive inheritance. RAB23, MEGF8
Pitfall Reminder: Clinical overlap is significant! Genetic testing is crucial for definitive classification within the acrocephalopolysyndactyly group.

Best Tests: Confirming the Diagnosis

  • Clinical Experience: Remains highly valuable for initial suspicion and guiding workup in acrocephalopolysyndactyly syndromes.
  • FGFR2 Gene Sequencing:
    • Finding mutations in FGFR2 confirms the patient's diagnosis belongs to the group of conditions including Apert, Crouzon, Pfeiffer, and Jackson-Weiss.
    • Crucial Point: Apert syndrome is the ONLY acrocephalopolysyndactyly syndrome caused SOLELY by mutations in FGFR2. Specific mutations (S252W and P253R) account for >98% of Apert cases.
  • Genetic Panel for Craniosynostosis Syndromes: May be used if clinical picture is unclear, to test for FGFR1, FGFR3, TWIST1, RAB23 etc.

Genetic Pattern:

  • Inheritance: Autosomal dominant.
  • Occurrence: Most cases occur sporadically (new mutations).
  • Paternal Age Effect: Increased risk with advanced paternal age.
  • Prenatal Diagnosis: Possible via ultrasonography (showing characteristic features) and molecular testing if a familial mutation is known or features are highly suggestive.

Management Pearls & Therapeutic Approach

A multidisciplinary team approach is essential, addressing individual deformities and potential complications.

Key Management Goals & Pearls:

  • Early Skull Decompression: Significantly improves final IQ compared with later surgery. Surgical interventions like craniotomy and fronto-orbital advancement may begin as early as 3 months of age and often require staged procedures.
  • Home Environment: Children raised at home have a significantly higher IQ compared with those who are institutionalized.
  • Cervical Spine Imaging: Crucial before general anesthesia due to risk of cervical fusion.
  • Orthopedic Surgery: Can improve hand and digit mobility in select cases with syndactyly and/or polydactyly.
  • Spinal X-rays: Important for early detection of serious spinal deformities.
  • Ocular Care - Aggressive Lubrication: Use eye drops and ointment to maintain proper corneal surface wetting, especially with proptosis.
  • Eyelid Entrapment/Severe Keratitis: May require lateral tarsorrhaphies (partial surgical fusion of eyelids).
  • Monitoring Optic Nerve: Regular checks for optic nerve compression in the optic canal allow for timely intervention to prevent vision loss.

Specific Therapies:

  • Cranial Surgery: Decompression, fronto-orbital advancement.
  • CNS Shunting: For hydrocephalus, if present.
  • Hand and Foot Surgery: Release of syndactyly, management of polydactyly. (e.g., Fearon JA. Plast Reconstr Surg. 2003)
  • Ophthalmologic Management: Strabismus surgery, management of exposure keratitis.
  • Dermatologic Management: For severe acne (e.g., isotretinoin has been used - Gilaberte M, et al. Pediatr Dermatol. 2003), and symptomatic management of hyperhidrosis.
  • Developmental & Educational Support.
  • Hearing Aids / Support: If neurosensory hearing loss is present.
