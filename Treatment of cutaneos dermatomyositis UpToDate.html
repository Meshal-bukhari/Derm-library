Cutaneous Dermatomyositis

A High-Yield Board Review for the Dermatology Resident

High-Yield First: Don't Miss These on Exam Day

Pathognomonic Signs 🎯

  • Gottron's Papules: Violaceous papules over IP/MCP joints.
  • Heliotrope Eruption: Pink-violaceous erythema +/- edema of periorbital skin.

The "Must Screen For" Associations 🚨

  • Internal Malignancy: ~14% in adults with CADM.
  • Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD): ~13% in adults with CADM.
  • (Juvenile DM is NOT associated with these risks).

Key Symptom & DDx Pearl 🔑

Overwhelming Pruritus: Often debilitating, a key feature distinguishing it from lupus, which is typically not as intensely pruritic.

DM vs. Lupus Face Rash: DM rash INVOLVES the nasolabial folds. ACLE malar rash traditionally SPARES them.

Core Treatment Principle 💡

Skin disease is often resistant to therapy. Most patients require systemic agents; photoprotection and topicals alone are rarely sufficient.

The Clinical Picture: Presentation and Classification

Cutaneous Findings

  • Gottron's Sign: Macular violaceous erythema over joints (elbows, knees).
  • "Shawl" and "V-sign": Confluent, violaceous erythema on upper back/shoulders and anterior neck/chest.
  • Scalp: Intensely pruritic, violaceous erythema with prominent scale and diffuse alopecia.
  • Periungual: Telangiectasias and cuticular hypertrophy are common.
  • Photosensitivity: Reduced Minimal Erythema Doses (MEDs) to UVB are common, suggesting UV light as a trigger.
  • Calcinosis Cutis & Panniculitis: Less common but known manifestations.

Impact & Course

  • Quality of Life: Impairment is significant, often greater than in psoriasis or atopic dermatitis due to severe, disabling pruritus and burning.
  • Disease Course: Often chronic, can persist for years. Mean duration of skin disease in one review was 4.5 years.

Classifying Cutaneous DM: Know the Subtypes

Classic Dermatomyositis (DM)

The most common presentation. Features both characteristic cutaneous disease AND muscle weakness. Skin findings can precede myositis by months (~30% of cases).

Term to Know: "Premyopathic DM" = skin findings <6 months, no clinical muscle disease yet.

Clinically Amyopathic DM (CADM)

An umbrella term for patients with characteristic skin findings for at least 6 months WITHOUT clinical muscle weakness. Comprises two subgroups:

  • Amyopathic DM ("DM sine myositis"): No clinical weakness AND no laboratory/radiologic/EMG evidence of myositis.
  • Hypomyopathic DM: No clinical weakness, BUT subclinical evidence of myositis is present (e.g., elevated muscle enzymes, abnormal MRI/EMG/biopsy).
Clinical Pearl: About 9-13% of patients with CADM for >6 months will eventually develop classic DM. Watch them closely!
Postmyopathic Dermatomyositis

Describes the common clinical scenario where muscle disease resolves with immunosuppressive therapy, but cutaneous symptoms persist.

Patient Evaluation: Workup & Diagnosis

Skin Biopsy: When & Why?

Perform a skin biopsy if:

  • Cutaneous findings are ambiguous.
  • Skin findings suggest DM but there are no signs of muscle disease (to confirm CADM).

Typical Histopathology:

  • Interface dermatitis (vacuolar change).
  • Superficial lymphocytic infiltrate.
  • Dermal mucin.

⚠️ Exam Trap: Histopathology is indistinguishable from acute/subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (ACLE/SCLE).

Essential Investigations

For any patient with cutaneous findings of DM, you must investigate for associated systemic disease.

  • Myopathy Screen:
    • Muscle examination.
    • Serum creatinine kinase (CK) and aldolase.
    • Follow-up: If no myositis initially, repeat labs every 2-3 months.
  • Malignancy Screen: Age- and sex-appropriate screening is crucial in adults.
  • Pulmonary Disease Screen: Evaluate for ILD, especially in CADM patients.

Treatment Strategy: A Step-by-Step Approach

Foundational Therapy for ALL Patients

☀️ Aggressive Photoprotection

Broad-spectrum SPF 30+, reapply q3-4h, sun-protective clothing. Consider Vitamin D supplementation.

♨️ Antipruritic Agents

Treat debilitating pruritus. Sedating antihistamines (hydroxyzine, doxepin) or gabapentin are often needed. Non-sedating are not helpful.

🧴 Topical Therapies

High-potency steroids for trunk/extremities. Low-potency for face. Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) are steroid-sparing options, especially for the face.

The Systemic Armamentarium: Drug Pearls

Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ)

Role: First-line for mild cutaneous DM.

Dosing Pearl: ≤ 5 mg/kg/day (based on real body weight) to minimize retinopathy risk.

Time to Effect: Slow onset. Counsel patient that it may take 6-12 weeks. Give a 3-month trial.

🚨 Board Exam Trap

A patient with DM started on HCQ develops a widespread morbilliform rash. What's going on? This is a known adverse effect! Cutaneous drug eruptions to HCQ are surprisingly common in DM (~30% of patients).

Key Monitoring: Baseline and annual ophthalmologic exam.

Pro-Tip: Smoking can decrease efficacy. Encourage cessation.

Methotrexate (MTX)

Role: First-line for severe cutaneous DM, or add-on for HCQ failure in mild disease.

Administration: Given WEEKLY. Can titrate up to 25 mg/week. Onset of action is 2-3 months at target dose.

Dosing Pearl: Consider splitting oral doses >15mg/week to improve absorption.

Supplementation: Prescribe Folic Acid 1mg daily to reduce side effects (GI upset, stomatitis).

🚨 Board Exam Trap

A patient with DM has concomitant ILD. Why is MTX a poor choice for their skin disease? MTX carries a risk of pulmonary toxicity/fibrosis and is a relative contraindication in patients with pre-existing significant lung disease.

Key Monitoring: Baseline and periodic CBC, LFTs, renal function. Screen for Hep B/C. Teratogenic.

Quinacrine

Role: Add-on therapy to HCQ for patients with an insufficient response.

Dosing: Typically 100 mg per day.

Key Advantage: Does NOT cause retinopathy, so it can be safely combined with HCQ.

Common Side Effect: Reversible yellow discoloration of the skin.

Availability Issue: Must be obtained from a compounding pharmacy in the US.

Chloroquine

Role:

An alternative for patients who fail HCQ, though data for superior efficacy is lacking.

Dosing Pearl: Dose should not exceed 3 mg/kg/day based on ideal body weight.

⚠️ Key Disadvantage: Higher risk of retinopathy compared to hydroxychloroquine. Not a preferred first-line agent.

Final Summary: Core Concepts

  • Diagnosis is Clinical: Based on pathognomonic findings (Gottron's, Heliotrope). Biopsy helps in ambiguous cases but histology mimics lupus. The key clinical differentiator is nasolabial fold involvement.
  • Always Screen: All adult patients need evaluation for myositis, ILD, and internal malignancy.
  • Treatment is Multifaceted: Photoprotection, antipruritics, and topicals are foundational for all, but systemic therapy is almost always required.
  • Treatment is Stratified by Severity:
    • Mild: Start with Hydroxychloroquine.
    • Severe: Start with Methotrexate (+/- HCQ).
  • Key Drug Contraindications: Avoid MTX in significant liver/lung disease. Remember the high rate of skin eruptions with HCQ in DM patients.
  • Patience is Key: Systemic therapies take 2-3 months to show full effect. Counsel your patients accordingly.
