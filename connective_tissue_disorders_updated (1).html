Connective Tissue Disorders Overview

"ECM Trio: EDS breaks, PXE calcifies, Cutis Laxa loses elasticity"
Quick Reference Guide

Key Distinguishing Features:

  • Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome: Skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, tissue fragility
  • Pseudoxanthoma Elasticum: Calcification of elastic fibers, angioid streaks, cardiovascular complications
  • Cutis Laxa: Loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity and resilience

High-Yield Points for Board Exam

  • All three conditions have specific genetic mutations affecting extracellular matrix proteins
  • Distinguish between skin manifestations: hyperextensible (EDS), yellowish papules/plaques (PXE), or loose/sagging (Cutis Laxa)
  • Know key diagnostic features: Beighton score for EDS, angioid streaks for PXE
  • Most dangerous form: Vascular EDS (arterial/bowel ruptures) with median survival of 50 years
  • Life-threatening complications: Pulmonary in Cutis Laxa, Cardiovascular in PXE
  • Tenascin-X deficiency (EDS-Hypermobility type) is associated with Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS)

"Collagen Crisis: Stretched skin, Stretched joints, Stressed vessels"
HIGH YIELD: 2017 International Classification recognizes 13 subtypes of EDS. Know the 6 main types, especially classical, hypermobile (most common), and vascular (most life-threatening).

Pathogenesis

Collagen Structure and Defects
  • Most EDS variants result from disruption of collagen fibrils
  • The collagen family includes 28 distinct proteins
  • Each collagen consists of three α-chain polypeptides
  • There are 45 distinct α-chains
Mnemonic for Collagen Structure: "3-28-45"
  • 3 α-chains per collagen molecule
  • 28 distinct collagen proteins
  • 45 distinct α-chain types

Clinical Assessment

Diagnostic Criteria and Tests
Beighton Scale for Joint Hypermobility

A score of ≥5/9 indicates hypermobility in adults:

  • Passive dorsiflexion of little fingers beyond 90° (1 point for each hand)
  • Passive apposition of thumbs to flexor forearm (1 point for each hand)
  • Hyperextension of knees beyond 10° (1 point for each knee)
  • Hyperextension of elbows beyond 10° (1 point for each elbow)
  • Forward flexion of trunk with knees fully extended and palms flat on floor (1 point)
Skin Hyperextensibility Assessment

Assessed on the volar surface of distal forearm/wrist

Upper limit of normal extensibility: 1-1.5 cm

Other Clinical Signs
  • Gorlin sign: Touching nose tip with tongue (present in 50% of classical EDS vs 10% in normal population)
  • Spheroids: Fat lobules that have calcified after losing blood supply, most commonly found over bony prominences (ulnar and tibial)
  • Atrophic scars: "Fish mouth" or "cigarette paper" widened scars from poor wound healing
Diagnostic Tests
  • Histopathology: Usually noncontributory in routine examination
  • Electron microscopy: Classic EDS shows collagen fibrils with variable diameters and occasional "cauliflower" fibrils
  • Genetic testing: Available for all major forms except hypermobile type
  • Protein electrophoresis of collagens from cultured dermal fibroblasts: Helps demonstrate specific collagen defects (vascular, arthrochalasia, dermatosparaxis)
  • Urine analysis: Increased deoxypyridinoline:pyridinoline ratio for kyphoscoliotic type
  • Serum ELISA: Detects Tenascin-X deficiency

Treatment Approaches

Management Strategies
  • Prevention of trauma to the skin is the most important intervention
  • Delay suture removal for up to twice as long as usual
  • Use sutures with prolonged tensile strength
  • For vascular EDS: Celiprolol (β1-antagonist with partial β2-agonist properties) may reduce arterial dissection risk
  • For pregnant women with vascular EDS (5% risk of fatal uterine/arterial rupture): Unclear if elective C-section reduces risk
CAUTION: Patients with vascular EDS are at high risk for arterial and bowel ruptures - know celiprolol as the medication that may reduce arterial complications.

Diagnostic Approach to Suspected EDS

Step 1: Evaluate Cardinal Features
  • Marked/generalized joint hypermobility vs. limited to digits vs. minimal
  • Skin hyperextensibility
  • Tissue fragility (history of abnormal scarring, easy bruising)
Step 2: Look for Type-Specific Features
Congenital bilateral hip dislocation or recurrent subluxations → Arthrochalasia
Scoliosis at birth, progressive hypotonia, ocular fragility → Kyphoscoliotic
Recurrent joint dislocations and chronic limb joint pain → Hypermobile
Thin translucent skin, arterial/intestinal/uterine fragility → Vascular
Gaping "fish-mouth" wounds from minor trauma → Classical
Step 3: Genetic Testing

Available for all major types except Hypermobile EDS

Pseudoxanthoma Elasticum (PXE)

"ABCC6 Missing: Calcification Kissing Arteries, Eyes, and Skin"
HIGH YIELD: PXE is characterized by calcification of elastic fibers affecting primarily the skin, eyes, and cardiovascular system. Angioid streaks are the classic ocular finding, while "peau d'orange" changes are the most common.

Pathogenesis

Molecular Mechanisms
  • Autosomal recessive inheritance due to mutations in ABCC6 gene
  • Absence of functional ABCC6 results in reduced ATP secretion from hepatocytes
  • Leads to reduced plasma concentrations of mineralization inhibitor – inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi)
  • Serum levels of fetuin A (anti-mineralization protein) are significantly reduced
  • Mineralization-promoting alkaline phosphatase is increased
Mnemonic for PXE Pathogenesis: "ABCC6 FAP"
  • ABCC6 mutation
  • Fetuin A decreased
  • Alkaline phosphatase increased
  • PPi (inorganic pyrophosphate) decreased
Related Conditions
  • ENPP1 mutations: Can cause Generalized Arterial Calcification of Infancy (GACI) or occasionally PXE.
    • Generalized Arterial Calcification of Infancy (GACI):
      • Inheritance: Autosomal Recessive (caused by biallelic mutations in ENPP1).
      • Severity: Usually lethal during the first six months of life.
      • Pathology: Severe arterial calcification and endothelial proliferation leading to myocardial ischemia, hypertension, and heart failure.
      • Features in Survivors (children with ENPP1 mutations who survive may develop):
        • Skin changes of Pseudoxanthoma Elasticum (PXE).
        • Angioid streaks.
        • Hypophosphatemic rickets due to elevated fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23).
  • ABCC6 mutations: Can underlie subset of GACI cases, showing pathogenic overlap
  • γ-glutamyl carboxylase gene (GGCX) mutations: Cause PXE-like skin changes plus deficiency in vitamin K-dependent coagulation factors

Epidemiology

Demographics
  • Autosomal recessive disorder
  • Slight female preponderance
  • Skin changes usually appear during childhood
  • Diagnosis frequently not made until serious systemic/ocular complications develop in 3rd or 4th decade

Clinical Manifestations

Skin Manifestations
  • Thin, yellowish papules begin in flexural areas during 1st or 2nd decade
  • Papules coalesce to form cobblestone-like plaques ("plucked chicken skin" or "cobblestone" appearance)
  • Lateral neck often affected first, followed by antecubital/popliteal fossae, wrists, axillae, and groin
  • Periumbilical area may be affected in multiparous women (distinguish from perforating calcific elastosis)
  • Severe disease: extension to non-flexural sites, "perforating PXE", secondary cutis laxa
  • Mucosal involvement: yellow papules most commonly on lower lip
  • Prominent chin creases
Ocular Manifestations
  • Angioid streaks: Classic finding, may be evident in first decade, found in virtually all patients by age 30
  • Usually asymptomatic, reflect breaks in calcified elastic lamina of Bruch's membrane
  • Can lead to choroidal neovascularization and hemorrhage with subsequent scarring
  • Results in progressive vision loss, especially central fields, after age 40
  • "Peau d'orange" changes: Mottling of retinal pigment epithelium, actually the most common ophthalmologic finding
  • Less common: macular degeneration, optic drusen, "owl's eyes" (paired hyperpigmented spots)
CAUTION: Angioid streaks are characteristic but not pathognomonic of PXE. Also seen in Paget's disease of bone, sickle cell anemia, thalassemia, EDS, lead poisoning, and age-related degeneration.
Cardiovascular Manifestations
  • May result in early death
  • Primarily affects mid-sized arteries, especially of extremities
  • Progressive calcification leads to atheromatous plaques
  • Sequelae: intermittent claudication, loss of peripheral pulses, renovascular hypertension, angina, MI, stroke early in life
  • Calcified gastric and intestinal mucosal vessels have increased propensity for hemorrhage, particularly from stomach
  • Increased prevalence of mitral valve prolapse

Pathology

Histopathologic Features
  • Distorted, fragmented elastic fibers in mid and deep reticular dermis
  • Advanced cases: calcium deposits visible as purple clumps in routine staining
  • Special stains: Verhoeff-van Gieson (elastin) and von Kossa (calcium)
  • In patients without skin manifestations, biopsy of pre-existing scar can confirm diagnosis
  • Normal serum calcium and phosphate levels

Differential Diagnosis

Similar Conditions
  • D-penicillamine-induced PXE-like changes: No calcification of abnormal fibers
  • Saltpeter (potassium nitrate) exposure: Shows calcification
  • Chronic kidney disease: Shows calcification
  • Beta-thalassemia/sickle cell anemia: PXE-like phenotype in up to 20% of patients
  • L-tryptophan-induced eosinophilia myalgia syndrome (historical)
Key Distinction: In secondary PXE-like conditions, ophthalmologic and cardiovascular findings are typically absent, and skin lesions may resolve after cessation of causative exposure.

Management

Multidisciplinary Approach
Skin:
  • No specific treatment available
  • Surgical intervention for excessive skin folds
Eyes - Prevention and Early Detection:
  • Biannual funduscopic examination
  • Regular use of Amsler grid to assess central visual field defects
  • Use of sunglasses
  • Diet/vitamins rich in antioxidants
  • Avoidance of head trauma, heavy straining, and smoking
Eyes - Treatment:
  • Intravitreal injections of VEGF antagonists (bevacizumab, ranibizumab)
  • Laser photocoagulation or photodynamic therapy (high recurrence rates)
  • Macular translocation (for extensive neovascularization)
Cardiovascular - Prevention and Monitoring:
  • Baseline ECG and echocardiogram
  • Annual cardiovascular examination
  • Healthy lifestyle, balanced diet, regular exercise, no smoking
  • Magnesium supplementation (may reduce connective tissue mineralization)
  • Moderate calcium intake
  • Low-dose aspirin if not contraindicated
Cardiovascular - Treatment:
  • Correction of hyperlipidemia and hypertension
  • Low-dose aspirin, pentoxifylline, cilostazol, or clopidogrel for claudication

Cutis Laxa

"Elastic Absence: The Skin That Stretched and Never Returned"
HIGH YIELD: Cutis Laxa is characterized by loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity due to sparse and fragmented elastic fibers. Pulmonary complications are the most serious and can cause significant morbidity and mortality.

Pathogenesis

Mechanisms
Inherited Forms
  • Sparse and fragmented elastic fibers
  • Multiple inheritance patterns: autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked recessive
  • AR forms (most common): Mutations in FBLN5, EFEMP2/FBLN4, LTBP4, ATPase, ATP6V0A2, PYCR1, and ALDH18A1
  • Affects both skin and systemic manifestations
  • PYCR1 mutations can lead to features of both Cutis Laxa and EDS or osteogenesis imperfecta
Acquired Forms
  • Enhanced degradation of elastic fibers is key mechanism
  • Often follows inflammatory skin conditions (drug reactions, urticaria, Sweet syndrome)
  • Release of elastases by inflammatory cells (neutrophils, monocytes, macrophages) leads to proteolytic degradation
  • Marshall syndrome: Cutis Laxa following Sweet syndrome-like neutrophilic dermatosis
  • α1-antitrypsin deficiency may increase susceptibility to proteolysis
D-Penicillamine-Induced

Two mechanisms:

  1. Copper chelation → reduced lysyl oxidase activity
  2. Chemical blockade of allysine and lysine residues that form desmosine crosslinks

Results in CL-like changes, elastosis perforans serpiginosa, and PXE-like findings

Clinical Manifestations

Cutaneous Features
  • Loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity and resilience
  • "Aged" facial appearance with down-slanting palpebral fissures and long philtrum
  • Present at birth in most heritable forms; may develop later in autosomal dominant or acquired variants
  • Usually progressive, though improvement with age noted in ARCL type 2A
  • Distribution often generalized; can be primarily acral in ARCL type 2B or acquired forms
Extracutaneous Manifestations
  • Emphysema - can cause considerable morbidity and mortality
  • Umbilical and inguinal hernias
  • Gastrointestinal and genitourinary tract diverticula
  • Cardiac or arterial abnormalities
  • Skeletal or joint involvement
  • Delayed growth and development
CAUTION: Pulmonary complications are the most serious feature of Cutis Laxa and can cause significant morbidity and mortality.

Acquired Cutis Laxa: Associated Conditions
Skin Disorders:
  • Urticaria and/or angioedema
  • Drug eruptions
  • Sweet syndrome-like eruptions (Marshall syndrome)
  • Arthropod bite reactions
  • Other inflammatory dermatoses (erythema multiforme, dermatitis herpetiformis, etc.)
Extracutaneous Disorders:
  • Rheumatoid arthritis, SLE
  • Nephrotic syndrome
  • Celiac disease
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Monoclonal gammopathy (especially acral CL with plasma cell dyscrasia)
  • Infectious diseases (febrile viral syndromes, Lyme disease, syphilis)
Drug-Induced:
  • Penicillamine
  • Isoniazid
Board Tip: Marshall syndrome refers to Cutis Laxa associated with Sweet syndrome-like neutrophilic dermatosis, occurring primarily in infants and young children.

Pathology and Diagnosis

Histologic Features and Differential
Histopathology:
  • Characteristic feature: sparse and/or fragmented elastic fibers
Differential Diagnosis:
  • Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome: Hyperextensible skin retains elasticity and recoil (except dermatosparaxis type); also shows skin fragility and bruising
  • Mid-dermal elastolysis
  • Anetoderma: Focal areas of flaccid skin (1-2 cm) with loss of elastic tissue; histologically identical to CL but more circumscribed

Other Heritable Connective Tissue Disorders

"Beyond the Big Three: Rare but Board Exam Worthy"

Buschke-Ollendorf Syndrome

Key Features
  • Autosomal Dominant (AD) disorder due to mutation in LEMD3/MAN1 gene → ↑TGF-β signaling
  • Cutaneous Finding: Dermatofibrosis lenticularis disseminata (collagen-type nevus)
  • Symmetric, small, uniform, yellow to skin-colored dermal papules coalescing into plaques
  • Typically appear on buttocks, proximal trunk, and limbs
  • Onset in childhood (typically in 1st year of life)
  • Radiographic Finding: Osteopoikilosis ("spotted bones")
  • Asymptomatic circular densities in carpal bones, tarsal bones, phalanges, pelvis, and epiphyses/metaphyses of long bones
  • Often noted incidentally on plain films (1- to 10-mm round opacities)
Board Tip: Know the association between Buschke-Ollendorf syndrome and osteopoikilosis ("spotted bones"), which is almost pathognomonic.
Histology: Abundant thickened collagen fibers and elastic fibers (often fragmented and clustered into nets)

Congenital Contractural Arachnodactyly (Beals Syndrome)

Key Features
  • Autosomal Dominant (AD) mutation in the fibrillin 2 (FBN2) gene
  • Characteristic Feature: Crumpled ears - the distinguishing feature from Marfan syndrome
  • Marfanoid habitus with arachnodactyly
  • Congenital contractures of small and large joints that usually improve over time
  • Kyphoscoliosis and pectus excavatum
  • Characteristic facial features:
    • High forehead
    • Down-slanting palpebral fissures
    • Hypertelorism
    • Anteverted nostrils
    • Low-set and abnormal auricles
    • Retromicrognathia
    • Short neck
  • Cardiac manifestations: mitral valve prolapse and aortic root dilation

Focal Dermal Hypoplasia (Goltz Syndrome)

Key Features
  • X-linked Dominant (XLD) disorder due to mutations in the PORCN (porcupine) gene (regulator of Wnt signaling proteins)
  • Majority of patients are heterozygous females (90%); males are usually postzygotic mosaics (10%)
  • Cutaneous manifestations:
    • Widely distributed linear/Blaschkoid areas of hypoplasia/atrophy of the skin, with telangiectasias
    • Soft, yellow to reddish-yellow nodular outpouchings caused by herniation of subcutaneous fat through thinned dermis
    • Large cutaneous ulcers that heal with atrophic scarring
    • Red ("raspberry-like") papillomas, favoring lips, anogenital region, larynx and acral skin
    • Thin or absent hair
    • Dystrophic or completely absent nails
  • Skeletal manifestations:
    • Oligodactyly, syndactyly, ectrodactyly (lobster claw deformity), and polydactyly
    • Asymmetric development of skull, pointed mandible
    • Scoliosis, kyphosis, spina bifida occulta
    • Osteopathia striata: vertical striations in long bone metaphyses on X-ray
  • Intelligence usually normal
Mnemonic "FOCAL" for Key Features:
  • Female predominance, X-linked Dominant
  • Osteopathia striata
  • Colobomas (of iris/choroid/retina/optic disc)
  • Aplasia of ectodermal elements
  • Lobster claw deformity
Board Tip: Remember the FOCAL acronym for the key features of Goltz syndrome.

Homocystinuria

Key Features
  • Autosomal Recessive (AR) disorder most commonly due to CBS (cystathionine β-synthase) enzyme deficiency
  • Results in increased plasma homocysteine levels
  • Cutaneous findings:
    • Malar flush
    • Livedo reticularis
    • Diffuse pigmentary dilution
    • Tissue paper-like scars
  • Extracutaneous manifestations:
    • Marfanoid habitus (similar to Marfan syndrome)
    • Lens subluxations (typically downward) - distinguishes from Marfan syndrome where lens displacement is upward
    • Venous and arterial thrombosis - a major cause of mortality
    • Mental retardation
CAUTION: Distinguishing homocystinuria from Marfan syndrome: both have Marfanoid habitus, but lens subluxation is downward in homocystinuria and upward in Marfan syndrome. Homocystinuria patients are also at high risk for thrombosis.

Infantile Systemic Hyalinosis (ISH) / Juvenile Hyaline Fibromatosis (JHF)

Key Features
  • Allelic Autosomal Recessive (AR) diseases caused by mutations in ANTXR2/CMG2 gene (capillary morphogenesis protein-2)
  • ANTXR2 (transmembrane protein) binds to Laminin and Collagen IV
  • ISH presents within first 6 months of life with cutaneous, mucosal, skeletal, and internal organ involvement with death in early childhood
  • JHF presents during early childhood with cutaneous, mucosal, and skeletal/joint involvement only, survival into adulthood
  • Cutaneous manifestations:
    • Thickened skin and hyperpigmentation overlying bony prominences (ISH)
    • Perianal nodules
    • Small pearly papules on ears and face
    • Scalp nodules are characteristic of JHF
  • Musculoskeletal manifestations:
    • Debilitating joint contractures and tumors
    • Osteolytic bone lesions (characteristic of JHF)
  • Normal intelligence
  • Prognosis:
    • ISH: Poor prognosis with death by 2 years of age
    • JHF: Survival into adulthood (often death by 4th decade)

Lipoid Proteinosis (Urbach-Wiethe Disease)

Key Features
  • Autosomal Recessive (AR) disorder due to mutations in the extracellular matrix protein 1 (ECM1) gene
  • Higher prevalence in South Africa
  • Thickening of basement membrane and deposition of hyaline material in dermis
  • Hoarse cry or weak cry from infiltrated vocal cords is the first clinical sign (occurs in infancy and persists for life)
  • Cutaneous lesions develop during first 2 years of life in two overlapping stages:
    • First stage: vesicles and hemorrhagic crusts that resolve with "ice-pick" scars
    • Second stage: yellow, waxy, coalescing papules/nodules on the face/neck and extremities
    • Beaded eyelid papules resembling "string of pearls" (50%)
    • Verrucous nodules on elbows/knees/hands
  • Oral manifestations:
    • Thickened oral mucosa
    • Infiltration of mucosa by yellow papules/plaques
    • Thickened "woody" tongue with inability to protrude
  • Neurologic manifestations: seizures, neuropsychiatric symptoms associated with pathognomonic sickle or "bean-shaped" calcifications in temporal lobes or hippocampus/amygdala
Board Tip: The combination of hoarse cry since infancy and "bean-shaped" calcifications in temporal lobes is virtually pathognomonic for lipoid proteinosis.

Marfan Syndrome (MFS)

Key Features
  • Autosomal Dominant (AD) mutations in FBN1 gene (encoding fibrillin-1)
  • Manifestations may not present until adolescence or later
  • Cutaneous findings:
    • Striae on upper chest, arms, thighs, and abdomen
    • Lack of subcutaneous fat
    • Increased risk of elastosis perforans serpiginosa
  • Skeletal manifestations:
    • Tall with long extremities (marfanoid habitus)
    • Arm span greater than height
    • Arachnodactyly
    • Kyphoscoliosis, pectus excavatum, dolichocephaly
  • Ocular manifestations:
    • Ectopia lentis (upward lens displacement) in 60% of patients
    • Ocular globe elongation leading to myopia (~40%)
    • Retinal detachment, cataracts, glaucoma
  • Cardiovascular manifestations (70%):
    • Dilatation of ascending aorta → regurgitation, CHF, dissection/aneurysm, rupture
    • Mitral valve prolapse
    • Left ventricular dilation
CAUTION: The key difference between Marfan syndrome and homocystinuria is the direction of lens subluxation - upward in Marfan and downward in homocystinuria.
