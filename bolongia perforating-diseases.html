Perforating Diseases of the Skin

High-Yield Review for Saudi Board Exam
Introduction & Key Features

Definition: Group of papulonodular skin disorders characterized by transepidermal elimination of dermal connective tissue through the epidermis.

Common Features: Papules or nodules with keratotic plugs or crusts.

Prototypic Primary Perforating Diseases:

  • Reactive Perforating Collagenosis (RPC): Primarily collagen fibers extruded.
  • Elastosis Perforans Serpiginosa (EPS): Primarily elastic fibers extruded.
  • Acquired Perforating Dermatosis: Often associated with diabetes mellitus and/or chronic kidney disease.

💡 Mnemonic: "PEC"

Perforating Diseases: Papules with Plugs

Elastosis Perforans Serpiginosa: Elastic fibers

Collagenosis: Collagen fibers

Clinical Pearl: Why "Perforating"?

The term "transepidermal elimination" is more accurate, as dermal connective tissue doesn't actively perforate the epithelium. The epithelium becomes hyperplastic and surrounds the abnormal connective tissue, similar to how it would with foreign bodies like wood splinters.

Detailed Clinical Features

Reactive Perforating Collagenosis (RPC)

Types: Familial (childhood onset, rare) and Acquired (adult onset, associated with:

  • Diabetes mellitus (often with nephropathy)
  • Chronic kidney disease (Stages 4‑5) / end‑stage renal disease → hemodialysis & uremic pruritus
  • Endocrine: Hypothyroidism, Hyperparathyroidism (reported but usually alongside CKD/diabetes)
)

Pathogenesis: Superficial trauma → keratotic papules

Clinical Features:

  • Erythematous papules with a central scale-crust core
  • Size: 5-8mm
  • Develop over 3-4 weeks following trauma
  • Koebner phenomenon common (lesions at sites of trauma)
  • Predilection for arms and hands
  • Spontaneously resolve over 6-10 weeks

Variant: Verrucous perforating collagenoma - very rare non-familial variant where severe trauma results in verrucous papules with transepidermal elimination of collagen

Note: Acquired RPC beginning in adulthood is usually associated with diabetes mellitus and/or chronic kidney disease, and is best classified as acquired perforating dermatosis

Elastosis Perforans Serpiginosa (EPS)

Onset: Childhood or early adulthood

Associated Conditions:

  • 40% of cases associated with genetic disorders:
    • Down syndrome
    • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
    • Osteogenesis imperfecta
    • Marfan syndrome
    • Pseudoxanthoma elasticum (PXE)
    • Rothmund-Thomson syndrome
    • Acrogeria
  • Penicillamine treatment (especially for Wilson disease, cystinuria, rheumatoid arthritis)

Clinical Features:

  • Keratotic 2-5mm papules arranged in serpiginous or annular pattern
  • Predilection for lateral neck
  • Also seen on face, arms, other flexural areas
  • Rings may reach several centimeters in diameter
  • Usually asymptomatic or mildly pruritic
  • May spontaneously resolve but generally persist for several years

Special Note: Penicillamine-induced EPS is due to disruption of desmosine cross-links in elastin

Acquired Perforating Dermatosis

Definition: Catch-all term for perforating diseases arising in adults, usually associated with diabetes mellitus and/or the pruritus of chronic kidney disease (stages 4-5)

Epidemiology:

  • Most common of the perforating disorders
  • Affects approximately 4.5-11% of dialysis patients
  • Most patients have diabetes mellitus with associated nephropathy

Risk Factors in Dialysis Patients:

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Reduced circulating levels of intact parathyroid hormone
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Elevated serum levels of high-sensitivity C-reactive protein

Other Associations:

  • Other causes of pruritus (insect bites, scabies, lymphoma, hepatobiliary diseases)
  • Healing herpes zoster
  • Various medications:
    • TNF inhibitors
    • Epidermal growth factor receptor inhibitors
    • Kinase inhibitors
    • Dipeptidyl peptidase-4 inhibitors
    • Antivirals
    • Monoclonal antibodies

Clinical Features:

  • Most commonly on legs but can be generalized
  • Extensor surfaces favored over flexures
  • Mucous membranes spared
  • Hyperpigmented papules and nodules with central keratotic core (often removed by patient)
  • Occasionally giant variant (up to 2cm)
  • Koebner phenomenon can occur (linear arrangement)

Note on Terminology: "Kyrle disease" sometimes used synonymously but controversial; some consider it to represent end-stage, excoriated prurigo nodules or folliculitis

Perforating Calcific Elastosis

Demographics: Middle-aged, obese, hypertensive, multiparous Black women

Clinical Features:

  • Plaques that favor the abdomen, especially periumbilical region
  • May involve breast in patients with chronic kidney disease
  • Plaques may be verrucous or have keratotic papules at periphery
  • Resistance felt during biopsy with grinding sound

Histopathology: Calcified elastic fibers (identical to those in pseudoxanthoma elasticum) undergoing transepidermal elimination

Note: Patients do not manifest other clinical findings of PXE

Pathogenesis

The precise pathogenesis of perforating disorders is not fully understood, but several mechanisms have been proposed:

Key Pathogenesis Question: What happens first?

(Hover to reveal)

Epithelium becomes hyperplastic and surrounds abnormal connective tissue, just as it would with foreign bodies. The dermal connective tissue does not actively "perforate" the epithelium.

Proposed Mechanisms:

  1. Enhanced expression of elastin receptors in the epidermis surrounding elastic material (in EPS and some cases of acquired perforating dermatosis)
  2. Advanced glycation end products (AGEs): Scratching exposes keratinocytes to AGE-modified extracellular matrix proteins (collagen types I and III), leading to terminal differentiation of keratinocytes via the AGE receptor (CD36)
  3. Fibronectin involvement: Elevated plasma fibronectin levels in diabetes and uremia may play a role in epithelial cell signaling, locomotion, and differentiation
  4. Type IV collagen transepidermal elimination demonstrated in RPC
  5. Increased expression of TGF-β3, MMP-1, and TIMP-1 observed in lesional skin of acquired RPC
  6. Other proposed mechanisms:
    • Abnormal vitamin A or D metabolism
    • Enzyme release from neutrophils
    • Microangiopathy related to diabetes
    • Deposition of uric acid, hydroxyapatite, or silicon

Understanding the "Perforation" Process

The perforation may be caused by the enlargement of a hyperkeratotic plug that penetrates through the base of the hyperplastic epidermis – the perforation is caused by the plug above rather than being due to the abnormal connective tissue below.

Pathology & Histological Features

Common histological features in all perforating diseases:

  • Plug of crusting or hyperkeratosis with variable parakeratosis
  • Epithelial hyperplasia around the plug
  • Characteristic "elephant snout" or "crab claw" appearance at site of perforation
  • Different stages may show different inflammatory infiltrates:
    • Early: Neutrophils within dermis
    • Later (especially EPS): Lymphocytes, macrophages, multinucleated giant cells
Reactive Perforating Collagenosis
  • Basophilic (degenerative) collagen fibers, some vertically oriented
  • Collagen fibers seen within plug or epidermis
  • Dermal connective tissue adjacent to plug appears unremarkable
  • Collagen fibers appear red with Verhoeff-van Gieson stain
Elastosis Perforans Serpiginosa
  • Elastic fibers seen within plug or epidermis
  • Increased amount of brightly eosinophilic elastic tissue in superficial dermis
  • Elastic fibers appear black with Verhoeff-van Gieson stain
  • In penicillamine-induced EPS: "Lumpy-bumpy" elastic fibers with lateral buds resembling a "bramble bush" (in both lesional and non-lesional skin)
Acquired Perforating Dermatosis
  • Variable findings based on stage of evolution
  • May resemble RPC, EPS, or perforating folliculitis
  • Often has amorphous degenerated material within perforations
  • Sometimes both collagen and elastic fibers are present
  • Material often cannot be clearly identified as collagen or elastic fibers

Histopathology Board Tip:

Verhoeff-van Gieson stain is the most useful special stain for differentiating perforating diseases:

  • Collagen fibers = RED
  • Elastic fibers = BLACK

However, elastic fibers higher in the epidermis may lose their ability to stain black in EPS.

Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis includes other disorders characterized by papules or nodules with central keratotic plugs or crusts:

Condition Differential Features
Prurigo simplex/nodularis More excoriated appearance, intense pruritus
Folliculitis Centered on hair follicles, may have pustules
Arthropod bites Acute onset, history of exposure, different distribution
Multiple keratoacanthomas Larger, dome-shaped with central crater
Verrucous lupus erythematosus/lichen planus Different clinical and histopathological features
Perforation of exogenous foreign material History of foreign body exposure
Perforation of endogenous substances Underlying specific disease process

For EPS specifically (annular lesions), consider:

  • Annular elastolytic giant cell granuloma (actinic granuloma)
  • Perforating pseudoxanthoma elasticum
  • Granuloma annulare
  • Tinea
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Discoid lupus erythematosus
  • Porokeratosis

With Koebner phenomenon, consider:

  • Psoriasis
  • Lichen planus
  • Verrucae (autoinoculation)

Diagnostic Challenge:

Since many patients with acquired perforating dermatosis have coexisting folliculitis and prurigo nodularis, there may be several different types of lesions at different stages of development. Histologic findings in a single biopsy specimen may not be representative of the entire process.

Treatment Approaches

General Considerations:

  • Well-designed, placebo-controlled treatment studies are lacking
  • Most treatments are anecdotal or based on small case series
  • Inherited forms (RPC and EPS) generally remain mild and localized
  • Acquired perforating dermatosis is often difficult to treat

First-Line Approaches:

  1. Address contributing factors:
    • Optimize management of diabetes mellitus
    • If end-stage chronic kidney disease, renal transplantation may lead to improvement
    • Discontinue culprit medications if possible
  2. Control pruritus:
    • Sedating antihistamines (e.g., doxepin)
    • Other anti-pruritic treatments
    • Avoid trauma and scratching (wearing gloves, trimming fingernails)
  3. Phototherapy: Broadband or narrowband UVB (level 2 evidence) - particularly good for patients with chronic kidney disease as it often relieves coexisting pruritus

Topical Treatments:

  • Retinoids (e.g., tretinoin, tazarotene)
  • Corticosteroids
  • Menthol, salicylic acid, sulfur, benzoyl peroxide
  • Imiquimod
  • Cantharidin

Systemic Treatments:

  • Corticosteroids (intralesional, intramuscular, oral)
  • Retinoids (vitamin A, acitretin, isotretinoin)
  • Antibiotics (doxycycline, clindamycin, erythromycin, rifampin)
  • Dupilumab (reported to improve acquired RPC in setting of atopic dermatitis)
  • Others: Colchicine, methotrexate, charcoal, allopurinol

Procedural Treatments:

  • Cryotherapy
  • Photodynamic therapy
  • Electrosurgical destruction
  • Laser ablation (CO2, Er:YAG, excimer, pulsed dye)
  • Excision of larger bothersome lesions
  • For EPS: Cellophane-tape stripping, tangential excision

Treatment Success by Condition:

  • RPC (childhood): May spontaneously resolve; avoid trauma
  • EPS: Local cryotherapy, laser therapy, or cellophane tape stripping (aggressive treatment may cause scarring)
  • Acquired perforating dermatosis: UVB phototherapy, intralesional corticosteroids, oral antibiotics, retinoids

Treatment Pearl:

Some dialysis patients with acquired perforating dermatosis have reported rapid improvement following a change in dialysis tubing or equipment, or alterations in dialysis procedure.

Board Exam Note:

While secondary perforating diseases are important to be aware of, most board questions will focus on the four major perforating diseases. Remember that perforation of tumor cells is often a nonspecific finding due to "perforation" of the epidermis by complexes of neoplastic cells.

High-Yield Clinical Images to Remember:

  • RPC: Erythematous papules with central scale-crust on arms/hands
  • EPS: Serpiginous or annular keratotic papules on neck
  • Acquired perforating dermatosis: Hyperpigmented papules/nodules with central keratotic core on legs
  • Perforating calcific elastosis: Periumbilical plaques in multiparous Black women

Key Diagnostic Clues:

  • Diabetic/CKD patient with itchy papules on extensor surfaces → Acquired perforating dermatosis
  • Annular keratotic papules on neck in young patient → EPS (look for associated genetic disorders)
  • History of trauma followed by keratotic papules → RPC
  • Grinding sound during biopsy of periumbilical plaque → Perforating calcific elastosis
Self-Assessment Quiz

1. A 45-year-old diabetic man on hemodialysis presents with pruritic hyperpigmented papules with central keratotic cores on his legs. The most likely diagnosis is:

  • Acquired perforating dermatosis
  • Elastosis perforans serpiginosa
  • Reactive perforating collagenosis
  • Perforating calcific elastosis

Correct! Acquired perforating dermatosis is the most common perforating disorder and is strongly associated with diabetes mellitus and chronic kidney disease requiring hemodialysis. The leg distribution and patient demographics strongly support this diagnosis.

2. Which special stain is most useful for differentiating between different types of perforating disorders?

  • PAS
  • Verhoeff-van Gieson
  • Alcian blue
  • Masson trichrome

Correct! Verhoeff-van Gieson stain is most helpful because collagen fibers appear red and elastic fibers appear black, allowing differentiation between RPC and EPS.

3. A 21-year-old man with Down syndrome presents with keratotic papules arranged in an annular pattern on his neck. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Granuloma annulare
  • Reactive perforating collagenosis
  • Elastosis perforans serpiginosa
  • Perforating folliculitis

Correct! Elastosis perforans serpiginosa (EPS) is associated with genetic disorders including Down syndrome. The annular arrangement of keratotic papules on the neck is classic for EPS.

4. In a patient with acquired perforating dermatosis, which treatment has the strongest evidence of efficacy?

  • UVB phototherapy
  • Imiquimod
  • Salicylic acid
  • Allopurinol

Correct! UVB phototherapy (broadband or narrowband) has level 2 evidence for efficacy in acquired perforating dermatosis and is particularly good for patients with chronic kidney disease as it often relieves coexisting pruritus.

5. A 38-year-old obese Black woman presents with periumbilical plaques with keratotic papules at the periphery. A grinding sound is noted during biopsy. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Pseudoxanthoma elasticum
  • Necrobiosis lipoidica
  • Perforating calcific elastosis
  • Elastosis perforans serpiginosa

Correct! Perforating calcific elastosis typically affects middle-aged, obese, hypertensive, multiparous Black women with characteristic periumbilical plaques. The grinding sound during biopsy is due to calcified elastic fibers.
