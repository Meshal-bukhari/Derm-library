Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)

Formerly known as Wegener Granulomatosis

A multisystem disease characterized by vasculitis and necrotizing granulomatous inflammation of small arteries and veins

🔍 Classic Triad HIGH YIELD
Board Exam Focus:

The classic triad of GPA is a high-yield concept for board exams. Remember that not all patients present with the complete triad, but recognizing these elements is essential for diagnosis.

1
Upper & Lower Respiratory Tract

Necrotizing granulomas affecting sinuses, nasal passages, trachea, and lungs

2
Systemic Vasculitis

Necrotizing small vessel granulomatous vasculitis affecting multiple organ systems

3
Glomerulonephritis

Usually focal segmental glomerulonephritis with renal involvement

🧠 Memory Aid: "WEG-ener's" Mnemonic
W - Wegener's (historical name) affects small vessels
E - ENT involvement (Ear, Nose, Throat) is characteristic
G - Granulomas & Glomerulonephritis

  1. Necrotizing granulomas of the upper and lower respiratory tract

  3. Glomerulonephritis (usually focal segmental)
📊 Demographics & Overview
  • Demographics: Most common in middle-aged patients of Northern European descent (80-90% of cases), but can occur in children and older adults
  • Disease Course: Presents on a continuum from limited involvement to generalized form
  • Prognosis: Untreated generalized form is fatal in less than 2 years
  • With Treatment: Long-term remissions possible in more than 90% of patients with cytotoxic therapy
Critical Concept:

GPA can present with varying severity from limited disease (which may remain limited for variable periods) to a generalized form with multi-organ involvement. The limited form may progress to the generalized form if not treated appropriately.

📋 Diagnostic Criteria BOARD FAVORITE
🔍 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) Criteria

The presence of 2 or more of the following 4 criteria has a diagnostic sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 92%:

  • Nasal or oral manifestation (painful or painless oral ulcers, or purulent or bloody nasal discharge)
  • Abnormal chest radiograph with nodules, fixed infiltrates, or cavities
  • Abnormal urinary sediment (microscopic hematuria with or without red cell casts)
  • Granulomatous inflammation on biopsy of an artery or perivascular area
Note: These criteria are the most widely accepted but not perfect. Diagnosis may be challenging without a confirmatory biopsy.
Diagnostic Pearl:

Consider GPA diagnosis for any patient presenting with necrotic ulcers on the lower extremities or palpable purpura. The saddle-nose deformity (from granulomatous involvement of the nasal septum) and "strawberry" gingivitis are hallmark signs.

c-ANCA / PR3-ANCA is positive in:

  • 40-75% of localized GPA cases

p-ANCA / MPO-ANCA is positive in only up to 10% of GPA cases.

🩺 Clinical Manifestations HIGH YIELD
Typical Presentation:

GPA typically presents with upper airway disease and nonspecific complaints such as malaise, arthralgias, fever, and weight loss.

👁️ Ocular (30-50%)
  • Scleritis and orbital disease most common
  • Decreased vision, eye pain, foreign body sensation
  • Proptosis, visual disturbance, diplopia
  • Conjunctivitis, episcleritis, uveitis
👄 Oral (up to 60%)
  • Oral ulcerations
  • Palatal perforation
  • "Strawberry" gingivitis - pathognomonic
👃 ENT
  • Sinusitis, rhinorrhea, rhinitis, epistaxis
  • Otitis media, hearing loss
  • Saddle-nose deformity - hallmark sign
  • Nasal mucosal ulceration
🫁 Pulmonary
  • Cough, hemoptysis, dyspnea, pleuritic pain
  • Wheezing, stridor
  • Parenchymal lung nodules, pulmonary vasculitis
  • Pulmonary fibrosis and hypertension
🧠 Neurologic
  • Cranial nerve disorders
  • Mononeuritis multiplex
  • Sensory neuropathy
  • Pachymeningitis
  • CNS mass lesions
🦿 Musculoskeletal
  • Myalgias
  • Polyarthralgias / asymmetric polyarthritis
🫀 Cardiovascular
  • Chest pain, myocarditis, pericarditis
  • Ischemia, cerebrovascular accident
  • Deep venous thrombosis
🧪 Renal
  • Hematuria, proteinuria
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Renal failure

Initial presentation in about 10% of cases. Activity of skin lesions often correlates with activity of other organ systems.

Common Manifestations:

  • Most common: Palpable purpura and nodules
  • Second most common: Necrotic ulcerations (most commonly on lower extremities) - may resemble pyoderma gangrenosum
  • Ulcerated papules symmetrically on extensor extremities (especially elbows) or buttocks

Less Common Manifestations:

  • Vesicles, bullae, urticarial plaques
  • Petechiae, subcutaneous nodules, subungual purpura
  • Panniculitis, pyoderma gangrenosum-like ulcers
  • Acute febrile neutrophilic dermatosis (Sweet syndrome)
  • Erythema elevatum diutinum

Presentation in Different Skin Tones:

  • Lighter skin: Erythema of macules, urticarial plaques, and panniculitic nodules more readily appreciated
  • Darker skin: Erythema may be subtle; edematous papules/plaques or firm, tender nodules may be the sole clinical finding; deep red, maroon, or violaceous hue may be seen
  • Postinflammatory hyperpigmentation: More marked and persistent in darker skin tones

Chest X-ray and CT Findings:

  • Bilateral, multiple, randomly distributed pulmonary nodules
  • Airspace consolidation or ground glass opacities (due to pulmonary hemorrhage)
  • Cavitation develops in approximately 50% of cases
  • Pleural effusions present in approximately 10% of cases
  • Hilar or mediastinal lymphadenopathy in 2-15% of cases
Clinical Pearl:

Laryngotracheal stenosis with stridor can be a complication of GPA and initially may be misdiagnosed as asthma.

🔄 Differential Diagnosis HIGH YIELD
Condition Key Differentiating Features
Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)
  • Absence of granulomatous inflammation
  • Sparing of upper respiratory tract
  • Overlap of clinical phenotypes is common
Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis
  • Associated with peripheral eosinophilia
  • History of asthma
Cocaine/Levamisole Toxicity
  • Can present with positive ANCA studies
  • History of cocaine use
Drug-induced Vasculitis
  • Positive ANCA studies
  • History of exposure to propylthiouracil, hydralazine, anti-TNF-α, sulfasalazine, D-penicillamine, or minocycline
Deep Fungal/Mycobacterial Infections
  • May have pulmonary symptoms mimicking GPA
  • Biopsy should demonstrate causative organisms
Pyoderma Gangrenosum
  • Lack of systemic involvement
  • Absence of ANCA
  • Characteristic histology

A - ANCA-associated vasculitides (MPA, EGPA)
C - Cocaine/drug-induced vasculitis
T - Tuberculosis and fungal infections
I - Infections (deep fungi, mycobacteria)
V - Vasculitis (other forms)
E - Embolic phenomena
🔬 Diagnostic Workup
Definitive Diagnosis:

Histologic confirmation showing granulomatous changes of the affected organ(s) is necessary for definitive diagnosis. However, granulomas can be distributed sparsely, and sampling may not always yield granulomas.

Key Laboratory Studies:

  • ANCA Testing:
    • c-ANCA / PR3-ANCA: Positive in 80-95% of generalized GPA and 40-75% of localized GPA
    • p-ANCA / MPO-ANCA: Positive in up to 10% of GPA
  • CBC with differential: May show normocytic normochromic anemia, leukocytosis, and/or thrombocytosis
  • Inflammatory markers: Elevated ESR and CRP
  • Renal function tests: Elevated serum creatinine
  • Urinalysis: May show proteinuria, hematuria, red cell casts, and dysmorphic red cells

Additional Investigations:

  • Liver function tests / enzymes
  • Haptoglobin, iron studies, coagulation tests
  • Serum protein electrophoresis, immunoglobulins, complement
  • Antinuclear antibodies, rheumatoid factor, cryoglobulins
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies, antiglomerular basement membrane
  • Viral serologies (HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C)
  • Chest radiography and CT of the paranasal sinuses and chest
  • Electromyogram, ECG, echocardiogram, renal ultrasound (if indicated)
Diagnostic Tip:

If clinical and laboratory criteria are suggestive of GPA and biopsy is nonconfirmatory, consider repeating the biopsy to decrease the likelihood of sampling error.

💊 Treatment Approach BOARD FAVORITE
Management Pearl:

Consultation with rheumatology, and possibly ophthalmology, pulmonology, or nephrology depending on end-organ involvement, is recommended for diagnosis and treatment.

Induction Therapy for Systemic GPA
  • Systemic glucocorticoids plus cyclophosphamide or rituximab
Maintenance Therapy
  • Azathioprine (check TPMT activity before starting)
  • Methotrexate
  • Rituximab
For Non-Organ-Threatening Disease
  • Systemic glucocorticoids plus methotrexate or mycophenolate mofetil
Milder Cases
  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (may help maintain remission but not for primary treatment)
New FDA-Approved Treatment:

Avacopan is FDA approved as an adjunctive treatment for adult patients with severe active ANCA-associated vasculitis (GPA and MPA) in combination with standard therapy including glucocorticoids.

Important Note:

Relapses with a different presenting manifestation from the initial presentation are common. Careful monitoring is required for all systemic therapies.

Pediatric Treatment:

Rituximab is the first drug approved by the FDA for GPA in children (aged 2 years and older); its safety profile in children was shown to be similar to that of adults.

📝 Board Exam Practice Questions
1. A 52-year-old woman presents with hemoptysis, bilateral pulmonary infiltrates on chest X-ray, and microscopic hematuria. Which laboratory test would be most helpful in diagnosing granulomatosis with polyangiitis?
  • c-ANCA / PR3-ANCA
  • Anti-glomerular basement membrane antibodies
  • Antinuclear antibodies (ANA)
  • Rheumatoid factor
Correct! c-ANCA / PR3-ANCA is positive in 80-95% of generalized GPA cases and is the most specific laboratory test for diagnosing GPA.
2. Which of the following is considered pathognomonic for granulomatosis with polyangiitis?
  • Oral ulcerations
  • "Strawberry" gingivitis
  • Palpable purpura
  • Scleritis
Correct! "Strawberry" gingivitis (hyperplastic gingival tissues and petechial hemorrhage resembling a strawberry) is essentially pathognomonic for GPA.
3. According to American College of Rheumatology (ACR) criteria, how many criteria are needed for the diagnosis of GPA?
  • 1 out of 4
  • 2 out of 4
  • 3 out of 4
  • 4 out of 4
Correct! The presence of 2 or more of the 4 ACR criteria has a diagnostic sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 92% for GPA.
