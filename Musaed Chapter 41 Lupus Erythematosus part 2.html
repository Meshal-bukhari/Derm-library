Acute Cutaneous LE (ACLE)

ACLE: The Systemic Flare Manifestation

Key Associations:

  • Associated with HLA-DR2.
  • +90% of patients will have SLE.
  • 60% of patients with lupus have a malar rash.

Clinical Features:

  • Localized (Malar Rash):
    • Sharply bordered erythema in a symmetrical "butterfly" distribution.
    • Spares the nasolabial folds.
    • Heals without scarring.
    • May be accompanied by diffuse hair thinning ("lupus hair").
  • Generalized:
    • Morbilliform, non-specific exanthem.
    • Sparing of the knuckles is characteristic (vs. Gottron's in DM).
    • Nailfold erythema and telangiectasia.
  • TEN-like ACLE:
    • Also known as Acute Syndrome of Apoptotic Pan-Epidermolysis (ASAP).
    • Must be differentiated from drug-induced TEN. Occurs on sun-exposed skin with a more insidious onset.
  • Mucosal:
    • Erythema, erosions, superficial ulcerations, petechiae in 7-45% with an acute flare.

Pathology:

  • Less impressive histologically compared to SCLE & DLE.
  • Cell-poor interface dermatitis.
  • Mild lymphohistiocytic infiltrate limited to the upper dermis. MCQ
  • Prominent dermal edema and mucin deposition.
  • DIF is positive in 60-100% of cases.

Other Forms of Cutaneous LE

Bullous Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
  • By definition, patient must fulfill criteria for SLE.
  • Antibodies against NC1 and NC2 domains of type VII collagen. MCQ
  • Presents with tense, subepidermal bullae.
  • ANA (+) in ~100%
  • Histo: Subepidermal bulla with neutrophils at the DEJ and in dermal papillae (mimics dermatitis herpetiformis).
  • DIF: Continuous granular to linear deposition of IgG, IgM, IgA and/or C3 along the BMZ.
  • Salt Split Skin (SSS): Dermal side staining.
  • Treatment: Dramatic response to dapsone.
Rowell's Syndrome

An erythema multiforme-like eruption that arises in the setting of ACLE, SCLE, or DLE. Typically associated with anti-Ro/SSA antibodies.

Non-Specific Skin Findings Suggestive of SLE
  • Diffuse non-scarring alopecia
  • Periungual telangiectasias and erythema
    Capillaroscopy Pearl: Lupus shows "wandering" dilated glomeruloid loops. This contrasts with DM and SSc (symmetric dilation and dropout) and Osler-Weber-Rendu (ectasia of half the capillary loop).
  • Mucosal ulcers
  • Vasculitis (cutaneous is most common)
  • Papulonodular mucinosis (~75% of pts will develop SLE)
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Eruptive dermatofibromas (a/w lupus, immunosuppression, and atopic dermatitis) MCQ

Complement Deficiency Lupus

SLE associated with Complement Deficiencies

Pathogenesis:

Deficiencies in early components of the classical pathway (C1, C4, C2) lead to impaired phagocytic clearance of apoptotic bodies, unmasking autoantigens (like Ro/SSA) and leading to loss of immune tolerance.

Deficiency of any early classical complement component (C1, C2, C4) is associated with an increased risk for SLE and infections with encapsulated bacteria.

Key Deficiency Associations:

C2 Deficiency-Associated SLE C1q/C4 Deficiency-Associated SLE
  • Most common hereditary complement disorder.
  • Low risk of SLE (10-20%), but the most common cause of complement-deficiency SLE due to its high prevalence.
  • Adult onset (~30 yo).
  • Less severe systemic disease (mild/absent renal disease).
  • Prominent photosensitivity and SCLE lesions.
  • Homozygous deficiencies are rare.
  • Very high risk of SLE: C1q (~90%) > C1r/s > C4 > C2.
  • Palmoplantar keratoses (C4 deficiency only).
  • Childhood onset.
  • Severe, recalcitrant renal disease.
  • CCLE or SCLE lesions.

Laboratory Findings:

  • Screening test: CH50 (markedly decreased).
  • Low or absent ANA titers.
  • Anti-Ro/SSA antibodies in the majority.

Lupus and Pregnancy

Key Considerations
  • Course is variable; can improve, worsen, or be stable.
  • Postpartum period is highest risk for flares.
  • Patients with lupus nephritis are at high risk of complications.
  • Fetal Complications: Preterm birth, preeclampsia.
  • Causes of Fetal Death: Anti-Ro Ab, Antiphospholipid Ab (e.g. anticardiolipin), Hx of previous fetal death.
  • Management in Pregnancy:
    • Continue hydroxychloroquine (reduces risk of NLE cardiac block).
    • Low-dose steroids and azathioprine can be considered.
    • Low-dose aspirin for Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APLS).

Diagnosis & Workup

Diagnostic Approach
High-Yield Biopsy Facts:
  • Skin biopsy for H&E is the test with the highest diagnostic value in LE. MCQ
  • Sample for DIF should be from lesional skin, not perilesional (this is a key difference from Bullous Pemphigoid). MCQ

Initial Workup for All Patients:

  • Thorough history and physical examination.
  • Skin biopsy (H&E and DIF).
  • Labs: CBC, ESR/CRP, LFT/RFT, Serologies (ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, etc.), Complement levels (C3, C4, CH50).

Diagnosis of Systemic Lupus (SLE):

Requires one of the following:

  1. Meeting ACR criteria (4 of 11).
  2. Meeting SLICC/EULAR criteria.
  3. Biopsy-proven nephritis compatible w/ SLE in the presence of ANA or anti-dsDNA antibodies.

Treatment of Cutaneous Lupus

General Principles & Local Therapy

1. Sun Protection (Foundation of Therapy)

Essential because UV light exacerbates cutaneous and systemic disease, increases malignancy risk, and decreases antimalarial efficacy.

  • Avoidance: Peak hours (11am-3pm), reflective surfaces (sand, water, snow).
  • Physical Protection: Protective clothing, hats, UV-blocking window films.
  • Sunscreens: Broad-spectrum (UVA/UVB), SPF ≥ 50, applied generously (2 mg/cm²). Physical blockers (Titanium dioxide, Zinc oxide) are excellent.

2. Smoking Cessation

Crucial for disease control and medication efficacy.

3. Topical Therapy

  • Corticosteroids: Superpotent (e.g., Clobetasol) BID for 2 weeks, often alternated with a TCI.
  • Calcineurin Inhibitors (TCIs): Tacrolimus 0.1% or Pimecrolimus 1%. Good for steroid-sparing on the face.
  • Intralesional Corticosteroids: Triamcinolone (2.5-10 mg/mL) injected monthly into active lesions.
  • Topical Retinoids: Can be useful, especially in hypertrophic DLE.
Systemic Therapy

Indicated for widespread disease, scarring forms, or disease unresponsive to local therapy.

Step 1: Antimalarials (First-Line)

The gold standard. Effect may take up to 3 months.

  • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil): Dose ≤ 5 mg/kg/day (real body weight).
  • Chloroquine: Dose ≤ 2.3 mg/kg/day (real body weight). More effective for cutaneous lupus but higher retinal toxicity risk.
  • Quinacrine: 100 mg/day. Does not carry retinal risk. Often added to HCQ or CQ if response is partial.
Ocular Screening: Baseline exam, then yearly after 5 years of use (or earlier if high-risk factors present).

HCQ Side Effects: DISCOID

  • Diarrhea (GI upset)
  • Irritability (neuromuscular)
  • Skeletal muscle weakness
  • Cutaneous (blue-gray pigmentation, psoriasis flare)
  • Ocular (retinopathy)
  • Insufficient cells (blood dyscrasias)
  • Decreased WBCs/RBCs
▼ If resistant or inadequate response ▼

Step 2: Add-on / Second-Line Agents

Choice depends on LE subtype and clinical picture.

  • Systemic Corticosteroids: Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg for short-term control of flares while other agents take effect.
  • Methotrexate (MTX): 7.5-25 mg weekly. Good for arthritis and skin.
  • Retinoids (Acitretin, Isotretinoin): Excellent for hypertrophic/keratotic DLE. Avoid in SCLE if Sjogren's is present (dryness).
  • Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF): Good for cutaneous disease, less so for systemic.
  • Dapsone: Primary choice for bullous LE, also useful for vasculitis and oral ulcers.
  • Thalidomide/Lenalidomide: Very effective for refractory DLE, but teratogenic and causes neuropathy.
  • Azathioprine: Another option for immunosuppression.
▼ For severe, refractory disease ▼

Step 3: Biologics & Novel Therapies

  • Belimumab (Benlysta): Anti-BLyS antibody. . It is approved by the FDA for the treatment of SLE in combination with other standard therapies and is part of certain regimens used to treat lupus nephritis. .
  • Rituximab (Rituxan): Anti-CD20. Used off-label for severe disease.
  • IFN Pathway Inhibitors: Anifrolumab (anti-IFN receptor Ab) is FDA approved for SLE.
  • JAK Inhibitors: Tofacitinib, Ruxolitinib (case reports).
  • IVIG: For severe, refractory cases.
  • Cyclophosphamide: Reserved for severe organ involvement (e.g., lupus nephritis).

Table 41.4: ACR 1997 Criteria for SLE (4 of 11 for diagnosis)
  1. Malar rash: Fixed erythema over malar eminences, sparing nasolabial folds.
  2. Discoid rash: Raised patches, adherent scale, follicular plugging, atrophic scarring.
  3. Photosensitivity: Skin rash from unusual reaction to sunlight.
  4. Oral ulcers: Oral or nasopharyngeal, usually painless.
  5. Arthritis: Non-erosive, involving ≥2 peripheral joints.
  6. Serositis: Pleuritis or Pericarditis.
  7. Renal disorder: Persistent proteinuria >0.5 g/day OR cellular casts.
  8. Neurologic disorder: Seizures or psychosis.
  9. Hematologic disorder: Hemolytic anemia, OR Leukopenia (<4000), OR Lymphopenia (<1500), OR Thrombocytopenia (<100,000).
  10. Immunologic disorder: Anti-dsDNA, OR Anti-Sm, OR positive Antiphospholipid antibodies.
  11. Antinuclear antibody (ANA): Abnormal titer.
