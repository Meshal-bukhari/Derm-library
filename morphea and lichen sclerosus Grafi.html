Key Concepts

  • Morphea and Lichen Sclerosus are distinct inflammatory skin disorders that lead to fibrosis
  • Morphea involves the dermis and subcutaneous tissue
  • Lichen sclerosus involves the epidermis and upper dermis
  • 6% of patients develop these two entities simultaneously
  • They may be triggered by the same mechanism

Morphea Overview

  • Vascular changes, inflammatory infiltrate, and fibrosis identical to systemic sclerosis
  • Female:Male ratio of 2.6:1
  • Asymmetric distribution
  • Rarely involves internal organs (mainly ocular and neurological in Perry-Romberg)
  • Can cause significant morbidity (pain, contractures, compromised joint mobility)
  • Sclerosis can lead to growth retardation, and in severe cases, microamelia

Lichen Sclerosus Overview

  • Inflammatory disorder primarily of the superficial dermis or submucosa
  • Female predominance (10:1)
  • Bimodal age distribution: First peak at 8-13 years, second peak at 40-50s
  • 85% of patients have genital involvement
  • 4-6% risk of SCC
  • In males, known as balanitis xerotica obliterans
  • Common complications: phimosis or scarring of vaginal introitus
Morphea and lichen sclerosus coexist in 6% of patients
Although both morphea and systemic sclerosis (SSc) have similar histopathology, they are easily differentiated clinically. Morphea is asymmetric and rarely involves internal organs, while systemic sclerosis presents with Raynaud's phenomenon, digital sclerosis, and GI or lung involvement.

Morphea (Localized Scleroderma)

The overall most common subtype of morphea is plaque-type, followed by linear morphea
The most common subtype of morphea in children is linear morphea

Clinical Classification

Plaque-type Morphea
  • Most common variant
  • Erythematous-violaceous indurated plaques
  • Undergoes centrifugal expansion
  • With time, progresses into sclerotic/scar-like tissue
  • Mostly on the trunk, but multiple asymmetric lesions may occur
  • Shiny atrophy centrally with violaceous ring peripherally (lilac border)
  • Lilac ring is a sign of disease activity
  • Loss of skin appendages
  • Can be asymptomatic or pruritic due to xerosis
  • 3-5 years then arrests, eventually self-resolves, with residual atrophy and dyspigmentation in most patients

Variants:

  • Guttate morphea: small superficial lesions
  • Nodular/keloidal: similar to keloids
  • Atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini: very superficial hyperpigmented atrophic plaques, "Cliff-drop" border
  • Deep morphea: involves deep dermis, subcutis, and sometimes deeper structures; can cause dystrophic calcinosis cutis
  • Bullous morphea: happens due to obstruction of lymphatics
Linear Morphea
  • 2/3 of cases occur in children
  • Begins as small plaque-type morphea that extends longitudinally as a series of plaques that join to form a scar-like band
  • Most common location: lower extremities
  • Tends to involve underlying fascia, muscle, and tendons
  • Can impact mobility, especially if overlying joints
  • Have prolonged periods of quiescence followed by reactivation

Variants:

  • En coup de sabre type: linear morphea of head/neck, extends from forehead into frontal scalp, unilateral, paramedian location most common, can involve brain/meninges (seizures)
  • Hemifacial atrophy (Perry-Romberg): severe variant with trigeminal nerve involvement; children need regular ophthalmology and neurology follow-up
Generalized Morphea
  • 4 or more plaques, 3cm in diameter, 2 anatomic locations (4,3,2 rule)
  • Starts as common plaque-type morphea but doesn't stop expanding
  • Spares the nipples
  • Involvement of intercostal muscles can impair breathing
Disabling Pansclerotic Morphea
  • Variant seen in children similar to generalized morphea
  • Can be deep enough to involve bones
  • Lifelong disabilities due to atrophy of underlying muscles and joint contractures
  • Periodontal atrophy may occur
  • Involvement of esophagus and lung is rarely seen
Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) risk due to chronic ulceration in disabling pansclerotic morphea

Pathogenesis

Trigger

Trauma, injections, vaccinations, X-irradiation, medications

Vascular Dysfunction

Reduced capillaries, tissue hypoxia

Immune Response

Th2 mediated, IL-4, IL-13, TGF-β

Sclerosis

Activated T cells stimulate fibroblasts to produce collagen

Autoantibodies in Morphea

Anti-ssDNA is associated with linear and generalized morphea
Eosinophilia may occur in morphea
  • Increased incidence of anti-topoisomerase IIa, -histone, -fibrillin1, and -phospholipid
  • ANA with high titers in linear and generalized morphea
  • Antibodies may correlate with disease activity
  • In linear morphea:
    • Anti-histone is associated with functional limitation
    • ANA is associated with extensive body involvement

Pathology

Note: Must take a deep biopsy

Early Findings:

  • Endothelial cell swelling
  • Dermal lymphocytic infiltrate composed of CD4+ T cells and sometimes eosinophils, plasma cells and mast cells
  • The infiltrate is most prominent at the dermal-subcutis junction (deeper than SSc)
  • Loss of CD34+ dendritic cells

Late Findings:

  • "Square biopsy sign"
  • The inflammatory infiltrate disappears
  • Flattened DEJ
  • Reduced capillaries
  • Homogeneous, densely packed collagen bundles
  • Atrophic and trapped eccrine glands
  • In deep morphea: replacement of involved structures (fat, fascia, muscle) with collagen bundles

Extracutaneous Manifestations

  • Musculoskeletal/articular is the most common (11%)
  • Neurological (4%)
  • Ocular (2%)
  • 85% of patients with Perry-Romberg have neurological and ocular manifestations
Melorheostosis = roughening of long bone surfaces underlying area of linear morphea (also seen in Buschke-Ollendorff syndrome) that resembles wax dripping down the side of a candle on X-ray

Lichen Sclerosus

  • Inflammatory disorder primarily of the superficial dermis or submucosa
  • 85% of patients have genital involvement
  • 4-6% risk of SCC
  • Bimodal age distribution: 8-13 years first peak, 40-50s second peak
  • Genital lichen sclerosus in males known as balanitis xerotica obliterans
  • Frequently co-exists with generalized or plaque-type morphea (6%)
Female predominance in lichen sclerosus is 10:1, but some studies found 1:1

Pathogenesis

  • Unclear etiology
  • Primarily a Th1 response (vs Th2 in morphea)
  • Associated with other autoimmune disorders, especially thyroid disease in females (15%)
  • Also associated with: pernicious anemia, localized scleroderma/morphea (6%), psoriasis, and vitiligo
  • Hormonal factors: predominance in postmenopausal women, resolution in pregnancy, and history of OCP use in patients with LS
HLA-DQ7 association with lichen sclerosus
Autoantibodies against ECM1 present in 80% of lichen sclerosus patients

Clinical Features

Anogenital Lichen Sclerosus (85%)

  • Sclerotic, ivory-white, atrophic, and flat-topped papules coalescing into plaques
  • Figure-of-eight configuration in women
  • Glans penis and foreskin in men (rare to have anal involvement)
  • Purpura within the lesion is a helpful diagnostic sign
  • With time: fusion of labia minora to majora, obliteration of clitoral hood, and narrowing of vaginal introitus in women; acquired phimosis or paraphimosis in men
  • Eruptive melanocytic nevi can occur
Pruritus is the most frequent symptom in anogenital lichen sclerosus

Other symptoms include dysuria, dyspareunia, pain upon defecation, and constipation in children

Extragenital Lichen Sclerosus (15-20%)

  • Favors the trunk and proximal extremities
  • Predilection sites: neck, shoulders, flexor surfaces of the wrists, and sites of physical trauma or continuous pressure
  • Scar-like papules and plaques, usually ivory in color with a shiny/wrinkled surface
  • Follicular plugging more prominent compared to genital LS
  • Involvement of other mucosal areas is rare
  • In oral mucosa: bluish-white papules up to 5mm in diameter may occur on the buccal mucosa
Extragenital lichen sclerosus is usually asymptomatic

Pathology

Follicular plugging is a histopathological feature of lichen sclerosus
Papillary dermal edema is a histopathological feature of lichen sclerosus
  • Compact orthohyperkeratosis
  • Epidermal atrophy
  • Band-like interface dermatitis
  • Homogenized collagen
  • "Red, white, and blue sign":
    • Red: pink-red orthokeratosis
    • White: white-pale upper dermis
    • Blue: band-like interface dermatitis

Differential Diagnosis

  • Erosive lichen planus
  • Vulvar eczema
  • Lichen simplex chronicus
  • Morphea
  • Mucous membrane pemphigoid
  • Child sexual abuse (in pediatric cases)

Treatment Approaches

Morphea Treatment

Treatment Based on Subtype & Severity

  • Superficial circumscribed lesions:
    • Topical steroids
    • Topical Vitamin D analogues
    • Topical retinoids
    • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
    • Imiquimod
  • Extensive disease without internal organ involvement:
    • Phototherapy: UVA1, PUVA (NB-UVB not effective)
  • Severe-moderate active morphea:
    • First line: Methotrexate
    • Systemic steroids for short duration to halt progression
  • Inactive lesions:
    • Referral as indicated
    • Local excision or fillers for atrophic areas
  • Other options:
    • Imatinib
    • JAK inhibitors
    • Penicillin

Lichen Sclerosus Treatment

First-line Therapy

  • Ultrapotent topical steroids
    • Daily application for 3 months, then decrease potency or duration
    • Maintenance: 2x/week
  • Soap substitutes and emollients
  • Circumcision in men with phimosis

Second-line Therapy

  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
    • Pimecrolimus better than tacrolimus
    • Theoretical risk of SCC and HPV
  • PUVA/UVA1 or MTX in resistant cases
  • Surgical correction for females with severe fibrosis

Treatment Algorithm for Morphea

Active morphea is characterized by:

  • Erythema and edema
  • Peripheral induration and erythema
  • Lesion expansion
  • New lesion formation

If any of these present:

  • Assess for functional impairment or significant disfigurement
  • If present: Methotrexate +/- systemic glucocorticoids and referral to physical therapy
  • If absent: Consider depth, extent, progression, and location

Treatment options based on presentation:

  • Single or few dermal plaques: Topical therapies (corticosteroids, tacrolimus, vitamin D analogs, or intralesional corticosteroids)
  • Extensive dermal plaques: Phototherapy
  • Extensive or rapidly progressing deep morphea: Methotrexate +/- systemic glucocorticoids
  • Limited, slowly progressing deep morphea: Intralesional corticosteroids or methotrexate

For inactive disease with damage:

  • Refer to specialists (PT/OT, orthopedics, plastic surgery) for functional or cosmetic impairment
  • Continue to monitor for recurrence of disease activity
In linear morphea affecting the face/head, regular monitoring by ophthalmology and neurology is essential to screen for involvement. Similarly, for linear morphea of extremities, assessment of muscle involvement (MRI/US + CK/aldolase) and joint mobility is important.

Board Exam Review

Focus on these high-yield MCQ points that have been tested in previous exams:

1. Which is the most common subtype of morphea overall?
A. Linear morphea
B. Plaque-type morphea
C. Generalized morphea
D. Deep morphea

Answer: B. Plaque-type morphea

The overall most common subtype of morphea is plaque-type, followed by linear. Linear morphea is the most common subtype in children.

2. Which antibody is associated with linear and generalized morphea?
A. Anti-dsDNA
B. Anti-ssDNA
C. Anti-centromere
D. Anti-Scl-70

Answer: B. Anti-ssDNA

Anti-ssDNA is associated with linear and generalized morphea. Anti-histone is associated with functional limitation, and ANA is associated with extensive body involvement in linear morphea.

3. What is the female to male ratio in lichen sclerosus?
A. 1:1
B. 3:1
C. 5:1
D. 10:1

Answer: D. 10:1

Female predominance in lichen sclerosus is 10:1, although some studies found 1:1. This is a point specifically noted as appearing in previous MCQs.

4. Which HLA is associated with lichen sclerosus?
A. HLA-B27
B. HLA-DQ7
C. HLA-DR4
D. HLA-A1

Answer: B. HLA-DQ7

HLA-DQ7 is associated with lichen sclerosus according to previous board exams.

5. What is the most frequent symptom in genital lichen sclerosus?
A. Pain
B. Pruritus
C. Dysuria
D. Dyspareunia

Answer: B. Pruritus

Pruritus is the most frequent symptom in genital lichen sclerosus. Other symptoms include dysuria, dyspareunia, and pain upon defecation.

6. Which finding is a histopathological feature of lichen sclerosus?
A. Mucin deposits
B. Follicular plugging
C. Granulomatous inflammation
D. Acantholysis

Answer: B. Follicular plugging

Follicular plugging is a histopathological feature of lichen sclerosus, as is papillary dermal edema.

7. Which malignancy is associated with disabling pansclerotic morphea?
A. Basal cell carcinoma
B. Squamous cell carcinoma
C. Melanoma
D. Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans

Answer: B. Squamous cell carcinoma

Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) due to chronic ulceration is associated with disabling pansclerotic morphea.

8. What percentage of lichen sclerosus patients have autoantibodies against ECM1?
A. 40%
B. 60%
C. 80%
D. 100%

Answer: C. 80%

Autoantibodies against ECM1 are present in 80% of lichen sclerosus patients.

9. Which of these is a characteristic feature of extragenital lichen sclerosus?
A. Usually symptomatic with burning pain
B. Usually asymptomatic
C. Always accompanied by genital involvement
D. Follicular plugging is less prominent than in genital LS

Answer: B. Usually asymptomatic

Extragenital lichen sclerosus is usually asymptomatic, in contrast to genital lichen sclerosus which commonly presents with pruritus.

10. Which laboratory finding may be present in morphea?
A. Eosinophilia
B. Thrombocytopenia
C. Elevated creatinine
D. Hypocalcemia

Answer: A. Eosinophilia

Eosinophilia may occur in morphea.
