Infantile Myofibromatosis

A High-Yield Board Review for Dermatology Residents

Key Insights from: Kimberley R. Zakka MD, MSC, Craig N. Burkhart MD, Dean Morrell MD, Susan Burgin MD

Synopsis: The Essentials

Infantile myofibromatosis (IM) is the most common fibrous tumor of infancy.

Age of Onset:

  • 90% present within the first 2 years of life.
  • 60% present at birth (congenital).
  • More common in boys (60%).
  • Mostly sporadic, but familial cases (AD, AR) reported. PDGFRB germline mutations associated in some cases (may predispose to cerebral aneurysms).

Three Clinical Forms of IM:

Solitary Form (Most Common)

VisualDx® Solitary Type: Papule or Nodule (Typically head/neck, trunk)
  • Presentation: Single papule or nodule.
  • Tissues Affected: Skin, subcutaneous tissue, or muscle.
  • Common Sites: Trunk, head, or neck.
  • Prognosis: Favorable, benign, self-limited. Low recurrence.

Multicentric Form

  • Onset: Appears more often at birth.
  • Gender: More common in girls.
  • Lesions: Skin, SC tissue, muscle PLUS involvement of bone and viscera (but NO severe visceral compromise).
  • Skeletal Sites: Skull, femur, tibia, spine, ribs (>50% of cases).
  • Prognosis (w/o visceral): Favorable.

Generalized Form (with Visceral Involvement)

  • Key Feature: Significant visceral involvement.
  • Prognosis: Usually poor.
  • Common Visceral Sites: Heart, lungs, bones, gastrointestinal (GI) tract.
  • Skin Clues (Generalized): Atrophic papules, hypopigmented macules may be present.

Diagnostic Codes

ICD10CM: Q89.8 - Other specified congenital malformations
SNOMEDCT: 254146000 - Infantile myofibromatosis

Clinical Presentation: What to Look For

  • Size: Can range from 1-7 cm.
  • Superficial Myofibromas: Rounded, firm papules or nodules. May be mobile.
  • Deeper Lesions: Immobile.
  • Overlying Skin: Sometimes red or purple discoloration (may mimic hemangioma ).
  • Generalized Form Skin Signs: Atrophic papules and hypopigmented macules.

Common Lesion Locations:

  • Solitary: Head & neck (most common), then trunk & extremities.
  • Multicentric/Generalized Visceral: Lungs, GI tract, myocardium.
Impact of Skin Color: In darker skin tones, associated color changes (red/purple) may be more subtle. Always maintain a high index of suspicion.

Diagnostic Pearls

Family History - Don't Forget!

Take a careful family history. While familial cases are rare, both autosomal dominant (AD) and autosomal recessive (AR) inheritance patterns have been reported.

Remember the association with PDGFRB germline mutations.

Differential Diagnosis & Pitfalls

Consider these when evaluating a suspected infantile myofibroma. (Board exam favorite!)

  • Leiomyoma
  • Infantile hemangioma (Key differentiator: myofibromas are firm, hemangiomas often softer/compressible, different natural history)
  • Congenital fibrosarcoma (More aggressive, biopsy is key!)
  • Metastatic neuroblastoma (see also congenital neuroblastoma)
  • Other infantile fibromatoses (e.g., juvenile hyaline fibromatosis)
Pitfall Alert: The reddish-purple hue of some superficial myofibromas can easily be mistaken for an infantile hemangioma. Palpation (firmness) and biopsy are crucial differentiators.

Best Tests for Diagnosis & Staging

Skeletal Survey: To detect bone involvement in multicentric/generalized forms.

Chest X-ray: Initial screen for pulmonary involvement.

Thorax and Abdominal CT: For detailed assessment of visceral involvement.

Whole-Body MRI (Consider!): The text mentions "Additional evaluation to exclude visceral involvement should be considered, even for those with apparent solitary lesions. Whole-body MRI is the most popular technique due to its excellent soft tissue resolution." This is a key management pearl often tested!

Skin Biopsy (Definitive Diagnosis):

Key Histopathological Features:
  • Fibroblasts with oval nuclei.
  • Arranged in interlacing whorled bundles.
  • Prominent thin-walled vessels (often in a "staghorn" pattern, though not explicitly stated in text provided).
  • Areas of necrosis with calcification may be present.

Management Pearls

Recurrence Insights

Recurrence is most likely due to inadequate removal of the tumor. Re-excision is usually curative.

Evaluation for Visceral Disease

CRITICAL: Additional evaluation to exclude visceral involvement should be considered, even for those with apparent solitary lesions.

Whole-body MRI is the preferred technique due to its excellent soft tissue resolution.

Genetic Counseling

All patients should be referred for genetic counseling, especially given the association with PDGFRB mutations and familial forms.

Therapy & Management Strategies

Solitary Lesions:

  • Surgical excision is typically performed.
  • However, surveillance and monitoring may be appropriate as most lesions spontaneously regress in 1-2 years. (Clinical judgment needed!)

Patients with Visceral Involvement (Generalized Form):

  • Higher risk of morbidity and mortality; require careful follow-up.
  • Tumors affecting function or causing local mass effect are excised or treated with chemotherapy.
  • PDGFRB Mutations: PDGFR-beta tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) have been used. (Targeted therapy!)

Recurrence:

  • Unusual, but if it occurs, it may be delayed to adulthood.
  • Surveillance and observation are thus recommended for those with multicentric and generalized disease.
  • Recurrence is treated with chemotherapy and surgical excision.
