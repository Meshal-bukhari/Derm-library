Reactive Arthritis (ReA)

Formerly Reiter Syndrome: A High-Yield Board Review

The Classic Board Vignette

An acute presentation of an asymmetric, predominantly lower extremity, painful arthritis in a young male should make you highly suspicious for ReA. Always look for conjunctivitis and palpate for lower extremity joint and tendon insertion pain. Remember, the triad may be incomplete or present sequentially.

Synopsis: The Core Concept

Reactive Arthritis (ReA) is a type of spondyloarthropathy that occurs 1-4 weeks after a gastrointestinal or genitourinary infection. The joint fluid is sterile, but bacterial antigens in the synovium are thought to induce the immune response.

The Trigger: Infection

Classic Pathogens:

  • Chlamydia trachomatis
  • Shigella
  • Salmonella
  • Yersinia
  • Campylobacter

Other Implicated Triggers:

  • Clostridioides difficile
  • Giardia
  • Other atypical infections

The Host: Demographics

  • Age: Onset commonly 20-40 years
  • Genetics: Associated with HLA-B27 (like other spondyloarthropathies)
  • Descent: Northern European
  • Gender:
    • Post-GI Infection: Males = Females
    • Post-GU (C. trachomatis): Males > Females

Board Trap: The "Classic Triad"

The famous mnemonic "Can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree" refers to the triad of Conjunctivitis, Urethritis, and Arthritis.

HOWEVER, BEWARE: Only a minority of patients present with the complete, classic triad. The most common presentation is an acute, painful oligoarthritis.

Look For: Clinical Manifestations

ReA is a systemic disease. Look for findings across multiple organ systems.

Rheumatologic (The "Arthritis")

  • Asymmetric, oligoarticular arthritis (fewer than 5 joints).
  • Predilection for lower extremities (knees, ankles).
  • Enthesitis: Inflammation at tendon/ligament insertion points is common (Patellar/Achilles tendons, plantar fascia).
  • Dactylitis: "Sausage digits."
  • Low back pain: Stiffness and pain, sacroiliac joint tenderness.

Ocular (The "Can't See")

  • Most Common: Bilateral, mucopurulent conjunctivitis. Often resolves in 2 weeks.
  • Less Common (Refer to Ophthalmology!):
    • Keratitis
    • Iritis
    • Uveitis (usually unilateral)
    • Corneal ulceration
    • Optic neuritis

GU / Oral (The "Can't Pee")

  • Genitourinary: Urethritis, cervicitis, dysuria.
  • Oral Ulcers: Often painless.
  • Geographic tongue.

Key Dermatologic Manifestations

The skin findings are high-yield for dermatology boards and can be pathognomonic.

Keratoderma Blenorrhagicum

Affects palms and soles. Starts as pustules, which become hyperkeratotic and scaly. May coalesce into psoriatic-like plaques.

CRITICAL POINT: This is clinically and histologically indistinguishable from pustular psoriasis. The diagnosis is made by the clinical context (post-infection, arthritis pattern, associated findings).

Circinate Balanitis / Vulvitis

  • Circinate Balanitis:
    • Uncircumcised males: Vesicles/pustules rupture to form serpiginous, asymptomatic erosions.
    • Circumcised males: Presents as hyperkeratotic plaques.
  • Circinate Vulvitis: Similar presentation with erythematous, crusted plaques on the perineum and vulva.

Nail & Other Skin Findings

  • Generalized Psoriasiform Eruption: May occur with ostraceous or limpet-like scales.
  • Nail Involvement (Common):
    • Most Prominent: Paronychia.
    • Other: Onycholysis, ridging, splitting, elkonyxis, brown-red discoloration, subungual hyperkeratosis.
    • NOTE: Pitting, characteristic of psoriasis, is typically NOT seen but can occur.

Differential Diagnosis & Workup

The diagnosis is clinical, supported by a careful history and exclusion of other conditions.

Best Tests & Prognosis

Diagnostic Approach

There is no specific diagnostic test panel. Diagnosis is clinical, based on history and exam, guided by criteria (ACR, International Workshop).

  • Labs: Leukocytosis, elevated ESR/CRP. ANA and RF are negative.
  • Joint Fluid: Sterile culture is key to rule out septic arthritis.
  • Urine: Mildly abnormal urinalysis is common.
  • ECG: May show minor changes (subclinical carditis).
  • Imaging: Often normal early. May show soft tissue swelling, then bony erosions adjacent to bony proliferation later.
  • HLA-B27: Not routinely recommended (associated with other spondyloarthropathies).

Prognosis & Systemic Features

  • Prognosis: For most, it's a self-limited disease with complete recovery in 2-6 months.
  • Chronic Arthritis: May persist in a minority, usually in HLA-B27 positive patients.
  • Recurrence: Possible upon re-exposure to the same organism.
  • Systemic Features: Fever, weight loss, fatigue.
  • Rare Complications: Cardiac conduction abnormalities, aortic insufficiency, pericarditis, pleuritis, nephropathy.
  • HIV/AIDS Context: Cutaneous involvement reported to be more frequent and severe.

Treating the Trigger & Skin

  • Active Infection: Treat if still active. For C. trachomatis, use Azithromycin 1g single dose OR Doxycycline 100mg BID x 7 days. (GI infections may not need antibiotics).
  • Cutaneous Disease:
    • Trunk/Limbs: Mid-potency topical corticosteroids (e.g., Triamcinolone, Mometasone).
    • Face/Intertriginous: Low-potency topical corticosteroids (e.g., Hydrocortisone 1-2.5%, Desonide). Use for max 14 days.
    • Resistant Skin: Methotrexate or Cyclosporine.

Special Considerations & Referrals

  • HIV/AIDS: ReA can be treatment-resistant. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) itself may lead to improvement. Acitretin has shown success in challenging cases. Consult an HIV/Infectious Disease specialist.
  • Physical Therapy: Helpful adjunct for joint issues.
  • Referrals:
    • Ophthalmologist: For iritis, uveitis, keratitis, optic neuritis.
    • Rheumatologist, Cardiologist, Nephrologist: As needed for systemic involvement.
