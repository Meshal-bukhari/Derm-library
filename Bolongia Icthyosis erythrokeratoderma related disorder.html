Introduction to Ichthyoses & Erythrokeratodermas

Ichthyoses and erythrokeratodermas are disorders of cornification characterized by abnormal differentiation and desquamation of the epidermis resulting in a defective cutaneous barrier.

The term "ichthyosis" is derived from the Greek root "ichthy" meaning fish, introduced by Willan in 1808.

Classification Approach

Modern classification incorporates both clinical features and molecular causes:

  • Common Ichthyoses (Ichthyosis vulgaris, X-linked ichthyosis)
  • Keratinopathic Ichthyoses (Epidermolytic ichthyosis, etc.)
  • Autosomal Recessive Congenital Ichthyoses (ARCI)
  • Syndromic Ichthyoses (Netherton, Sjögren-Larsson, etc.)
  • Peeling Skin Syndromes (CDSN, TGM5, CSTA mutations, etc.)
  • Erythrokeratodermas (EKVP, KID syndrome, etc.)
  • X-linked Dominant Ichthyosiform Disorders (CHILD syndrome, etc.)
  • Rare Syndromic Forms (MEDNIK, CEDNIK, etc.)

Key diagnostic considerations in ichthyoses:

  • Age of onset (congenital vs. later onset)
  • Distribution and morphology of scale
  • Presence/absence of erythroderma, blistering, and adnexal abnormalities
  • Inheritance pattern
  • Associated syndromic features

Diagnostic Approach for Neonates/Infants

Important diagnostic clues include:

  • Prenatal/birth history (prolonged labor, prematurity)
  • Family history and consanguinity
  • Type and distribution of skin findings
  • Evaluation of hair, nails, and mucous membranes
  • Associated extracutaneous abnormalities

SCALES mnemonic for evaluation:

  • Scale appearance (fine/coarse, white/dark)
  • Collodion membrane presence
  • Adnexa involvement (hair, nails)
  • Location and distribution
  • Erythema presence/absence
  • Syndromic features

Key Laboratory Evaluations

  • Complete blood count
  • Electrolytes and liver function
  • Immunoglobulin levels (including IgE)
  • Peripheral blood smear (for vacuolated leukocytes)
  • Hearing screen
  • Genetic testing (multigene panels preferred)

Histopathologic Features

  • Hyperkeratosis (ortho- vs. parakeratotic)
  • Granular layer (normal, reduced, absent)
  • Acanthosis or papillomatosis
  • Presence of blistering or clefting
  • Specialized findings (e.g., epidermolytic hyperkeratosis)
  • Ultrastructural abnormalities

General Treatment Principles

Therapy is symptomatic and primarily aimed at reducing hyperkeratosis:

  • Topical management with emollients, keratolytics, and retinoids
  • Oral retinoids for severe cases (benefits vs. side effects must be carefully considered)
  • Supportive care (especially for neonates with compromised skin barrier)
  • Emerging therapies targeting specific molecular defects

Common Ichthyoses

Ichthyosis Vulgaris

Genetics: Loss-of-function mutations in the filaggrin gene (FLG) resulting in haploinsufficiency. Approximately 8% of European and 3% of East Asian individuals harbor at least one FLG mutation.

Key Features
  • Most common disorder of cornification (1:100-1:250)
  • Autosomal semi-dominant inheritance
  • Usually not evident at birth
  • Fine, white, flaky scales typically on extensor surfaces
  • Sparing of flexural areas
  • Hyperlinear palms and soles
  • Seasonal variation (worse in winter, better in summer)
  • Improves with age

Associated features: Keratosis pilaris and atopic triad (asthma, hay fever, atopic dermatitis). 25%-50% of patients with ichthyosis vulgaris have atopic dermatitis, and up to 50% of Northern Europeans with atopic dermatitis have ichthyosis vulgaris.

The boundary between dry skin (xerosis) and mild ichthyosis vulgaris is not always clear-cut. Look for classic distribution pattern, hyperlinear palms, and family history to aid diagnosis.

Pathology

  • Mild orthokeratotic hyperkeratosis
  • Diminished or absent granular layer
  • Reduced or absent filaggrin staining by immunohistochemistry

Treatment

  • Emollients, humectants, and keratolytic agents (urea, glycolic acid, lactic acid, salicylic acid)
  • Skin irritation limits use of topical retinoids
  • Systemic retinoids rarely indicated
  • Moisturizing cleansers and humidifiers

Steroid Sulfatase Deficiency (X-linked Ichthyosis)

Genetics: Deletion of the entire STS gene on chromosome Xp22.31 in ~90% of patients, inactivating mutations in others.

Key Features
  • Incidence: 1:2000-1:9500 male births
  • X-linked recessive inheritance
  • Presents during neonatal period with mild erythroderma and peeling
  • Later develops dark brown, adherent scales
  • Characteristic "dirty neck" appearance
  • Involvement of preauricular area and neck
  • Sparing of palms, soles, and face
  • Does not significantly subside with age

Mothers pregnant with affected fetuses often experience delayed or prolonged labor due to low estrogen levels (inadequate deconjugation of DHEAS by placental steroid sulfatase).

Associated Features

  • Corneal opacities (10%-50% of patients)
  • Cryptorchidism
  • Higher prevalence of attention deficit hyperactivity and mood disorders
  • Increased risk of atrial fibrillation/flutter in men

Male patients with larger contiguous gene deletions may have Kallmann syndrome, X-linked chondrodysplasia punctata, ocular albinism, intellectual disability/autism, or short stature.

Diagnostic Tests

  • FISH and microarray analysis to detect STS deletion
  • Steroid sulfatase enzymatic activity in leukocytes/fibroblasts
  • Increased migration of B-fraction in serum lipoprotein electrophoresis
  • Elevated cholesterol sulfate in serum or scales

Treatment

  • Topical humectants (especially propylene glycol)
  • Keratolytics and retinoids
  • Vitamin D analogues less effective and cause irritation
  • Systemic retinoids rarely necessary

Keratinopathic Ichthyoses

Epidermolytic Ichthyosis (EI)

Genetics: Heterozygous mutations in KRT10 (~75%) and KRT1 (~25%). Mutations alter highly conserved amino acid residues at the boundaries of the α-helical rod region (helix initiation and termination motifs).

Key Features
  • Incidence: 1:200,000-1:350,000
  • Autosomal dominant inheritance (rare AR cases)
  • Presents at birth with erythroderma, blistering, and erosions
  • Later develops hyperkeratosis with cobblestone pattern
  • Ridging of the flexures
  • Skin fragility and blistering decrease over time
  • Palmoplantar keratoderma (PPK) with KRT1 mutations
  • Malodor, skin infections, and contractures

Histopathology: Dense orthokeratotic hyperkeratosis, prominent acanthosis, hypergranulosis, and cytolysis of the suprabasal and granular layers leading to small intraepidermal blisters. Keratinocytes exhibit marked intracellular vacuolization and dense clumps of keratin intermediate filaments.

KRT1 mutations typically result in severe palmoplantar keratoderma, while KRT10 mutations often spare palms and soles. KRT1 mutations also tend to respond less well to retinoid therapy.

Variants of Epidermolytic Ichthyosis

  • Superficial Epidermolytic Ichthyosis (SEI): Less severe, caused by KRT2 mutations, cytolysis limited to granular layer, characteristic "molting" of skin, palms and soles spared
  • Epidermolytic Epidermal Nevi: Mosaic form of EI with unilateral or bilateral streaks following lines of Blaschko; postzygotic KRT1/10 mutations

Treatment

  • Neonatal intensive care (prevent dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, infections)
  • Topical emollients, keratolytics, and gentle mechanical debridement
  • Antiseptics to control bacterial colonization
  • Oral retinoids may reduce hyperkeratosis but increase skin fragility
  • Low initial doses with gradual increase and careful monitoring

Ichthyosis with Confetti

Key Features
  • Rare autosomal dominant disorder
  • Heterozygous frameshift mutations in KRT10 or KRT1
  • Born with ichthyosiform erythroderma
  • During childhood, development of hundreds to thousands of small, confetti-like spots of normal-appearing skin
  • Normal skin areas increase in size and number over time
  • Represents revertant mosaicism via mitotic recombination

The MAUIE variant (micropinnae, alopecia universalis, ichthyosis, and ectropion) is associated with an increased risk of melanoma and keratinocyte carcinomas.

Ichthyosis Hystrix Curth-Macklin

Key Features
  • Heterozygous mutations in KRT1 causing abnormal keratin protein with aberrant tail
  • No skin fragility (unlike epidermolytic ichthyosis)
  • Cobblestone-like or ridged hyperkeratosis over joints and in skin folds
  • May have severe, mutilating PPK
  • Diagnostic ultrastructural findings: shell-like network of tangled KIFs

Note: "Ichthyosis hystrix" is a descriptive term for massive hyperkeratosis with verrucous or spiny appearance, not a specific entity. Can be seen in several disorders including EI, ichthyosis hystrix Curth-Macklin, and HID syndrome.

Autosomal Recessive Congenital Ichthyoses (ARCI)

ARCI encompasses a clinically and genetically heterogeneous group of nonsyndromic ichthyoses that are present at birth or shortly thereafter and feature generalized scaling with variable erythema.

Clinical Spectrum of ARCI

The spectrum of ARCI phenotypes ranges from:

  • Lamellar Ichthyosis (LI): Large, dark, plate-like scales with minimal or no erythroderma
  • Congenital Ichthyosiform Erythroderma (CIE): Fine white scales and pronounced erythroderma
  • Harlequin Ichthyosis (HI): Most severe form with armor-like hyperkeratosis at birth
  • Intermediate phenotypes: Variable degrees of erythema and scaling

Common features include PPK, hypohidrosis, heat intolerance, scarring alopecia, and conductive hearing loss.

Molecular Genetics of ARCI

ARCI is caused by pathogenic variants in at least 13 genes encoding proteins that contribute to the structure and function of lipid and cornified cell envelopes:

  • TGM1 (transglutaminase-1): Most common cause (30-70% of ARCI), especially in classic LI
  • ABCA12: Complete absence causes harlequin ichthyosis; partial function results in CIE/LI phenotype
  • ALOX12B/ALOXE3: Lipoxygenases involved in lipid processing; often CIE phenotype
  • NIPAL4 (ichthyin): Magnesium transporter involved in lipid processing
  • CYP4F22: Cytochrome P450 enzyme involved in acylceramide formation
  • Other genes: PNPLA1, CERS3, SULT2B1, SDR9C7, LIPN, KDSR, etc.

Collodion Baby

  • Shared presentation of several congenital ichthyoses
  • Not a distinct disorder
  • Taut, shiny, transparent membrane at birth
  • Leads to ectropion, eclabium, and restricted movement
  • Membrane dries, cracks, and sheds within 2-4 weeks
  • Transitions to underlying disease phenotype (usually LI/CIE)
  • In self-improving collodion ichthyosis (~10%), skin becomes normal or has mild scaling

Neonatal Management

  • Humidified incubator
  • Monitor for fluid/electrolyte imbalances
  • Prevent skin infections and sepsis
  • Wet compresses and light emollients
  • Avoid manual removal of collodion membrane
  • Release of constrictive bands if needed
  • Systemic retinoids rarely necessary

Lamellar Ichthyosis (LI)

Genetics: Most commonly caused by TGM1 deficiency (~70-90% of classic LI cases).

Key Features
  • Usually presents with collodion membrane at birth
  • Large, brown, plate-like scales in a generalized distribution
  • Minimal to no erythroderma
  • Centrally attached scales with raised borders
  • Ectropion, eclabium, and ear deformities
  • Scarring alopecia
  • Palmoplantar keratoderma in 50% of cases
  • Hypohidrosis and heat intolerance

"Bathing suit ichthyosis" is a variant caused by temperature-sensitive TGM1 mutations that primarily affects the trunk and scalp.

Congenital Ichthyosiform Erythroderma (CIE)

Key Features
  • Genetics: Heterogeneous disorder with mutations in multiple genes:
    • ALOXE3 & ALOX12B: Involved in production of epidermal lipids, important for skin barrier function
    • NIPAL4: Ichthyin mutations affect transmembrane protein
    • ABCA12: Controls lipid transport protein, may cause severe forms of the disorder, some harlequin ichthyosis cases evolve into CIE
    • TGM1: Some mutations of transglutaminase 1 cause nonbullous CIE
    • Sphingosine-1-phosphate lyase: Loss-of-function mutations, associated with nephrosis, ichthyosis, and adrenal insufficiency
  • Presents at birth with erythroderma or collodion membrane
  • Generalized erythroderma with fine, white, powdery scales
  • More pronounced erythema than LI
  • Scales may become larger and darker on lower extremities
  • Severe palmoplantar involvement
  • Hypohidrosis and heat intolerance
  • Growth retardation in severe cases
  • Unlike LI, typically no significant ear or nose deformities
  • Ectropion may be present but usually less severe than in LI
  • Increased risk of skin cancer in adulthood

Harlequin Ichthyosis (HI)

Genetics: Biallelic loss-of-function mutations in ABCA12, leading to complete absence of this transporter protein in the skin.

Key Features
  • Most severe form of congenital ichthyosis
  • Incidence approximately 1:300,000 births
  • Usually born prematurely
  • Encased in a hard, armor-like thickened stratum corneum
  • Massive hyperkeratotic plates separated by deep, red fissures
  • Severe ectropion, eclabium, and ear deformities
  • High risk of dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and infections
  • Survivors develop severe CIE-like phenotype
  • Prenatal Diagnosis:
    • Fetal skin biopsy - Absent lamellar bodies on electron microscopy
    • Premature hyperkeratosis around hair follicles on H&E stain
  • 🔍 Postnatal Histology:
    • Extraordinarily thickened and compact orthokeratotic stratum corneum
    • Variable parakeratosis
  • Poor Prognosis: Most infants die within weeks due to:
    • Respiratory failure
    • Sepsis
    • Hypothermia
    • Hypernatremic dehydration
    • Complications of prematurity

    • Early administration of oral retinoids (acitretin, 0.5-1 mg/kg daily) has improved survival rates in HI. With improved care, >50% of affected individuals now survive beyond the neonatal period.

    Ichthyosis Prematurity Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Rare autosomal recessive condition
    • Mutations in SLC27A4 (encoding fatty acid transport protein 4/FATP4)
    • Presents with premature birth
    • Thick, yellow-white, spongy hyperkeratosis resembling vernix caseosa
    • Cobblestone-like appearance favoring scalp, forehead, and trunk
    • Neonatal asphyxia and respiratory distress
    • Neonatal findings resolve within a few months
    • Later mild, fine scaling and follicular keratoses
    • Peripheral eosinophilia and atopic manifestations

    ARCI Treatment

    • Topical therapy: Emollients, keratolytics, urea, lactic acid, sodium bicarbonate baths
    • Systemic retinoids: Often necessary for severe disease (acitretin preferred)
    • Emerging therapies: IL-17 or IL-12/23 inhibitors for inflammatory component
    • Experimental approaches: Topical enzyme replacement, acylceramide supplementation, gene therapy
    • Supportive care: Management of hypohidrosis, ectropion, infections
    • Psychosocial support: Patient advocacy groups like FIRST

    Syndromic Ichthyoses

    Netherton Syndrome

    Genetics: Biallelic mutations in SPINK5 gene, encoding the multi-domain serine protease inhibitor LEKTI. Uncontrolled serine protease activity affects lipid processing and skin barrier function.

    Key Features
    • Incidence: Possibly as high as 1:50,000
    • Autosomal recessive inheritance
    • Presents at birth with generalized erythroderma and scaling
    • No collodion membrane
    • Two principal phenotypes:
      • Ichthyosis linearis circumflexa (ILC) - polycyclic erythematous plaques with double-edged scale
      • CIE-like generalized erythroderma and scaling
    • Pruritus and eczematous plaques
    • Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair)
    • Highly elevated serum IgE levels
    • Atopic manifestations and food allergies
    • Failure to thrive in infancy
    • Recurrent infections
    • Histopathology:
      • Psoriasiform dermatitis
      • Parakeratosis
      • Premature secretion of lamellar body contents
      • Subcorneal clefting

    Hair shaft abnormalities: Trichorrhexis invaginata ("bamboo hair") with ball-and-socket appearance is pathognomonic. Hair from the eyebrows provides the highest diagnostic yield. Other abnormalities include pili torti, trichorrhexis nodosa, and "golf tee" or "matchstick" appearances.

    Laboratory findings: Markedly elevated serum IgE levels (100 to >10,000 IU/ml). Eosinophilia and nonspecific aminoaciduria may also be present.

    Netherton syndrome patients have an increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma of the skin and oral mucosa. They also have a dramatically increased susceptibility to mucocutaneous infections, especially with Candida albicans.

    Diagnosis & Treatment

    • Diagnosis: Clinical features, trichoscopy, light microscopic hair shaft analysis, serum IgE measurement, SPINK5 mutation analysis, immunostaining with anti-LEKTI antibodies
    • Treatment: Topical emollients, keratolytics, calcipotriene; topical calcineurin inhibitors (with monitoring for systemic absorption); IVIG; oral antihistamines; anti-IL-17 or IL-12/23 agents; nutritional support

    Sjögren-Larsson Syndrome

    Genetics: Deficiency of microsomal fatty aldehyde dehydrogenase (FALDH) due to mutations in ALDH3A2.

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive neurocutaneous disorder
    • Presents at birth with variable erythema and scaling
    • Later develops ichthyosis ranging from fine to plate-like scaling
    • Predilection for lower abdomen, neck, and flexural areas
    • Palmoplantar keratoderma in 70% of patients
    • Prominent pruritus in almost half of patients
    • Pathognomonic perifoveal glistening white dots in fundus
    • Progressive neurologic manifestations:
      • Delayed motor development
      • Spastic di- or tetraplegia
      • Intellectual disability
      • Speech defects
      • Seizures in 40% of patients
      • White matter disease on brain MRI
    • Pathology:
      • Histopathologic features are nonspecific
      • Primarily orthokeratotic hyperkeratosis, papillomatosis, and moderate acanthosis with well-developed granular layer
      • Ultrastructural abnormalities not diagnostic
      • Keratinocytes may show mitochondrial proliferation and prominent Golgi apparatus, with lamellar specificity
    • Diagnostic Tests:
      • Cultured skin fibroblasts, keratinocytes, and leukocytes have drastically reduced or absent FALDH activity
      • Assay allows detection of unaffected carriers (who exhibit half of normal levels)
      • Detection of elevated free fatty alcohols in cultured fibroblasts and plasma

    Diagnosis & Treatment

    • Diagnosis: Clinical features, reduced FALDH activity in cultured fibroblasts/leukocytes, ALDH3A2 mutation analysis
    • Treatment: Multidisciplinary approach, topical keratolytics and emollients, oral retinoids, zileuton (5-lipoxygenase inhibitor) for pruritus in some patients

    Neutral Lipid Storage Disease with Ichthyosis AKA Chanarin-Dorfman Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in ABHD5, encoding an activator of adipose triglyceride lipase
    • Generalized fine, white scaling with variable erythema
    • Systemic manifestations:
      • Hepatomegaly with fatty liver
      • Myopathy
      • Cataracts
      • Hearing impairment
      • Developmental delay
      • Ataxia
    • Pathognomonic lipid-containing vacuoles in circulating granulocytes (Jordan anomaly)
    • Diagnostic Findings:
      • Highly characteristic: numerous lipid-containing vacuoles in circulating granulocytes (Jordan anomaly) and monocytes
      • Detectable on fresh peripheral blood smear (not by automated blood-counting methods)
      • Lipid stains (oil red O, Sudan III) or antibodies (adipophilin) can detect cytoplasmic lipid droplets in skin sections
      • Lipid droplets found in epithelium of eccrine sweat glands and ducts, fibroblasts, and basal keratinocytes
      • Ultrastructural abnormalities include globular electron-lucent inclusions disrupting lamellar bodies in granular layer

    The presence of lipid vacuoles in circulating granulocytes (Jordan anomaly) is easily detectable on a fresh peripheral blood smear and highly diagnostic of NLSDI.

    Trichothiodystrophy with Ichthyosis

    Key Features
    • Primarily autosomal recessive inheritance
    • Sulfur-deficient brittle hair with characteristic "tiger tail" pattern under polarized microscopy
    • Collodion membrane in ~30% of patients
    • Generalized scaling with absent or minimal erythroderma
    • Photosensitivity in ~50% of patients
    • Hair Manifestations:
      • Obligatory finding: short, unruly, fragile hair of scalp, eyebrows, and eyelashes due to abnormally low sulfur content (deficient cysteine and/or methionine)
      • Body, axillary, and pubic hair may also be affected
      • Nail changes: thinning, longitudinal ridging, yellow discoloration, splitting, and onychogryphosis
    • Associated features:
      • Intellectual disability
      • Short stature
      • Microcephaly
      • Nail abnormalities
      • Gonadal abnormalities
    • Pathology:
      • Hair shafts show transverse fractures (trichoschisis) with irregular surface and diameter
      • May show nodal appearance similar to trichorrhexis nodosa or 180° twists as in pili torti
      • Polarizing light microscopy is gold standard: characteristic light and dark bands ("tiger-tail" or "zigzag" pattern)
      • Transmission electron microscopy shows sulfur-deficient longitudinal intercellular fibrils
      • Histopathology usually similar to ichthyosis vulgaris, occasionally with perinuclear vacuoles

    Genetics: Mutations in genes encoding components of the nucleotide excision repair pathway, particularly ERCC2/XPD and ERCC3/XPB in photosensitive forms. Unlike xeroderma pigmentosum, there is no increased risk of skin cancer.

    Peeling Skin Syndromes

    Peeling skin syndromes (PSS) are a heterogeneous group of disorders characterized by continual, spontaneous shedding of the stratum corneum. They can be classified based on clinical presentation and genetic causes.

    Classification of Peeling Skin Syndromes

    Type Gene/Protein Inheritance Key Features
    Generalized Inflammatory PSS CDSN (corneodesmosin) AR Erythroderma with fine white scales, widespread peeling, pruritus, atopic manifestations, high IgE
    Acral PSS TGM5 (transglutaminase 5) or CSTA (cystatin A) AR Peeling and erosions primarily on hands and feet, painless, asymptomatic
    Autosomal Recessive Exfoliative Ichthyosis CSTA (cystatin A) or SERPINB8 AR Peeling skin on palms, soles, elbows, knees; fine white scaling on trunk and extremities
    Generalized Non-inflammatory PSS CHST8 or FLG2 (profilaggrin 2) AR Generalized non-inflammatory scaling and peeling, especially on extremities; improves with age
    PLACK Syndrome CAST (calpastatin) AR Peeling skin, leukonychia, acral punctate keratoses, cheilitis, knuckle pads
    Erythrokeratolysis Hiemalis CTSB (cathepsin B) AD Episodic or seasonal circinate erythematous plaques with scaling and centrifugal peeling, primarily on palms/soles

    Generalized Inflammatory Peeling Skin Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in CDSN (corneodesmosin)
    • Presents at birth or in early infancy
    • Generalized erythroderma with fine white scales
    • Widespread, patchy superficial (subcorneal) peeling of the skin
    • Pronounced pruritus
    • Fine, sparse hair during infancy
    • Mild failure to thrive and staphylococcal skin infections during infancy
    • Atopic manifestations (asthma, food allergies)
    • Elevated serum IgE levels

    Netherton syndrome may also present with a phenotype of generalized peeling skin and erythroderma, clinically resembling generalized inflammatory PSS. Hair shaft examination for trichorrhexis invaginata and measurement of serum IgE can help distinguish these conditions.

    Histopathology

    • Hyperkeratosis, parakeratosis
    • Subcorneal or intracorneal separation
    • Superficial perivascular infiltrate
    • Detachment occurs above granular layer

    Acral Peeling Skin Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in TGM5 (transglutaminase 5) or CSTA (cystatin A)
    • Painless, spontaneous periodic peeling of the skin limited to dorsal and palmar aspects of hands and feet
    • Worsened by mechanical trauma, heat, humidity
    • More superficial peeling with TGM5 mutations
    • CSTA mutations may cause more diffuse fine scaling
    • No associated extracutaneous manifestations

    PLACK Syndrome

    Key Features

    Peeling skin, Leukonychia, Acral punctate keratoses, Cheilitis, Knuckle pads

    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in CAST (calpastatin), a protease inhibitor
    • Generalized peeling of the skin
    • White discoloration of the nails (leukonychia)
    • Small keratotic papules on palms and soles
    • Inflammation and scaling of the lips
    • Hyperkeratotic plaques over knuckles

    Erythrokeratolysis Hiemalis

    Key Features
    • Also known as keratolytic winter erythema or Oudtshoorn disease
    • Autosomal dominant disorder
    • Segmental duplications involving CTSB (cathepsin B)
    • Episodic or seasonal circinate erythematous plaques
    • Scaling and centrifugal peeling
    • Predominantly affects palms and soles, may extend to limbs
    • Occasional blisters in center of new plaques
    • Often triggered by viral infection or fever
    • Typically worse in winter or cooler months
    • Improves with age

    Treatment of Peeling Skin Syndromes

    • Primarily symptomatic and supportive
    • Emollients and moisturizers
    • Topical keratolytics for hyperkeratotic areas
    • Topical steroids for inflammatory variants
    • Treatment of secondary infections
    • Antihistamines for pruritus
    • Systemic retinoids may be beneficial in some cases
    • Avoidance of triggers (mechanical trauma, heat, etc.)

    Erythrokeratodermas

    Erythrokeratodermas are disorders of cornification characterized by sharply demarcated keratotic plaques that are symmetrically distributed, often with migratory erythematous patches.

    Erythrokeratodermia Variabilis et Progressiva (EKVP)

    Genetics: Heterozygous mutations in GJB3 (connexin 31), GJB4 (connexin 30.3), or occasionally GJA1 (connexin 43).

    Key Features
    • Predominantly autosomal dominant inheritance
    • Presents in infancy (90% within first year)
    • Two main components:
      • Transient, migratory erythematous patches (most prevalent during childhood)
      • Stable hyperkeratotic plaques (persist for months to years)
    • Well-demarcated, geographic plaques with sharp borders
    • Burning/stinging sensation may precede erythema
    • Predilection for extremities, buttocks, and lateral trunk
    • Face and scalp often spared
    • Palmoplantar keratoderma in ~50% of patients
    • Progressive worsening in childhood, stabilization after puberty

    EKVP encompasses both classic erythrokeratodermia variabilis (with migratory erythema) and progressive symmetric erythrokeratoderma (without migratory component), as they can be caused by identical mutations and observed within the same family.

    Treatment

    • Topical keratolytics, urea, retinoids for mild disease
    • Systemic retinoids (acitretin, isotretinoin) for extensive disease
    • Often dramatic improvement at lower doses than required for other ichthyoses
    • Camouflaging makeup for erythematous patches
    • Avoidance of triggers (temperature changes, friction, irritation)

    Keratitis-Ichthyosis-Deafness (KID) Syndrome

    Genetics: Heterozygous missense mutations in GJB2, encoding connexin 26. The D50N mutation is most common (>80% of cases).

    Mosaic forms: Can cause porokeratotic eccrine nevi. As with other mosaic conditions, the existence of a Porokeratotic eccrine nevi may confer increased risk of transmitting a connexin mutation causing KID syndrome in offspring.

    Key Features
    • Rare disorder, >90% sporadic cases
    • GJB6 (connexin 30) mutations = rare cause with congenital atrichia
    • Transient erythroderma at birth or in infancy
    • Well-demarcated, erythematous, hyperkeratotic plaques
    • Predilection for face and extremities
    • Characteristic radial furrows around mouth
    • Diffuse thickening of skin with "coarse-grained leather" appearance
    • Palmoplantar keratoderma with stippled surface
    • Follicular keratoses
    • Follicular occlusion triad (puberty onset)
    • Cysts and proliferating pilar tumors
    • Congenital sensorineural hearing loss
    • Progressive vascularizing keratitis
    • Increased susceptibility to infections, especially Candida albicans
    • Nail dystrophy and hair abnormalities

    Complications: 10-20% of patients develop squamous cell carcinoma of the skin or oral mucosa, with a median age of 25 years. The G45E and A88V mutations cause a very severe, often fatal form of KID syndrome in infancy.

    Hystrix-like ichthyosis with deafness (HID) syndrome is now considered a clinical subtype of KID syndrome with more severe skin findings but less severe keratitis.

    Progressive Symmetric Erythrokeratoderma (PSEK)

    Key Features
    • Fixed, slowly progressive, erythematous, hyperkeratotic plaques
    • Sharp, figurate borders
    • Favor cheeks, knees, elbows
    • Different genetic causes:
      • KRT83 (keratin 83) mutations
      • TRPM4 (transient receptor potential cation channel) mutations - perioral distribution
      • LOR (loricrin) mutations - associated with mutilating PPK/pseudoainhum
    • No transient, migratory erythematous component (unlike EKVP)

    Erythrokeratoderma-Cardiomyopathy Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal dominant disorder caused by DSP (desmoplakin) mutations
    • Congenital, generalized erythrokeratoderma/ichthyosiform erythroderma
    • Palmoplantar keratoderma
    • Pruritus
    • Early childhood onset of progressive dilated cardiomyopathy (life-threatening)
    • Sparse, coarse hair
    • Nail dystrophy
    • Dental abnormalities (enamel defects, hypodontia)
    • Variable photophobia/corneal opacities
    • Response to ustekinumab reported

    Periorificial and Ptychotropic Erythrokeratoderma (PERIOPTER) Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • KDSR (3-ketodihydrosphingosine reductase) mutations
    • Hyperkeratotic plaques with predilection for periorificial sites and skin folds
    • Transgredient palmoplantar keratoderma
    • Associated thrombocytopenia

    Erythrokeratodermia with Spinocerebellar Ataxia

    Key Features
    • Also known as Giroux-Barbeau syndrome
    • Variant of spinocerebellar ataxia-34
    • Autosomal dominant inheritance
    • ELOVL4 (elongase-4 for very long-chain fatty acids) mutations
    • Migratory erythematous patches and/or erythematous, hyperkeratotic plaques in infancy/childhood
    • Fine, white, adherent scales on extremities (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists)
    • Skin lesions diminish or disappear during adulthood
    • Progressive neurologic symptoms develop during 4th-5th decades:
      • Nystagmus
      • Ataxia
      • Dysarthria
      • Hyporeflexia

    X-linked Dominant Ichthyosiform Disorders

    CHILD Syndrome

    Genetics: Inactivating mutations in NSDHL, encoding 3β-hydroxysteroid-dehydrogenase, a key enzyme in the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway.

    Key Features
    • X-linked dominant disorder (lethal in most affected males)
    • Presents at birth with striking unilateral distribution of skin lesions
    • Initial erythema and yellowish adherent scale
    • Later development of verrucous hyperkeratotic plaques or bands
    • Sharp demarcation at the midline
    • Predilection for skin folds (ptychotropism)
    • Right side more commonly affected than left
    • Ipsilateral skeletal abnormalities (hypoplasia to amelia)
    • Ipsilateral organ hypoplasia (brain, kidneys, heart, lungs)
    • Stippled epiphyses in early infancy

    Name: CHILD = Congenital Hemidysplasia with Ichthyosiform erythroderma (or nevus) and Limb Defects

    Pathogenesis-based therapy targeting the underlying cholesterol pathway defect using topical simvastatin or lovastatin (with or without cholesterol) has shown substantial benefit in CHILD syndrome.

    Conradi-Hünermann-Happle Syndrome

    Genetics: Mutations in EBP, encoding emopamil-binding protein with 3β-4β-sterol isomerase activity in the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway.

    Key Features
    • X-linked dominant disorder (lethal in most males)
    • Presents at birth with erythroderma and feathery, adherent scale
    • Linear streaks and whorls of hyperkeratosis along lines of Blaschko
    • Erythroderma resolves within weeks to months
    • Later development of follicular atrophoderma and patchy scarring alopecia
    • Stippled epiphyses during infancy
    • Asymmetric skeletal anomalies
    • Unilateral or sectorial cataracts
    • Short stature and facial dysmorphism

    The allelic X-linked recessive MEND syndrome (male EBP disorder with neurologic defects) affects males and features a collodion-like presentation at birth, variable ichthyosis, and multisystem involvement.

    Histopathology

    • Hyperkeratosis with focal parakeratosis
    • Follicular plugging with dystrophic calcification
    • Small vacuoles with needle-like calcium inclusions in keratinocytes

    Diagnosis

    • Radiographic evidence of stippled epiphyses
    • Elevated 8(9)-cholestenol and 8-dehydrocholesterol in plasma, scales, or cultured fibroblasts
    • Molecular analysis of EBP gene

    Ichthyosis Follicularis, Atrichia, and Photophobia (IFAP) Syndrome

    Key Features
    • X-linked disorder due to MBTPS2 mutations
    • Affects primarily males (severe phenotype)
    • Female carriers may show linear streaks of follicular hyperkeratosis and patchy alopecia
    • Congenital atrichia with absence of eyebrows and eyelashes
    • Follicular keratoses ("nutmeg grater" skin)
    • Psoriasiform plaques
    • Photophobia and vascularizing keratitis leading to blindness
    • Increased susceptibility to infections
    • BRESHECK syndrome represents a severe variant with additional features:
      • Brain anomalies
      • Retardation (intellectual disability)
      • Ectodermal dysplasia
      • Skeletal malformations
      • Hirschsprung disease/Hernias
      • Ear/Eye anomalies
      • Cleft palate/Cryptorchidism
      • Kidney dysplasia/hypoplasia

    IFAP syndrome, keratosis follicularis spinulosa decalvans, and Olmsted syndrome are allelic disorders caused by different mutations in MBTPS2, highlighting the phenotypic diversity resulting from mutations in the same gene.

    Rare Syndromic Ichthyoses

    MEDNIK Syndrome

    Key Features

    Mental disability, Enteropathy, Deafness, Neuropathy, Ichthyosis, Keratoderma

    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in AP1S1 (adaptor-related protein complex 1 σ-1 subunit)
    • Erythematous patches and hyperkeratotic plaques of variable sizes and shapes
    • Chronic diarrhea and enteropathy
    • Sensorineural hearing loss
    • Peripheral neuropathy
    • Intellectual disability and growth delay
    • Abnormal copper metabolism

    CEDNIK Syndrome

    Key Features

    Cerebral dysgenesis, Neuropathy, Ichthyosis, and Keratoderma

    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in SNAP29 (SNARE protein involved in intracellular vesicle transport)
    • Normal skin in infancy, later widespread large scale with variable erythema
    • Palmoplantar keratoderma
    • Microcephaly, neuropathy, intellectual disability
    • Hearing impairment, optic nerve atrophy
    • Cachexia, dysmorphic facial features
    • Usually fatal within first decade of life

    Keratitis-Ichthyosis-Deafness, Autosomal Recessive (KIDAR) Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in AP1B1 (adaptor-related protein complex 1 β-1 subunit)
    • Ichthyosiform erythroderma from birth or early infancy
    • Thick, adherent yellow scale on the scalp
    • Mild palmoplantar keratoderma
    • Sensorineural hearing loss
    • Ectropion, photophobia, and corneal scarring
    • Failure to thrive during infancy
    • Mild growth and developmental delay
    • Thrombocytopenia

    Arthrogryposis-Renal Dysfunction-Cholestasis (ARC) Syndrome

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in VPS33B (~75%) or VIPAS39 (~25%)
    • In ~50% of patients, generalized fine to large scales with sparing of skin folds
    • Arthrogryposis (joint contractures)
    • Renal tubular dysfunction
    • Neonatal jaundice, cholestasis
    • Hemorrhage due to platelet dysfunction
    • Often lethal within first year of life

    ARC syndrome is allelic with ARKID (autosomal recessive keratoderma, ichthyosis, deafness) syndrome, which has a milder phenotype with diffuse PPK, mild generalized ichthyosis, and progressive sensorineural hearing loss.

    Refsum Disease

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in PHYH (phytanoyl-CoA hydroxylase) or PEX7
    • Ichthyosis vulgaris-like scaling in ~50% of patients
    • Onset of ichthyosis typically in childhood
    • Clinical manifestations usually begin in adolescence:
      • Night blindness (earliest symptom)
      • Mixed motor/sensory peripheral polyneuropathy
      • Cerebellar ataxia
      • Retinitis pigmentosa
      • Hypo/anosmia
      • Later: sensorineural hearing loss, cataracts, renal dysfunction, cardiomyopathy
    • Markedly elevated plasma phytanic acid levels (>200 μmol/L)

    Treatment with a diet severely restricted in phytanic acid (found in dairy products and animal fats) can arrest neurologic progression in Refsum disease, though it cannot reverse established retinal changes.

    Multiple Sulfatase Deficiency

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in SUMF1 (sulfatase-modifying factor 1)
    • Scaling develops within first two years of life
    • Similar to steroid sulfatase deficiency, but often less severe
    • Features may resemble metachromatic leukodystrophy or mucopolysaccharidoses:
      • Coarse facies
      • Skeletal abnormalities (dysostosis multiplex)
      • Sensorineural deafness
      • Progressive neurologic deterioration
    • Reduced activity of multiple sulfatases

    Rhizomelic Chondrodysplasia Punctata

    Key Features
    • Autosomal recessive disorder
    • Mutations in PEX7 (peroxisomal type 2 targeting signal receptor)
    • Ichthyosis and alopecia in ~25% of patients
    • Erythroderma and thick, sticky vernix with underlying adherent scale at birth
    • Later follicular atrophoderma and scarring alopecia
    • Rhizomelia (short proximal limbs)
    • Stippled epiphyses on radiographs (visible only during infancy)
    • Facial dysmorphism
    • Bilateral congenital cataracts
    • Severe neurodegeneration, usually fatal by 2 years of age

    Specialized Diagnostic Testing

    Establishing the correct diagnosis in a patient with ichthyosis requires a systematic approach utilizing clinical, laboratory, and genetic tests in conjunction.

    Histopathological Evaluation

    Disorder Key Histopathological Features
    Ichthyosis vulgaris Diminished or absent granular layer, orthokeratotic hyperkeratosis
    Epidermolytic ichthyosis Epidermolytic hyperkeratosis: hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, vacuolization of upper epidermis, clumped keratin filaments
    Lamellar ichthyosis Massive orthokeratotic hyperkeratosis, normal or mildly acanthotic epidermis, normal granular layer
    Congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma Parakeratosis (focal or extensive), pronounced acanthosis, increased epidermal cell turnover
    Netherton syndrome Psoriasiform dermatitis, parakeratosis, premature secretion of lamellar body contents, subcorneal clefting
    Neutral lipid storage disease Lipid-containing vacuoles in epidermis and eccrine gland epithelium
    Peeling skin syndromes Subcorneal or intracorneal separation, detachment above granular layer
    CHILD syndrome Parakeratosis with retained round nuclei, foamy histiocytes in dermal papillae (verruciform xanthoma-like)

    Hair Shaft Analysis

    Netherton Syndrome

    • Light microscopy: Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair) with ball-and-socket appearance
    • Other findings: pili torti, trichorrhexis nodosa, "golf tee" or "matchstick" appearance
    • High-yield sampling: eyebrow hairs often show abnormalities earlier than scalp hairs
    • Multiple hairs should be examined from different sites
    • Repeated sampling may be necessary if initial results are negative

    Trichothiodystrophy

    • Light microscopy: Transverse fractures (trichoschisis), irregular surface and diameter
    • Polarized microscopy: "Tiger tail" or "zigzag" banding pattern
    • Sulfur content analysis: Reduced cystine/sulfur content (50-60% of normal)
    • Amino acid analysis: Decreased cysteine after hydrolysis
    • Scanning electron microscopy: Flattened hair with irregular surface and trichoschisis

    Specialized Biochemical Testing

    Transglutaminase-1 Testing (Lamellar Ichthyosis)
    • Immunohistochemistry: Staining with anti-TGM1 antibodies to detect presence/absence of TGM1 protein
    • In situ TGM1 activity assay: Covalent incorporation of biotinylated substrate peptides into skin cryostat sections
    • Cell envelope analysis: Testing for presence of cross-linked cell envelopes
    • TGM1 activity in cultured keratinocytes: Quantitative measurement of enzyme activity

    Note: These tests can be performed even in collodion babies before transition to underlying disease phenotype.

    Steroid Sulfatase Deficiency Testing
    • Lipoprotein electrophoresis: Increased migration of B-fraction
    • LC-MS analysis: Elevated cholesterol sulfate in serum or scales
    • Enzyme activity assay: Measurement in leukocytes, fibroblasts, or keratinocytes
    • Maternal hormones: Low or absent estrogen in urine and amniotic fluid during pregnancy
    • Molecular testing: FISH or microarray for STS deletion
    Sjögren-Larsson Syndrome Testing
    • FALDH activity assay: Cultured skin fibroblasts, keratinocytes, or leukocytes show drastically reduced or absent activity
    • Elevated free fatty alcohols: Detected in cultured fibroblasts and plasma
    • Ophthalmologic examination: Perifoveal glistening white dots in fundus (pathognomonic)
    • Brain MRI: White matter disease
    • Molecular testing: ALDH3A2 gene sequencing
    Neutral Lipid Storage Disease Testing
    • Peripheral blood smear: Lipid vacuoles in circulating granulocytes and monocytes (Jordan anomaly)
    • Histochemical stains: Oil red O or Sudan III to detect cytoplasmic lipid droplets in frozen sections
    • Immunohistochemistry: Adipophilin antibodies to detect lipid droplets in paraffin-embedded sections
    • Electron microscopy: Globular electron-lucent inclusions disrupting lamellar bodies
    • Molecular testing: ABHD5 gene sequencing
    CHILD Syndrome and Conradi-Hünermann-Happle Syndrome Testing
    • Radiography: Stippled epiphyses (visible only during infancy)
    • Sterol analysis:
      • CHILD: Normal plasma cholesterol despite defective biosynthesis
      • CHH: Accumulation of 8(9)-cholestenol and 8-dehydrocholesterol in plasma, scales, or cultured fibroblasts
    • Molecular testing: NSDHL (CHILD) or EBP (CHH) gene sequencing

    Genetic Testing Strategies

    Molecular diagnosis can be established in ~90% of patients with ichthyosis using appropriate genetic testing.

    Next-Generation Sequencing Panels

    • Comprehensive ichthyosis/keratoderma panels include genes for various forms
    • Cost-effective compared to sequential single-gene testing
    • May identify unexpected genetic causes
    • Particularly useful when clinical diagnosis is unclear
    • Limited by gene content of panel (may miss rare causes)

    Single-Gene Testing

    • May be appropriate when clinical diagnosis is highly suggestive
    • Examples:
      • FLG for classic ichthyosis vulgaris
      • STS for X-linked ichthyosis
      • TGM1 for classic lamellar ichthyosis
      • SPINK5 for Netherton syndrome
    • Less cost-effective for heterogeneous disorders

    In ARCI patients with a collodion membrane at birth, plate-like scale, ectropion, and/or alopecia, TGM1 mutations are approximately four times more likely than mutations in other ARCI genes.

    Prenatal Diagnosis

    • Molecular prenatal diagnosis: Utilizing samples of chorionic villi, amniotic fluid, or circulating fetal cells
    • Fetal ultrasonography:
      • Harlequin ichthyosis: Intrauterine growth retardation, polyhydramnios, "snowflake sign" due to floating scales in amniotic fluid
      • Conradi-Hünermann-Happle syndrome: Asymmetric limb shortening, stippled epiphyses
    • Biochemical analysis:
      • X-linked ichthyosis: Decreased maternal serum estriol levels or presence of non-hydrolyzed sulfated steroids in maternal urine

    Establishing a molecular diagnosis is essential for:

    • Accurate genetic counseling
    • Prognostic information
    • Family planning
    • Prenatal and preimplantation genetic diagnosis
    • Guiding therapy selection

    Comprehensive Treatment Approaches

    Topical Therapy

    Emollients and Humectants

    • Mechanisms: Increase water content of stratum corneum, restore barrier function
    • Examples: Petrolatum, mineral oil, glycerin, propylene glycol
    • Application: Multiple times daily, especially after bathing
    • Considerations: Ointments more effective but greasier than creams or lotions

    Keratolytics

    • Mechanisms: Promote desquamation, reduce hyperkeratosis
    • Examples:
      • Urea (5-40%)
      • Salicylic acid (2-6%)
      • α-hydroxy acids (lactic acid, glycolic acid)
      • N-acetylcysteine
    • Cautions: Irritation, systemic absorption (especially salicylic acid in children)

    Retinoids

    • Mechanisms: Normalize keratinocyte differentiation and proliferation
    • Examples: Tretinoin (0.025-0.1%), tazarotene (0.05-0.1%), adapalene
    • Side effects: Irritation, erythema, dryness
    • Limitations: Often poorly tolerated in ichthyosis

    Other Topical Agents

    • Vitamin D analogues: Calcipotriene (calcipotriol) can be beneficial in some forms (e.g., Netherton syndrome)
    • Calcineurin inhibitors: Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus for inflammatory component (caution with systemic absorption in Netherton syndrome)
    • Antimicrobials: For secondary infections (chlorhexidine, sodium hypochlorite baths)
    • Compound formulations:
      • Lactic acid + propylene glycol
      • Urea + N-acetylcysteine

    Addition of sodium bicarbonate to bath water (2 handfuls in half-full standard tub) to raise pH to ~7.9 one to three times weekly may facilitate scale removal in lamellar ichthyosis.

    Systemic Therapy

    Oral Retinoids: First-line systemic therapy for severe ichthyoses. Acitretin is generally preferred due to its favorable risk-benefit profile.

    Retinoid Dosing Indications Response
    Acitretin Initial: 0.5 mg/kg/day
    Maintenance: 0.2-0.5 mg/kg/day    		 ARCI, epidermolytic ichthyosis, erythrokeratodermas Excellent in ARCI, variable in other types
    Isotretinoin Initial: 0.5 mg/kg/day
    Maintenance: 0.1-0.5 mg/kg/day    		 Similar to acitretin, shorter half-life beneficial in women of childbearing potential Generally less effective than acitretin
    Alitretinoin 10-30 mg/day KID syndrome, recalcitrant cases Limited data, promising in some cases

    For harlequin ichthyosis, early administration of oral retinoids (acitretin, 0.5-1 mg/kg daily) has dramatically improved survival rates by accelerating shedding of massive hyperkeratotic plates.

    Retinoid Side Effects and Monitoring

    • Common side effects: Mucocutaneous dryness, cheilitis, conjunctivitis, hair loss, pruritus
    • Monitoring parameters:
      • Lipid profile (elevated triglycerides, cholesterol)
      • Liver function tests
      • Complete blood count
      • Pregnancy testing (teratogenic)
    • Long-term concerns:
      • Skeletal abnormalities (diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis, ligament calcification)
      • Premature epiphyseal closure in children
      • Reduced bone mineral density

    Emerging and Targeted Therapies

    Biologic Agents
    • Anti-IL-17 agents: Secukinumab, ixekizumab
      • Target Th17-associated inflammation in ichthyosis
      • Reports of benefit in Netherton syndrome
      • Modest results in clinical trials for erythrodermic ichthyoses
    • IL-12/23 inhibitors: Ustekinumab
      • Case reports of benefit in erythrokeratodermia-cardiomyopathy syndrome and Netherton syndrome
    • TNF inhibitors: Under investigation for Netherton syndrome
    • IL-4/13 inhibitors: Under investigation
    Enzyme Replacement and Substrate Reduction
    • Topical enzyme replacement:
      • Recombinant human TGM1 for TGM1-deficient ARCI
    • Topical lipid therapy:
      • Acylceramide supplementation for ARCI
    • Topical simvastatin/lovastatin:
      • With or without cholesterol for CHILD syndrome
      • Targets underlying cholesterol pathway defect
    • Reactive aldehyde species inhibitor:
      • Reproxalap under investigation for Sjögren-Larsson syndrome
    Gene and RNA Therapy
    • Topical gene therapy:
      • Replication-defective herpes simplex virus type 1 vector encoding human TGM1 for TGM1-deficient ARCI
    • Allele-specific siRNA:
      • To inhibit expression of mutant GJB2 in KID syndrome
    • Read-through compounds:
      • For nonsense mutations that cause premature termination codons
    • Ex vivo gene correction:
      • Using CRISPR/Cas9 or TALEN technology for epidermolytic ichthyosis and other forms

    Management of Specific Complications

    Ectropion Management

    • Regular ophthalmologic evaluation
    • Lubricating eye drops and ointments
    • Topical retinoids (e.g., tazarotene) to soften periocular skin
    • Surgical repair for severe cases

    Heat Intolerance/Hypohidrosis

    • Air conditioning and humidifiers
    • Frequent moistening of skin with water
    • Avoiding excessive heat exposure
    • Adequate hydration

    Neonatal Management

    • Humidified incubator
    • Monitoring for fluid/electrolyte imbalances
    • Prevention of skin infections and sepsis
    • Release of constricting bands if needed
    • Caloric supplementation

    Multidisciplinary care is essential for many syndromic ichthyoses and may involve:

    • Dermatologists
    • Ophthalmologists
    • Otolaryngologists (for hearing impairment)
    • Neurologists (for neurologic manifestations)
    • Orthopedic surgeons
    • Dietitians
    • Psychosocial support (patient advocacy groups like FIRST)

    High-Yield Clinical Associations

    Ichthyosis vulgaris and atopic dermatitis: 25-50% of patients with ichthyosis vulgaris have atopic dermatitis, and up to 50% of Northern Europeans with atopic dermatitis have ichthyosis vulgaris.

    KID syndrome and squamous cell carcinoma: 10-20% of patients develop SCC of the skin or oral mucosa, with a median age of 25 years.

    X-linked ichthyosis and labor complications: Mothers pregnant with affected fetuses often experience delayed or prolonged labor due to low estrogen levels (inadequate deconjugation of DHEAS by placental steroid sulfatase).

    Netherton syndrome diagnostic triad: Ichthyosis (often ichthyosis linearis circumflexa), trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair), and atopic manifestations with elevated IgE levels.

    Sjögren-Larsson syndrome pathognomonic finding: Perifoveal glistening white dots in the ocular fundus, representing a form of juvenile macular dystrophy.

    High-Yield Histopathology

    Must-Know Histopathologic Features

    • Ichthyosis vulgaris: Diminished or absent granular layer
    • Epidermolytic ichthyosis: Epidermolytic hyperkeratosis - vacuolization and clumping of keratin in upper epidermis
    • Netherton syndrome: Psoriasiform dermatitis, premature secretion of lamellar body contents
    • Neutral lipid storage disease: Jordan anomaly - lipid vacuoles in granulocytes
    • CHILD syndrome: Parakeratosis with round nuclei, foamy histiocytes
    • Peeling skin syndromes: Subcorneal or intracorneal separation

    Hair Shaft Abnormalities

    • Netherton syndrome: Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair) - best seen in eyebrow hairs
    • Trichothiodystrophy: "Tiger tail" pattern under polarized microscopy, trichoschisis
    • Menkes syndrome: Pili torti, monilethrix, trichorrhexis nodosa
    • Ichthyosis-hypotrichosis syndrome: Pili torti/pili bifurcati

    Key Differential Diagnostic Pearls

    Neonatal Erythroderma with Scaling/Peeling
    • Collodion membrane present: ARCI (especially LI/CIE), self-improving collodion ichthyosis, trichothiodystrophy
    • Blisters/erosions: Epidermolytic ichthyosis, staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome, epidermolysis bullosa
    • Continuous peeling: Netherton syndrome, peeling skin syndromes
    • Unilateral/linear distribution: CHILD syndrome, Conradi-Hünermann-Happle syndrome
    • Extreme hyperkeratosis with fissures: Harlequin ichthyosis
    Double-Edge Scale Disorders
    • Netherton syndrome: Ichthyosis linearis circumflexa with double-edged scale, elevated IgE, hair shaft abnormalities
    • Peeling skin syndromes: No hair shaft abnormalities, no associated atopic features (except in inflammatory PSS)
    • Acrodermatitis enteropathica: Perioral, perianal, acral distribution; associated with zinc deficiency
    Ichthyosis with Hair Abnormalities
    • Netherton syndrome: Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair), elevated IgE
    • Trichothiodystrophy: Brittle, sulfur-deficient hair with "tiger tail" pattern, photosensitivity in 50%
    • IFAP syndrome: Congenital atrichia, photophobia, follicular keratoses
    • Ichthyosis-hypotrichosis syndrome: Sparse, brittle scalp hair, woolly appearance
    Ichthyosis with Neurologic Features
    • Sjögren-Larsson syndrome: Spastic di/tetraplegia, intellectual disability, perifoveal glistening dots
    • Refsum disease: Peripheral neuropathy, cerebellar ataxia, retinitis pigmentosa, elevated phytanic acid
    • Multiple sulfatase deficiency: Progressive neurodegeneration, features resembling mucopolysaccharidoses
    • CEDNIK syndrome: Cerebral dysgenesis, neuropathy, microcephaly
    • MEDNIK syndrome: Mental disability, peripheral neuropathy, abnormal copper metabolism
    • Erythrokeratodermia with spinocerebellar ataxia: Ataxia developing in 4th-5th decades

    Treatment Pearls

    Specific Therapies

    • CHILD syndrome: Topical simvastatin/lovastatin ± cholesterol
    • Refsum disease: Dietary restriction of phytanic acid
    • Sjögren-Larsson syndrome: Zileuton (5-lipoxygenase inhibitor) for pruritus
    • KID syndrome: Regular skin cancer surveillance

    On the board exam, when presented with a case of a newborn with skin peeling/scaling and prolonged labor, think of X-linked ichthyosis due to steroid sulfatase deficiency.

    For a collodion baby, the management approach should include a humidified environment, emollients, and avoidance of manual membrane removal; not early intensive topical keratolytic treatment.

    When examining a patient with suspected Netherton syndrome, always sample eyebrow hairs for microscopic examination, as they more consistently show trichorrhexis invaginata compared to scalp hairs.

    Quick Reference Flashcards

    Collodion Baby Differential

    What are the most common underlying causes?

    • 1. Lamellar ichthyosis (TGM1 mutations)
    • 2. Congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma
    • 3. Self-improving collodion ichthyosis (10%)
    • 4. Trichothiodystrophy (30% have collodion)
    • 5. Rarely: Sjögren-Larsson, Gaucher, MEND syndrome

    Pathognomonic Findings

    What are the diagnostic clinical findings for these disorders?

  • Netherton: Trichorrhexis invaginata (bamboo hair)
  • Sjögren-Larsson: Perifoveal glistening white dots
  • NLSDI: Jordan anomaly (lipid vacuoles in granulocytes)
  • TTD: "Tiger tail" pattern of hair under polarized light
  • CHILD: Unilateral distribution with sharp midline demarcation
  • X-linked ichthyosis: Corneal opacities (50-90% of cases)
  • Refsum disease: Retinitis pigmentosa and peripheral neuropathy

    • High Risk Cancer Associations

    Which ichthyosis types have increased cancer risk?

    KID syndrome: SCC of skin/oral mucosa (10-20%, median age 25)

    Netherton syndrome: SCC (especially anogenital)

    CIE: SCC and BCC in adulthood

    Ichthyosis with confetti: Melanoma and keratinocyte carcinomas (MAUIE variant)
