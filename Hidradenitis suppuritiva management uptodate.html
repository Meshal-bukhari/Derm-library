Hidradenitis Suppurativa

A Board-Focused Clinical Management Masterclass

Core Concepts & Staging

HS is a chronic, painful, follicular occlusive disease of intertriginous skin. Management goals are threefold:

1

Reduce new lesions, tunnels, & scarring.

2

Treat existing lesions & symptoms (pain, drainage).

3

Minimize impact on Quality of Life (QoL) & psychological morbidity.

Clinical Staging: The Hurley System is Key

I

Stage I (Mild)

Single or multiple abscesses (nodules).
NO skin tunnels.
NO scarring.

II

Stage II (Moderate)

Recurrent abscesses with widely separated lesions.
YES, skin tunnels.
YES, scarring.

III

Stage III (Severe)

Diffuse or near-diffuse involvement.
MULTIPLE interconnected tunnels & abscesses.

Foundation of Care: Interventions for ALL Patients

💡 Patient Education & Support

  • Reassure: It is chronic, inflammatory, NOT contagious, and NOT due to poor hygiene.
  • Assess Impact: Inquire about QoL, screen for depression/anxiety.
  • Provide Resources: HS Foundation, support groups.

🩹 Wound & Skin Care

  • Dressings: Use non-adherent dressings (apply petrolatum). Avoid adhesive tape; use fishnet dressings.
  • Cleansing: Topical antiseptic washes (chlorhexidine 4%, benzoyl peroxide) may be helpful for some patients but are not routinely prescribed to avoid stigma.

💊 Pain Management

  • First-line: NSAIDs for pain and inflammation.
  • Severe Pain: Opioid analgesia may be needed, but requires conscientious selection and a multidisciplinary approach for chronic pain.

⚖️ Lifestyle Factors

  • Smoking Cessation & Weight Loss: Advise routinely due to potential HS benefit and proven general health benefits. Obesity-related friction, occlusion, and hormonal changes are proposed contributors.

Treatment Algorithm: A Step-Wise Approach

HURLEY I: Inflammatory Lesions WITHOUT Tunnels or Scarring

First-Line Therapy

Oral Tetracyclines

Initial choice for reducing disease burden. Anti-inflammatory effects are key.

Doxycycline 100mg QD-BID

Precautions: GI distress, photosensitivity.

Adjunctive / Alternative First-Line Options

Antiandrogenic Agents (Females)

Consider early for menstrual flares or PCOS. Use OCPs or Spironolactone.

Spironolactone 50-100mg daily

Precautions: Menstrual irregularities, breast tenderness, hyperkalemia. Contraindicated in pregnancy.

Metformin

Consider early in patients with obesity/metabolic syndrome. Promotes modest weight loss.

Titrate up to 1500mg/day (e.g., 500mg TID)

Precautions: GI side effects are common. Contraindicated in severe renal dysfunction.

Topical Clindamycin 1%

Often used as adjunct. Monotherapy is rarely sufficient.

Apply BID to affected areas

Refractory Mild Disease (Failure of above)

For patients who fail to improve after ~3 months.

Clindamycin + Rifampin

A potent combination for more resistant disease.

See Hurley II/III dosing
Dapsone

Good for early, neutrophil-mediated lesions.

50-100mg daily

Precautions: Hemolysis (check G6PD!), methemoglobinemia, agranulocytosis.

Acitretin

Systemic retinoid option.

Absolute Contraindication: Patients of childbearing potential. 3-year washout period post-treatment.

HURLEY II/III: Inflammatory Lesions WITH Tunnels or Scarring

Initial Therapy

Oral Tetracyclines

For less severe end of this spectrum (Hurley II).

Doxycycline 100mg QD-BID
Clindamycin + Rifampin

Preferred initial regimen for extensive, inflammatory disease (Hurley III).

Clinda 300mg BID + Rifampin 300mg BID for 10-12 weeks

Precautions: Rifampin causes orange secretions & reduces OCP efficacy. Multiple drug interactions.

+ Adjuncts (Antiandrogens/Metformin) as needed

Failure of Initial Therapy → Biologics!

Assess after ~3 months. If response is poor, move to biologics or acitretin.

🥇 Preferred First Biologic: Adalimumab (TNF-α)
Adalimumab (Humira)

FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe HS. Supported by high-quality trial data (PIONEER I & II).

Adults: 160mg Day 1 → 80mg Day 15 → 40mg weekly starting Day 29

Precautions: Standard TNF-α inhibitor risks (infection, etc). Screen for latent TB before starting.

💡

Non-Biologic Alternative: Acitretin
Reserved for patients who cannot use or prefer to avoid biologics. Remember the teratogenicity!

Failure of Adalimumab

Assess after 12-16 weeks. If poor response, switch biologic agent.

🥈 Next-Line Biologic Options
Secukinumab (IL-17A)

Strong trial data (SUNSHINE/SUNRISE). FDA Approved.

Caution: May exacerbate or trigger IBD (rare).

Bimekizumab (IL-17A & IL-17F)

Strong trial data (BE HEARD I/II). Shows rapid response.

Risk of oral candidiasis. Similar IBD caution as other IL-17i.

Infliximab (TNF-α)

IV infusion. Some evidence suggests higher/more frequent dosing than for psoriasis may be needed.

Precautions: Infusion reactions, standard TNF-α inhibitor risks.

Targeted Interventions & Procedures

Acute, Symptomatic Lesions

For rapid relief of a single painful, inflamed nodule:

  • ✅ Intralesional Corticosteroids: Triamcinolone 10 mg/mL (0.2-1 mL). Reduces pain/inflammation in 1-2 days.
  • ✅ Punch Debridement: Minor "unroofing" to evacuate an acute nodule.
  • ✅ Topical Resorcinol 15%: Peeling agent applied directly to new nodule BID.
  • ❌ Routine Incision & Drainage (I&D): NOT recommended. Promotes recurrence and tunnel formation. Reserve only for immediate relief of a large, painful fluctuant abscess when other options aren't feasible.

Skin Tunnels & Recurrent Nodules

These persistent structures do not respond well to medical therapy alone and require a procedural approach.

  • ✅ Surgical Unroofing (Deroofing): The gold standard. The skin overlying the tunnel is removed, and the wound heals by secondary intention. High success rate for preventing local recurrence.

Severe, Refractory Disease (Hurley III)

For extensive, end-stage disease refractory to medical therapy.

  • ✅ Wide Local Excision: Offers the greatest chance for resolution in the treated area. A major procedure with significant recovery time. Best performed when inflammation is controlled medically.
  • ✅ Rescue Medical Therapies: For patients failing multiple biologics, consider IV Ertapenem, Ustekinumab, or Anakinra based on small studies and expert opinion.

Board Exam Spotlight & Clinical Traps

Trap: HS patient with new, rapidly growing ulcer in a chronic perianal plaque.

Answer: Suspect Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC). Long-standing inflammation is a major risk factor. Biopsy is mandatory.

Trap: Which common acne medication is generally INEFFECTIVE for HS and may worsen it?

Answer: Isotretinoin. While a mainstay for severe acne, it provides limited benefit for HS and should be avoided as monotherapy.

Trap: A 25-year-old female with Hurley II HS fails doxycycline. What is a key contraindication for the next-line oral option, acitretin?

Answer: Its potent teratogenicity. It is contraindicated in patients of childbearing potential. Pregnancy must be avoided for 3 years after stopping the drug.

Trap: Patient on clindamycin + rifampin is starting hormonal birth control. What is the critical counseling point?

Answer: Rifampin is a potent enzyme inducer and will significantly reduce the effectiveness of hormonal contraception. An alternative, non-hormonal method of birth control is required.

Trap: What is the primary management for persistent, draining skin tunnels?

Answer: Surgical Unroofing (Deroofing). Medical therapy alone is insufficient to resolve established tunnels.
