Hunter Syndrome (MPS II)

Mucopolysaccharidosis Type II - High-Yield Board Review

Definition

X-linked recessive lysosomal storage disease caused by deficiency in iduronate-2-sulfatase, leading to accumulation of heparan sulfate and dermatan sulfate in various organs.

Epidemiology

Rare disorder (1 in 170,000 live births). Higher incidence in Ashkenazi and Sephardic Jewish populations. Predominantly affects males but can occur in females (skewed X chromosome inactivation).

Onset & Prognosis

Severe form: Symptoms typically onset between 2-4 years. Average life expectancy 10-15 years.
Attenuated form: Minimal neurologic symptoms, often normal intelligence.

Clinical Manifestations

Mnemonic: "HUNTER"

Heavy facial features (coarse, distinctive)
Underdeveloped mental capacity (in severe form)
Nodular skin lesions (ivory-white papules)
Thoracic & cardiac concerns (valvular heart disease, obstructive airway)
Ear infections (recurrent) & hearing loss
Restricted joint mobility & skeletal deformities

Look specifically for distinctive facial features: broad nose, prominent supraorbital ridges, thick lips, enlarged and protruding tongue, large head circumference.

Distinctive Skin Findings

Ivory-white papules that often coalesce - a key dermatologic feature to recognize on Board exams.

Radiologic Findings

Dysostosis multiplex: abnormally thick bones, irregular epiphyseal ossification of joints in the upper limbs, abnormal rib shape.

Diagnostic Approach

Think of lysosomal storage disorders such as Hunter syndrome in a child with coarse facial features and developmental delay.

In cases highly suspicious for an MPS, a normal urine glycosaminoglycan (GAG) analysis does not rule out the diagnosis due to the potential for false negatives.

Diagnostic Workflow

Screening: Urine GAG measurement (excess urinary dermatan sulfate and heparan sulfate)
Confirmation: Absent or low iduronate-2-sulfatase activity in serum, leukocytes, or cultured fibroblasts
Genetic Testing: IDS gene analysis to identify affected males and carrier females
Prenatal Testing: If available, using enzyme activity assays or molecular analysis

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
MPS I or MPS VII Similar to Hunter syndrome, these patients excrete dermatan sulfate and heparan sulfate in urine. See Hurler syndrome (MPS I-H).
MPS III-VI Urinary GAG composition can help distinguish these from Hunter syndrome. See Maroteaux-Lamy syndrome (MPS VI).
Multiple sulfatase deficiency Multiple enzyme deficiencies affecting several pathways
Mucolipidosis type II Similar physical features but different enzyme deficiency
Mucolipidosis type III Similar physical features but different enzyme deficiency

Management & Treatment

Due to the multisystem nature of the disease, management of Hunter syndrome involves a multidisciplinary approach.

Multidisciplinary Management

Cardiology

Routine echocardiograms to monitor for valvular heart disease

Pulmonology

Routine pulmonary function testing

Sleep Medicine

Sleep study if obstructive sleep apnea is suspected

Otolaryngology (ENT)

Hearing evaluations

Ophthalmology

Routine eye examinations

Neurology

  • Developmental assessments
  • Consider nerve conduction studies for carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Consider head or cervical MRI if hydrocephalus or spinal cord compression is suspected

Treatment Options

Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT)

Mainstay of treatment in Hunter syndrome:

  • Drug: Idursulfase
  • Dosing: Weekly infusions at 0.5 mg/kg over 3 hours
  • Benefits: Improved 6-minute walking distance and forced vital capacity (FVC)
  • Limitations: Does not cross blood-brain barrier; doesn't affect cognition or behavior
  • Adverse Effects: Primarily infusion-related reactions

Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation

Has been used to increase enzyme activity, but its use is controversial given the potential for morbidity and mortality.

Research & Future Directions

Recent studies focusing on treatment mechanisms for central nervous system manifestations of the disease.

⚠️ Special Considerations During Procedures

Special care needs to be taken with sedation in these patients:

  • Endotracheal intubation can be difficult secondary to the anatomy of the mouth and throat
  • Neck hyperextension should be minimized due to atlantoaxial instability

Iduronate-2-sulfatase

High-Yield Board Questions

Question 1: Which enzyme deficiency is responsible for Hunter syndrome?

A. Alpha-L-iduronidase
B. Iduronate-2-sulfatase
C. N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase
D. Beta-glucuronidase

Correct Answer: B. Iduronate-2-sulfatase

Hunter syndrome (MPS II) is caused by a deficiency in iduronate-2-sulfatase. Alpha-L-iduronidase deficiency causes MPS I (Hurler syndrome), N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase deficiency causes MPS IVA (Morquio A syndrome), and beta-glucuronidase deficiency causes MPS VII (Sly syndrome).

Question 2: Which compounds accumulate in Hunter syndrome?

A. Heparan sulfate and dermatan sulfate
B. Keratan sulfate and chondroitin sulfate
C. Sphingomyelin and cholesterol
D. Gangliosides and ceramides

Correct Answer: A. Heparan sulfate and dermatan sulfate

In Hunter syndrome, the deficiency in iduronate-2-sulfatase leads to an accumulation of heparan sulfate and dermatan sulfate in various organs.

Question 3: What is a key dermatologic finding in Hunter syndrome?

A. Cafe-au-lait macules
B. Ivory-white papules that often coalesce
C. Angiofibromas on the face
D. Telangiectasias

Correct Answer: B. Ivory-white papules that often coalesce

Distinctive skin findings in Hunter syndrome include ivory-white papules that often coalesce, which is a key dermatologic feature to recognize.

High-Yield Points for Board Exam

  • Hunter syndrome is an X-linked recessive lysosomal storage disease caused by iduronate-2-sulfatase deficiency
  • Primarily affects males but can occur in females due to skewed X chromosome inactivation
  • Coarse facial features, developmental delay, and multisystem involvement are hallmark features
  • Distinctive skin finding: ivory-white papules that often coalesce
  • Diagnostic confirmation requires enzyme activity testing
  • Urinary GAGs include dermatan sulfate and heparan sulfate
  • Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) with idursulfase is the mainstay of treatment
  • ERT does not cross the blood-brain barrier and doesn't affect CNS symptoms
  • Special care is needed during sedation procedures due to difficult airway anatomy
  • X-ray shows dysostosis multiplex: abnormally thick bones, irregular epiphyseal ossification
