Megalencephaly‑Capillary Malformation (M‑CM)

PIK3CA‑mosaic (post‑zygotic)
Domain High‑Yield Details Exam Pearls
Genetics/Timing Activating PIK3CA mutations; mosaic, post‑zygotic. Presentation typically at birth. PI3K‑AKT‑mTOR axis Mosaic overgrowth
Core Triad

Megalencephaly: progressive; symmetric or asymmetric → macrocephaly with frontal bossing.

Capillary malformation (CM): widespread reticulated CM with sharp midline demarcation (esp. abdomen); persistent midfacial nevus simplex (glabellar “angel kiss”).

Asymmetric overgrowth: segmental body overgrowth.

 Triad = name! Glabellar nevus simplex persists Midline cutoff is classic

  • Developmental delay
  • Possible seizures
 Major morbidity driver Think neuro workup early
Characteristic MRI
  • Ventriculomegaly / hydrocephalus

MSK & Skin
  • Syndactyly (esp. 2nd–3rd toes)

  • Joint laxity / hypermobility
  • Hyperelastic, soft, “doughy” skin
 Digits: syndactyly is common
Differentiation M‑CM vs KTS/DCMO: Presence of megalencephaly + neurologic findings (hypotonia, DD) points to M‑CM; KTS/DCMO usually have normal intellect & head size. Head size + neuro = M‑CM Don’t be fooled by CM alone

Beckwith‑Wiedemann Syndrome (BWS)

11p15.5 imprinting defects (sporadic & variable)
Mnemonic Feature High‑Yield Details Board Tips
B Big Body & Hemihyperplasia
  • Macrosomia (LGA at birth)
  • Hemihyperplasia (segmental, asymmetric overgrowth)
 Key cancer‑risk subgroup Document laterality
I Insulin (Hyperinsulinism) Severe neonatal hypoglycemia requiring urgent management to prevent neurologic injury. Most critical neonatal emergency
G Gut (Abdominal Wall) Omphalocele (major criterion); also umbilical hernia, diastasis recti. Omphalocele screams BWS
M Macroglossia Prominent enlarged tongue → feeding issues, airway obstruction (sleep apnea), speech problems. Most recognizable feature
O Organomegaly Visceromegaly: kidneys (nephromegaly), liver (hepatomegaly), adrenals. Multiorgan overgrowth
U Unique Ear Creases/Pits Anterior earlobe creases & posterior helical pits. Soft sign but classic
T Tumors
  • Overall malignancy risk ≈ 7.5%, almost all in childhood (<8y)
  • Hallmark: Wilms tumor, Hepatoblastoma
  • Others: neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma (less common)
  • Risk higher with hemihyperplasia
 Long‑term management focus Think renal & hepatic screening
H Head & Skin
  • Facies: midface hypoplasia, prominent occiput
  • Skin: nevus flammeus (glabellar/forehead); possible café‑au‑lait macules
 “Stork bite on steroids”
Hallmark Exam clue High‑risk
Etiology: complex genetic/epigenetic defects of imprinted genes on 11p15.5; presentation is highly variable and often sporadic.
