Conradi Disease

Conradi-Hunermann-Happle Syndrome | Chondrodysplasia Punctata (X-linked dominant type)
ICD-10: Q77.3 | SNOMED-CT: 398958000

Quick Facts

Inheritance Pattern
X-linked dominant, transmitted in a mosaic fashion
Prevalence
Rare, fewer than 100 cases reported worldwide
Prognosis
Lethal in males, but affected females have normal intelligence and life span
Key Cause
Defect in cholesterol synthesis due to mutation of the emopamil-binding protein (EBP) gene

Synopsis

Conradi-Hunermann-Happle syndrome is a rare subtype of chondrodysplasia punctata, a heterogeneous group of skeletal dysplasias. It is caused by a defect in cholesterol synthesis due to a mutation of the emopamil-binding protein (EBP) gene, encoding EBP, and transmitted in a mosaic X-linked dominant fashion.

Conradi disease is characterized by a distinct constellation of features:

  • Short stature
  • Craniofacial defects
  • Sectorial cataracts
  • Ichthyosis
  • Coarse hair
  • Alopecia

Pathogenesis Simplified

Mutation in EBP gene on X chromosome
Defective cholesterol synthesis
Abnormal sterol accumulation (8(9) cholesterol)
Impaired embryonic development affecting multiple systems (skeletal, cutaneous, ocular, etc.)
Mosaic expression in females (explains Blaschko line distribution)

Clinical Presentation by Age

Birth
  • Diffuse erythroderma or bands of erythema
  • Patterned ichthyosis occurring along Blaschko lines
  • Unilateral sectorial cataracts at birth or soon after
  • Sometimes microphthalmia or microcornea
First Year
  • Scaling or hyperkeratosis
  • Coarse and lusterless hair
  • Nail changes
  • X-rays show stippled calcification of the trachea, spine, or long bone epiphyses (resolving with growth)
Adults
  • Linear or whorled atrophic lesions
  • Fine scaling and/or pigmentary (hyper- and hypopigmentation) lesions in Blaschko lines (mainly on limbs)
  • Scarring alopecia
  • Coarse and lusterless hair
  • Nail changes
Diagnostic Pearl: Always examine the mother for often subtle skin signs, as this is an X-linked dominant condition.

Skeletal & Systemic Manifestations

Skeletal Anomalies
  • Short stature
  • Shortening of the rhizomelic limbs
  • Epiphyseal stippling (pathognomonic)
  • Craniofacial defects
Ocular Features
  • Unilateral sectorial cataracts
  • Sometimes microphthalmia
  • Sometimes microcornea
Other System Involvement
  • Congenital heart defects
  • Sensorineural deafness
  • Central nervous system malformations
  • Renal malformations
Variable Features
  • Rounded or asymmetrical facies
  • Frontal bossing
  • Broad and flat nasal bridge
  • Kyphoscoliosis
  • Supernumerary digits

Differential Diagnosis

Disease Key Differentiating Features
CHILD Syndrome Erythroderma is usually unilateral
Ichthyosis linearis circumflexa (Netherton Syndrome) Double-edged scale, trichorrhexis invaginata, absence of atrophoderma
Congenital ichthyosiform erythroderma Has generalized skin changes (not following Blaschko lines)
Incontinentia pigmenti Atrophic changes do not evolve with age
High-yield for Saudi Board Exam: Understanding the distribution pattern of cutaneous findings is crucial for differentiating these conditions. Conradi disease has a characteristic Blaschko line distribution pattern that evolves with age.

Diagnostic Tests

Click to expand diagnostic approach
  • Radiography: X-rays show stippled calcification of the trachea, spine, or long bone epiphyses in the first year of life (resolving with growth)
  • Biochemical testing: Accumulation of 8(9) cholesterol can be shown in plasma with mass spectrophotometry
  • Genetic testing: Mutation analysis of the EBP gene
  • Histopathology: May show features of ichthyosis

Remember for your exam: The epiphyseal stippling seen on X-rays is most visible during the first year of life and then resolves with growth. This timing is critical for diagnosis.

Management

Management Pearl: Orthopedic and ophthalmology consults are needed.

Treatment of Cutaneous Manifestations

  • Scaling can be helped with emollients
  • Use of urea or lactic-acid products for hyperkeratosis
  • Antimicrobial therapy may be needed for skin infections (especially recurrent flexural infections)

Multidisciplinary Approach

  • Orthopedic management for skeletal anomalies
  • Ophthalmologic care for cataracts and other eye abnormalities
  • Cardiology evaluation if congenital heart defects are present
  • Audiology assessment for potential sensorineural deafness
  • Neurology consultation if central nervous system malformations are present

Conradi-Hunermann-Happle syndrome is the X-linked dominant type of chondrodysplasia punctata, characterized by Blaschko line distribution of skin findings and affected females with normal lifespan.

Mnemonic: "CONRADI" Features

Cataracts (sectorial, unilateral)

Osseous abnormalities (epiphyseal stippling)

Nail changes

Rhizomelic limb shortening

Alopecia (scarring)

Dominant X-linked inheritance

Ichthyosis along Blaschko lines

Exam Pitfall: Don't confuse the inheritance patterns of the different types of chondrodysplasia punctata. Conradi-Hunermann-Happle is X-linked dominant, while rhizomelic chondrodysplasia punctata is autosomal recessive.
