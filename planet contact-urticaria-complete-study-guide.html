Immunologic Contact Urticaria

An immune-mediated urticarial reaction to plant contact, distinct from toxin-mediated (non-immunologic) urticaria.

1️⃣ Definition & Overview

  • Type: Immune-mediated reaction (IgE/Type I Hypersensitivity)
  • Morphology: Primarily wheals, but can also present as erythema and dermatitis
  • Frequency: Relatively uncommon compared to non-immunologic plant reactions
  • Timing: Rapid onset (<30 minutes)

💭 Recall Question: What are the key differences between immunologic and non-immunologic contact urticaria?

Immunologic Contact Urticaria:

  • IgE-mediated reaction (Type I hypersensitivity)
  • Requires prior sensitization
  • Positive prick/scratch tests
  • Can progress to systemic symptoms
  • Can develop into chronic eczematous pattern (PCD)

Non-Immunologic (Toxin-Mediated) Contact Urticaria:

  • Direct effect of plant toxins/chemicals
  • No prior sensitization needed
  • No positive allergy tests
  • Usually limited to contact site
  • Does not develop into chronic dermatitis pattern

2️⃣ Epidemiology & Risk Factors

💡 Memory Hook: "F.O.O.D" Factors

  • F Frequent exposure to fresh fruits and vegetables
  • O Occupational - 95% of cases are work-related
  • O Ongoing dermatitis (pre-existing) increases risk
  • D Dermal barrier disruption (wet, macerated skin)

Key Risk Groups

  • Food Handlers: Long-time food handlers, especially with underlying dermatitis
  • Atopic Individuals: Atopy is a risk factor (but remember: 50% of PCD patients are NOT atopic)
  • Occupational Exposure: Approximately 95% of cases are work-related

🧠 Clinical Pearl: The Atopy Paradox

While atopy is a risk factor for immunologic contact urticaria, fully half of patients presenting with protein contact dermatitis (PCD) are not atopic. Don't rule out PCD in non-atopic patients with appropriate occupational exposure and symptoms.

💭 Recall Question: What is the main mediator in immunologic contact urticaria and what other mediators are involved?

Main mediator: Histamine (released from mast cells upon IgE-mediated activation)

Augmenting mediators: Prostaglandins, kinins, and leukotrienes

These mediators collectively produce the inflammatory response, with histamine playing the primary role in the development of urticarial wheals and pruritus.

4️⃣ Causative Agents (Plants)

Common Plant Triggers

  • Vegetables: Celery, onions, potatoes, lettuce
  • Fruits: Tomatoes, bananas, lemons
  • Herbs: Parsley, dill
  • Nuts: Various types
  • Others: Algae, lichens, shrubs, trees, grasses

Effect of Processing

🔍 Clinical Insight

Processing methods generally reduce allergenicity of plant triggers:

  • Cooking
  • Deep-freezing
  • Crushing
  • Industrial processing

This explains why patients may tolerate cooked forms of foods that cause reactions when raw.

5️⃣ Clinical Features

⏱️Timing

  • Rapid onset - typically within 30 minutes of contact
  • Usually requires contact with fresh plant material/foods

🔍Common Symptoms

  • Pruritus
  • Erythema
  • Urticarial swelling (wheals)
  • Occasionally, dyshidrotic-like vesicles
  • Sometimes only subjective: burning or tingling without objective findings

💡 Memory Hook: "PEWS" - Key Clinical Features

  • P Pruritus (itching at contact site)
  • E Erythema (redness of the skin)
  • W Wheals (urticarial swelling)
  • S Subjective symptoms (burning/tingling sometimes without visible changes)

Specific Syndromes

Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS)

Mechanism

Cross-sensitization between pollen allergens and similar allergens in certain fruits or vegetables.

Example: Birch pollen allergy cross-reacting with apples, celery, potatoes, etc.

Clinical Features

  • Sudden oral cavity itching, stinging, pain
  • Edema of lips, tongue, palate, pharynx
  • Potential progression to gastrointestinal symptoms or anaphylaxis
  • Typically better tolerance of the same foods when heated

Protein Contact Dermatitis (PCD)

Mechanism

Chronic dermatitis pattern resulting from repeated Type I urticarial reactions to large protein allergens.

Clinical Features

  • Affects hands and fingertips
  • Presents as chronic dermatitis
  • Characteristic sign: Immediate urtication (wheals/itching) within minutes of contact with the offending allergen
  • Testing pattern: Patch tests typically negative, but prick tests positive
  • 50% of patients are NOT atopic

💭 Recall Question: A 35-year-old chef presents with chronic hand dermatitis that flares with immediate itching and wheals when handling raw tomatoes. What is the likely diagnosis and what tests would you perform?

Likely diagnosis: Protein Contact Dermatitis (PCD)

Key diagnostic tests:

  • Prick test with fresh tomato (expected to be positive)
  • Patch test with tomato (expected to be negative)

Clinical reasoning:

  • Occupational exposure (chef - falls into the 95% occupational association)
  • Chronic dermatitis pattern on hands (typical location for PCD)
  • Immediate flare with contact (characteristic of Type I/IgE-mediated reaction)
  • Development of wheals on contact (urticarial component typical of PCD)

Remember: Even if this patient is not atopic, PCD is still possible as 50% of PCD patients have no atopy.

6️⃣ Diagnosis

Diagnostic Approach

  • Prick tests: Most sensitive for demonstrating IgE-mediated reaction
  • Scratch-chamber tests: Alternative method for confirming Type I reaction
  • Patch testing: Typically negative in Protein Contact Dermatitis (helps differentiate from ACD)
  • Clinical testing pattern: PCD = Prick (+), Patch (-)

💡 Memory Hook: "PIPE" - Differentiating PCD from ACD

  • P Prick test positive in PCD (negative in ACD)
  • I Immediate reaction in PCD vs. delayed in ACD
  • P Patch test negative in PCD (positive in ACD)
  • E Eczematous appearance in both, but PCD urticates on contact

7️⃣ Treatment

Primary Management

  • Avoidance: Identification and avoidance of the causative plant/food
  • Barrier protection: Gloves, protective clothing for occupational exposure
  • Processing modification: Cooking or processing foods when possible

Acute Treatment

  • Mild local reactions: Antihistamines, topical steroids
  • Systemic/Anaphylactic reactions: Parenterally administered epinephrine (adrenaline)
  • Chronic PCD: Management of underlying dermatitis with appropriate barrier repair

🎯 MUST MEMORIZE (Saudi Board Focus)

High-Yield Points

  1. Mechanism: IgE-mediated (Type I Hypersensitivity)
  2. Onset: Rapid (<30 minutes) after contact with fresh plant material
  3. Main Mediator: Histamine (released from mast cells)
  4. Key Risk Group: Food handlers (occupational, ~95% of cases)
  5. Allergenicity Reduction: Cooking/processing reduces allergenicity
  6. Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS): Pollen cross-reactivity → oral symptoms upon ingestion → often tolerated if heated
  7. Protein Contact Dermatitis (PCD): Chronic dermatitis pattern + immediate urtication on contact + Negative Patch Test + Positive Prick Test. Half of PCD patients are NOT atopic.
  8. Diagnosis: Prick/Scratch tests are key for demonstrating IgE mediation
  9. Treatment: Avoidance; Epinephrine for anaphylaxis

⚠️ POTENTIAL CONFUSION/TRICK AREAS FOR EXAMS

Watch Out For These Traps!

  1. PCD vs. ACD: This is the biggest trap. PCD looks like chronic eczema (like ACD might) but is caused by repeated Type I reactions. The key differentiator is the immediate urtication upon re-exposure and the testing pattern: PCD = Prick (+), Patch (-); ACD = Prick (-), Patch (+). Don't mistake chronic hand dermatitis in a food handler for simple ACD without considering PCD.
  2. Atopy Role in PCD: While atopy is a risk factor for immunologic urticaria generally, remember the specific point that 50% of PCD patients are NOT atopic. An exam question might present a non-atopic food handler with chronic hand dermatitis that flares immediately on contact with certain foods – this could still be PCD.
  3. Immediate vs. Delayed: Immunologic contact urticaria is immediate. Don't confuse it with delayed-type hypersensitivity reactions like standard plant ACD (e.g., poison ivy) or phytophotodermatitis.
  4. Clinical Morphology: It's not always just wheals. Think erythema, burning/tingling, vesicles, or the chronic eczematous pattern of PCD.
  5. OAS vs. Food Allergy: OAS is a contact urticaria of the oropharynx due to cross-reactive proteins, usually linked to aeroallergen sensitization (pollen). While it can lead to systemic symptoms, it's often milder than primary food allergy and frequently triggered by raw foods only.
  6. Testing Interpretation: Know why you'd do a prick test (for IgE/Type I/PCD) versus a patch test (for Type IV/ACD). A negative patch test does not rule out PCD.

1️⃣ Definition & Nomenclature

  • Type: Non-immunologic reaction (direct pharmacological effect)
  • Synonym: "Non-immunologic urticaria = Toxin-mediated urticaria"
  • Mechanism: Direct action of pharmacologically active substances (toxins) in the plant
  • Sensitization: Does not require prior exposure or sensitization

💭 Recall Question: How do toxin-mediated and immunologic contact urticaria differ in terms of who gets affected and why?

Toxin-Mediated (Non-immunologic) Contact Urticaria:

  • Affects anyone exposed to sufficient toxin
  • No prior sensitization is required
  • If 10 people touch a stinging nettle, all 10 will develop a reaction
  • Direct pharmacological action of toxins on skin

Immunologic Contact Urticaria:

  • Affects only previously sensitized individuals
  • Requires prior exposure to develop IgE antibodies
  • If 10 people touch a food allergen, only those sensitized will react
  • Immune-mediated (IgE/Type I hypersensitivity)

2️⃣ Epidemiology

Affected Population

  • Anyone exposed to sufficient toxin
  • No predisposing factors required
  • Far more common than immunologic contact urticaria
  • True incidence unknown - many don't seek medical attention

Common Causative Plants

  • Most Common (US): Stinging nettle (Urtica dioica)
  • Most Severe: Stinging Trees (Dendrocnide species) in Eastern Australia
  • Plant Families: Urticaceae, Euphorbiaceae, Hydrophyllaceae
  • Genera: Urtica, Laportea, Dendrocnide, Acidoton, Cnidoscolus, Wigandia

💡 Memory Hook: "HASTE" Mechanism

  • H Hypodermic needle-like trichomes
  • A Acetylcholine (key mediator)
  • S Serotonin (key mediator)
  • T Toxin directly injected (non-immunologic)
  • E Everyone exposed will react

4️⃣ Clinical Features

⏱️Timing & Duration

  • Onset: Very rapid
  • Wheal Peak: Maximal size 3-5 minutes after contact
  • Main Symptoms: Last 1-2 hours (erythema, burning, pruritus)
  • Persistent Symptoms: Paresthesias may last 12+ hours

🔍Common Symptoms

  • Wheals (urticaria)
  • Erythema
  • Burning
  • Pruritus
  • Sometimes only subjective symptoms (burning/tingling without objective findings)

🧠 Clinical Pearl: Severity Spectrum

Not all toxin-mediated reactions are equal:

  • Urtica dioica (Stinging Nettle): Common, usually benign, self-limited
  • Dendrocnide species (Stinging Trees): Can cause severe reactions lasting weeks, reactivated by water/cold, with severe intermittent stabbing pains along lymphatics. Potentially life-threatening in severe cases.

5️⃣ Pathology

Based on studies of Urtica dioica (stinging nettle) reactions:

Early Changes (5 minutes)

  • Dermal edema
  • Telangiectasias
  • Mild spongiosis may or may not be present
  • Mast cells not prominent at this stage

Later Changes (12 hours)

  • Edema resolves
  • Vasodilation persists
  • Spongiosis with neutrophilic and/or lymphocytic infiltrate may develop
  • Mast cells observed in the papillary dermis at this later time point

💭 Recall Question: Why do toxin-mediated reactions sometimes cause prolonged paresthesias that last much longer than the visible skin reaction?

According to the text, the mechanism for persistent paresthesias (that may last 12+ hours) is unclear and not fully explained by the known mediators (histamine, acetylcholine, and serotonin) that cause the early urticarial reaction.

This suggests there are likely additional, unidentified neurologically active components in the injected toxin mixture that affect sensory nerve function for a prolonged period.

This is particularly notable in severe reactions to Dendrocnide species (stinging trees), where symptoms can last for weeks and be reactivated by water or cold exposure.

6️⃣ Differential Diagnosis & Testing

Diagnostic Approach

  • Open application test: Most reliable test for toxin-mediated urticaria
  • Procedure: Apply 0.1 ml samples from dilutions onto 3x3 cm skin areas
  • Observation: Every 10-15 minutes for 1 hour
  • Result Timing: Maximal erythema/edema typically occurs 30-40 minutes after application

  • O Open application is the test of choice
  • P Pharmacologic direct action (non-immunologic)
  • E Everyone will test positive (no sensitization needed)
  • N Nettle (and similar plants) are typical causes

7️⃣ Treatment

Treatment Approaches

  • General approach: Most stings (e.g., from nettles) are benign, self-limited, and require no specific treatment
  • Trichome removal: Can be attempted using glue and gauze
  • Symptomatic relief:
    • Topical pramoxine (local anesthetic) may help
    • Oral analgesics may provide some relief

High-Yield Points

  1. Mechanism: NON-immunologic, direct TOXIN effect. Affects EVERYONE.
  2. Synonym: Toxin-mediated urticaria.
  3. Plant Structure: Trichomes (specialized stinging hairs).
  4. Chemicals (Nettle): Histamine, Acetylcholine, Serotonin.
  5. Onset: Rapid (wheals max 3-5 min).
  6. Duration: Symptoms mostly 1-2 hours (paresthesias longer).
  7. Common Cause (US): Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica).
  8. Severe Cause: Dendrocnide (Australian stinging trees).
  9. Testing: Open Application Test.
  10. Treatment: Usually none needed; symptomatic relief.

⚠️ POTENTIAL CONFUSION/TRICK AREAS FOR EXAMS

Watch Out For These Traps!

  1. Immunologic vs. Non-immunologic: This is the core distinction. Non-immunologic affects all, no prior sensitization needed, direct toxin effect. Immunologic requires sensitization (usually IgE), affects only sensitized individuals. An exam might describe a group exposure where everyone reacts – points to non-immunologic.
  2. Mediators: While histamine is involved here (like in allergic reactions), it's directly injected/released by the toxin, not primarily via IgE-mast cell degranulation. Also, acetylcholine and serotonin are key players here, less emphasized in standard allergic urticaria.
  3. Persistent Paresthesias: Note that the text specifically says histamine/acetylcholine/serotonin don't fully explain the prolonged paresthesias sometimes seen with nettles. Don't assume these mediators account for all symptoms.
  4. Testing: Open application test for toxin-mediated vs. Prick/Scratch test for immunologic. Don't mix them up.
  5. Trichomes vs. Glochids: Trichomes (nettles) are stinging hairs containing chemicals. Glochids (cacti) are barbed hairs causing mechanical irritation primarily. Ensure you know the difference.
  6. Severity Spectrum: Don't assume all non-immunologic urticaria is mild like typical nettle stings. Remember the Dendrocnide example for potentially life-threatening reactions from a toxin-mediated mechanism.

💭 Final Recall Question: A group of hikers walked through a patch of plants and all 5 of them developed immediate wheals, erythema, and burning sensations within minutes, which mostly resolved in 2 hours except for some persistent tingling. What is the most likely diagnosis, causative plant, and appropriate management?

Diagnosis: Toxin-Mediated (Non-immunologic) Contact Urticaria

Likely causative plant: Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica)

Key diagnostic features:

  • ALL exposed individuals developed reactions (indicates non-immunologic mechanism)
  • Very rapid onset (within minutes - characteristic of toxin-mediated reactions)
  • Self-limited symptoms (mostly resolved in 2 hours)
  • Persistent paresthesia (tingling) lasting longer than other symptoms

Management approach:

  • Usually no specific treatment needed for stinging nettle reactions
  • For symptomatic relief: topical pramoxine (local anesthetic) and/or oral analgesics
  • Attempt removal of any remaining trichomes using glue and gauze
  • Reassurance about benign, self-limited nature of typical nettle reactions
