Cutis Laxa: Essential Overview

Definition & Core Concept

Cutis laxa is a group of rare connective tissue disorders characterized by loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity due to defects in elastic fiber formation or structural abnormalities.

Memory Anchor: "L.A.X.A."

Loose skin (clinical hallmark)
Autosomal forms (dominant or recessive)
X-linked recessive form
Acquired variants

Classification at a Glance

Clinical Pearl: The Universal Finding

All forms of cutis laxa share the histological finding of sparse and/or fragmented elastic fibers, which underlies the clinical presentation of loose, inelastic skin.

High-Yield Exam Focus

Board exams frequently test on:

  • Distinguishing genetic variants (AD vs. AR vs. XLR)
  • Associated internal manifestations (especially severe in AR forms)
  • Recognizing "hound-dog facies" as a classic presentation
  • Common causes of acquired cutis laxa
  • Differential diagnosis from other connective tissue disorders
What is the most common genetic pattern in cutis laxa?

Autosomal Recessive (AR) forms are most common. These present early (birth to early childhood) and feature severe internal involvement along with skin manifestations.

Autosomal Dominant (AD) Cutis Laxa

Key Characteristics

  • Frequency: Less common than AR forms
  • Presentation Age: Early adulthood
  • Internal Involvement: Uncommon
  • Primary Affected Area: Skin
Genetic Mutations
  • Elastin gene (ELN) mutations
  • Fibulin 5 (FBLN5) mutations
  • These mutations lead to dysregulation of elastic fiber network in the skin
Clinical Manifestations

Primary Features:

  • Generalized cutaneous findings
  • Cardiac valve abnormalities
  • Aortic dilatation (variable)
  • Emphysema (uncommon)
  • Hernias

AD Type: Board Exam Focus

Remember that AD cutis laxa primarily affects the skin with minimal internal involvement. This is in stark contrast to AR forms, which have severe multi-organ manifestations.

What are the two main gene mutations in AD cutis laxa?

The two main gene mutations in AD cutis laxa are:

1. Elastin gene (ELN)

2. Fibulin 5 (FBLN5)

Autosomal Recessive (AR) Cutis Laxa

Key Characteristics

  • Frequency: Most common form
  • Presentation Age: Birth to early childhood
  • Pattern: Skin manifestations + severe internal involvement
Genetic Mutations
  • FBLN5
  • EFEMP2/FBLN4
  • LTBP4
  • ATPase
  • ATPGVOA2
  • PYCR1
  • ALDH18A1

ARCL Type I

ARCL Type I

Genetic Mutations:

  • FBLN5
  • EFEMP2
  • LTBP4

Clinical Features:

  • Potentially fatal involvement of lungs (hypoplastic lungs and emphysema)
  • Cardiovascular abnormalities (aortic tortuosity and aneurysms)
  • Inguinal/diaphragmatic/umbilical hernias
  • GI/GU diverticula
  • Joint laxity, arachnodactyly, and fractures (variable)

ARCL Type II

ARCL Type II

Genetic Mutations:

  • ATP6V0A2 (Type IIA)
  • PYRC1 (Type IIB)

Clinical Features:

  • Craniofacial anomalies
  • Cutaneous features may be primarily acral
  • Pachygyria (Type IIA)
  • Absent corpus callosum (Type IIB)
  • Translucent skin (Type IIB)

ARCL Type III (De Barsy Syndrome)

ARCL Type III (De Barsy Syndrome)

Genetic Mutations:

  • ALDH18A1 (Type IIIA)
  • PYRC1 (Type IIIB)

Clinical Features:

  • Developmental delay/dystonia/neurologic deterioration
  • Progeroid appearance
  • Athetosis
  • Corneal clouding/cataracts

Memory Anchor: "AR-CL 1-2-3"

ARCL Type I: Lungs & Vascular (Pulmonary & Cardiovascular)
ARCL Type II: Brain & Face (Craniofacial & Neurological)
ARCL Type III: De Barsy (Developmental & "Old appearance")

ARCL: Board Exam Focus

In board exams, expect cases presenting with multi-system involvement in infants/children. The combination of loose skin and internal organ complications, especially pulmonary/cardiovascular in Type I, should trigger ARCL diagnosis.

Clinical Pearl: De Barsy Syndrome

De Barsy syndrome (ARCL Type III) is characterized by its distinctive progeroid (premature aging) appearance combined with neurological dysfunction, making it a unique presentation among cutis laxa variants.

X-Linked Recessive (XLR) Cutis Laxa

Key Characteristics

XLR cutis laxa is now termed "occipital horn syndrome" (previously called Ehlers-Danlos syndrome [EDS] type IX)

Genetic Mutations
  • Mutations in ATPase, Cu(2+)-transporting, alpha polypeptide (ATP7A)
  • Allelic to Menkes disease
Clinical Manifestations
  • Easy bruising and coarse hair (variable)
  • Long face with high forehead and hooked nose
  • Wedge-shaped occipital calcifications (occipital horns) - pathognomonic
  • Hip dislocations (due to joint laxity)
  • Loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity

Clinical Pearl: Occipital Horns

The presence of occipital horns (wedge-shaped calcifications in the occipital bone) is pathognomonic for this condition and serves as an important radiological marker for diagnosis.

XLR Type: Board Exam Focus

Remember that XLR cutis laxa has been reclassified as "occipital horn syndrome." If you see a question about a male patient with cutis laxa features, coarse hair, and distinctive bone abnormalities, think of this condition.

What is the pathognomonic radiological finding in XLR cutis laxa?

Wedge-shaped occipital calcifications (occipital horns) are the pathognomonic radiological finding in XLR cutis laxa (occipital horn syndrome).

Acquired Cutis Laxa

Key Characteristics

  • Population: Primarily adults
  • Pattern: Sagging of skin with little associated internal involvement
  • Distribution: May be primarily acral; generalized involvement typically begins on the face
Associated Factors

Medications:

  • Penicillamine
  • Isoniazid

Cutaneous Disorders:

  • Cutaneous lymphoma
  • Sweet's syndrome-like eruption
  • Interstitial granulomatous dermatitis
  • Cutaneous mastocytosis

Systemic Diseases:

  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Infectious disorders

Clinical Pearl: Drug-Induced Cases

Penicillamine is the most frequently reported medication causing acquired cutis laxa. Always check medication history in adult patients presenting with new-onset skin laxity.

Acquired Type: Board Exam Focus

In the exam, distinguish acquired cutis laxa from hereditary forms by: adult onset, preceding inflammatory condition or drug exposure, and distribution pattern (often begins on face or is acral).

What medications are associated with acquired cutis laxa?

The two main medications associated with acquired cutis laxa are:

1. Penicillamine

2. Isoniazid

Universal Clinical Features

Across all forms of cutis laxa, these features are consistently present:

  • Loose, sagging skin with reduced elasticity and resilience
  • "Aged" facial appearance (hound-dog facies) with down-slanting palpebral fissures and a long philtrum
  • Deep voice due to vocal cord laxity
  • Histology: sparse and/or fragmented elastic fibers

Differential Diagnosis

Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS)

Characterized by joint hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility, and tissue fragility. Unlike cutis laxa, the skin in EDS snaps back after being stretched.

Marfan Syndrome

Features tall stature, arachnodactyly, lens dislocation, and aortic dilatation. Skin is not excessively lax as in cutis laxa.

Pseudoxanthoma Elasticum

Shows characteristic yellow papules in flexural areas with "plucked chicken skin" appearance. Elastic fiber calcification rather than fragmentation.

Mid-dermal Elastolysis

Selective loss of elastic fibers in the mid-dermis, with fine wrinkling rather than pendulous skin folds.

Comparative Board Exam Focus

Board exams often test on distinguishing features between these forms. Remember key discriminators:

  • Age of onset
  • Pattern of inheritance
  • Severity and type of internal organ involvement
  • Specific clinical markers (e.g., occipital horns in XLR form)
