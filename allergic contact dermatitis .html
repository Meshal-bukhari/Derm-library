I Definition and Classification

Contact Dermatitis Types
  • Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): Delayed-type (Type IV) hypersensitivity reaction (~20% of CD cases)
  • Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD): Direct cytotoxic effect on keratinocytes (~80% of CD cases); usually more "burning" than itchy
  • Contact Urticaria: Immediate (Type I) hypersensitivity reaction with wheals and flares

Epidemiology

  • Affects individuals of all ages, races, and both sexes
  • Sex differences based on exposure patterns (e.g., nickel allergy more frequent in women due to jewelry)
  • Occupational and recreational exposures significantly influence prevalence
What percentage of Contact Dermatitis cases are allergic vs. irritant?

Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): ~20% of cases

Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD): ~80% of cases

II Pathophysiology

Type IV Hypersensitivity (T-cell mediated, delayed reaction)

ACD is a classic example of a Type IV hypersensitivity reaction, which is cell-mediated rather than antibody-mediated.

Two-phase process

1. Sensitization Phase

  • Chemical (hapten) penetrates skin
  • Processed by Langerhans cells (key antigen-presenting cells)
  • Langerhans cells migrate to lymph nodes
  • Presented to T cells → memory T cell formation

2. Elicitation Phase

  • Re-exposure to allergen in sensitized individual
  • Rapid presentation to primed T cells
  • Release of cytokines and chemotactic factors
  • Inflammatory infiltrate (lymphocytes, monocytes, sometimes eosinophils)
  • Clinical eczema (spongiosis, vesicles, erythema, pruritus)

Once sensitized, only low concentration of allergen needed to elicit reaction

"S-E" for "Sensitization-Elicitation"
What are the two phases of ACD pathophysiology and which cells play the key role?

Two phases: Sensitization Phase and Elicitation Phase

Key cells: Langerhans cells (antigen-presenting cells)

III Clinical Presentations

Time-based Classification

  • Acute ACD: Erythema, vesiculation, bullae, weeping, edema; often very pruritic
  • Subacute: Less vesicular, more papular, erythema, scaling, fissuring
  • Chronic: Lichenification, scaling, fissuring, hyperkeratosis, hyper/hypopigmentation

Distribution Patterns

  • Localized/Well-demarcated: Confined to allergen contact site
  • Linear Streaks: Especially with poison ivy/oak or plant contact
  • Diffuse/Patchy: With airborne allergens, rinse-off products
  • Autoeczematization (Autosensitization): Widespread eczema from localized inflammatory focus

Common Distribution Patterns by Allergen

  • Nickel: Earlobes (earrings), neck (necklaces), wrists (watches), umbilicus (jeans buttons), hands (keys/coins)
  • Rubber: Hands (gloves), feet (shoes), elastic areas (underwear, bra)
  • Cosmetics/Preservatives: Face, eyelids, neck
  • Hair dye (PPD): Scalp, ears, forehead, neck
  • Plants (poison ivy): Linear or streaked vesicular lesions where plant touched skin
  • Shoes: Dorsum and soles of feet in pattern of shoe contact
  • MI/MCI: Hands, face, anogenital area (wet wipes)

Site-Specific Distribution Patterns

  • Scalp: Fairly resistant to allergens; often only surrounding skin involved (neck, cheeks, postauricular); common allergens: hair products (dyes), perms, rinse-off products (shampoo)
  • Eyelids/Periocular: Nail products (tosylamide > acrylates, formaldehyde, resin), cosmetics (false eyelashes, adhesives, mascara, makeup sponges), volatile gases, fragrances, preservatives
  • Lips: Gallates, dyes, flavorings, sunscreens, propolis
  • Perioral/Baboon syndrome: Flavorings, foods, cosmetics, shellac, medications, sunscreens
  • Neck: Fragrances, hair products
  • Wrists: Chromates (leather), watches
  • Hands: Gloves (latex, rubber [thiuram], acrylates in medical gloves)
  • Fingertips: Common in florists (tulips #1 cause)
  • Clothing dermatitis: Spares the folds (axillary vault), accentuated where clothing fits tightly (waistline); common allergens: fabric finishers (formaldehyde), dyes (disperse blue 106 and 124), rubber
  • Perianal: Lidocaine, preservatives (MCI/MI)
  • Shoe dermatitis: Spares toe webs, begins at base of great toe and spreads over dorsal surface (plantar surfaces generally spared); causes: adhesives (colophony), rubber accelerators (mercaptobenzothiazole), leather (chromates), dyes

Special Distribution Patterns

  • Airborne ACD: Usually from plants (Compositae = #1 cause), affects exposed areas
  • Systemic ACD: Diffuse dermatitis due to systemic allergen (e.g., IV aminophylline in patients with ethylenediamine sensitivity)
  • Occupational ACD: Common allergens: rubber > nickel > epoxy and other resins > aromatic amines
  • Ulcers: Bacitracin, neomycin, lanolin
  • Oral stomatitis: Dental fillings (mercury/gold/amalgam → lichenoid reaction), epoxy resins, flavorings (mint/cinnamon)
  • Adhesive reactions: Most tape reactions are irritant contact dermatitis; ACD to tape: rubber, resins, and acrylates

Uncommon Presentations

  • Pigmented: fragrances, bactericides; often facial
  • Lichenoid: color film developers, dental acrylates
  • Erythema multiforme-like: tropical woods, PPD, poison ivy
  • Pigmented purpura: rubber diving suits
  • Pseudolymphomatous: Compositae plants
  • Granulomatous: zirconium
  • Pustular: fragrances
What are the key distribution patterns for nickel and poison ivy ACD?

Nickel: Earlobes (earrings), neck (necklaces), wrists (watches), umbilicus (jeans buttons), hands (keys/coins)

Poison Ivy: Linear or streaked vesicular lesions where plant touched skin

Key Allergen Categories

1. Metals

  • Nickel: Most common worldwide; jewelry, buckles, snaps, phones
    • Dimethylglyoxime Test: Quick in-office test; pink color indicates nickel presence
  • Cobalt: Often used with nickel; causes "poral" reaction pattern in patch test when patch testing to cobalt appears as erythematous to violaceous dots. This is not an allergic reaction but is believed to result from the allergen residing within the acrosyringia; often co-sensitized with nickel (80%)
  • Chromium: Leather, cement; poor prognosis for cement workers

2. Preservatives

  • Methylisothiazolinone (MI)/Methylchloroisothiazolinone (MCI): Wet wipes, cosmetics, paints. They are preservatives active against bacteria, yeast, fungi, and algae. They are widely used in personal, household, and industrial product.
  • Formaldehyde and releasers: Allergy to formaldehyde is commonly seen in association with other formaldehyde-releasing preservatives: quaternium-15, imidazolidinyl urea, diazolidinyl urea, DMDM hydantoin, 2-bromo-2-nitropropane-1,3-diol, and tris(hydroxymethyl) nitromethane
  • Benzisothiazolinone: Household detergents, water-based paints

3. Fragrances

  • Fragrance mix I & II: Combined detect ~75% of fragrance allergies
  • Myroxylon pereirae (balsam of Peru): Natural fragrance found in many products including chinease ontiment , tiger balm , tooth paste , cough sryup , scabies and hemorrhoid treatment, cross-reacts with Benzoin , colophony. Patients should avoid: citrus fruits/products, tomatoes, spices (cinnamon, cloves, vanilla, curry, nutmeg, allspice, anise, ginger), colas, and alcoholic beverages (wine, beer, gin, vermouth) contains sweet flavoring and spacies .
  • Hydroperoxides of linalool/limonene: Oxidized fragrance components, more potent than parent compounds

4. Rubber chemicals

  • Thiuram mix: Rubber accelerator in gloves
  • Carba mix: Rubber accelerator in gloves
  • Mercaptobenzothiazole (MBT): Rubber accelerator
  • Black Rubber Mix: Antioxidants in black rubber products
  • Clinical Relevance:
    • Latex gloves: Contains natural rubber latex proteins and rubber accelerator
    • Nitrile gloves: Contain rubber accelerators (thiurams, carbamates, MBT) that can cause allergic contact dermatitis
    • Safe alternative: Vinyl gloves are generally free of rubber accelerators and latex proteins, making them suitable for patients with rubber chemical allergies

5. Plant Allergens

  • Poison Ivy/Oak/Sumac (Anacardiaceae family):
    • Allergen: Urushiol (oleoresin)
    • Cross-reactions: Mango rind, cashew nut shell oil and Japanese lacquer tree
    • Clinical: Linear streaks of erythematous papulovesicles, intense pruritus
    • Treatment: Systemic steroids for 3 weeks to prevent rebound in severe cases
    • Prevention: Bentoquatam 5% solution
"U" for "Urushiol" in "pUshy PLANTS": Poison ivy, Lacquer tree, Anacardiaceae, Nuts (cashew), Tree of Mango, Sumac

6. Topical medications

  • Neomycin: Most common topical antibiotic allergen
  • Bacitracin: Often combined with neomycin (Neosporin®)
  • Corticosteroids: 3 groups based on cross-reactivity patterns
    • Group 1: Non-methylated (hydrocortisone, budesonide, tixocortol-21-pivalate) -> Most allergic reactions are due to corticosteroids in Group 1
    • Group 2: C16/C17 cis ketal/diol structure (triamcinolone acetonide, fluocinonide)
    • Group 3: Halogenated and C16-methylated (betamethasone, clobetasol)

7. Hair/nail products

  • p-Phenylenediamine (PPD): Hair dye, temporary tattoos
  • Tosylamide formaldehyde resin: Nail polish

8. Textile Allergens

  • Disperse blue dyes 106 and 124: In darkly colored clothing
  • Ethyleneurea/melamine formaldehyde resin: In permanent press clothing

Cross-reactions: Mango rind, cashew nut shell oil, Japanese lacquer tree

V Diagnostic Approach

Patch Testing (Gold Standard)

Patch Testing Technique
  • Apply allergens to upper back using chambers (Finn Chambers®, allergEAZE®, IQ chambers)
  • Remove after 48 hours and mark sites
  • First reading at 48 hours
  • Second reading at 72-96 hours (crucial for delayed reactions like corticosteroids, gold, neomycin)

Interpretation (International Grading System)

  • +/- = Doubtful reaction, faint macular erythema
  • + = Weak reaction with erythema, infiltration, papules
  • ++ = Strong vesicular reaction with erythema, infiltration, papules
  • +++ = Spreading bullous reaction
  • - = Negative reaction
  • IR = Irritant reaction, Caused by high concentrations of test substances.
"48-72 Rule" - Patch on for 48 hours, read again by ~72 hours; "++ Vesicles, +++ Bullae"

Relevance Assessment

  • Current relevance: Allergen explains current dermatitis
  • Past relevance: Allergen explained previous dermatitis
  • Unknown relevance: No clear exposure identified

Testing Series

  • T.R.U.E. TEST®: 35 allergens, FDA-approved (limited scope)
  • ACDS Screening Series (80 allergens)
  • NACDG 70 Series
  • European Baseline Series
  • Specialized series for specific occupations (hairdressers, florists, etc.)

Additional Testing Methods

  • Dimethylglyoxime Test: Spot test for nickel detection in metal objects (pink color indicates nickel)
  • Repeat Open Application Test (ROAT): Apply suspected product twice daily to clear skin for 1-2 weeks. Limitation: Does not identify specific allergens within a product
  • Patient Products Testing: Test "leave-on" products as is, dilute "rinse-off" products (1-10% in water). Never test unknown products blindly

Factors Affecting Patch Test Results

  • Recent topical corticosteroid use (avoid 1 week prior)
  • Systemic corticosteroids (limit to ≤20mg prednisone daily)
  • Immunosuppressants and biologics may impact results
  • Sunburn at test site
  • Poor adherence of patches

VI Differential Diagnosis

  • Irritant contact dermatitis: Less well-defined margins, burning sensation, history of irritant exposure
  • Atopic dermatitis: Endogenous eczema, chronic, family history, flexural predilection
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: Greasy scales, scalp, face, chest involvement
  • Stasis dermatitis: Lower legs, associated with venous insufficiency
  • Psoriasis: Well-demarcated, silvery scales, extensor surfaces
  • Tinea: Fungal infection, positive KOH preparation
  • Rosacea (erythematous form): Facial erythema, telangiectasias, papules/pustules
  • Mycosis fungoides: Consider in chronic cases, histology to differentiate
  • Protein contact dermatitis: Immediate reaction to protein allergens
  • Juvenile plantar dermatosis: Spares toe webs and dorsum of feet, dryness and fissuring of plantar skin
What are the key differences between Allergic Contact Dermatitis and Irritant Contact Dermatitis?

Allergic Contact Dermatitis:

  • Type IV hypersensitivity reaction
  • Requires prior sensitization
  • Well-defined borders
  • More pruritic (itchy)
  • Positive patch test

Irritant Contact Dermatitis:

  • Direct cytotoxic effect
  • No prior sensitization needed
  • Less well-defined margins
  • More burning sensation than itchy
  • Negative patch test

VII Management

Most Important Step

Allergen identification and avoidance is the cornerstone of ACD management.

Patient education

  • Written information on identified allergens (ACDS website resources)
  • Contact Allergen Management Program (CAMP) to identify safe products
  • Label reading (look for "fragrance-free" not just "unscented")
  • Consider consort dermatitis (Allergentransfer from partners)

Symptomatic treatment

  • Topical corticosteroids: First-line for localized ACD
    • Choose potency by body site (low potency around eyes, high potency for thick plaques)
    • Ointments preferred (fewer preservatives than creams/lotions)
  • Systemic corticosteroids: For severe/widespread cases
    • 3-week taper for poison ivy to prevent rebound
  • Calcineurin inhibitors: Helpful for face, eyelids (off-label)
  • Emollients: Barrier repair (white petrolatum safest option)
  • Wet dressings: For acute, weeping lesions
  • Antihistamines: For pruritus relief

Prognosis

  • May take 6+ weeks for clearing even with allergen avoidance
  • Chronic cases may have residual lichenification or persistent sensitivity
What is the recommended duration of systemic steroid treatment for severe poison ivy reactions?

3-week taper to prevent rebound

VIII Special Forms of ACD

Systemic Contact Dermatitis (SCD)

Occurs when patient with prior cutaneous sensitization is exposed systemically to same/cross-reacting chemical

Routes

  • Oral, intravenous, intranasal, intra-articular

Common Triggers

  • Foods: Posion ivy (cashew, mango), balsam of Peru (citrus, spices, tomatoes, sodas, cough syrup, mouthwashes, toothpaste)
  • Medications: Neomycin (Steptomycin and gentamicin), ethylenediamine hydrochloride(aminophylline, Hydroxzine/Cetrizine), Topical corticosteroids (systemic corticosteroids)
  • Orthopedic implants: Metals (nickel, cobalt, chromium)

Clinical Forms

  • Generalized eczema: Symmetrical distribution
  • "Baboon syndrome" (SDRIFE): Symmetric erythema of gluteal/inguinal area plus other flexural sites
"Baboon: Red behind" → symmetrical red patches in the buttock and flexures

Airborne Contact Dermatitis

  • Due to airborne allergens contacting skin
  • Predominates on exposed areas: Eyelids, face, V of neck, arms, legs
  • Common causes:
    • Plants (especially Compositae, ragweed)
    • Fragrances, essential oils
    • Woods
    • Epoxy resins
    • Preservatives in paints (MI)
    • Acrylates

Photoallergic Contact Dermatitis

  • Requires UV plus chemical (e.g., certain sunscreens like benzophenones)
  • Distribution: Sun-exposed areas (face, V of neck, dorsum of hands)
  • Spares under chin or behind ears (unlike airborne dermatitis)

Textile Dermatitis

  • Usually due to dyes (especially disperse blue 106 and 124) and resins
  • Occurs in areas where clothing fits tightly
  • More common in women
  • Positive patch test reactions may be delayed up to 7-10 days

Pigmented Contact Dermatitis

  • Hyperpigmentation is the main feature (sometimes minimal itching)
  • Causes: Fragrances (musk ambrette), topical phenols in cosmetics, PPD in hair dyes
What is "Baboon Syndrome" in Systemic Contact Dermatitis?

"Baboon syndrome" (SDRIFE) refers to symmetrical erythema of the gluteal/inguinal area plus other flexural sites that occurs in systemic contact dermatitis.

It's named this way because the distribution of redness resembles a baboon's characteristic red buttocks.

  • Top allergens include nickel, preservatives, topical antibiotics, fragrances

  • Temporary tattoos with PPD are common cause of sensitization
  • Wet wipes (MI) often cause anogenital dermatitis

XII Potential Exam Confusion Points

  1. ACD vs. ICD: Both can have similar appearance, especially in chronic form. ACD requires prior sensitization; ICD does not. Patch testing distinguishes them.
  2. Corticosteroid allergy:
    • May need delayed reading (96h) due to anti-inflammatory effects
    • May show "edge effect" (erythema only at rim of test chamber)
    • Screening with tixocortol-21-pivalate and budesonide detects ~75% of cases
  3. Relevance assessment: Positive patch test doesn't always mean current relevance; differentiate between current, past, and unknown relevance.
  4. Fragrance avoidance: "Unscented" products may contain masking fragrances; patients should look for "fragrance-free" products.
  5. T.R.U.E. TEST® limitations: Only screens for 35 allergens; extended testing may identify 37-76% more positive reactions.
  6. Late patch test reactions: Some allergens (gold, neomycin, corticosteroids) can show delayed positivity (day 4-7).
  7. Allergies to "organic" or "natural" products: Patients often assume "natural = safe," but essential oils, propolis, lanolin, and botanical extracts can be highly sensitizing.
  8. Irritant vs. Allergic hand dermatitis:
    • Sharply demarcated, extremely pruritic, positive patch test = ACD
    • More dryness, fissuring in high-friction areas, negative patch test = ICD
  9. Orthopedic implants: Role of patch testing for metal allergy remains controversial; may neither predict future problems nor aid in removal decisions.
  10. High-risk topical medications: Neomycin, bacitracin
  11. Duration of treatment for poison ivy: Systemic steroids often required for 3 weeks to prevent rebound.
How can you distinguish between irritant and allergic hand dermatitis?

Allergic hand dermatitis:

  • Sharply demarcated lesions
  • Extremely pruritic (itchy)
  • Positive patch test

Irritant hand dermatitis:

  • More dryness and fissuring
  • Affects high-friction areas
  • Negative patch test
