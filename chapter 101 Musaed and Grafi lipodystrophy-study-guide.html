Lipodystrophy - Comprehensive Board Review

Study Mode Recall Mode
Introduction & Key Concepts
💡 Core Concept

Though the terms lipodystrophy and lipoatrophy are often used interchangeably:

  • Lipoatrophy: Selective loss of fat
  • Lipodystrophy: Redistribution of fat, partly due to hypertrophic compensation of non-atrophic fat

Lipodystrophy syndromes represent a heterogeneous group of disorders characterized by:

  • Lipoatrophy with fat accumulation in characteristic distribution patterns
  • Development of metabolic syndrome including:
    • Insulin resistance
    • Diabetes mellitus
    • Hyperinsulinemia
    • Dyslipidemia
    • Cardiovascular disease
    • Hepatic steatosis

Classification

Lipodystrophy can be classified based on:

  1. Origin: Inherited or acquired
  2. Distribution:
    • Generalized
    • Partial (extensive but not generalized)
    • Localized (limited to an isolated area)
EXAM POINT

Of the partial lipodystrophies, the one associated with antiretroviral therapy (ART) for HIV infections has become the most common form.

Pathogenesis

  • Adipose tissue functions as an endocrine organ via secretion of hormones and adipocytokines (leptin, TNF-α, IL-6, adiponectin)
  • Final common pathway: Defective adipocyte triglyceride storage
  • This defect can result from:
    • Impaired triglyceride synthesis (enzyme deficiency)
    • Defective adipocyte development (mutations in critical genes)
    • Adipocyte apoptosis
    • Autoimmune or drug-mediated adipocyte destruction
  • Adiponectin (ADIPOQ gene) regulates insulin sensitivity and glucose/lipid homeostasis
  • Serum adiponectin and leptin levels are reduced in lipodystrophies
  • Leptin infusion can reverse insulin resistance
Lipodystrophy Classification
  • Congenital Generalized Berardinelli-Seip syndrome
  • Familial Partial FPLD (Köbberling-Dunnigan)
  • Acquired Generalized Lawrence syndrome
  • Acquired Partial Barraquer-Simons syndrome
  • HIV-associated ART-induced
  • Localized Various forms
Clinical Pearls
  • Metabolic complications correlate with degree of fat loss
  • Look for fat loss patterns to distinguish types
  • Acquired forms often have autoimmune associations
  • Progressive facial fat loss is a key diagnostic feature
Congenital Generalized Lipodystrophy (CGL)
🧬 Key Features
  • AKA Berardinelli-Seip Syndrome
  • Autosomal recessive
  • Present since birth
  • Generalized loss of metabolically active fat
  • Cadaveric facies & muscular appearance

Genetics & Types

  • Type 1: AGPAT2 mutation - decreased triglyceride and phospholipid synthesis
  • Type 2: BSCL2/seipin mutation - lipid droplet morphology abnormalities
  • Type 3: CAV1 mutation - affects caveolin (binds fatty acids, translocates to lipid droplets)
  • Type 4: CAVIN1/PTRF mutation - affects caveolae and caveolin expression

Clinical Features

  • Fat Distribution:
    • Loss of fat from face, trunk, extremities, viscera
    • Types 1, 3, 4: Preservation of mechanical adipose tissue (palmoplantar, retro-orbital)
    • Type 2: Loss of both metabolically active and mechanical adipose tissue
    • Types 1, 2: Lack of bone marrow fat
    • Types 3, 4: Preserved bone marrow fat
  • "Anabolic" Features:
    • Muscular hypertrophy with prominent veins
    • Acromegalic features
    • Voracious appetite
    • Increased metabolic rate
    • Heat intolerance
    • Accelerated growth
    • Advanced bone/dental age
    • Osteosclerotic/lytic changes
    • Masculine features in females
    • Enlarged genitalia (especially in infancy)
  • Metabolic Abnormalities:
    • Insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia (early infancy)
    • Diabetes mellitus (appears at puberty)
    • Hypertriglyceridemia (may cause pancreatitis)
    • Low HDL cholesterol
    • Low plasma leptin levels
  • Skin Findings:
    • Acanthosis nigricans (early, widespread)
    • Hypertrichosis (increased, often curly scalp hair at birth)
    • Hyperhidrosis
    • Coarse skin on upper body
    • Hyperkeratotic epidermal papillomatosis
    • Xanthomas
  • Complications:
    • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (can be fatal)
    • Liver failure/cirrhosis
    • Organomegaly (multiple organs)
    • CNS abnormalities (in some cases)
    • Proteinuric nephropathy

Type 2 is More Severe: Higher incidence of intellectual disability and cardiomyopathy

Type 4: Distinctive features include prominent skin veins, cardiac conduction defects, pyloric stenosis, muscle weakness

Prognosis: Mean age of death is 32 years

Familial Partial Lipodystrophy (FPLD)
🧬 Key Features
  • Previously known as Köbberling-Dunnigan syndrome
  • Typically autosomal dominant
  • Onset after puberty
  • Fat loss predominantly affects extremities, sparing face

Genetics & Types

  • FPLD1 (Köbberling type): Unknown gene
  • FPLD2 (Dunnigan type): LMNA mutation (lamin A/C) - disrupts nuclear function, leading to apoptosis
  • FPLD3: PPARG mutation
  • FPLD4: PLIN1 mutation
  • FPLD5: CIDEC mutation (autosomal recessive)
  • FPLD6: LIPE mutation (autosomal recessive)

Clinical Features

  • Fat Distribution:
    • FPLD1: Fat loss on extremities with increased fat on face/neck, trunk
    • FPLD2: Fat loss on extremities and trunk, increased fat on face/neck (double chin), muscular hypertrophy
    • FPLD3: Fat loss on extremities/buttocks (less severe than FPLD2), trunk/viscera spared
    • FPLD4: Fat loss on lower extremities with small adipocytes and increased fibrosis
    • FPLD5: Fat loss on lower extremities/buttocks; small, multiloculated lipid droplets histologically
    • FPLD6: Fat loss on lower extremities, increased fat of abdomen and axillae
  • Clinical Course: In FPLD2, normal childhood followed by progressive, symmetric loss of subcutaneous fat
  • Metabolic Abnormalities:
    • Insulin resistance
    • Diabetes mellitus
    • Hypertriglyceridemia
    • Low HDL cholesterol
  • Complications:
    • Acute pancreatitis
    • Hepatic steatosis/cirrhosis
    • Menstrual abnormalities

Clinical Pearl: FPLD3 has milder clinical features but may have more severe metabolic disturbances

Gender Difference: Affected women have more severe triglyceride elevations compared to men

Related Conditions

Familial Partial Lipodystrophy with Mandibuloacral Dysplasia (MAD)

  • Onset in childhood or puberty
  • Males > Females
  • Autosomal recessive
  • Types: A (LMNA) and B (ZMPSTE24)
  • Features: Premature aging, hypoplasia of mandible, clavicles and terminal digits, short stature, scleroderma-like skin with mottled pigmentation
Acquired Generalized Lipodystrophy (AGL)
🧬 Key Features
  • Also known as Lawrence syndrome
  • Onset in childhood or adolescence
  • Female predominance (3:1)
  • Similar to congenital form but milder and later onset

Clinical Features

  • Fat Distribution:
    • Fat loss affecting face, trunk, extremities
    • Preservation of bone marrow fat (differentiates from CGL types 1 and 2)
  • Metabolic Abnormalities:
    • Insulin resistance
    • Diabetes mellitus
    • Hypertriglyceridemia (correlates with degree of lipodystrophy)
  • Associations:
    • 1/3 have preceding autoimmune disease (e.g., juvenile dermatomyositis, Sjögren syndrome)
    • 25% have preceding panniculitis
  • Complications:
    • Liver failure/cirrhosis
    • Proteinuric nephropathy

Subtypes

  1. Panniculitic variant (type 1):
    • Panniculitis precedes lipoatrophy
    • Mean age of onset: 7 years
    • Slight female predominance (~1.5-fold)
    • Associated symptoms: low-grade fever, malaise, arthralgias, abdominal pain
    • Milder metabolic derangements
    • Histology: lymphohistiocytic subcutaneous infiltrate ± granulomatous foci
  2. Autoimmune variant (type 2):
    • Concurrent or prior autoimmune disease without panniculitis
    • Mean age of onset: 15 years
    • Female predominance (~3-fold)
    • Hepatomegaly invariably present
    • Most patients develop hypertriglyceridemia and diabetes
  3. Idiopathic (type 3):
    • No evidence of panniculitis or autoimmune disease
    • Mean age of onset: 20 years (range: <2-60 years)
    • Female predominance (~3-fold)

Clinical Pearl: Shorter latency period before development of diabetes (~4 years after fat loss) compared to congenital variant

Noteworthy: Higher incidence of hepatomegaly, cirrhosis, and liver-related mortality than in CGL

Acquired Partial Lipodystrophy (APL)
🧬 Key Features
  • Also known as Barraquer-Simons syndrome, progressive lipodystrophy, or cephalothoracic lipodystrophy
  • Onset in childhood or adolescence (rarely adults)
  • Female predominance (3:1)
  • Face usually affected first, with cephalocaudal progression
EXAM POINT

This is the second most common type of non-localized lipodystrophy, after HIV/ART-related lipodystrophy.

Genetics & Pathophysiology

  • Sporadic or autosomal dominant: LMNB2 (Lamin B2) mutation in some cases
  • Due to complement-dependent lysis of adipocytes
  • Almost all patients have low C3 levels and circulating C3 nephritic factor

Clinical Features

  • Fat Distribution:
    • Fat loss: face, upper extremities, trunk with spread in cephalocaudal direction
    • Spares lower extremities
    • May have increased fat on hips and legs
  • Phenotypic Subtypes:
    • Fat loss in the upper half of the body
    • Fat loss in the upper half with hypertrophy in the lower half
    • Hemilipodystrophy (one half of face or body affected)
  • Metabolic Abnormalities:
    • Rarely associated with insulin resistance, diabetes, hypertriglyceridemia
    • Metabolic syndrome less common than in other lipodystrophies
  • Associations:
    • Often preceded by febrile illness
    • 1/5 have mesangiocapillary glomerulonephritis
    • Low C3 predisposes to Neisseria meningitidis infections
    • Autoimmune diseases: SLE, dermatomyositis, systemic sclerosis, Sjögren syndrome, etc.
Localized Lipodystrophy

Causes of Localized Lipoatrophy

  • Inflammatory:
    • Pyogenic abscesses
    • Lobular panniculitides (especially lupus profundus and panniculitic dermatomyositis)
    • Localized autoimmune connective tissue diseases (e.g., morphea)
    • Subcutaneous panniculitis-like T-cell lymphoma
  • Iatrogenic:
    • Injected medications (see table below)
    • Acupuncture sites
  • Mechanical:
    • Repetitive trauma
    • Pressure-induced changes

Injected Medications That Can Cause Localized Lipoatrophy

Medications
Most Common
Insulin
  • Non-human > human, insulin analogues
  • Rarely, can occur at distant sites
Corticosteroids
Less Common
Antibiotics
  • Benzathine penicillin (can cause semicircular lipoatrophy)
  • Amikacin
Immunomodulatory drugs
  • Glatiramer acetate (multiple sclerosis)
  • Methotrexate (can cause semicircular lipoatrophy)
Vaccines
  • Diphtheria-pertussis-tetanus vaccine
  • Quadrivalent human papillomavirus vaccine
Others
  • Growth hormone
  • Heparin
  • Iron dextran
  • Exogenous material

Clinical Variants

  • Involutional lipoatrophy:
    • Idiopathic focal loss of fat
    • Predominantly on buttocks and proximal extremities
    • Often corresponds to injection sites but can occur without prior injections
  • Atrophic connective tissue panniculitis:
    • Rare, often on extremities
    • "Pure" cases have no prior clinical inflammation
    • Histologic evidence of lymphocytic panniculitis
  • Lipoatrophia semicircularis:
    • Primarily in women in 20s-30s, rarely in children
    • Symmetric, asymptomatic, 4-8 cm curvilinear horizontal depressions on anterolateral thighs
    • Appear over weeks, resolve spontaneously between 9 months to 4 years
    • May recur
    • MRI can differentiate from inflammatory panniculitis
  • Lipodystrophia centrifugalis abdominalis infantilis:
    • Largely reported from East Asia (Japanese, Korean, Chinese)
    • Onset usually before 3 years (>90% by age 5)
    • Four-fold female predominance in Koreans
    • Well-demarcated area of lipoatrophy with periphery of erythema and scale
    • Usually on abdomen but can affect face, neck, lumbosacral region
    • Often with regional lymphadenopathy
  • Progressive hemifacial atrophy (Parry-Romberg syndrome)
  • Non-progressive late-onset linear hemifacial lipoatrophy:
    • Occurs on malar cheek, primarily in elderly

Lipohypertrophy

💉 Causes of Lipohypertrophy
  • Insulin therapy - Risk factors: duration, frequent injections, non-rotation of sites, poor technique
  • Pegvisomant (growth hormone receptor antagonist) injections
  • Growth hormone injections
  • Excess corticosteroids - Cushing syndrome/disease
  • HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy

Clinical Significance: Injection of insulin into lipohypertrophic sites leads to erratic absorption and poor glycemic control

HIV/ART-Associated Lipodystrophy
EXAM POINT

HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy has become the most common form of non-localized lipodystrophy.

🧬 Key Features
  • Onset: 2 months to 2 years after initiation of ART
  • Linked to protease inhibitors (PI) and nucleoside analogue reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)
  • Affects up to 40% of patients within 1-2 years of ART initiation
  • Combination of lipoatrophy and lipohypertrophy in characteristic patterns

Pathogenesis

  • Mechanisms of ART-induced fat loss:
    • Impaired pre-adipocyte differentiation
    • Increased adipocyte apoptosis
    • Impaired lipogenesis and increased lipolysis
    • Mitochondrial toxicity
  • Drug-specific effects:
    • NRTIs (stavudine, zidovudine): Inhibit mitochondrial DNA polymerase-γ
    • PIs: Affect sterol regulatory element-binding proteins (SREBP) and other transcription factors
  • HIV viral protein R (vpr) decreases PPAR-γ expression, suggesting direct role of HIV in pathogenesis

Clinical Features

  • Lipoatrophy:
    • Affects face, limbs, heel pads, buttocks
    • More strongly associated with NRTIs (especially stavudine)
  • Lipohypertrophy:
    • Intra-abdominal fat accumulation ("protease paunch", "Crix belly")
    • Dorsocervical fat pad ("buffalo hump")
    • Breast enlargement
    • Anterior neck/lateral mandibular region
    • Lipoma formation
    • More commonly associated with PIs
  • Metabolic Abnormalities:
    • Insulin resistance
    • Diabetes mellitus
    • Dyslipidemia (hypertriglyceridemia, decreased HDL)
    • Bone disorders (osteopenia, osteoporosis)

Risk Factors

  • Age >40 years
  • Female gender
  • Disease severity at therapy onset (CD4 count <200/mcl)
  • Longer duration of therapy
  • Baseline body fat distribution
  • Combination therapy (two NRTIs and a PI)

Treatment

Lipoatrophy (largely irreversible)

  • Prevention: Use tenofovir/emtricitabine instead of zidovudine/lamivudine
  • Switching from thymidine analogues to tenofovir or abacavir
  • Pioglitazone (but not rosiglitazone) can increase limb fat mass
  • Facial fillers: poly-L-lactic acid, calcium hydroxylapatite
  • Autologous fat transfer

Lipohypertrophy

  • Diet and exercise modifications
  • Tesamorelin (growth hormone-releasing hormone analogue) - decreases visceral adipose tissue
  • Liposuction for dorsocervical and submental fat

Metabolic Management

  • Regular screening for dyslipidemia, diabetes, hypertension
  • Statins for elevated LDL
  • Fibrates when both LDL and triglycerides are elevated
Pathology

The histologic picture of various lipodystrophy syndromes is generally non-inflammatory.

Involutional Lipoatrophy

Characterized by small fat lobules with reduction in size and number of adipocytes. Two histologic subtypes:

  1. First Type:
    • Faintly acidophilic, small fat cells that retract from one another and surrounding connective tissue
    • Appearance similar to embryonic fat
    • Lobules often have an eosinophilic appearance
    • Inflammation absent to sparsely mononuclear
  2. Second Type:
    • Small atrophic adipocytes with normal fat cell membrane
    • Surrounded by prominent vasculature
    • Collapsed lobules oriented parallel to skin surface
    • May see acid mucopolysaccharide deposition, septal fibrosis, and occasionally lipomembranous changes
Differential Diagnosis

The excessive fat accumulation in FPLD2 and HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy could be confused with Cushing syndrome. Acromegaly can have overlapping features with generalized lipodystrophy.

Differential for Generalized Lipodystrophy in Infants

  • Leprechaunism (Donohue syndrome):
    • Generalized lipodystrophy, severe insulin resistance, acanthosis nigricans, hirsutism
    • Unlike CGL: Elfin facies, severe intrauterine growth retardation, prominent nipples, loose skin
    • Mutations in insulin receptor gene
    • Death during infancy
  • SHORT syndrome:
    • Short stature, hyperextensible joints/hernia, ocular depression, Rieger anomaly, teething delay
    • Congenital lipoatrophy of face and upper body
    • Mutations in PIK3R1
  • Progeria-type syndromes:
    • Limb lipoatrophy, cardiovascular disease, diabetes
    • Accompanied by muscle wasting, sclerodermatous changes, cataracts, signs of premature aging
    • Some have LMNA mutations (similar to FPLD)
  • Cockayne syndrome:
    • Lipoatrophic changes, growth delay, retinal abnormalities, photosensitivity, DNA repair defects
  • AREDYLD syndrome:
    • Congenital generalized lipoatrophic diabetes with acrorenal field defect and ectodermal dysplasia

Differential for Localized Lipodystrophy

  • Initial phases of progressive lipodystrophy
  • Morphea
  • Lupus panniculitis
  • Atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini
  • Poland syndrome (unilateral absence of breast/pectoralis major + ipsilateral symbrachydactyly)
Treatment Approach

Management focuses on three key areas:

  1. Cosmetic concerns
  2. Metabolic derangements
  3. Systemic associations

Cosmetic Management

  • Localized lipodystrophy may resolve spontaneously
  • Surgical options (variable success):
    • Flaps for facial lipoatrophy
    • Soft tissue augmentation with fillers
    • Fat transplantation via lipoinjection

Metabolic Management

  • Medications:
    • Thiazolidinediones (TZDs/glitazones) - PPAR-γ agonists
    • Fibrates - PPAR-α agonists for hypertriglyceridemia
    • Omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids
    • Recombinant methionyl human leptin (r-metHuLeptin)
      • Improves glycemic control, hyperlipidemia, hepatomegaly, proteinuria
      • Does not improve lipodystrophy itself
  • Lifestyle: Low-fat diet, exercise, weight management
  • Endocrinologist management recommended

Management for Specific Types

Acquired Partial Lipodystrophy

  • Monitor for autoimmune disease, thyroid disease, renal disease
  • Check for proteinuria, low C3, C3 nephritic factor
  • Consider renal biopsy if needed
  • Treatments:
    • IVIg (contains neutralizing antibodies against C3NeF)
    • Eculizumab (mixed results)

Insulin Lipodystrophy

  • Prevention: Rotate injection sites (same site not more than once monthly)
  • Liposuction may improve hypertrophic form

Recall Questions

Lipodystrophy Classification

Question 1: What are the three major classification groups of lipodystrophy based on distribution pattern?

(1) Generalized, (2) Partial (extensive but not generalized), (3) Localized (limited to an isolated area)

Question 2: Which form of lipodystrophy is the most common non-localized type?

HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy

Congenital & Familial Types

Question 3: What are the four main genetic types of Congenital Generalized Lipodystrophy and their associated genes?

Type 1: AGPAT2
Type 2: BSCL2/seipin
Type 3: CAV1
Type 4: CAVIN1/PTRF

Question 4: In Familial Partial Lipodystrophy (FPLD2), when does fat loss typically occur and what is the distribution pattern?

Onset after puberty. Fat loss predominantly on extremities and trunk, with sparing or increased fat on face/neck (double chin). Characterized by muscular hypertrophy.

Acquired Types

Question 5: What distinguishes Acquired Generalized Lipodystrophy from Congenital Generalized Lipodystrophy types 1 and 2?

Preservation of bone marrow fat in AGL (unlike CGL types 1 and 2 where bone marrow fat is lost)

Question 6: What are the three phenotypic subtypes of Acquired Partial Lipodystrophy (Barraquer-Simons syndrome)?

1. Fat loss in the upper half of the body
2. Fat loss in the upper half with hypertrophy in the lower half
3. Hemilipodystrophy (one half of face or body affected)

HIV/ART-Associated Lipodystrophy

Question 7: Which antiretroviral drugs are most strongly associated with lipoatrophy in HIV patients?

Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs), especially stavudine and zidovudine

Question 8: What are the main risk factors for developing HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy?

- Age >40 years
- Female gender
- Disease severity at onset of therapy (CD4 count <200/mcl)
- Longer duration of therapy
- Combination therapy with two NRTIs and a PI

Treatment & Management

Question 9: What treatment has been FDA-approved for reduction of visceral adiposity in HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy?

Tesamorelin, a growth hormone-releasing hormone analogue

Question 10: What are the effects of recombinant methionyl human leptin (r-metHuLeptin) in lipodystrophy patients?

Improves glycemic control, hyperlipidemia, hepatomegaly, and diabetic complications like proteinuria. However, it does not improve the lipodystrophy itself.

Study Tip: For board exams, focus on distinguishing the clinical and laboratory features of different types of lipodystrophy. Pay special attention to HIV/ART-associated lipodystrophy as it's the most common form and frequently tested.
