Chapter 29: Pemphigus

Saudi Board Exam Preparation | High-Yield Content

Definition & Overview

Pemphigus

A group of disorders characterized by intraepidermal blistering and antibodies against cell surface of keratinocytes (KCs).

Epidemiology

  • Male:Female ratio: Equal
  • Mean age: 50-60s
  • Ethnic predisposition: More common in Jews
  • Geographic distribution: PV more common than PF except in Tunisia, Finland, and Brazil (1:17 ratio in Brazil)

MCQ Alert!

Pay attention to these geographic variations. The 1:17 ratio of PV:PF in Brazil is a high-yield fact for board exams.

Pathogenesis

Key Mechanism

Autoantibodies against desmosomal cadherins lead to loss of cell-cell adhesion → blister formation

Molecular Basis

  • Cadherins: Divided into classic adherens (E-, P-, N-cadherins) and desmosomal cadherins (desmogliens and desmocollins)
  • Binding: Cadherins bind to plaque proteins (α-catenin, β-catenin and plakoglobin)
  • Structure: Plaque proteins bind to the cell cytoskeleton
  • Autoantibodies: IgG against DSG1 (160kDa), DSG3 (130kDa), or both are present in pemphigus

Memory Aid: Desmoglein Distribution

  • DSG1: Primarily in superficial epidermis
  • DSG3: Primarily in lower epidermis and mucosa
  • DSG2: Present in the heart
  • DSG4: Present in the hair follicle (mutation leads to autosomal recessive localized hypotrichosis)

MCQ Alert!

  • DSG2 is present in the heart
  • DSG4 is present in the hair follicle, and its mutation leads to autosomal recessive localized hypotrichosis
  • Armadillo proteins include plakoglobin, plakophilin, and β-catenin

Compensation Theory

Understanding the compensation theory explains the different clinical manifestations:

  • In the skin, both DSG1 and DSG3 are present, with DSG1 more in the superficial layers and DSG3 more in the deeper layers
  • In mucosal surfaces, predominantly DSG3 is present
  • In PV (anti-DSG3): Causes mucosal lesions because no DSG1 to compensate in mucosa
  • In PF (anti-DSG1): No mucosal involvement because DSG3 compensates in mucosa, but superficial skin blisters occur

Clinical Features

Pemphigus Vulgaris (PV)

  • Mucosal involvement: 100%
  • Cutaneous involvement: 50%
  • Subgroups: Mucocutaneous and mucosal-dominant
  • Lesions: Flaccid bullae and vesicles with erosions and hemorrhagic crust
  • Throat involvement: Hoarseness and difficulty swallowing
  • Other sites: Esophagus can also be involved
  • Signs: +ve Nikolsky and Asboe-Hansen signs

Unusual Presentations

  • Paronychia/onychomadesis
  • Dyshidrotic eczema
  • Macroglossia
  • Foot ulcers
  • Crusted plaques on the face

Associations

  • Autoimmune thyroiditis
  • Myasthenia gravis
  • Thymoma

Pemphigus Vegetans

Vegetative variant of vulgaris

  • Mechanism: Reactive vegetation
  • Evolution: Starts as flaccid bullae that rupture and form a fungoid vegetation
  • Distribution: Affects the intertriginous areas and the scalp/face
  • Oral finding: The tongue may show cereberiform appearance
  • Types: Neumann (severe) and Hallopeau (mild)

Pemphigus Foliaceus (PF)

  • Lesions: Scaly crusted erosions on an erythematous base
  • Mucosal involvement: None
  • Distribution: Seborrheic (face, scalp, upper chest and back)
  • Notable feature: Most common vesiculobullous disease to cause erythroderma
  • Signs: +ve Nikolsky

Pemphigus Erythematosus

Also known as Senear-Usher syndrome

  • Classification: Localized variant of PF
  • Distribution: Involves the malar region of the face
  • Immunopathology: Deposition of IgGs within the cell surface of KCs (PF) as well as the basement membrane (lupus, hence pemphigus erythematosus)

Herpetiform Pemphigus

  • Variant of: Can be a variant of PV or PF (PF > PV)
  • Appearance: Herpetiform arrangement of vesicles

Drug-Induced Pemphigus

  • Common drugs: Penicillamine and captopril
  • Ratio: PF:PV = 4:1
  • Mechanisms:
    • Immunologic: Drugs lead to autoantibody production
    • Non-immunologic: Drugs contain sulfhydryl groups that directly interact with DSG sulfhydryl groups → loss of adhesion

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus (PNP)

  • Associated with: Both malignant and benign neoplasms
  • Common associations:
    • Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (38%)
    • CLL (18%)
    • Castleman tumor (18%)
  • In children: Castleman tumor (86%)
  • Hallmark: Painful erosive stomatitis
  • Severity: Typically severe and resistant to treatment
  • Mucosal involvement: Other mucosal surfaces may be involved, leading to scarring and morbidity
  • Cutaneous features: Polymorphic, ranging from flaccid or tense bullae to lichenoid papules and plaques
  • Major complication: Bronchiolitis obliterans, which can be fatal due to respiratory failure (MCC of death excluding underlying malignancy)

IgA Pemphigus

  • Antibodies: IgAs and no IgGs
  • Subtypes:
    • Intraepidermal
    • Subcorneal (subcorneal pustular dermatosis of IgA pemphigus)
  • Appearance: Sterile pustules that coalesce to form rings → "Sunflower-like"
  • Common location: Axilla and groin
  • Mucosal involvement: Rare
  • Similar to: Histologically and clinically resembles Sneddon-Wilkinson syndrome, the only way to differentiate between them is by DIF
  • Target antigens: The target in the subcorneal subtype is desmocollin 1, while it is unknown in the intraepidermal subtype

Clinical Pearl: Pemphigus vs. Pemphigoid

Remember the key differences:

  • Pemphigus: Intraepidermal, autoantibodies against desmogleins, flaccid blisters
  • Pemphigoid: Subepidermal, autoantibodies against hemidesmosomal proteins, tense blisters

Pemphigus Vulgaris Histopathology

  • Level of blister: Suprabasal blister with acantholysis
  • Characteristic feature: "Row of tombstone" appearance of the basal cell layer
  • Infiltrate: Eosinophilic infiltrate

Pemphigus Vegetans Histopathology

Same as PV plus:

  • Papillomatosis
  • Acanthosis
  • Eosinophilic microabscesses

Pemphigus Foliaceus Histopathology

  • Level of blister: Subcorneal blister with acantholysis
  • Infiltrate: Mixed eosinophilic neutrophilic infiltrate

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus Histopathology

Variable presentation:

  • PV-like
  • EM-like
  • LP-like

Histology depends on the lesion clinically

IgA Pemphigus Histopathology

  • Level of pustule: Intraepidermal pustules (suprabasal or subcorneal)
  • Content: Content of the pustule is primarily neutrophils

Memory Aid: Blister Levels

  • Pemphigus Vulgaris: SUPRA-basal (deeper in epidermis)
  • Pemphigus Foliaceus: SUB-corneal (more superficial)
  • Remember: Vulgaris goes deeper, Foliaceus stays superficial

Diagnosis

Gold Standard

Identification of autoantibodies through immunological techniques

Diagnostic Techniques

  • DIF: Most reliable and sensitive diagnostic test for all forms of pemphigus
  • IIF: Detects circulating autoantibodies
  • ELISA: Can be used to monitor activity and treatment response
  • Other techniques: Immunoblotting, immunoprecipitation

DIF Patterns - High-Yield for Exams!

Remember the pattern for each pemphigus type:

  • PV & PF: IgG deposition in intercellular spaces ("chicken wire", "fishnet", or "honeycomb" pattern)
  • Pemphigus Erythematosus: IgG deposition both intercellularly and at basement membrane zone (lupus-like)
  • Paraneoplastic Pemphigus: IgG deposition both intercellularly and along basement membrane
  • IgA Pemphigus: IgA (not IgG) deposition in intercellular spaces

What is the key feature that differentiates Sneddon-Wilkinson syndrome from IgA Pemphigus?

(Click to reveal answer)

Direct Immunofluorescence (DIF) findings. Both conditions have similar clinical and histological features, but IgA Pemphigus shows intercellular IgA deposition on DIF.

First Line
  • Rituximab + Oral Steroids
    • Rituximab dose: 1g 2 weeks apart, then 0.5-1g q6-12 months
    • Steroids dose:
      • For significant disease: 0.5mg/kg tapered over 3 months

  • Other steroid sparing agents:

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus Treatment

  • Primary approach: Treat the underlying malignancy
  • Supportive therapy: Systemic steroids
  • For hematological malignancies: Rituximab

Treatment Algorithm Summary for Exams

  • PV/PF (PDAI ≥15): Rituximab + Steroids (dose based on severity)
  • PV/PF (PDAI ≤14): Steroids + Dapsone/Tetracycline
  • IgA Pemphigus: Dapsone first line
  • PNP: Treat underlying malignancy first

Self-Assessment Quiz

Question 1: A 55-year-old man presents with painful oral erosions that have been present for 2 months, followed by the development of flaccid blisters on the trunk. Nikolsky sign is positive. Which pemphigus type is most likely?

  • Pemphigus vulgaris
  • Pemphigus foliaceus
  • Pemphigus erythematosus
  • IgA pemphigus

Question 2: A 60-year-old woman with history of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma presents with severe painful oral erosions, conjunctival inflammation, and polymorphic cutaneous lesions including tense bullae and lichenoid papules. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Pemphigus vulgaris
  • Bullous pemphigoid
  • Paraneoplastic pemphigus
  • Erythema multiforme

Question 3: A patient with pemphigus vulgaris has a PDAI score of 50. What is the appropriate first-line treatment?

  • Oral steroids 0.5mg/kg + dapsone
  • Azathioprine monotherapy

  • Pemphigus foliaceus
