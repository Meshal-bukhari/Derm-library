WHIM Syndrome — Rapid Board Review

Warts • Hypogammaglobulinemia • Infections • Myelokathexis — concise, high-yield facts for exams

Quick Summary Box
Acronym Warts, Hypogammaglobulinemia, Infections, Myelokathexis
Genetics Autosomal dominant — gain-of-function mutation in CXCR4
Pathophysiology CXCR4 gain-of-function → defective chemokine signaling → retention of mature neutrophils in marrow (myelokathexis) → peripheral neutropenia
Hallmark skin Extensive, recalcitrant verruca vulgaris and condyloma (HPV-driven)
Targeted therapy CXCR4 inhibitors (e.g., mavorixafor, plerixafor) — increase neutrophil counts & reduce warts
Topic Key Points (Concise)
Definition Primary immunodeficiency caused by CXCR4 gain-of-function → acronymic phenotype (W,H,I,M).
Clinical — Cutaneous (Warts)
  • Severe, treatment-resistant verruca vulgaris and anogenital warts are classic and often the presenting clue.
  • Figure examples: extensive perianal warts are typical.
Clinical — Infections
  • Recurrent bacterial sinopulmonary infections, cellulitis, pyodermas.
  • Occasional mucosal/skin ulcers.
Laboratory / Hematology
  • Myelokathexis: Peripheral neutropenia with normal/hypercellular bone marrow containing mature neutrophils (retained).
  • Quantitative immunoglobulins: ↓IgG (most consistent), ± ↓IgA/IgM → hypogammaglobulinemia.
Diagnostics
  • Suspect WHIM in patients with refractory warts + neutropenia ± low IgG.
  • Workup: CBC with differential, bone marrow exam (if indicated), quantitative immunoglobulins, and CXCR4 genetic testing for confirmation.
Management
  • Supportive: infection prevention, immunoglobulin replacement therapy when indicated.
  • Targeted: CXCR4 inhibitors — mavorixafor (phase 3 benefit: ↓ warts & infections); plerixafor showed neutrophil ↑ and wart improvement in early trials.
  • Local wart therapies often fail without correcting underlying immune defect.
Prognosis / Complications
  • Significant morbidity from recurrent infections and HPV-related disease; prognosis depends on infection control and response to targeted therapy.
High-Yield Exam Pearls
  • Pathognomonic paradox: severe peripheral neutropenia + normal/hypercellular marrow with mature neutrophils = myelokathexis.
  • If you see extensive, recalcitrant warts → check CBC (neutrophils) and quantitative IgG.
  • Therapy logic: CXCR4 GOF → CXCR4 antagonists (mavorixafor, plerixafor) are disease-targeted treatments.
Rapid tip: For MCQs — Warts + neutropenia + low IgG → think WHIM; next steps are CBC + IgG then CXCR4 testing. Targeted therapy = CXCR4 inhibitors.
